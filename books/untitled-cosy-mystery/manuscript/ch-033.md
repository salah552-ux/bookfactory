# CHAPTER THIRTY-THREE
## Assembly

She sat in David's armchair on the Thursday morning after the arrest, with her notebook in her lap and the small spiral pad she had carried from court for thirty years, and she walked through it. In order. From the beginning.

It was not for Claire — Claire had a charge, a warrant, a cross-referenced file. Not for Edmund, who had done his own accounting at the Chapter House window. Perhaps not even for herself. But Helen had spent thirty-five years in rooms where the dead needed to be spoken for clearly, and the habit did not leave because the case was closed. She would do it the way she had always done it: in order, from the first thing she had written.

---

The mug.

It was the first thing she had written in her notebook. One word, that morning, while Claire was still finding her way through the close gate and Thomas was answering the same questions for the fourth time. The mug was on the second tread of the stair, in the angle of the wall, and it was plain white china, and it was scrupulously clean. Not left-behind-in-a-hurry clean. Not rinsed-perfunctorily clean. The kind of clean that comes from a deliberate wipe-down, the inner base only partially caught, the residue ring just barely visible when the light was at the right angle.

She had known, in the sixty-second forensic survey she had given herself before the police process began, that the mug meant something. She had not said so aloud, because a mug being clean is not evidence of anything on its own. She had noted it and waited. Four weeks later, the lab report: digoxin in coffee solution. The wiping had failed at the inner base. Whatever Rosalind had used to clean it — a damp cloth, a sleeve, something grabbed in the dark — had not quite reached the curved join at the bottom.

Yes. She had seen that. She had not understood it. Now she did.

---

The medication.

Edmund had found the gap. A row of bottles on Marcus's desk, arranged by descending height, and the digoxin missing from its place as if it had simply not been restocked. He had mentioned it to Claire as an afterthought — his voice entirely level, the way Edmund's voice became leveller as the stakes increased, which was a reliable indicator she had come to depend on across these weeks. She had watched Claire note it and file it.

Then the archive. Bottle behind the 1830s parish registers, two tablets remaining, cap loose. Her hands had trembled, briefly. She noted this without judgment. The bottle was Marcus's own prescription, his batch number, the label from Wychford Pharmacy, dated January. And across the close, in a private dispensary in Clifton, a second bottle was already logged as dispensed: Mrs. B. Ferris, 11 March. The name of a woman who had been dead for eighteen months.

Yes. She had seen the missing bottle. She had understood it eventually. The second bottle she had not anticipated — no one had, entirely — but when it arrived in the evidence chain, it landed with the particular thud of something that was always going to be there.

---

The rehearsal.

Rosalind had told her, in the kitchen at No. 7, with shortbread on the plate and the afternoon light coming in through the window and the grief between them entirely genuine: she had gone to the organ room at half-past eight to remind Marcus about Thursday's choir practice. She had been there ten minutes. She had come home.

Helen's wall calendar — the cathedral printed schedule, posted to every close residence in January — had been pinned above the kettle for four months. She had not looked at it while Rosalind was there. She had looked at it that evening: Thursday rehearsal cancelled three weeks earlier, in Rosalind's own marker pen. "Cancelled — Confirmation rehearsals taking precedence." Rosalind's hand. The cancellation Rosalind herself had made.

And Julian, on the close path at half past nine, had seen Marcus leaving Rosalind's house. Walking steadily toward the north transept. Not at eight-thirty. Not at a quarter to nine. At nine-thirty, or close to it. A discrepancy of sixty minutes, in which a great deal had been decided and done.

Yes. She had heard the lie. She had not understood the shape of it immediately. Later she had understood it completely.

---

The money.

Edmund's audit had found forty-seven off-pattern payments across twelve years. Small amounts — individually unnoticeable, cumulatively approximately seven thousand pounds a year — disbursed to names that were, on examination, shelf entities, PO boxes, nothing at the end of the address. J. Whitaker, L. Pellow Print Services, Aldgate Choral Supplies. Three accounts, rotated. The audit had been the scene in which Edmund had written in his notebook: *I wonder if Rosalind has known the policy lapsed.* Two underlines beneath "wonder." His code, which she had learned to read.

And the money went to Clifton, Bristol, to a sort code that resolved to an account in the name of Mrs. Bridget Ferris. A woman in the Clifton Convalescent Home. Specialist dementia care, approximately four thousand pounds a month. A woman who had needed looking after, and had received it, and had died eighteen months ago. A woman whose daughter had continued to serve the cathedral that funded her care, conducting services and leading worship and offering tea to the bereaved, for twelve years without any of them knowing.

Yes. She had seen the money. She had understood the motive before the method was confirmed, which was, she thought, the correct order. Method without motive is mechanics. Motive is what makes the thing human and therefore comprehensible and therefore, in its way, unbearable.

---

The cardigan.

Thomas Oakes had mentioned it on the first morning, almost as an afterthought, while Claire was still processing the scene. A pale blue cardigan, hip-length, probably cashmere, small folded collar, a woman's — he had thought it was Rosalind because she owned one, he had seen it on her at evensong twice that week. He had been thirty minutes late on his rounds because his niece had rung. He had come around into the close at four minutes past eleven and seen the figure leaving the stair door: five foot six, five foot seven, brisk walk, hair up.

Four weeks later, a forensic imaging contractor in Bristol had enhanced seven stills from a Song School corridor camera. Double-rib collar construction. Cabled button band. A specific cashmere garment, consistent with high-quality handknit or limited production. Stride asymmetry index 0.04. The figure had walked through the storeroom with the familiarity of someone who had been in that space many times, over thirty years, without needing to think about where the walls were.

Yes. She had seen the cardigan. She had seen it on Rosalind's shoulders at the memorial service — pale blue over charcoal — and at evensong and at Chapter meetings and on an ordinary Tuesday afternoon at a kitchen table with shortbread on the plate. She had noted it and filed it and waited and now the filing cabinet was closed.

---

She sat with it for a moment. The full picture, assembled for the last time, each piece in its correct position, each one readable now in a way it had not been entirely readable when it was first placed in her hands. This was the reader's reward, she supposed — the backwards comprehension, the small clicks of *yes, that was always it.* She had been, this time, both the reader and the detective, and the feeling was more complicated than she had expected.

The clock on the mantle marked eleven. Outside, the cathedral was exactly there, stone and glass and the first cold light of a Thursday morning.

She picked up her phone. She put it down. She picked it up again and sent Edmund a message: *All right?* 

Two minutes later, his reply: *Adequately so. You?*

She considered this. She typed: *The same.* She sent it. She put the phone down.

That was all, for now. It was enough.
