# CHAPTER THREE
## Sixty Seconds

Helen took the long way round and crouched.

She did not touch him. The floor was the cold polished stone she had walked over for three years on her way to evensong and her hands knew it before her knees did. She put one hand flat on the flagstone, six inches from Marcus's right shoulder, and steadied herself.

Sixty seconds, in her old job, had been the working unit.

The first ten seconds were for posture.

He lay supine. Head turned a quarter to the left. Arms by his sides. Legs almost straight, the right slightly crossed over the left at the ankle, as though someone had taken a moment to arrange him. His green cardigan was buttoned. The collar of his shirt was tucked correctly. Not a button askew, not a cuff ridden up. The fall of an unconscious man from eighteen feet did not produce this. The fall of an unconscious man from eighteen feet produced a thing one had to read twice. This was a body laid out for viewing. She noted it. She did not say it.

The second ten seconds were for the hands.

Marcus's hands were entirely undamaged.

A man falling backwards from a gallery puts his hands out. Reflex — older than thought. The wrists break, the palms abrade, the fingers splay. Marcus's hands were clean and intact and lay slightly cupped, palms to the floor, exactly where a man would let his hands rest if he were already unconscious when he came over the rail.

The third ten seconds were for temperature.

She put the back of her hand, briefly, against the side of his throat — light, professional, the touch she had been making for forty years on bodies in worse conditions than this. The skin was cool. Not cold, not yet. But cool in a way that did not match seven o'clock as time of death. Marcus had been dead for longer than the discovery suggested. Hours longer. She would not commit to a number without a thermometer; but she would commit, in court, to "longer than three hours and certainly less than ten." That put the death some time before five in the morning. It put the death, possibly, before four. It put the death, possibly, around the time someone had been playing the organ in the dark.

She closed her eyes for half a second.

The fourth ten seconds were for the field of view.

She lifted her head and looked, slowly, in a half-circle. The base of the pillar. The cracked glasses. The chess-square of polished stone. A music score, fanned open, two yards behind him on the floor, as though it had been placed there. The base of the stone stair, with a single thumbprint of dust disturbed on the lowest tread. The door of the stair, now closed (Thomas, she remembered).

She came back to Marcus.

The fifth ten seconds were for the small thing.

There was always a small thing. Forty years had taught her this, more reliably than any of the formal forensic protocols she had drilled into the trainees. The body and the floor would tell you what they were going to tell you. The small thing was different. The small thing was the detail the killer hadn't accounted for, or the detail the dying person had reached for in the last half-minute, or — most often — the detail that did not know it was a detail and would have to be argued for later in a courtroom against barristers paid to make it look ordinary.

She let her eyes drift.

Marcus's left foot. A button on the cuff of his cardigan, slightly loose, the thread frayed. The polished stone. The base of the choir stalls.

The mug.

It sat on the second tread of the stone stair, in the angle of the wall, where Thomas's torch beam had not reached and where the cathedral's overhead lights only fell at a glance. White china. Plain. The cathedral kitchen mug — she had drunk from one a hundred times. It sat squarely on its base. It was unmissable when you looked at it. It had simply not been the thing one looked at first.

She rose, carefully, and crossed three paces and crouched again.

The mug was empty. The mug was clean.

That was what was wrong with it.

A coffee mug abandoned on a stair after an evening of organ practice, by a man who had been at the organ since before nine the previous evening, would have a residue. A faint dark ring at the bottom. A meniscus mark a quarter of an inch up the inside. The cathedral kitchen washed mugs with a vigour that left them streaked but never spotless; coffee in china at midnight, left for seven hours, dried into a film that did not lift without effort.

This mug was not streaked. This mug was not filmed. This mug was the colour of china in a cupboard.

Helen looked at it for ten seconds without moving.

She thought, in the precise flat way she had thought through five thousand mornings like this: the mug has been wiped.

She rose, and she took her notebook out of her pocket — a small spiral-bound thing, a habit kept from court — and she wrote, in pencil, in her small hand, three lines, of which the third was the word *mug* with no further comment, because she did not yet wish to commit to the further comment in writing, in her own hand, in a building that contained Edmund and Thomas and Rosalind and the Dean and the body of a man she had liked.

She put the notebook away.

The minute was up. She turned and went back to Edmund.

—

He was at the screen with Thomas. Thomas was pale. Edmund looked at her face and did not ask the question.

She said, very quietly, "He didn't fall, Edmund. Look at his hands."

Edmund looked.

She watched him see it. The sequence of his face was a small and terrible thing: he saw the hands, and he saw the position, and he saw the absence of damage, and he understood. He turned his face away from the body before he had finished understanding, and she saw the cost of it pass through him.

He said, "Thomas. Tell Helen what you told me."

Thomas told it again. He had come at six fifty. He had walked in. He had seen Marcus. He had touched his neck. He had rung Edmund. He had closed the door of the stair before he saw, because the door was not supposed to be open. He was sorry.

"Don't apologise, Thomas," Helen said. "Anything else? Anything from yesterday evening, last night, the rounds — anything that struck you?"

Thomas thought.

"Nothing struck me, Mrs Marsh. Nothing — well. There was a woman in a cardigan."

Helen said, "When?"

"Eleven o'clock. About. I was a bit late on rounds — I don't know if I should — anyway. I came across the close at, well, gone eleven. Pale blue cardigan. Coming out of the stair door. Could have been Mrs Ferris. Could have been anyone. I didn't think much of it." He looked at Edmund. "The Precentor checks on him sometimes. Of an evening."

"You didn't see her face."

"I didn't, Mrs Marsh. I was at the south end."

"Cashmere?"

"Pardon?"

"The cardigan. Was it cashmere."

Thomas blinked. "I couldn't say. It looked — soft. Soft-looking. A woman's, I'd say. The way it sat on her."

"Thank you, Thomas."

Thomas, accepting the dismissal with relief, withdrew to the south porch, where he stood looking at his hands.

Edmund waited.

"The mug on the stair is wiped," Helen said. "He has not fallen. He has been arranged. The pathologist will tell us what killed him. I will tell you now that he was dead by five at the latest and possibly earlier. The cardigan needs to be on record." She paused. "Edmund. Are you all right."

He said, very dryly, "I'm a cathedral lawyer. I'm never all right at this hour."

She permitted herself, briefly, a small movement of the mouth.

The siren came in then, full and close, and a moment later DS Claire Marsh came up the south aisle at the steady walk of a woman whose day had just acquired a paragraph she had not budgeted for.

Claire stopped six feet from Helen and looked at her.

She said, "Oh God."

Helen said, "Hello, Claire."
