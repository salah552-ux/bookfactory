# CHAPTER FORTY
## Something in the Archive

Helen's photograph arrived on his phone at twelve-fourteen, which was the middle of the Chapter meeting, and he did not look at it until the meeting was over.

He went to his office afterward and sat at the cherrywood table — the audit table, the table on which he had spread the music fund records across four weeks in April and found the shape of something he had not wanted to find — and opened the photograph.

It was a letter. He could see that from the first second: the format, the quality of paper even through a phone screen, the formal salutation and the brevity. He pinched the image to enlarge it and read it once, carefully, in the way he read any legal document — the date first, then the addressee, then the body of the text, then the signature.

July 1887.

To the Hartley family.

*In settlement of the matter of the east transept.*

A payment acknowledged — no amount, he noted; amounts in settlement letters were sometimes omitted when the specifics of the payment were themselves the thing being sealed. The Dean's signature, in the forward-leaning hand of the Victorians. And the final line: *The Chapter is grateful for the family's discretion and understanding.*

He looked at the photograph for a moment longer. Then he put his phone face-down on the table and looked out the window at the cloister garth.

The Chapter House was built in the thirteenth century and extensively rebuilt in 1503, a fact he had known since his first month at Wychford and periodically told visitors who asked about the vaulted ceiling. The archive beneath it was the original cellar — pre-thirteenth century, or at least so the 2003 archaeological survey had concluded, though the survey had been commissioned for the heating upgrade and its conclusions were not as thorough as he might have wished. The archive held parish records back to 1347 — he knew this because Helen had told him, in the first week of her tenure as archive volunteer, in the way she told you things she found interesting: as facts, without ornament, delivered as if information were a courtesy and not a performance.

He picked up his phone again. He enlarged the letter once more.

The east transept.

He had worked in this building for twenty-two years. He knew the architecture reasonably well — the Norman foundation, the Early English nave, the Perpendicular tower that was the thing most people photographed. He knew the fabric committee's concerns: the south tower cracking (the Dean's property deal), the west porch pointing that needed attention, the stained glass conservation programme that had been underfunded for twelve years. He knew what was in the maintenance reports going back to 2003, because his office managed the long-term fabric schedule.

He did not know what matter, in the east transept, had required a settlement payment to a family called Hartley in July of 1887.

He sat with this for a moment. Then he opened his notebook — the amber-covered one, bought the previous week — and on the second page wrote: *Hartley family — July 1887 — east transept — settlement — cf. Helen's photograph, 14 May.* He paused. He wrote: *Rose Hartley. Song school.* He drew a line between the two entries. Then he wrote: *I think there is something in the archive.*

He underlined the last sentence once.

He looked across the cloister to the song school. He could not see it directly from the cherrywood table — he would have had to move to the window — but he knew it was there, on the south side, behind the chapter house wall, accessible via the corridor whose CCTV he had spent a significant portion of April examining. Rose Hartley was in it, most days, managing the children's schedule and the choir's practice calendar and the song school's small administrative life with the competence of someone who had been there five years and understood the rhythm of the place. He knew her by sight and by passing greeting and by her name on the Song School key ledger. He had not spoken with her at any length during the investigation, because she was not part of the investigation, because she had nothing to do with Marcus Vayne's death, because she was simply one of the forty people who lived and worked in the close and who had had, as they all had, a difficult six weeks.

Her family's name was in a July 1887 letter in Helen's archive. The letter contained the phrase *settlement of the matter of the east transept* and the Dean's express gratitude for *discretion and understanding.*

He did not pursue this. It was not, today, his to pursue. It was a whisper — Helen's word, or what he imagined would be Helen's word for it: a whisper, not a revelation. Today was a Tuesday in May with a Chapter meeting and a fabric committee report to review and a sentencing character statement to begin drafting and an archive volunteer who had found something she had not been looking for and had photographed it and sent it to him without explanation, because that was how Helen communicated the things she thought were worth attention.

He rang her.

She answered on the second ring. "I wondered how long," she said.

"Fourteen minutes from the end of the Chapter meeting," he said. "I went to my office first."

"I know. I could hear the meeting from the archive."

He looked at the notebook, at the two entries and the line between them. "The Hartley family," he said. "It's not a common name in Wychford."

"No," said Helen. "It isn't."

A pause. Outside his window, or near it, the organ had begun the pre-Evensong voluntary: a chorale prelude, something Lutheran and unhurried, the notes building above the nave with the particular patience of music that is not in a hurry to arrive because it is confident of arriving. The coloured light from the east window would be falling across the choir stalls now, the blue and amber bars of it that were there every afternoon at this hour, moving as the sun moved, in the slow reliable way of things that have been doing the same thing for several hundred years.

"I think there's something in the archive," he said.

A brief silence. Then Helen said: "I know. I've thought so for some time."

He looked at the page in his notebook. The two entries. The line. The underlined sentence.

He closed the notebook — the amber-covered one, bought the previous week, the first entry in which was *May 1. Morning Prayer. Reeve, collect. Three in the congregation.* The second entry: *I chose truth. The institution survived it. I had not expected that.* The third: *Hartley family — July 1887 — east transept — settlement.* Three pages, and the investigation done, and the city still outside and the cathedral still there and the morning prayer still at seven.

He put the phone down. He picked up the fabric committee report. There was work to do.

The organ played. The coloured light fell through the east window in the way it had fallen, at this hour, for the entirety of the building's existence, landing on floor and stall and stone in the colours the glaziers of the thirteenth century had not known they were choosing but had chosen anyway, because glass in a window that faces into the afternoon light is always going to become something the maker did not intend. The close continued. The cathedral continued.

It was not finished with them yet.
