# CHAPTER EIGHT
## The Audit

Edmund had asked for the music fund disbursement records back to 2014, and the cathedral office had sent them in the form of three pdfs and one apologetic email about a printer that was failing in stages. He printed the pdfs himself, on his own printer, in the corner of his office where the spider plant was failing in stages of its own.

He laid the printouts in date order across the cherrywood table by the window.

The morning was bright.

The cathedral office had given him, in addition, a single-line summary spreadsheet: total disbursed per quarter, going back to the financial year 2014–15. He had printed that too and laid it across the bottom of the row, like a footing.

He sat down with his tea and his battered notebook and a green ballpoint pen — the green ballpoint was a small private vanity, used only for audit work — and he began at the top.

The shape was almost immediately visible.

Each quarter, three or four small disbursements. None individually larger than three hundred pounds. Most around the hundred-and-fifty mark — payable, in each case, to a different supplier. Sheet music. A printer's invoice for the choir programme. A travel claim for a chorister recital. The names of the suppliers were drawn from a small, plausible, varying pool.

In each quarter, one of these disbursements was off-pattern.

The off-pattern one was always the smallest. Always between sixty and a hundred and ten pounds. Always payable, in each case, to a name Edmund had never previously encountered. The names were not invented in the careless way the inexperienced fraudster invents names. They were small, plausible, English names — *J. Whitaker, Music Educational Resources*; *L. Pellow Print Services*; *Aldgate Choral Supplies*. Names that one would not look at twice if one were skimming.

He skimmed the first sheet. He looked at it as he would have looked at it three years ago, before any of this had happened. He saw nothing. He approved every line.

Then he went back and looked properly.

He went back and looked at the addresses. The addresses were post-office boxes. Three different post-office boxes, all in Bristol. Quarter by quarter, the boxes rotated.

He traced them in pencil on the printout. He drew a small ringed *X* by each.

By half past eleven he had counted forty-seven such payments since 2014. He had not yet completed the spreadsheet sum. He would shortly do so. The visible cumulative figure was, he estimated, somewhere on the wrong side of seven thousand a year.

He sat back. He put his pen down.

He did not, in his notebook, write a name.

He wrote: *forty-seven items. All from a small set of three Bristol PO boxes. Pattern is consistent. Pattern is sustained. Pattern is procedurally undetectable from the disbursement record alone unless one is looking for it. There is a discrepancy of approximately twelve years' standing.*

He underlined the word *discrepancy*. He did not underline it twice.

He thought, with the small clean discipline he had cultivated for thirty years of canon-law work: *I cannot yet say who. I can say that.*

Then he stood, and he walked the length of the office twice, and he came back to the cherrywood table and looked at the rows of paper and let himself feel, for one minute, what it had cost a person to construct this — to construct anything, twelve years long, this carefully, this small. The discipline of it. The patience of it. The not-greed of it.

He thought: this is not the work of a person who wanted to get rich.

He thought: this is the work of a person who needed not to be caught.

He sat back down.

—

A knock at the door.

He had not heard footsteps in the corridor. The corridor was wood and the corridor was loud, and he had not heard them, which meant he had been somewhere else inside himself for some time.

"Edmund."

It was Rosalind.

She had a tray. The tray had a teapot, two cups, a small jug, and a plate of biscuits which Edmund recognised from Margaret's tin. Rosalind came in carefully, set the tray on the corner of his desk that was not covered in printouts, and looked round.

"You're working."

"I am."

"I've brought you tea. Margaret made me promise. She said you would be missing your eleven o'clock and that she would not on her own conscience let that happen."

"Margaret is a precious resource."

"She is."

He turned slightly so that the cherrywood table was, by the angle of his body, at his back.

Rosalind did not glance at the table. He noted that she did not glance at it. He noted that she noted not glancing at it. The two of them stood, in his office, and her hand poured the tea, and her hand did not shake.

"Sit, Rosalind."

She sat. She poured his cup first and then her own. The pour was even.

"You've been at the funeral arrangements."

"I have," she said. "The bishop's office want him at the cathedral on Friday. The family — there's a sister, Edmund, did you know — the sister wants him in Cumbria. They're talking. I've offered to draft a service that could be carried at either end. There are two pieces that he wrote. If she'll allow them, we'd —" She paused. "I keep talking about what to play. I think it's because I cannot bear to talk about anything else."

"That is allowed, Rosalind."

"How are you, Edmund."

"Tired. I have been a cathedral lawyer for twenty-two years. I am a cathedral lawyer most acutely on days like this."

She smiled. The smile was small and warm. He did not allow himself to read it for any other content.

"What are you working on," she said.

She did not turn her head to look at the cherrywood table. She did not, in fact, look at anything that was not him. The question was offered in the tone of polite enquiry, and the tone was perfect.

"Finance," he said, with what he hoped was the appropriate absence of weight. "Routine. The Dean wants the books in order before the Bishop's secretary asks any questions."

"Of course she does."

"I am being a slow Edmund."

"You are being a thorough Edmund," Rosalind said. She lifted her cup in both hands. "Edmund. May I ask you something."

"You may."

"Will the inquiry — will the police inquiry — will it look at our finances. The cathedral's. The funds. Or is that —"

He took his time. He had time.

"It will," he said. "If a death is suspicious, the SIO will satisfy herself that there are no parallel pressures. Money is a common parallel pressure. She will look. She will be courteous about it."

"I see."

"Is there a fund you are concerned about."

He had asked it without meaning to. The asking had emerged from a thirty-year habit and not from a decision.

She looked at him. Her face did not change. Her hands, on the cup, did not change. She said, "I am concerned about all of them. I am the precentor. I should like everything to look exactly as it is."

He said, "It will."

"Thank you, Edmund."

She stayed for nine more minutes. They talked about the funeral. They talked about Father Reeve's growing competence with the responses. They talked about the pity of an instrument left unplayed and the difficulty of finding a substitute who was not also vain.

She left, taking the empty teapot back on the tray.

He sat with the door closed behind her for some minutes before he returned to the cherrywood table.

—

At three he was in the cloister, on his way out of the cathedral office, when he met Webb coming the other way.

Webb stopped on the flag path. He was wearing his black overcoat and the expression of a man for whom the day had so far provided no comfortable place to put himself.

"Edmund."

"Alistair."

"They have been asking. The police. About me."

"Yes."

"It is — it is not a moment in which I expected to be asked questions of this kind."

"No."

"I would very much like, Edmund, for you to know — for the cathedral to know — that I have nothing to do with whatever happened. To Marcus. I had — Marcus and I had differences. Of long standing. They are known. But nothing of — nothing of *that*."

Edmund said, "Alistair. The police are speaking to everyone. You among them."

"Yes."

"Did you tell them where you were."

The pause was longer than it should have been.

"I did."

"Have you told them everything."

The pause this time was very long indeed.

"Edmund —"

"I am not, Alistair, asking on behalf of the police. I am asking on behalf of you."

Webb looked at him. His face did a small, frightened thing. He said, "I will speak to Anne first."

He moved past Edmund and went on up the cloister.

Edmund watched him go. He thought, with the small flat clarity he had been keeping all day: *that is a man with a secret, and the secret is real, and the secret is probably not what we are looking for.*

He went back into his office and wrote it in his notebook in two lines.

Then he went to the cherrywood table and counted, for the second time, to forty-seven.
