# CHAPTER SIXTEEN
## Keys

Edmund took the key ledger to the desk by the window and opened it to the current-year entries.

It was a paper ledger, spiral-bound, in use since 1994, replaced three times without anyone changing the format. He uncapped the green pen and read the first line: Rosalind Ferris, Precentor, issued the same year she took the role — thirty years ago. Then Marcus Vayne, Director of Music. Then Rose Hartley, Song School administrator, five years ago on appointment. Three keys. Three people.

He turned back through the history pages.

The key was a medium-weight brass, of a specific cut that Edmund had seen before on the Song School gates. He noted the serial numbers in his notebook: RKF-001 (Rosalind), RKF-002 (Marcus — now held by police in evidence), RKF-003 (Rose Hartley).

In the thirty years since Rosalind had taken the role, no Song School key had been issued to a canon with pastoral responsibilities, which was Webb's role. No Song School key had been issued to the Dean. No Song School key had been issued to any member of the cathedral Chapter who was not the Precentor or the Director of Music. This was not an oversight — the earlier policy pages showed that the key access was restricted by formal Chapter resolution in 1998, which Edmund turned to: *Resolved: that Song School master key access be limited to Precentor, Director of Music, and Song School administrative staff, and that no additional keys be issued except by majority resolution of the full Chapter.*

The resolution bore the signatures of six Chapter members. Canon Alistair Webb's name was not among them; he had not been a member of the Chapter in 1998.

Edmund closed the ledger.

Webb had never held a Song School key. He had never been given a basis to request one. His name was absent from every page of the thirty-year record. The archive corridor, which lay behind the Song School and was accessible via the Song School's southern interior door, could only be reached from within the building — and the building was locked from seven-thirty in the evening to eight in the morning, opened only by the master key.

Edmund wrote in his notebook: *Webb — no access to Song School corridor. Ever. Confirmed by ledger, by 1998 resolution, by no extant request.* He underlined the word *confirmed* once.

He took the ledger to Claire in the afternoon.

She received it without expression, which meant she had expected it. She made two notes. She said: "I'll need the Precentor's key traced through the month of March." Edmund said he would ask. He did not say: there is no tracing it, because the key is a physical object and the ledger only records issuance and return, and there was no requirement for Precentor keys to be logged out daily. She knew this. He saw the knowledge in her face. They were both, at this point, in the position of knowing what they knew and having to build the formal record around it.

The Chapter meeting was at three o'clock, which was unusual — Chapter meetings were first Tuesdays — but Patricia Knowles had called an emergency session to address the investigation's impact on the cathedral's pastoral programme. Edmund had come to these meetings for twenty-two years. He had seen them go well and he had seen them go very badly and he had seen one, in 2007, go so badly that the Dean of that era had retired abruptly the following spring. He knew the room's temperature by the time the second person sat down.

Today's temperature was anxious, which was appropriate, and the anxiety was the kind that was looking for an outlet.

Rosalind chaired the standing orders section in Patricia Knowles's absence — the Dean had a diocesan commitment and had arranged for Rosalind, as Precentor, to open the meeting. This was standard procedure. Edmund had seen it a dozen times.

What he watched today was different from what he had seen before.

She came in three minutes before three and set her papers at the head of the table with a precision that was not visible as precision — it looked simply like organisation, the natural efficiency of a person who had chaired things before. She had a printed agenda, distributed at each place. She had a bottle of water and a glass. She had a pen, uncapped, and she did not look at it, which meant she had arranged it exactly where she wanted it without needing to check. The pale blue cardigan was over a grey wool dress today; she had a small silver brooch at the collar, a treble clef, which Edmund had seen her wear at chapter meetings before.

The anxious room settled fractionally when she began to speak. That was the thing he watched most carefully, because he had been present at the beginning of the investigation and he had been present at the memorial and he had been present in the cloister and the corridor and the correspondence ledger and he had held five things in his hand and had written "I wonder if it has been Rosalind" and underlined "wonder" twice. And yet the room settled.

"We are here," she said, opening the meeting with the ordinary formula, "to address the practical matters arising from the ongoing inquiry into the death of our colleague Marcus Vayne." She did not say *murder*. She said *death*. Edmund noted this without knowing if it was significant; he had heard her say *death* since the beginning, and he could not determine whether this was institutional habit, professional avoidance, or something more precise.

She went through the agenda items with a quiet efficiency that she did not need to perform, because it was simply what she was — organised, thorough, two steps ahead of any question before it was asked. The choir provision for the summer term: she had the schedule already drafted, distributed, and she was open to revisions. The pastoral care of the choristers: she had spoken with three families already and had a contact at the diocesan education office for additional support. The organ: she had been in conversation with the cathedral's organ consultants about temporary cover and a schedule for the autumn.

Father Reeve looked relieved. Margaret Holt, who appeared at Chapter meetings as Chapter clerk despite being officially retired from the clerk's role, made notes in her own personal shorthand that Edmund had never successfully decoded. Rose Hartley, who attended as Song School representative, said nothing, as she almost always said nothing.

Edmund watched Rosalind. He watched her hands — she made small, precise gestures when she was emphasising a point, the kind of gestures that drew attention to the thing being said rather than to the person saying it. He watched her eyes, which moved around the table in the way of a person who was managing a room and doing it well, catching each face long enough to make them feel addressed and not long enough to put them under pressure.

This was, he thought, the thing that made the difference between a person who was capable of what she had done and a person who was not. Not cruelty. Not absence of conscience. But the ability to continue. To sit at the head of a table and manage a room with exactly the competence she had always managed it with, because the competence was real, and the devotion to the cathedral was real, and the grief she expressed for Marcus was not entirely performed, and the person who had made a decision on the eleventh of March and had gone to Bristol and had come back and had waited and had then done what she had done — that person was the same person as the one who was now distributing choir schedules.

That was the thing, Edmund thought, that a person had to understand before they could understand any of this. Not: how could she have done it. But: how did she become the person who could.

He wrote nothing in his notebook. He was very careful about his notebook in the current period.

After the meeting, Rosalind stopped him in the corridor.

"Edmund," she said. She touched his arm briefly — the kind of touch that was warmth, not supplication. "How are you finding it? The whole thing."

He thought about the question and answered it accurately. "I find it," he said, "as I expect everyone finds it. Difficult in ways I hadn't anticipated."

"Yes," she said. "I think that's right." She looked down the corridor toward the entrance, the cold light coming in from the close. "We'll get through it. The cathedral always does."

"Yes," said Edmund.

She left. He stood in the corridor for a moment, looking at the space where she had been.

She was very good at this.
