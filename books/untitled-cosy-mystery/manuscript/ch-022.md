# CHAPTER TWENTY-TWO
## Building

He uncapped the green pen and wrote: *Rosalind Ferris. What we know.*

Then, in a numbered column, the facts. Not the inferences — the facts, each one verifiable, each one underlined only after he had satisfied himself it would hold in a courtroom. He had done this since he was a pupil barrister at Chancery Lane, sitting in a library at eleven at night with three sets of contradictory evidence. The system worked because it required him to be plain. He was, in his private opinion, not naturally plain. The system corrected for this.

He wrote the first line. Then the second.

*1. Music fund — sole signatory — twelve years.*
*2. Three PO box accounts confirmed as shells (Bristol).*
*3. Destination: Mrs. Bridget Ferris account, Clifton branch. Care home fees.*
*4. Withdrawals began twelve years ago. Stopped eighteen months ago — within one month of Bridget Ferris's death.*
*5. Marcus discovers irregularity — three months before story. Formal complaint letter, 14 March — intercepted by Rosalind, never forwarded to the Bishop.*
*6. Second digoxin bottle: Avon Private Dispensary, Clifton. Cash. 11 March. Batch DA-6650/11. Patient: Mrs. B. Ferris. GMC number fraudulent.*
*7. Coffee mug: wiped. Digoxin traces in residue. Item 17.*
*8. Rosalind stated she visited Marcus at 8:30pm to remind him about Thursday rehearsal. Julian Marsh saw Marcus leaving her house at 9:30pm. Thursday rehearsal cancelled three weeks earlier in Rosalind's own hand.*
*9. Thomas Oakes: pale blue cardigan, ~5'6"–5'7", brisk walk, 11:04pm, leaving organ loft stair door.*
*10. Song School CCTV: pale hip-length garment, storeroom access, 22:15–22:26. Eleven minutes.*

He stopped. He looked at the list.

Then he turned to the back of his notebook where he kept a separate running account — the financial analysis, which he had been cross-referencing since the full audit in the third week of the inquiry. He had noted the music fund withdrawals in a supplementary column with dates: every month for twelve years, small, rotating through the three shell accounts. In the past week he had been doing something more specific, which he had told no one about yet, not even Helen, because he had not been quite certain he was right until this afternoon.

He had taken the music fund payment records for March — the final month before the murder — and he had laid them against the dispensary record.

Avon Private Dispensary. Cash. 11 March. Digoxin 0.25mg, sixty tablets. The price of such a purchase from a private dispensary without an NHS prescription: he had rung the pharmacy association's general line and asked, without giving his name, what a private out-of-pocket payment for sixty tablets of digoxin at the 0.25mg dose would have cost in March. The voice on the line — a woman, distracted, eating something — said it depended on the dispensary's pricing policy but a fair estimate was sixty to ninety-five pounds, cash.

He looked at his notes.

On 8 March, the music fund had disbursed £83 to J. Whitaker, the Clifton PO box. The last of the three-month cycle. This was not unusual — the pattern had held for twelve years. What was unusual was the date: eight March, three days before the dispensary visit. A cash withdrawal from a PO box account, untraceable, £83.

He wrote: *£83 — 8 March — J. Whitaker — music fund. Dispensary visit: 11 March. Dispensary estimate: £60–£95.*

He circled both numbers. He drew a line between them.

Concordat, he thought. Omnia probat — *It proves everything.* In this context, it proved that the same money that had funded her mother's care for twelve years had, at the end, funded the instrument of Marcus's death. The embezzlement and the murder were not two decisions. They were one decision, followed to its conclusion.

He sat with that for a while. A lamp from No. 6 across the close came on — orange through the leaded glass — and then another, and then the cathedral's own night lighting caught the east window and threw a dim rose-coloured shape across the cloister garth. Edmund heard the close gate click. Someone returning from town.

He thought about what Rosalind had said when she brought him tea during the audit. *I should like everything to look exactly as it is.* She had said it with perfect equanimity. She had been standing three feet from the cherrywood table where his audit papers were laid out, and she had said it without any flicker, and Edmund had heard only the sentence, not what was underneath it.

He heard it now.

What it was underneath: *Tell me what you can see. I need to know how close you are.*

He wrote the timeline on a fresh page:

*Marcus leaves Rosalind's house: 9:30pm (Julian Marsh, witness).*
*Marcus at organ, coffee administered: estimated 9:30–10pm.*
*Marcus loses consciousness: estimated 10–10:30pm.*
*Marcus falls from gallery: 10:30pm (estimated — post-mortem temperature consistent).*
*Rosalind accesses storeroom (CCTV): 22:15–22:26.*
*Thomas Oakes sees figure leaving loft: 23:04.*

The sequence was internally consistent. The gaps were not gaps — they were the natural intervals of a plan being executed by a person who was calm enough to wait.

She had brought him coffee. She had stayed long enough for him to drink it. And then she had waited, somewhere in the organ loft, in the dark, for the digoxin to work.

Edmund looked at that sentence in his notebook for some time. Then he underlined it once.

He wrote the one thing that was not yet certain: *CCTV enhancement — 2 days. Cardigan confirmation.*

Without the enhancement, the figure in the corridor was a pale hip-length garment and a familiar walk. Suggestive. Not conclusive. With the enhancement, it would be the cardigan — the specific collar, the specific weave, the garment that Thomas Oakes had described as cashmere, powder pale blue, and that had been seen on Rosalind at evensong within the same week.

One gap remained. He did not name the gap. He circled the empty space in his notebook and waited.
