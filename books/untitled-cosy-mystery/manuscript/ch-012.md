# CHAPTER TWELVE
## The Letter

The complaint letter from Marcus was in the ledger.

Registered. Signed for. *R. Ferris, 14 March* — the same careful forward-leaning hand he had seen on the cancellation note in the music binder. He had found it at eleven o'clock on a Tuesday morning, standing at the cherrywood table, and he had stood with his finger on the line for a very long time before turning the page.

The forwarding column was blank.

---

The south tower had been cracking since 1987.

Edmund learned this on a Tuesday morning from Patricia Knowles, who had asked him to come to the deanery at nine and who had then kept him standing in her front hall for eleven minutes while she made tea she did not offer him, and who had then sat him down in her study and told him, in a tone that managed to combine genuine embarrassment with an implicit accusation that he was partly responsible for the situation, that the south tower structural survey — the one she had commissioned privately in January — had found a fault in the south transept buttressing that would cost, conservatively, half a million pounds to address properly and which, if left unaddressed, would begin to be visible to the casual visitor within a decade.

"The Chapter," she said, "would have buried it in consultation."

Edmund thought: that is why we have a Chapter. But he had learned, over twenty-two years, when to say what he thought and when to sit quietly and make the expression of a man who had no immediate objection to offer. "And the property?"

"Glebe land. Twelve acres on the Wychford Abbots road. Agricultural, mostly, but the planning authority has indicated informally — very informally — that it might countenance a change of use application for residential development." She turned her teacup in its saucer. "I have been in conversation with a developer. The value, at a conservative estimate, would cover the south tower work and leave the cathedral solvent into the next decade."

Edmund said: "Against Chapter rules."

"Yes."

"Without consultation."

"Yes." She looked at him directly. "I am not Marcus, Edmund. I am not filing complaints. I am asking you, as cathedral legal officer, to tell me whether I have broken any provision of the Cathedrals Measure that would require disclosure to the Bishop."

Edmund, who had been awake since four-thirty and who had the five things written in his notebook in the interior jacket pocket against his chest as he sat in the Dean's study, said: "You have not broken any provision of the Measure, so long as no contract has been signed." He paused. "Has a contract been signed?"

"No."

"Then you are, at present, in an irregular negotiation rather than a breach." He paused again. "I would recommend disclosure to the Chapter."

She made a small expression. "Yes. I rather thought you would."

He walked back through the close in the April cold, the lime trees casting a thin, still shadow on the path, and thought about what Marcus's complaint letter to the Bishop had actually said. He had seen the reference in Claire's files — Marcus had filed a formal complaint three weeks before the murder, about financial irregularities in the cathedral. Claire had been investigating it as potential motive for Patricia Knowles.

The complaint, as Edmund now understood it, had not been about the Dean's property negotiation. Patricia had not known the deal existed until he had just told her about the complaint. She had spent five minutes trying to understand what the financial irregularities were and had drawn, entirely independently, the conclusion that Marcus had been complaining about her, which he had not. He had, evidently, been complaining about something else.

Edmund went to his office and, for the second time in a week, sat down at the cherrywood table and opened the correspondence ledger for the current calendar year.

Rosalind signed for all formal Chapter correspondence as Precentor. It was one of the roles — routine, administrative, the kind of task that accumulated to a person because that person was organised and reliable and did not make a drama of small things. She had been doing it for eleven years. The records showed her signature on each item — received, acknowledged, forwarded.

The complaint letter from Marcus was in the ledger. Registered. Signed for. *R. Ferris, 14 March* — the same careful forward-leaning hand he had seen on the binder cancellation note.

The forwarding column was blank.

Edmund turned the pages. Every other item in the ledger for the current year had a forwarding entry — date sent, to whom, method. Some by email (noted: *email conf*), some by recorded post (noted: *RP*), some hand-delivered within the diocese (noted: *HD, initials*). The complaint letter: received, signed for, and — nothing. The forwarding column held only a small horizontal line, the mark Rosalind made when an item was pending.

*Pending* was not a status that applied to a formal complaint letter to a Bishop. A formal complaint letter to a Bishop was required, under the Ecclesiastical Offices Act and under the cathedral's own governance framework — Edmund had reviewed both last week — to be forwarded within fourteen days. The letter had been received on the fourteenth of March. Five weeks had passed.

Edmund sat very still and looked at the blank forwarding column and thought about what Marcus had been doing in the weeks before his death — what it would have felt like to file a complaint and receive no acknowledgement, to have written a careful letter to a Bishop and to have heard nothing back, to have sat at his 1962 Olivetti in the unheated office on the first floor of the Song School and known that the woman who ran the music of the cathedral and whose name was on every disbursement from the fund he had complained about was also the woman who signed for all the post.

He thought about what that knowledge would have done to a man who was difficult, and who was not moveable, and who, once he had decided something, had decided it.

He thought about what it had done to Rosalind.

Edmund turned to the earlier pages of the ledger — the previous year, the year before. He was looking for J. Whitaker. He found it: fourteen entries across two years, listed as a music printing supplier in the supplier index that Rosalind maintained. L. Pellow Print Services appeared twenty-three times. Aldgate Choral Supplies — twelve. All entered in Rosalind's hand. All forwarding column entries: *RM* (returned mail, Edmund assumed, which was wrong — he revised: remittance?) or *PMT* (payment). All three index entries listed Bristol addresses.

He took out his green ballpoint pen and wrote three addresses in his notebook. Then he underlined each one.

He had, in his years at Wychford, developed a relationship with the Royal Mail postal code database through the cathedral's property office. He opened it now and typed the first postcode.

A PO box in a postal servicing centre in Clifton, Bristol. Registered to no named individual. Opened twelve years ago.

The second postcode.

A PO box at a different Bristol servicing centre. Registered to an individual: one L. Pellow. Date of registration: twelve years ago. The individual L. Pellow's address matched the Clifton servicing centre of the first PO box.

Edmund sat back.

The third postcode returned an error. He tried it again. The database returned: *address not recognised*.

He wrote in his notebook: *Aldgate Choral Supplies — PO box no longer active*. Then: *L. Pellow Print Services — PO box registered to individual whose address is a different PO box*. Then: *J. Whitaker — Bristol, Clifton area, registered 12 years ago, no individual name*.

He underlined nothing. He did not need to.

Three shell companies — or entities that served the purpose of shell companies, assembled from the basic components available to a person who had twelve years and a steady nerve and a very good reason to make payments that passed through the cathedral accounts as ordinary operational expenditure without attracting the attention of an audit. The reason the audit had worked at all was that the audit had been conducted by a person who had written the financial policy and who knew exactly where the exceptions would be filed.

Margaret appeared at the door at half-past eleven with his morning coffee and a piece of shortbread and the information that Father Reeve had telephoned and that Julian Marsh had been into the station and that the detective sergeant would be calling on Edmund at two o'clock.

Edmund said: "Thank you, Margaret."

Margaret said: "Edmund." She had been calling him that for eight years, which was the period in which she had concluded, correctly, that she knew his moods well enough to name them. "Is it very bad?"

He thought about how to answer this. "It is complicated," he said. "In the way that things are complicated when they turn out to be exactly as simple as they first appeared."

She looked at him for a moment with the expression of a woman who has worked for forty years in an institution full of careful men who said things in the particular way they said them, and she understood, he thought, approximately what he meant. She did not press him. She put the shortbread on the corner of the table and left.

He looked at the ledger again. At Rosalind's signature, the same in all seventy-three entries — *R. Ferris*, the small upward flick on the final s.

The letter had never reached the Bishop. Rosalind had signed for it herself.
