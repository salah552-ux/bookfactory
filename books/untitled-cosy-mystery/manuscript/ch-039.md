# CHAPTER THIRTY-NINE
## The Archive

The fifteenth-century bishop's correspondence was not where she had left it.

This was not the archive's fault. She had left it in some disorder on the Thursday of the arrest week, pushed to the side of the sorting table while she waited for Claire's call, and had not returned to it in the ten days since. In the interval, the archive assistant had come in for her Wednesday afternoon session and had very properly re-filed what appeared to be a stray box, which had placed the bishop's correspondence back in the stack in approximately the correct position but not the exact one. Helen spent fifteen minutes locating it, during which she also found two mid-sixteenth-century Chapter act books that had been misfiled in the 1890s section and a Victorian map of the close that she had not known existed and could not resist opening.

The close as it had been in approximately 1880: the Chapter House in the same position, the archive cellar presumably the same cellar — she was standing in it — the cloister garth with what appeared to be a kitchen garden. The song school was marked in a different position from where it stood now, slightly to the south and smaller. She could see, in the map's very precise hand, the labels for each residential property: the Precentor's house (No. 4, it seemed, had always been the Precentor's house); the cathedral lawyer's; the Dean's. The same community, a hundred and forty years ago, in the same stone enclosure.

She put the map face-down on the sorting table — she would ask the assistant to catalogue it properly — and found the bishop's correspondence, and resumed where she had left off.

It was methodical, pleasant work, the kind she was suited to: sorting, reading, transcribing where required, noting the anomalies that would become footnotes in the cathedral's own history, the small human decisions that were preserved in ink long after the people who made them were not. She had been here eight months and she had worked through approximately a third of the archive's uncatalogued material. At her current rate she would be here four or five years. She found this a satisfying prospect.

At half past ten, she reached the end of the bishop's box and set it aside for final cataloguing. She got up and walked the north stack — she always walked the north stack before deciding which box to take up next, the way some people run a finger along a library shelf — and considered the remaining options. She had a system: she was working roughly by period, pushing backward from the Victorian material she had started with, which meant the next section was the later nineteenth century, which was, per the archive's imprecise sorting, somewhere between the 1870s and the 1900s.

She pulled the first box from the 1880s section and carried it to the table.

It was a financial records box — cathedral accounts, vestry disbursements, gift records. Not the most interesting archive material, in her experience, but sometimes the most revealing: the specific amounts, the names, the dates of payment that told you, if you were patient, the shape of a community's anxieties. She worked through the first twelve files efficiently, finding nothing unusual: quarterly accounts, building maintenance, the organ fund (the same music fund, she noted, under an earlier name), a gift to the chapter from a local family.

The thirteenth file was thicker than the others and had been placed slightly apart, not integrated with the others in the box but sitting slightly on top of the file behind it, as if it had been inserted at a different time, by a different hand, and had never been properly settled.

She opened it.

Most of the contents were routine: building invoices, a water rate assessment, a printed schedule of cathedral appointments. She worked through these in the usual way. At the back of the file, behind the schedule of appointments, was a single folded sheet of heavier cream paper — a letter, not a form — with a different quality of paper from the invoices and a hand that was more careful than the clerk's hand on the administrative records.

She unfolded it.

The date was July 1887. The salutation was formal: *To the Hartley family, in settlement of the matter of the east transept.* What followed was a brief acknowledgment of a payment made — the amount was not specified, she noted, which was itself a thing to note — and an expression of the Dean's confidence that the matter would be considered closed. There was a signature at the bottom: the then-Dean, whose name she recognised from the portrait in the Chapter House. And a brief note beneath the signature: *The Chapter is grateful for the family's discretion and understanding.*

She read it twice.

She set it face-down on the sorting table.

She was not sure what the east transept matter was. She did not know the Hartley family, not as a Victorian matter — she was aware that Rose Hartley worked upstairs in the song school, as she was aware of most of the close's residents, but the connection between the name and this letter was not something she could trace without more context. The letter itself said very little: a payment, a matter closed, gratitude for discretion.

Discretion was, in her experience, a word that institutions used when they meant *silence*.

She photographed the letter on her phone — both the front and the back, and the file label, and the box label — and replaced it in the file, and replaced the file in the box. She placed the box to one side with a note for the assistant: *Please do not re-file. To be looked at.* She added her initials.

Then she went back to the bishop's correspondence, because that was the work she had come to do and the morning was still young and the letter would keep.

At noon she heard, very faintly from the cellar's small window, the sound of the organ beginning the pre-service voluntary for the midday Eucharist — a Bach chorale, something she associated with Lent but which was appropriate enough in the general run of the year. The new organist, she thought. Warmer and less precise, as Edmund had said. It would do, for now.

She put the lid on the bishop's box and collected her coat and her bag. She sent a photograph of the letter to Edmund, with no message, on her way up the cellar stairs.

She could hear, as she reached the top step and opened the door into the Chapter House corridor, the song school above her: a class in progress, children's voices doing scales in unison, the sound of the piano leading them up and then down, the particular cheerful chaos of children being asked to be musical and mostly managing it. Rose Hartley's class, probably, at this hour.

She went out into the close and locked the archive door behind her.
