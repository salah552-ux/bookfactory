# CHAPTER SEVENTEEN
## The Arithmetic of Devotion

The music fund supplier invoices arrived in Helen's email at 7:14am, forwarded from Claire without comment. Three attachments. Helen opened them at the kitchen table with her second cup of tea and the feeling, familiar from thirty-five years of court preparation, that she was about to confirm something she already knew.

She had printed them before she sat down. The paper came warm from the printer and she weighted it flat with her reading glasses case before she looked at it.

Three sets of invoices. J. Whitaker. L. Pellow Print Services. Aldgate Choral Supplies. Six pages between them, one year each — 2014, 2015, 2016 — selected by Claire's team as representative. Helen had asked for March specifically, and Claire had added surrounding months without being asked. It was the kind of small competence Helen had always admired in her.

The invoices were good. Convincingly formatted. Amounts plausible for their stated purposes — sheet music, printing costs, choral library maintenance. Helen had spent four years giving expert testimony in fraud cases and she noted, without writing it down, that whoever had prepared these templates had known enough about legitimate supplier invoices to make them nearly indistinguishable from the real thing. The letterheads were correct. The VAT numbers were plausible. The Bristol addresses were real streets.

Nearly indistinguishable was not the same as indistinguishable.

She turned to the second page and found what she was looking for in the third invoice: L. Pellow Print Services, October 2019, £80, "ongoing maintenance of choral library holdings, Part IV." The bank details in the footer. Sort code 00-19-47. She wrote it in her notebook without comment and reached for the phone.

It took her forty minutes and three separate phone calls — two to bank liaison contacts she had not spoken to in eleven years, one to Claire — to get the answer. The sort code resolved to a branch in Clifton, Bristol. The account had been opened twelve years ago under a single individual's name, not a trading name.

The account holder was a Mrs. Bridget Ferris.

Helen set her pen down.

She sat with that for a moment. Then she stood up and looked out of the kitchen window at the close. The cloister garth was still in the early morning light, the stone pale gold where the sun was just reaching it, the shadow of the east arcade lying in a long diagonal across the grass. A blackbird was investigating something near the far end with considerable purposefulness. The cathedral bell marked the half hour.

She sat back down and opened her laptop.

The Clifton Convalescent Home appeared on the second search result. Private. Specialist in dementia care. Established 1989. Fees on application.

She did not need the fees on application. She had Edmund's figure: forty-seven off-pattern payments since 2014, estimated seven thousand pounds per year. She thought about that for a moment — the care homes she had seen in her coroner years, the ones that were clean and properly staffed and smelled of food rather than disinfectant. Those ones cost money. That kind of money.

The home's website had a brief history. Three paragraphs. She read them once and then a second time, more slowly. *The Clifton Convalescent Home has provided specialist residential care for adults living with dementia and related conditions since 1989. We take a small number of residents to ensure each person receives the individual attention and continuity of care they deserve.*

Continuity of care.

Helen made a third cup of tea, which she did not drink, and looked up the home's records through a different route — the CQC inspection database, which was public. The most recent inspection report was dated fourteen months ago. Rating: Good. The one before that, thirty-three months ago: Good. The one before that, fifty-four months ago: Good.

Consistent. Careful. The kind of place that kept its rating because someone was paying attention.

She pulled Edmund's audit figures back up on her phone — he had sent her the summary the previous week in a message that said only "for your attention" and a list of numbers. She had already done the arithmetic. She did it again now, out of habit.

Forty-seven payments. Three-year sample rate suggesting a twelve-year pattern. Average payment: approximately eighty-four pounds. Quarterly rotation between the three shell accounts. Annual total: approximately seven thousand, probably slightly more in recent years as care home fees rose.

Twelve years at seven thousand pounds per year was eighty-four thousand pounds.

The Clifton Convalescent Home charged, according to the care quality commission's minimum benchmark for private specialist dementia care in the Bristol area, somewhere between four and six thousand per month. Helen had looked this up not two minutes ago and was looking at the figure now: four thousand per month minimum for the level of care that produced a consistent Good rating.

Four thousand per month was forty-eight thousand per year.

The music fund withdrawals — seven thousand per year — did not cover the full cost. Of course they did not. Helen thought about that for a moment: what did cover the rest? Rosalind's salary as Precentor, probably. Her pension contributions. Whatever remained after her own household costs. The piano in the front room of No. 4 — not new, Helen noted, now that she thought about it. The kitchen she had sat in three weeks ago: decent, clean, the kind of kitchen that had been good twenty years ago and had not been replaced since.

A woman who had been quietly running out of money for twelve years, filling the gap with the only other fund she administered alone.

The small dishonesties that compound. The thing that begins as a temporary measure in a moment of desperation and becomes, through sheer repetition, a way of living.

She thought about what Rosalind had said in this kitchen, three weeks ago, about her mother: *a long and mannerless death*. She had said it without bitterness, which Helen had thought at the time was restraint, and still thought was restraint, and thought something else about now as well. The flat acknowledgment of a person who had been watching that death for a very long time and had found, or made, the resources to watch it without flinching.

Helen pulled up the Companies House records on her laptop and found the registration date for J. Whitaker: twelve years ago, June. The Clifton Convalescent Home's inspection records went back fourteen years. She noted this in her book with a small asterisk and no further comment.

She tried to find a record of when Bridget Ferris had been admitted to the home and could not — private records, as they should be. She looked instead for the death notice. It was in the Bristol Evening Post archive, eighteen months ago, three lines: *Bridget Mary Ferris, aged 87, peacefully. Much loved mother. Private funeral arrangements.* No mention of Wychford. No mention of the cathedral.

Helen read the three lines twice. Then she looked up the dates of the music fund withdrawals that Edmund had given her in his summary. The last payment to J. Whitaker was eighteen months ago, April. The last to L. Pellow Print Services: eighteen months ago, February. The last to Aldgate Choral Supplies: nineteen months ago, December.

The payments had stopped within a month of Bridget Ferris's death.

Helen closed her laptop. She sat in the kitchen where Rosalind had sat three weeks ago with shortbread from the freezer and grief that had been entirely real, and thought about the specific arithmetic of it: twelve years of small theft from an institution she loved, in service of a woman she loved more. A death she had watched approach for twelve years and paid for with money that was not hers to give.

She thought about Marcus. Marcus, who had discovered it. Marcus, who had given her the chance to repay quietly. Marcus, who had then decided — for reasons Helen could guess at but not confirm — that quiet repayment was no longer sufficient. Marcus, who had written to the Bishop and watched the letter go into Rosalind's hands and known, possibly, that it would not reach its destination.

She understood now. Not just the mechanism. The whole of it.

She thought about a thing her husband David had said once, after a particularly difficult inquest: *The trouble with understanding why someone did something is that it doesn't change what they did.* She had thought at the time that this was wisdom. She still thought it was wisdom. She thought it was slightly harder to hold onto than wisdom usually was.

She picked up her pen. In her notebook, under the sort code and the account holder's name, she wrote three words: *care home confirmed.* Then she wrote the dates — twelve years of start, eighteen months of stop — and underlined them once.

Then she put her pen down and sat for a long time in the kitchen with the blackbird still audible in the garth and the cathedral bell marking another quarter hour, and did not move.

She had been paying for her mother's care. With money that was not hers. And Marcus had found out.
