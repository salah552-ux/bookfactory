# CHAPTER THIRTY-FIVE
## The Cost

The formal aftermath happened in stages, the way formal aftermaths do: the charge, the remand hearing, the early preliminary paperwork that Claire navigated with the brisk competence of someone who found procedural clarity a relief after six weeks of inference. Helen was not required for most of it. She read what Claire sent her, signed what needed signing, and returned to the archive, which had a great deal still to be catalogued and made no demands on her in excess of her skills.

Helen noted that she had expected to feel more. This was not an unusual observation after the resolution of something that had occupied six weeks of continuous attention. The body's assumption was that resolution produced a corresponding shift in affect. It did not always. Sometimes it produced only the sudden visibility of the absence of tension, like noticing you had been cold only when you stepped into warmth.

The close reorganised.

This is what institutions do, and the close was an institution as well as a community, and in some ways the institutional impulse was the kinder one — it gave people tasks, it gave them a structure to carry, it gave them something to do with their hands in the days when the thing was too large to look at directly. Father Simon Reeve took on the choir rehearsals, with Margaret Holt as his unofficial co-ordinator, because Margaret had been co-ordinating things for forty years and did not require a formal title to do it well. He was not Rosalind. No one expected him to be Rosalind. He was earnest and slightly too young for the role and he loved music in a way that was purer and less technical than hers, and the choir tolerated him with the affectionate condescension of people who understand something better than the person in charge of them, and that arrangement would resolve itself in time.

Margaret brought shortbread to the first Chapter meeting after the arrest as if nothing had changed, which was either a deeply human act of continuity or was precisely what it was: nothing had changed for Margaret, internally, because Margaret's faith in the cathedral was of a kind that did not depend on any individual's behaviour. Both things were true simultaneously. Helen did not say this aloud.

Webb came to Chapter. He sat in his usual seat. Edmund chaired in the Dean's absence — the Dean had retreated to a series of meetings with the bishop that were described, in the close, as "consultations" and were understood, by everyone, as damage management. Edmund chaired with the meticulous care of a man who understands that a meeting's orderliness is a form of mercy to the people in it. He got through the agenda in fifty-seven minutes. This was, by the close's reckoning, a record.

On the Thursday of the week after the arrest, Helen walked the close at dusk.

The light was that particular April dusk light — still blue at the top of the sky, gold at the horizon, the cathedral's limestone shifting between them and deciding, at this hour, to be both. She walked the perimeter: out of No. 7, south along the close path past Nos. 8 through 12, around the corner into the west front approach, along the cloister walk, back into the garth. The jackdaws had gone to bed. The cloister smelled of its usual self: old stone, dampness from the flower beds, a thread of candle wax from a window someone had left open in the vestry.

She stopped at the point where the cloister walk opened into the view of the full cathedral exterior — west front, towers, the medieval windows catching the last of the light from inside with a warmth that was entirely accidental and entirely precise.

The music would continue. She had not been certain of this in quite the way she was certain of it now. Choral Evensong had not been cancelled once since the arrest. Father Reeve had led it for the first three evenings before the substitute organist arrived, with the choir covering the organ parts they knew from memory, and the coverage had been imperfect and genuine and the congregation — thinner than usual, but present — had received it in the spirit it was given. Something very old had continued something very old, with different hands. The cathedral had looked, Helen thought, completely unbothered by this. Seven centuries of different hands.

She thought about what was gone from it specifically. Not what would be replaced — the music would be replaced, the role would be filled, the position of Precentor would be advertised and interviewed and filled by someone who would spend the first two years not-quite-measuring-up to their predecessor in the memories of people who had liked their predecessor. That was the institutional cycle and it was uninteresting.

What was gone was the specific warmth of Rosalind Ferris. Her questions instead of statements, in the meetings and the corridors and the casual Thursday-afternoon teas that had been one of the things that made the close a community rather than an address. Her first names for everyone — "Edmund," "Helen," "Simon," from the first day with every person, as if she had already decided they were friends and the formality was simply a social convention she was choosing to skip. Her hands on the choir folders, the silver treble clef at her collar, the way she had of making every evensong feel simultaneously prepared and in the moment.

Gone from it permanently. Not gone from the record — Rosalind's name was in the close history, in the music archive, in thirty years of service sheets and Chapter minutes and the organ gallery's own logbook. She would be gone from it in the way that only live presence is gone, and that absence had a specific shape, and the shape had a specific name, which was grief.

Helen had grieved David. She had done it badly, in some ways — too efficiently, too soon returned to work, too little willing to sit with the shapelessness of it. She understood grief now in a way she had not understood it then, which was a useless kind of understanding, too late for David and too much for Rosalind.

She turned her back on the west front and walked north, past the song school, past No. 4 with its blue door and its terracotta pots — someone had watered the narcissi, she noted; they were still upright — past No. 3 where Webb's light was on. She stopped at Edmund's door at No. 2 and looked at it for a moment, and then she did not knock, and she walked home to No. 7.

This was where she was. Here and not there. Present and useful, and carrying something that was not quite grief and not quite guilt and not quite anything she had a professional term for. She let herself into the front hall and stood a moment in the smell of the house — wood polish, old paper, the tea she had made that morning and left too long — and she thought: I was right. I was right from the beginning. Being right is not, it turns out, the same as being finished.

She stood there a little longer than she needed to. The coat rack. The worn runner on the hall floor. The postcard Julian had sent two Christmases ago, still propped against the lamp because she had never found a reason to put it away.

The cathedral bell rang the hour. She counted to eight. Then she went into the kitchen to make tea, because the alternatives were unclear, and tea at least was something she knew how to do.
