# CHAPTER ELEVEN
## The Loan

Julian was still in Wychford. She had not expected that.

He had taken a room at the King's Head — three nights now, the barman had apparently told Margaret, who had apparently told Father Reeve, who had mentioned it to Helen in a tone that conveyed, with some precision, that he was not gossiping but rather ensuring she had all relevant pastoral context. The close was a machine for the circulation of information. It had always been this.

She found Julian at the bench by the lych-gate, where she had left him three days ago, or where he had found his way back to, which amounted to the same thing. He was wearing the same jacket. He was looking at the cathedral the way people looked at it when they had been standing outside somewhere for a long time and had run out of other things to look at.

"I've been thinking," he said, when she sat down, "that I should probably tell you properly."

Helen said nothing. She understood that he was not asking permission. He was building himself up to it, and anything she said would give him an opportunity to stop.

The morning was sharp and still — that particular April cold that arrived after a night of clear sky, the kind of chill that had no weight to it, just edge. The cathedral bells had finished their seven o'clock peal seven minutes ago. Through the close wall, someone was burning garden waste; the smell of it reached them in the intervals between a light easterly that moved the tops of the lime trees and not much else.

Julian said: "He loaned me twelve thousand pounds."

Helen looked at the cathedral. The east window, from here, was a blaze of coloured light even on a morning like this — deep red and cobalt blue and the particular gold that medieval glass made when it had had six hundred years to deepen.

"When?"

"Nine years ago." He paused. "During the — after Dad died, and then you and I." He did not finish the sentence. He did not need to. "I was between things. I needed a deposit for the flat. I couldn't go to the bank. I couldn't come to you."

Helen registered: he could not come to me. She filed it.

"And you never repaid him."

"I sent him four hundred once. He sent it back. Said keep it, pay me when you're flush." Julian's jaw tightened on the last word, some residue of an older argument that Marcus was no longer present to have. "And then six weeks ago he wrote to me and said he wanted the full amount, with interest. I don't know what changed."

Helen knew what had changed. She did not say so. There was a great deal she was not saying, and she had learned, over thirty-five years, that the geography of what you did not say was at least as important as what you said.

"How much interest?"

"He said seventeen thousand. I told him that was—" Julian stopped. "We argued."

"I know. By the gate." She had heard about this from Thomas, who had heard it from one of the organ scholars, who had seen it from the cloisters. "That afternoon."

"I shouldn't have come down. I thought if I saw him in person. But Marcus was—" He looked for the word. "He wasn't moveable. Once he'd decided something, he'd decided it." He turned then and looked at her properly. He had her colouring, Julian — the grey eyes, the careful mouth. He had David's hands: wide, blunt-fingered, good for making things. "I didn't kill him, Mum. I want you to know that. Not because you'd think I did, but because—"

"Julian." She said it very quietly. "I know."

He looked at her for a moment and she could see something in him loosen fractionally — just the slight alteration that came when a pressure that had been building was told it no longer needed to.

They sat in the cold for a moment. A blackbird was working along the base of the close wall, aggressive and systematic, overturning dead leaves with absolute conviction.

"Was there anything else?" Helen said. "While you were here. Before. The evening he died."

Julian looked at the cathedral again. "I went to the King's Head after we argued. Two pints. I got there about seven, half seven — I left around half nine." He paused. "I walked back through the close. I had to — the gate's the only way in for residents and I still had the key from Mum — from you." He corrected himself, and she noted that correction, and also noted that she noted it. "It was about nine-thirty. There's a light in the lane behind the back of the close — only one, it's on the corner by the wall — and I saw Marcus."

Helen said: "Go on."

"He was walking from the direction of Rosalind's house." Julian frowned. "I thought it was odd, because Rosalind's at the south end of the row and the organ loft entrance is the other way. But it was dark. I wasn't sure. And I didn't know — I didn't think it meant anything."

"Was he walking steadily?" Helen asked.

Julian considered. "He was walking the way Marcus always walked. Like he had somewhere to be and slightly resented getting there. But yes — steadily. Not— he wasn't in any difficulty."

"Did he see you?"

"I don't think so. I was on the other side of the gate. I had my key out. He went past toward the north transept." Julian turned to her. "Does that— is that important?"

Helen looked at the east window. Marcus Vayne, walking briskly and with mild resentment toward the organ loft at nine-thirty at night, leaving Rosalind Ferris's house. Already dosed. Already carrying in his blood the thing that would take him to the floor of the gallery within the hour.

Or perhaps not yet. She was not certain of the timeline.

She said: "Did you tell this to Claire? To the detective sergeant?"

"She asked where I was the evening. I told her the pub. She asked if I saw anything unusual. I said no." He looked uncomfortable. "I didn't think— Marcus coming from the south end of the close after dark wasn't — I didn't know what mattered."

"It matters," Helen said.

"How much?"

She considered how to answer this accurately and usefully. "A great deal," she said. "You need to tell Claire today. Today, Julian, not tomorrow. Do you understand?"

He nodded. He seemed, she thought, relieved again — the relief of a man who has been carrying something and has finally been given somewhere legitimate to put it down. She recognised that relief. She had felt it herself, once, when she had finally admitted to a coroner's inquiry that she had seen the evidence she had seen and that there was only one possible interpretation of it. The body of knowledge that refused to resolve itself into a different shape.

She put her hand on his arm briefly. It surprised him; she could feel the slight recoil, and then the deliberate stillness when he didn't pull away. They were, she thought, both doing their best with the vocabulary they had.

"Where are you going after?" she asked.

"Back to Bristol." He paused. "Unless—"

"Come to dinner," she said. "Tonight. Seven o'clock. I'll make something adequate."

Julian almost smiled. "That's a strong guarantee." He stood. "Mum."

"Yes."

"He — Marcus — he did help me. Whatever else he was, he helped me when I needed it. That matters." He looked at the cathedral one last time. "I can't quite — I don't know what I'm supposed to do with that."

"I don't think you're supposed to do anything with it," Helen said. "I think you're just supposed to hold it."

He left. She sat for a moment in the lime-tree cold, her pearls cool against her collar, the blackbird still working the wall. She turned in her mind the thing Julian had said, the specific weight of it: Marcus, walking from the direction of Rosalind's house, at half-past nine. Rosalind, who had told Helen she left at half past eight, ten minutes.

Ninety minutes. Ninety minutes in which anything could have been given to Marcus Vayne in a cup — in a mug — with warmth, and professional concern, and the natural authority of a woman who had run this cathedral's music for thirty years and who knew how to make someone comfortable enough to drink what was put in front of them without wondering about it.

Helen stood up.

She needed to tell Edmund. She needed to tell Claire.

She needed, first, to establish precisely what the timing meant, because she was not prepared to put this into a sentence until she was certain the sentence was correct.

Whatever happened between nine-thirty and seven in the morning had happened in the organ loft.
