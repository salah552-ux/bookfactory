# CHAPTER TWENTY-EIGHT
## The Enhancement

Patel's call came at twelve seventeen.

Edmund was in his office. He had not, since returning from the police station at ten o'clock, left it. He had sat at the cherrywood table for two hours and done nothing in particular — not worked, not read, not made notes, which was itself unusual enough that he was aware of it. He had looked at the cloister garth and at the cathedral's east end and at the slow movement of pigeons on the chapter house roof, and he had waited.

The call was brief and technical. Patel gave the information in the order he had assembled it: first, the garment identification; second, the stride analysis confirmation; third, the CCTV still count and the confidence interval.

Edmund wrote it down as Patel spoke, the green pen moving quickly for the first time in two days.

Garment: hip-length, buttoned, collared. Enhancement confirmed a distinctive double-rib collar construction and a cabled button band — consistent with a high-quality handknit or limited-production cashmere garment, specific to a small number of manufacturers. The collar's folded edge was visible in three of the seven enhanced stills. Pattern distinguishable under enhancement from machine-produced alternatives. It was not a generic pale blue cardigan. It was a specific one.

Edmund had seen that cardigan at three Chapter meetings, at Evensong on four occasions, and brought to his own office on a cold morning in the second week of the inquiry when the heating had not come on and Rosalind had arrived with tea and stood three feet from the audit table and asked him how the investigation was going.

He said to Patel: "The collar matches what Thomas Oakes described."

Patel said: "He described a small folded collar. That's what this shows. Yes."

Stride analysis: the figure's gait had been measured against reference footage of other persons using the same corridor in the week before and after the night in question. Walking speed consistent with a person aged 55–70, in good physical condition, comfortable in heels or low-heeled shoes. The asymmetry index — the technical measure of any visible gait irregularity — was 0.04, which Patel described as "near-symmetrical." Rose Hartley's characteristic left-foot roll would register, in the same measure, at approximately 0.18 to 0.22.

Edmund wrote: *0.04 — near-symmetrical. Not Rose Hartley. Not a man. Consistent with RF.*

He wrote the initials deliberately, for the first time in the notebook.

He called Helen.

She was at the door of his office within four minutes, which told him she had been waiting close by. She read what he had written. She looked at the RF at the bottom of the page — just the initials, underlined once.

"The second bottle," she said.

Edmund turned to the back section of his notebook. He had been carrying the £83 concordance for two days, waiting for the moment when sharing it would add certainty rather than create pressure. He showed Helen the column: the 8 March music fund payment to J. Whitaker, £83. The dispensary cost estimate. The three-day gap between payment and collection. The batch number DA-6650/11 on the dispensing record against the patient name Mrs. B. Ferris.

Helen read it. She read it twice. "You had this."

"Yes."

"Since when?"

"Wednesday."

She looked at him for a moment. He did not apologise. There was no apology he could make that would have been accurate — he had withheld it because he had wanted to be certain, and he had been right to wait, and it had cost two days of Webb's confinement, and both of those things were true simultaneously. He did not attempt to resolve them.

"We need to go to Claire," she said.

"Yes."

They went to the station together, in Edmund's car, without discussing who would drive or what they would say. The ring road was quiet at lunchtime — a Monday in spring, the particular low activity of a city that has not quite committed to its afternoon. Edmund parked on Minster Lane and they went in.

Claire had the interview room — the larger one on the first floor, not the room where Webb had been sitting that morning. She was alone. She saw their faces as they came through the door and she said nothing; she simply closed her notebook and set her pen down and turned her chair to face them.

Edmund put the enhanced CCTV stills on the table first. Then the stride analysis report. Then his own notes: the timeline, the £83 concordance, the dispensing record against the care home account, the Song School key ledger, the Thomas Oakes description measured against the enhancement's collar and cabled button band.

Helen put the pharmacy records beside them. The archive bottle. The dispensary record. The batch numbers.

Claire looked at it. She looked at all of it. She picked up the enhanced stills and moved through them one by one, setting each one down precisely.

She was silent for a long time.

"The collar," she said finally. "Oakes described a small folded collar."

"Yes," Edmund said.

She put the final still down. She looked at the table — at the spread of papers, each one a piece of a structure that had been building for six weeks.

"Show me everything," she said. "From the beginning."

Webb was released at half past four. 

His solicitor — a young woman who had the air of someone who had been called from something else and had not quite recovered from the interruption — handled the paperwork with a brisk efficiency that suggested she had done this before and intended to be back at whatever she had been called from within the hour.

Edmund was at the station. He had stayed to complete his formal statement, which Claire had taken herself, recording each element in order, asking careful questions at the places where precision mattered. Helen had given hers first and gone back to the close. Edmund was still in the corridor, his coat on, his notebook in his pocket, when Webb came through the door.

Webb stopped when he saw Edmund. He had been at the station for nine hours. He was wearing the same clothes he had been arrested in. He looked — not older exactly. Worn through, in the specific way of someone who has held a position under force for too long.

Edmund said: "Alistair."

Webb looked at him. He did not speak. His face was doing several things simultaneously that could not be easily parsed — the relief and the exhaustion and something underneath both of them that Edmund thought was perhaps the beginning of understanding. Not of the murder. Of the shape of what had been done in his name, to him, by a machinery that had moved in the wrong direction and had now, at considerable cost, corrected itself.

He nodded once. It was the nod of a man who does not trust his voice.

Edmund said: "I'm sorry this was the way of it."

Webb made a small sound that might have been acknowledgement or might have been something else. He turned and walked toward the door, and his solicitor fell into step beside him, and they went through the glass door into Minster Lane, and the door swung closed behind them.

Webb left without looking at any of them. He walked out into Minster Lane and did not look back at the building, and Edmund watched him go and felt, without entirely understanding why, something close to shame.
