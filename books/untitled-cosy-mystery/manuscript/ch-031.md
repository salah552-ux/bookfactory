# CHAPTER THIRTY-ONE
## The Morning

The archive opened at eight. Helen was there at ten past.

She had not slept well, but this was not unusual. Seventy years of sleeping had produced, in the end, a competent but unreliable practitioner of it, and the last three weeks had done nothing to improve her average. She dressed before the light was full — pearls, tweed, the low shoes she wore in the cellar because the stone was uneven — and made coffee in the kitchen while the close was still quiet enough that she could hear the starlings in the cloister eaves.

The cathedral had been singing before she arrived. Evensong residue, she thought — the acoustics held music in the stone for hours afterwards, a physical fact the architects had not planned for and could not have prevented. What she actually heard, stepping through the south porch at eight o'clock, was the organist from the college doing early morning practice with the stops half-drawn: Bach, the middle section of something she couldn't quite name, the sound arriving in pieces as she crossed the nave. She stopped once and listened. Then she went down the cellar stairs to the archive and unlocked the door.

The archive was cold and smelled of itself: damp stone, old leather, the particular mustiness of documents kept at temperatures that preserved them without ever warming them. Helen found it clarifying rather than unpleasant. She had done some of her best thinking in this room. She did some of it now.

The box she had left on the sorting table on Tuesday was where she'd left it. Fifteenth-century bishop's correspondence, half transcribed. She sat down, put her coffee on the side shelf where she had learned it was safe from condensation drips, and opened the box. The documents were written in a hand that required, even now, sustained effort to read — a late medieval secretary hand, abbreviations everywhere, the ink still vivid against the vellum in a way she found quietly astounding. Five hundred and fifty years, give or take. The ink was still there. People and their urgencies were not.

She worked for an hour without thinking about Rosalind.

This was not avoidance. It was a discipline she had trained herself into over thirty-five years in pathology: you do not think about the case when you have done everything you can think of to do about the case. You wait. You work. You let the rest of the brain continue its operations without interference. The interventions, when they came, arrived better for the space.

At nine-fifteen, she heard the quarter-bell from the tower, then the half, then the full. The full bell on a Thursday morning meant Morning Prayer had ended ten minutes ago. This was a Thursday morning.

She put down the bishop's correspondence and looked at the wall.

The stone was dressed ashlar, pale limestone, the same limestone as the rest of the cathedral — quarried, according to Edmund's historical papers, from the same bed as the building's foundations. She had stood in this room once, waiting for a box to be found on a high shelf, and counted the courses of stone from floor to vault. There were twenty-six. She had not done this on purpose. She had simply looked at the wall and counted.

This was the building seven centuries had made. Human urgency, absorbed and contained. Not solved. Not forgiven. Simply — held. The bishop who had written the letters upstairs in the box she was working through had had his own tragedies, his own betrayals, his own unforgivable decisions made in a single afternoon. The stone held all of it equally.

She thought: Marcus loved this place for twenty-five years. He played the organ in the dark at two in the morning because something in the music asked for it and he was not the kind of man who refused that call. He had been difficult. He had been brilliant. He had had thirty years of love letters in a locked box that no one knew about. He had discovered that a colleague he had worked beside for two decades was stealing from the fund they both served, and he had given her the chance to make it right, and when she would not make it right he had done what the situation required of him. That was also who he was. That was the same man.

She thought about the coffee mug. Plain white china. Wiped so thoroughly that only the inner base had retained a trace. She had noted it in her sixty-second survey and written one word in her spiral notebook and closed it, and she had not been wrong.

She thought about the warm afternoons with Rosalind — shortbread, tea, the piano visible through the front window when the door was open. Questions instead of statements, because that was how Rosalind gathered information. First names, always, even now — "Helen" from the very first day, not "Dr Marsh," as if they had been close friends for twenty years. She had not known, then, what that warmth meant. She knew now.

She thought about what it cost to be right about this particular person.

It was not the same as being right about a body. In thirty-five years of the coroner's office, she had been right about bodies — their causes of death, their testimony, the stories they told about the people who had interacted with them last. The rightness had never cost her much. It had simply been work, done well. This was different. This was a woman she had liked. This was a woman she had sat with and made tea for and, in some quiet corner of herself that she did not advertise, had thought: there is someone who understands the close in the same register I understand it.

The difference between Rosalind and her was that Rosalind had killed someone. That was the entire difference. It was very large.

Outside the archive window — a small frosted casement at ground level, looking up into the cloister garth — she could see the feet of the jackdaws that were always there on Thursday mornings. They were always there. They did not know why it was Thursday or what had happened in the close in the last six weeks. They were in the garth because the garth had food in it, and the cloister had shelter, and Thursday was no different from Wednesday.

The cathedral would be exactly like the jackdaws. This was not a consoling thought. It was just a true one.

She picked up the bishop's correspondence and continued reading.

At ten forty-five, her phone screen lit against the table: DS Claire Marsh. She knew what it meant. She let it ring once, so she would answer calmly.

She answered calmly.

"I'll be there," she said.

She poured the cold coffee down the cellar sink, put her coat on, and went.

On the way out, she passed the wall she had counted to twenty-six courses. The archive key was cold in her hand. She locked the door behind her and climbed toward the light.
