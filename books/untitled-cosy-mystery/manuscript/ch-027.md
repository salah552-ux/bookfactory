# CHAPTER TWENTY-SEVEN
## Forty-Eight Hours

By ten o'clock, the close knew.

Helen had come home from the police station to find two curtains moved in windows she could see from her front gate, and Margaret's kitchen light on at an hour when Margaret's kitchen light was not usually on, and Father Reeve standing in the cloisters with his hands behind his back looking at the pavement in the specific attitude of a young man who has heard something that has altered the shape of the morning.

She went inside. She put the kettle on. She sat at the kitchen table and waited.

At ten twenty, her doorbell rang.

Rosalind stood on the step in her coat and gloves, which was itself information — Rosalind's coat was the dark wool one she wore for formal occasions and cold weather, the gloves were the kidskin pair that Helen had seen once before, the morning of the memorial service. She had dressed, Helen registered, for something.

"You've heard," Helen said.

"Just now. Margaret rang." Rosalind's voice was even. Her face was — what was the word? Shattered was too theatrical. It was the face of a person who had received something they had been expecting and hoping they were wrong about expecting. "I'm sorry to come without warning."

"Come in."

They sat in the kitchen. Helen made tea. This was automatic — the kettle had already been heating; she moved through the familiar actions of warming the pot, measuring leaves, watching the water colour — and she was aware of doing it automatically, which was itself a kind of attention, the body doing something reliable while the mind kept very still.

Rosalind kept her gloves on until she sat down, then removed them and folded them neatly on the table beside her. Her hands without the gloves looked the same as they always did — steady, capable, the hands of a woman who had been lifting scores and setting tempos and playing the piano for fifty years. She looked at her tea when Helen put it in front of her. She did not immediately drink it.

"This is wrong," she said.

"Yes," Helen said, which was accurate in every direction.

"Alistair has— whatever his failings are, he did not do this. I know Alistair. We have worked side by side for twenty-two years. He is difficult and he is often unkind about it, but he is not capable of this." She paused. "I know how little that matters legally. But I wanted to say it because I believe it."

Helen was looking at Rosalind's hands, which were resting on either side of the teacup with the particular stillness that hands had when their owner was making a deliberate effort. She thought: twenty-two years. You have known him for twenty-two years and you know he is not capable of this. You know because you are.

"Has Claire said anything to you?" Rosalind asked. "About the direction of the inquiry? I'm not asking you to — I understand there are things you can't say. I just want to know whether the investigation is still — whether there are other—" She stopped. "Whether Alistair is their only focus."

This, Helen thought, is the question. Delivered with a completely steady voice and a genuine expression of distress and an entirely plausible reason for asking it. A woman worried about an innocent colleague. A woman trying to understand how the machinery of the investigation had arrived at this conclusion so that she could perhaps redirect it. Both of those things at once.

"The investigation is Claire's," Helen said. "I don't have sight of its direction."

Rosalind nodded. She picked up her tea. She drank some of it, and for a moment she looked simply tired — the tiredness of someone who has been managing something large for a very long time and has not expected to be still managing it at this particular moment.

"Marcus would have hated this," she said. "Not the investigation — he would have understood the investigation. But this. An innocent person in a police interview room because of something Marcus was part of." She turned the cup slightly on its saucer. "He was always very certain about what justice looked like. He would have thought this was wrong."

Helen thought about Marcus typing a letter about financial irregularities on a 1962 Olivetti. She thought about Marcus keeping Webb's secret for three years. She thought about what Marcus had said to someone in the Chapter House two Wednesdays before he died: *I have told you what I am going to do and I am going to do it.*

She thought about what it cost to be certain about what justice looked like.

"Would you write a character reference?" Rosalind asked. "For Alistair. For whatever proceedings follow. I know you don't know him well, but your name — a former forensic pathologist — it would carry weight."

Helen considered this. It was a reasonable request. It was also, she thought, a way of testing whether Helen's private certainty about Alistair's innocence matched her public position.

"I'll think about it," she said.

Rosalind was quiet for a moment. She looked at her hands. Then she looked up at Helen with an expression that Helen could only describe as honest — which was the wrong word, she knew, and was also the right one. The emotion was honest. The person producing it was a different question.

"I keep thinking about the close after this," she said. "Whatever the outcome. We will all have been through this together, and we will not be quite the same." She looked at the window. Outside, the cloister bells were beginning — quarter past eleven, the sequence of notes that the close had heard six hundred years. "I hope we come out the other side of it still knowing how to be kind to each other."

She finished her tea. She put her gloves back on — the same neat fold, the same deliberate smoothing — and stood up.

"Thank you, Helen," she said. She touched Helen's arm at the kitchen doorway, the same brief contact she had been offering for weeks: the dry press of a hand, there and gone.

Helen walked with her to the front door and watched her go. She stood in the doorway until Rosalind had crossed the close and let herself into No. 4.

She went back to the kitchen.

Helen made tea. She did not drink it.
