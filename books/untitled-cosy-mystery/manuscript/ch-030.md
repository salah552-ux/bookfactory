# CHAPTER THIRTY
## The Final Piece

On Thursday morning, Claire called Edmund at eight o'clock and said: "The warrant has been issued."

He thanked her and set the phone down on the cherrywood table and sat looking at his notebook.

The notebook was almost full. He had been using the same brown leather notebook since January — since before the murder, since before Marcus's death had reorganised the shape of his working life. The first thirty pages were ecclesiastical business: an easement query from the diocese, the south tower structural survey, correspondence about the Chapter's obligations under the Cathedrals Measure, the minutes from February's meeting. Then, from page thirty-one, the investigation. He had used it differently for the investigation — more densely, the entries dated and timed, the underlinings purposeful rather than decorative, the margins occasionally annotated with questions he had resolved or left unresolved in subsequent pages.

He looked at the last entry: *Warrant issued. 8:04am.*

He had written it immediately after Claire's call. It was three words and a time. He underlined it once, which was what he did with confirmed facts, and then he looked at it.

The warrant had been issued for Canon Rosalind Ferris, Precentor of Wychford Cathedral. It named her in connection with the death of Marcus Vayne on the night of the fourteenth to fifteenth of April. The charge would be murder. Edmund knew the charge would be murder because the evidence was clear and the law was clear and there was no version of what the evidence showed that was not murder, whatever Rosalind had intended and however the decision had been made.

He thought about the decision. He thought about what it must have been like to arrive at it — not the execution, not the pharmacy, not the eleven minutes in the archive storeroom. The afternoon of it. The specific afternoon when Marcus's letter had become undeniable and the repayment impossible and the disclosure unthinkable, and something in her had said: *or else.*

He could not get inside it. He did not share that particular variety of desperation — thirty years of identity invested in a single institution, a single position, a single role through which everything else was understood. His faith and his work had always seemed to him to be different things, each a check on the other. He did not know what it was to have them entirely fused. He thought Rosalind might.

This was not, he knew, the same as understanding. It was the nearest he could get.

He thought about the tea.

She had brought it on a tray with biscuits from Margaret. The audit papers were on the cherrywood table. He had moved some of them to make room — routine courtesy, the kind he applied without thinking, the kind that had been his professional habit for forty years — and she had set the tray down and asked how the investigation was going and looked at the papers and then at him, and her hands had been steady throughout.

*Will the inquiry look at our finances?* she had asked.

*I am the precentor. I should like everything to look exactly as it is.*

She was standing three feet from the evidence that would eventually lead to her arrest, and her hands had been entirely steady, and her voice had been entirely warm, and she had asked the right question to assess how close he was without asking the question she actually needed answered. It was extraordinary. Edmund turned this over in his mind the way he turned complex legal questions over — looking for the angle that made it make sense, the framing that allowed for internal coherence.

The only framing that worked was: she was telling the truth.

Not about the murder. But about herself. She was the Precentor. She was the person who had devoted thirty years to making the music of this cathedral as good as it could be, who knew every chorister's name and which bar of which anthem had given them trouble, who had sat with dying members of the close and spoken at the funerals of men and women she had known for decades, who had loved the architecture and the liturgy and the specific quality of light through the east window at Evensong in late April, and who had, on one specific afternoon, done an unforgivable thing to protect all of it.

*I should like everything to look exactly as it is.* She had meant the accounts. She had also meant herself. She had wanted to be exactly what she was in that room, holding the tray, offering tea, warm and competent and trusted — because that was true, and the murder was also true, and both were the same person.

Edmund stood up and went to the window.

The close below was ordinary in all its details. Thomas Oakes was crossing to the cathedral with the bell-ringing schedule — he carried it on Thursdays, a single folded sheet — and the delivery van from Monday was back for whatever it brought every week, and the jackdaws were in the cloister garth again, or different jackdaws, it was impossible to say. The close did not know the warrant had been issued. It was functioning at its Thursday pace, which was the pace it had had for a very long time.

He looked at No. 4. The curtains were open in the front room. The piano was visible behind the glass — dark, still, lid down, the stack of scores on the bench. A lamp was on inside, which it didn't need to be, because the morning was perfectly clear. She had left a lamp on.

He thought: she is in there now.

He thought: she will have known since the weekend, or earlier, or perhaps long before the weekend. The encounter with Helen at the window — Helen had told him about it in two sentences, precise and unelaborated, the two of them standing in the cloister east walk on Tuesday afternoon. *She knew. I could see that she knew.* Edmund had not asked what that meant, because he had understood it exactly.

She had been waiting.

The way she had waited in the organ loft, in the dark, for the digoxin to take effect, was perhaps not so different from the way she had been waiting since. A person who was able to wait — who had the internal resources to hold a thing and continue, who could bring tea to an audit table and ask the right questions and conduct evensong with entirely steady hands — was a person who had decided that waiting was what the situation required, and had waited.

He hoped, in a way that surprised him slightly by its sincerity, that the waiting had not been entirely terrible.

He turned from the window and walked back to the table. He sat down. He looked at his notebook, at the tight column of entries from the investigation: forty pages of inference and confirmation and crossed-out alternatives and facts underlined once or twice, the language of a man who had been thinking very carefully in a very small space for six weeks.

He closed his notebook. He would not open it in the close in quite the same way again.
