# CHAPTER FIVE
## A Condolence Visit

Rosalind brought shortbread.

She came round at four, in the pale blue cardigan and a grey skirt, with a Tupperware box held in both hands like an offering one was uncertain would be wanted. Helen opened the door and Rosalind looked at her and Helen saw, with a clarity that did not feel like a kindness, that Rosalind had been crying and had washed her face and had decided to come anyway.

"I shouldn't have come empty-handed," Rosalind said. "I'm sorry. It's only the freezer batch. It's not very good."

"Rosalind. Come in."

She came in.

Helen did not, generally, do tea. Helen did, generally, stand at the threshold and exchange three sentences and close the door. The close knew this. The close had taken thirty months to forgive her for it, and had eventually settled on "Helen is who she is", which was, Helen suspected, a more honest verdict than most close residents received.

But Rosalind had brought shortbread, and Rosalind had been crying, and Rosalind had asked, in the cathedral that morning, to be employed by the catastrophe; and Helen, who had spent the day in court rooms and morgues and pathologists' notes for forty years, knew the look of a person who needed not to be alone for an hour.

She put the kettle on.

—

Rosalind sat at the kitchen table and watched her hands. The Tupperware was open. Two pieces had been taken out. Neither had been eaten.

"Edmund has been very kind," Rosalind said.

"Edmund usually is."

"He gave me the schedule to do. It was — it was the right thing. I needed it. I needed to be doing something."

"I know."

"You always know that," Rosalind said. She looked up briefly and the smile that came was not a smile so much as a small movement of recognition between two women who had each, in their separate decades, known what it was to put work between themselves and a calamity. "I've always thought that about you, Helen. That you know the use of work."

Helen poured the tea. She said nothing.

"I keep thinking," Rosalind said. "I keep thinking — he was alone. You see. That's the thing I cannot put down. He was alone in the cathedral. In the dark. And I —" She paused. The pause was honest; Helen heard it as such.

"I went round there last night," Rosalind said.

Helen did not move.

"Briefly," Rosalind said. "Only briefly. Just at — well — half-past eight, perhaps. I'd been making notes for the Easter responses with him at half five and I'd promised I'd remind him, before I forgot, about Thursday's choir practice. We'd shifted the start time to seven, because of the Confirmation. I came round and put my head in the door and told him and went home. He was — he was perfectly Marcus. Difficult. Lovely. Difficult."

She lifted her tea and put it down again without drinking.

"I keep thinking," she said, "that perhaps if I had stayed — if I had sat with him for half an hour — even ten minutes — we would have talked about something. The anthem for Easter Two. The third tenor. I don't know what. Anything. And he wouldn't have been —"

She did not finish.

Helen said, quietly, "Rosalind. You couldn't have known."

"No."

"You went home."

"I went home."

"Half-past eight."

"About then. I'd been there perhaps ten minutes."

Helen let it sit.

She watched Rosalind in the way she had watched a thousand witnesses across a coroner's table, which was to say without watching, holding the question of whether-this-was-true loosely in her mind so that it did not pull her face. Rosalind did not look like a woman lying. Rosalind looked like a woman who had been telling the truth about other things, and then had told this thing in passing, the way a person tells the small fact next to the large one, hoping the large one would carry it.

Helen drank her tea.

She said, "How long had you known him."

"Twenty-five years," Rosalind said. "More. I came to Wychford six years before Marcus did. When he arrived — he was already Marcus, almost immediately. As if the place had been waiting for him." She looked at her hands. "We were not, you know — we weren't friends, exactly. Marcus didn't have friends, exactly. But we worked together for twenty-five years, and there is a particular thing that grows between people who have done that. Something I will not be able to replace. Something I will not, probably, ever fully describe, including to myself."

Helen said, "I am sorry, Rosalind."

She meant it. She noted herself meaning it.

—

Rosalind stayed for an hour. They did not talk only about Marcus. Rosalind talked about her mother — eighteen months gone, of dementia, a long and mannerless death — and about the cathedral choir's recent struggles to recruit boy trebles, and about how the eight o'clock had fallen below twelve people on three Sundays in a row, and Helen listened with the half of herself that listened, and with the other half thought about Thursday's choir practice.

Helen kept a wall calendar in the kitchen that was not, strictly, hers. It had come with the house when she had moved into No. 7, and it was the cathedral's printed schedule of services and choir rehearsals for the year, posted to every close residence in January. It hung beside the back door. While she stood at the kettle for the second pot of tea, Helen could see it in the corner of her eye.

She did not look at it while Rosalind was there. She did not need to. She had walked past that calendar twice a day since January and she could call up the page for this week without looking, the way a courtroom witness could call up a date she had spent five years preparing to give.

There was no choir practice on Thursday this week.

There had been one penned in for Thursday, at the start of term, in the printed grid; and there was a black diagonal line through it, drawn with a marker pen, with a small note in the margin in Rosalind's own handwriting that said *cancelled — Confirmation rehearsals taking precedence*. The note was three weeks old.

Helen had read it on the morning it had gone up.

She poured the second tea. Her hand did not shake. There was no reason for it to shake. She made a thing about the milk, and she sat back down, and Rosalind talked about the Easter season and Helen made the small noises that women of her generation had been trained from childhood to make at the appropriate intervals, and somewhere underneath that, she filed the fact carefully.

She did not file it as evidence.

She filed it as the kind of thing that could be remembered, accurately, later, if it turned out to matter.

—

When Rosalind rose to go, the light over the close had begun to soften towards five o'clock, and the bell rang the half-hour. Rosalind crossed to the back door and looked at the calendar without seeing it, the way one looks at a familiar object in another woman's kitchen.

"I should go and finish Edmund's schedule," she said. "I want it on his desk before evensong. You'll come, won't you. To evensong."

"I might."

"Come if you can. The choir will manage. Father Reeve will read. The cathedral has done this before. Marcus would —" She stopped. Then, more carefully, "He would have wanted us to have evensong. He would have wanted it to be properly sung. He was very particular about that."

"Yes," Helen said. "I know."

At the front door Rosalind turned and put her hand, briefly, on Helen's arm. The touch was light and dry and entirely not theatrical.

"Thank you, Helen," she said. "Thank you for letting me sit. I won't do it again. But thank you."

Helen said, "Come back when you want to."

She watched Rosalind go down the path and across the close in the slant April light, the pale blue cardigan moving against the grey of her skirt, and Helen stood in her doorway and watched until Rosalind's gate closed behind her.

Then Helen went back to the kitchen and stood at the calendar.

She did not need to read it. She knew what it said.

She read it anyway.

She read the Thursday slot twice, and the diagonal line, and the marker pen note in Rosalind's own hand, and she stood there until she had memorised the date of the cancellation, and then she sat down at the kitchen table opposite the half-eaten shortbread and put her hands flat on the table either side of the cup, and let the silence tell her what it had to tell her.

She did not yet name it.

But she heard it.
