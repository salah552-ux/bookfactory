# CHAPTER TWO
## Morning Prayer

Edmund Hale had been a cathedral lawyer for twenty-two years, which meant that he had become a man who answered the telephone before it had finished its first ring, on the working assumption that anyone who rang a man like him before seven in the morning was ringing because something had broken that the man like him would have to put back together.

When Thomas Oakes said, "Canon — it's Marcus," Edmund had already swung his legs out of bed, and the cold of the floorboards through his pyjama trousers was a sensation he would remember, later, with peculiar precision: as though the body had been first to understand.

"What about Marcus, Thomas."

There was a pause. Edmund could hear, behind Thomas's breathing, the particular acoustic of the nave — the high, vacant resonance that meant a man was standing somewhere near the rood screen, with his phone held at a distance from his face, perhaps because he was looking at something that he could not look away from.

"He's at the foot of the stair," Thomas said. "The north transept stair. I think — Canon, I think you'd better come."

"Thomas. Is he conscious."

"No, Canon."

"Is he breathing."

The pause this time was longer.

"No," Thomas said.

Edmund said, "Don't touch him. Don't move him. Don't let anyone else in. I'll be there in three minutes. I'm calling 999 now." He put the receiver down without waiting for an answer, because Thomas was a man who preferred to be told what to do in moments of difficulty, and because Edmund had registered, somewhere in the cool back room of his mind, that what he was being told would shortly become an event, and that the way he behaved in the next hour would be looked at later by people whose job it was to look at such things.

He called 999. He gave the address with the brisk efficiency he kept for the magistrate's court. He pulled on yesterday's trousers and a shirt and the cassock he used for working mornings, because something in him said that if Marcus Vayne was dead at the foot of the organ loft stair, he was going to want to be wearing the cassock when he stood next to the body.

He picked up his notebook from the bedside table and put it in his cassock pocket without opening it.

Then he rang Helen.

She answered on the first ring, which was not characteristic.

"Helen. It's Edmund. Something has happened. Can you come."

"To the cathedral."

"Yes."

"I'll be there in two minutes."

She rang off without asking what. Edmund stood for a moment in his hallway with the receiver still in his hand and registered that this, also, was something he would have cause to think about later.

—

He let himself in by the south door, because the north transept door was, presumably, still where Thomas was. The cathedral at seven in the morning had a particular quality — the night cold still in the stone, the smell of wax and old hymn-books, a thin grey light through the clerestory that had not yet found any colour in the glass. He walked up the south aisle past the empty stalls. The bell began to ring for Morning Prayer. Thomas had not got as far as silencing the bell.

Edmund came through the screen and saw, first, Thomas standing at a careful distance, holding his cap, and then he saw the body.

Marcus lay on his back at the foot of the stone stair, with his head turned a little to the left, as though he had been considering something on the floor. His arms were at his sides. His legs were straight. He was wearing the dark green cardigan he wore in the loft. His glasses were not on his face. They were two feet away from him, against the base of the pillar, and the right lens had cracked across.

The position was very, very calm.

Edmund had only seen one body in his life that had fallen from any height — a roofer in 2009, at the deanery — and he remembered that body as a thing the eye refused, briefly, to read. This body did not refuse the eye. This body lay as a man lies on a beach.

He registered the thought and pushed it down.

He said, "Thomas. Tell me."

Thomas told him: he had come in at six fifty to set up for Morning Prayer. He had walked through to put the kneelers out. He had seen Marcus on the floor. He had touched his neck. He had rung Edmund.

"Was the door open."

"Which door, Canon."

"Any of them."

Thomas looked at him with the expression of a man who had been answering questions truthfully for the better part of an hour and was beginning to find the experience tiring.

"The north transept stair door was open," he said. "I closed it when I came in. Sorry, Canon, I closed it before I saw — I closed it because it wasn't supposed to be open."

"Don't apologise, Thomas."

The first siren reached them then, faint, from the far end of the close. Edmund looked at his watch. Helen would be at the south door in fifteen seconds.

He glanced at the loft up above them, eighteen feet of shadow, and then he looked back at Marcus, and he found himself thinking — quite clearly, as though the thought were a printed page — that there was something wrong with the body, but that he did not have the technical vocabulary to say what it was.

He thought: Helen will know.

—

By eight o'clock the south transept was a working room.

Edmund stood by the screen and watched the close arrive in pieces. The Dean, in her overcoat, came at a careful pace from the deanery and did not look at the body. Father Reeve came at a half-run from the chaplains' house and stopped twenty feet away and put his hand over his mouth.

Webb came from his door at No. 5. He stopped at the end of the south aisle and looked. He did not come closer. He stood with his hands at his sides and looked at the body for what Edmund timed at twenty-two seconds. Then he turned and walked out the way he had come, and Edmund noted, against his will, that Webb had not asked anyone what had happened.

Margaret Holt arrived in her green coat, and burst into tears, and was led away by Father Reeve.

Rosalind came last, from No. 4. She came across the close at a run that was not quite a run — a quick, light walk, the walk of a person who has been told something terrible and is trying not to be undignified. She came through the south door and saw the body and her hand went to her mouth and she said, very softly, "Oh, Marcus."

She stopped where she was. She did not come closer. She stood with her hand at her mouth and she looked at the body, and then she looked at Edmund, and her eyes filled, and she said, "What happened."

"We don't know yet."

"Was he — was he playing."

"We don't know yet, Rosalind."

She nodded several times, as though the nodding helped. Then she said, "Tell me what to do, Edmund. Tell me what I can do."

He said, "Sit. Sit there." He pointed to the canon's stall. She went and sat.

The Dean had crossed the chancel by then and was standing at his elbow. Her overcoat was buttoned to the throat. She looked at the body for about three seconds and then she looked at Edmund and said, "Will this affect Easter services."

He looked at her.

"I beg your pardon, Dean."

"The Easter services," she said. "Will we — should we be making decisions."

"He has been dead an hour, Patricia."

She nodded. She had heard herself. Her face went, briefly, the colour of a person who has heard herself and would prefer not to have. She put her hand on Edmund's arm and said, "Of course. I'm sorry. Of course." And then she said, "Tell me what you need."

He looked at her hand on his sleeve and thought: I have known you for eight years and I have never known whether to like you. He did not say it.

He said, "I need Helen."

—

Helen came through the south door at one minute past eight, in her tweed coat, with her pearls at her throat, and her face entirely without expression. She looked once at the body, and once at Edmund, and once at Rosalind in the canon's stall, and once back at the body.

She walked the long way round, so as not to disturb the floor.

She stopped six feet from Marcus and looked.

Then she said, quietly, only to Edmund: "How long until the police are here."

"Eight minutes."

"Good," she said. "I want a minute first."
