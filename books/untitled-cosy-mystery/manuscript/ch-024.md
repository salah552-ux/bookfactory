# CHAPTER TWENTY-FOUR
## Pressure

Father Simon Reeve had never been asked to give a formal statement before, and he approached the task with the combination of conscientiousness and underlying anxiety that Edmund had come to associate with people who were innocent of everything but wanted very badly to be understood as such. He had requested the meeting himself — this was important — and he had brought notes. He sat in Edmund's office on Friday morning with a single sheet of A4 and looked at the items on it with an expression that suggested he had memorised them and was re-reading them mainly for reassurance.

Edmund made tea. He placed the cups on the cherrywood table, moved the audit papers aside, and sat at the corner rather than across from the young priest, which removed the adversarial geometry without eliminating it entirely.

"I want to be accurate," Father Reeve said. He had a Bristol burr that he didn't entirely suppress, even in formal speech.

"I know," Edmund said. "Take your time."

"The argument between Canon Webb and Marcus — I've been thinking about whether 'argument' is the right word." He turned the sheet over and then back. "What I heard was one side of a conversation through the Chapter House wall, two Wednesdays before Marcus died. A raised voice. Marcus's, I thought — I recognised it. The pitch he used when he was conducting and the tenors weren't quite right. Not angry exactly. More like insistent."

"What did you hear him say?"

"Two complete sentences. 'It is not your decision.' And then, approximately one minute later — there was silence in between — 'I have told you what I am going to do and I am going to do it.'" Father Reeve set the sheet down. "And then silence, and then footsteps, and then nothing."

Edmund made a note. He wrote both sentences and underlined "I am going to do it."

"Was there a response?"

"I didn't hear one. The walls are thick. I may simply have been in the wrong position to hear the other voice — I was moving. I wasn't trying to listen." He paused. "I want to say that clearly. I wasn't eavesdropping."

"I understand."

"I didn't think anything of it at the time. Marcus was often that way in working conversations — insistent, I mean. It was only after, when I began to think about it—" He stopped. He looked at his hands. "Do you think it was about Canon Webb?"

Edmund chose his words with some care. "I think," he said, "that there are a number of things Marcus might have been insistent about in his last weeks. We are exploring all of them."

Father Reeve looked at him for a moment. He was a clever young man, Edmund thought. Not quite clever enough to ask the next question yet, but he would be, given a year or two and a few more difficult pastoral situations.

After the priest left, Edmund sat for some time with the two sentences.

*It is not your decision. I have told you what I am going to do and I am going to do it.*

The difficulty was that this fitted Webb perfectly. Webb had lost the Director of Music position to Marcus fifteen years ago. The resentment was documented, by multiple witnesses, across multiple years. Marcus telling Webb in the Chapter House, two Wednesdays before the murder, that Webb's objections to some decision were irrelevant and that Marcus intended to proceed regardless — this was entirely consistent with Webb's profile, with the documented workplace relationship, and with Webb's subsequent evasion and hostility when questioned.

It was also consistent with Marcus saying the same words to Rosalind.

Edmund put that thought into the notebook beside the two sentences. He bracketed both possibilities. He did not indicate a preference between them, because a preference was not his place, and because — more honestly — the brackets kept both possibilities formal and alive in a way that a preference would close off.

At two o'clock, his phone rang. It was Detective Inspector Hargreaves — Claire's superior, a man Edmund had met twice, both times in the context of conversations he had not found pleasant. Hargreaves had a manner of being warm without being friendly, which Edmund found disconcerting in the way that structural ambiguity in a legal document was disconcerting: not wrong exactly, but a situation waiting for the wrong occasion to go wrong.

"Mr. Hale," Hargreaves said. "I understand you've been helpful to DS Marsh's inquiry."

"I hope so," Edmund said.

"We're at a point where we need to make a decision about next steps." A brief pause. The sound of a pen tapping on a desk. "You'll understand that an inquiry of this duration — six weeks, the cathedral community, considerable press interest — creates its own pressures."

"I do understand that."

"DS Marsh has a working suspect. The evidence is circumstantial but substantial. I want to make sure we're not waiting indefinitely for something that may or may not add to what we already have."

Edmund was quiet for a moment. He was thinking about the forensic imaging contractor in Bristol, about the urgent flag that Patel had applied to the enhancement request, about two to three days that had now been two days and might be one more.

"The enhancement of the Song School CCTV footage," he said, "was flagged as urgent. I was given a two-to-three day window."

"Flagged by whom?"

"By DS Marsh's own technical officer."

"I see." The pen stopped tapping. "And you feel this would materially affect the direction of the inquiry?"

"I think," Edmund said carefully, "that it would be wise to allow the enhancement to arrive before any decisions about next steps are made. I think there are aspects of the access question — particularly regarding the Song School key system — that would benefit from review before any charging decision."

"Canon Webb's access?"

"His access, yes. And that of other parties."

The pause this time was longer. Edmund heard something in the background on Hargreaves's end — a door, someone speaking. Then Hargreaves said: "I'll speak to DS Marsh."

The call ended.

Edmund set his phone down. He looked at the window, at the close beyond it, at the empty stretch of cobbled path between his office and the cathedral's south door. The afternoon was beginning to grey. He could smell rain in the air through the gap at the bottom of the old window, which had never fully sealed in forty years of attempts to fix it.

He thought about Webb. He thought about Webb in that Monday interview, sitting with Anne close but not touching, the particular quality of fear that a man displayed when he was hiding something that was not what he was being questioned about. It was a specific fear — not the fear of a murderer, but the fear of a small man caught in a large thing.

He had perhaps bought one day. Perhaps a day and a half.

He looked at the notebook and thought: two days. Then he looked at the window — the gap at the bottom of the frame, the rain smell coming through, the same gap that had never been fully sealed in forty years of attempts.

Some things, he thought, were simply designed to let the outside in.

He did not know whether two days would be enough.
