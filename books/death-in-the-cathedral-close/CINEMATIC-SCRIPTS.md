**[BODY — spoken narration, slow and deliberate]**

Helen heard the first wrong note before she'd decided to be awake.

She lay in the dark of the cathedral close — no streetlamps, no moon — and she listened.

Organ music. Bach. Then it broke off.

Then Mendelssohn. Then silence.

She's seventy years old. She's a retired forensic pathologist. She knows what it means when a pattern breaks.

She got up. Put her feet on the cold floorboards. Went to the window.

The organ loft light was off.

Someone was playing the organ in a dark building at two in the morning.

And then she saw it.

The north transept door — ten feet of oak, famously stiff, always closed — was open.

Just the width of a hand.

She stood at her window and asked herself the question she'd been trained for forty years to ask:

*What is the simplest explanation that accounts for all of these?*

She went back to bed.

She didn't sleep.

## VERSION 2 — YOUTUBE (3 minutes 30 seconds)

**Target:** ~530 words spoken | Full atmospheric chapter retelling
**Visual:** Single location — dimly lit study or window at night. One candle. No movement.
**Music:** Organ music (Bach, solo) — very low, barely audible. Fades in at 0:00, fades out at 0:30, returns at end.
**Voice:** Same — measured, precise, unhurried. Long pauses where marked [PAUSE].

---

**[COLD OPEN — spoken over black screen]**

*The first wrong note woke her.*

[PAUSE — 3 seconds]

**[TITLE CARD: Death in the Cathedral Close]**

**[NARRATION begins — camera slowly reveals window, night outside]**

It was two in the morning in Wychford Cathedral Close. Helen Marsh lay on her back in the particular dark that only a medieval close can produce — no streetlamps, no traffic, only the faint silver of the west front bleeding through the curtain gap.

She heard the organ.

Bach, she thought. Not played well — played in fragments. A few bars, then broken off, as if the player had remembered something else mid-phrase. Then silence. Then a different piece. Mendelssohn. The Wedding March. A few bars of that. Then nothing.

[PAUSE]

She's seventy years old. She spent thirty-five years attending postmortems, giving evidence in crown courts, cataloguing the small, exact facts that tell you how a person died. She knows what a normal pattern looks like. And she knows when something has broken it.

She got up.

Cold floorboards. Navy dressing gown. The cathedral rising against the night sky through the gap in the curtains — a darker shape of dark.

She looked at the organ loft.

The slit windows were unlit.

Marcus Vayne had been organist at Wychford Cathedral for twenty-five years. He kept irregular hours — Helen knew that. She'd seen the warm yellow square of the loft light at midnight in February, his white shirt cuff moving against the keys.

But Marcus played with the lamp on.

Marcus played with his music open.

Marcus did not play in the dark.

[PAUSE]

She went downstairs without turning on the hall light, opened the front door an inch, and stood.

The smell of the close at night: cold stone, wood-smoke, the faint sweetness of the Daphne by the gate. The cathedral forty yards away across the shared lawn, the west front faintly lit by the perimeter security lamps.

And the north transept door.

Ten feet of oak, iron-studded, famously stiff. The door that was always closed.

It was open.

Not wide. The width of a hand.

[PAUSE — 4 seconds]

She stood in her dressing gown and her slippers and she asked herself the question she had been trained for forty years to ask:

*What is the simplest explanation that accounts for all of these?*

She thought of one. It was reasonable. It was probably right.

It did not account for the silence that had now lasted four minutes.

She went back inside. Turned the deadbolt. Did not put the chain across — she couldn't have explained why.

She lay in her cold bedroom and listened to the cathedral clock mark the hours.

Half past two. Three. Half past three. Four.

She did not sleep.

[PAUSE]

At a little after five she made tea and stood at the kitchen window watching the cathedral go slowly grey in the first of the light.

At six fifty-eight, the telephone rang.

She knew, before she put her feet on the floor, that it was Edmund.

She knew, before she lifted the receiver, what he was going to say.

[PAUSE — 5 seconds. Organ music returns, very low.]

The organ loft light had been off.

The music had been playing in the dark.

## PRODUCTION NOTES

**Voice delivery guidance:**
- Read at 80% of your natural talking speed
- The long pauses are not mistakes — they are the atmosphere
- The word "dark" should land heavy every time
- "She did not sleep" — flat. No drama. That IS the drama.

**TikTok posting time:** Tuesday or Thursday, 7–9pm UK / 2–4pm ET
**YouTube posting:** Any day, upload as both a Short (60s cut) and full video

**Hook variations to A/B test (swap first 3 seconds):**
- Option A (used above): *"Someone was playing the organ. In the dark. At two in the morning."*
- Option B: *"She heard the organ at 2am. The light in the loft was off."*
- Option C: *"The door was always closed. It was open."*
