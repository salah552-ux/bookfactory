# CHAPTER NINETEEN
## What We Both Know

Edmund called at eleven. Not to her door — to her phone, which was unusual; he preferred to knock. "Would you be at home this afternoon?" he said. "I've had a development with Webb. And I suspect you've had one of your own."

"Two o'clock," Helen said.

She spent the morning in the archive, which she did when she needed to think in a room that had already absorbed seven centuries of complicated thought and could manage one more. She was cataloguing a set of Victorian faculty records — faculties for alterations to the nave seating in 1891, meticulously argued and now entirely superseded — and she moved through them with the automatic competence of long practice while her mind arranged and rearranged what she was going to say to Edmund and in what order.

It was not, she thought, that she needed Edmund's confirmation. She had not needed it since she had sat in her kitchen that morning and looked at the number — Mrs. Bridget Ferris, sort code 00-19-47, account opened twelve years ago. She had needed it before that, or what she told herself had been need was probably the discipline of not acting on certainty until certainty had been established by something other than her own reasoning.

She was aware that this was, to a degree, a form of procrastination dressed as procedure. She was a scientist. Scientists double-checked. She had not needed Edmund's confirmation; she had needed to have checked the work, and she had checked it, and now she had it, and what remained was the telephone call to Claire that she did not want to make, which was interesting, because she had made harder calls in her professional life and had made them cleanly, without this particular quality of reluctance.

She thought: *the difference is Marcus's professional life. This is the people around me. These are the people I walk past every morning.*

Edmund arrived at five minutes before two and she opened the door before he knocked, which she knew would register and did not bother to disguise. He came in, removed his coat, and stood in the hallway looking at her with an expression she recognised — it was the expression of a man who has worked something out and knows that the person in front of him has also worked it out and is deciding whether to go first or second.

"Webb," Edmund said. "He came to me this morning. He was with Sarah Clifton — the choir soprano — on the night. Her house in Vicars' Row. Nine until about half past ten."

Helen considered this. "The affair."

"Yes."

"That explains all of it. The evasion. The inconsistency. 'I will speak to Anne first.'"

"Yes." Edmund looked at the sitting room door. "Is there tea?"

There was tea. She had made it before he arrived, which meant by now it was slightly too strong, which was how Edmund preferred it. She poured without being asked and they sat on opposite sides of the fireplace in what had become, she realised, something close to their customary arrangement — she in the upright chair she had used since David's death, he in the wing chair that David had preferred and that no one else had sat in for eight years, until Edmund had sat in it three weeks ago without appearing to notice its significance.

He was sitting in it now. She noted this and did not comment on it.

"He also told me," Edmund said, "that Marcus knew about the affair. Had known for three years. Said nothing."

"That's consistent," Helen said. "With the rest of it. The locked box. The complaint letter sent through proper channels."

"Yes." Edmund looked at his cup. "What have you found?"

She told him. The sort code. The account holder. The Clifton Convalescent Home. The death notice — three lines, eighteen months ago. The dates of the last withdrawals. She told it in the order she had found it, which was the efficient order, without editorialising. He listened with the quality of attention she had come to recognise in him over the past weeks: completely still, the notebook open but the pen not moving, the information being held rather than immediately processed.

When she finished he was quiet for a moment. The clock — David's grandfather clock, which kept slightly approximate time in a way Helen had never corrected — marked the quarter hour.

"Bridget Ferris," Edmund said.

"Yes."

"Twelve years."

"Yes."

He looked at the fire, which was unlit — April did not quite require it, though the archive had made her cold that morning and she had been mildly tempted. "I wonder," he said, and stopped. She waited. "I wonder if Marcus knew. The full shape of it. The care home, the mother. Or whether he only knew it was the music fund."

"I don't know," Helen said. "I note it probably wouldn't have changed his decision. He had made up his mind."

"No." Edmund was quiet again. Then: "The complaint letter. He had tried to do it properly. Through the Bishop."

"She intercepted it."

"And then he was dead within three weeks." Edmund set his cup down with a precision that suggested he was thinking carefully about something other than where to put the cup. "The Bristol pharmacist on the eleventh of March — before the complaint letter. Three days before it. That premeditation."

"She had already decided," Helen said. "The complaint letter made it urgent. It wasn't what caused it."

"No." He picked up his pen. He wrote something in the notebook — she did not look at it and he did not show it to her, which was their arrangement. "Helen." He looked up. "Have you told Claire?"

"Not yet."

"That's why I'm here."

"Yes."

They sat with that for a moment. The rain from the morning had cleared and the afternoon light was coming through the window at the low late-April angle that made everything in the room look slightly more substantial than it was — the edges of things definite, the colours warmed. She could hear, through the window glass, the faint sound of the organ in rehearsal from inside the cathedral: someone working through a passage over and over, finding the same place where it fell slightly short, going back.

"She will want to know," Edmund said, "what we have and in what order we found it. She will want it all."

"I know."

"And she will want to know—" He stopped. "She will want it to be enough. Before she can move."

"What we have is enough," Helen said. "I note the care home fact is not in itself the arrest. But the care home fact, combined with the key, the CCTV request, the pharmacy records, the letter interception, the two bottles — combined with all of that, it is enough to formally redirect the investigation."

"Yes," Edmund said. "It is."

She picked up her phone from the table beside her chair. Edmund looked at the window. She dialled and it rang twice and then Claire answered and said, in the tone that meant she was not alone: "Dr Marsh."

"DS Marsh," Helen said. "I have some information. I wonder if you might come to No. 7 this afternoon when you can, or if you prefer, Edmund and I can come to the station. We have found the music fund withdrawals destination. And I believe you should hear it today."

A pause. Short, but Helen knew Claire well enough to read what was in it: the shift from professional caution to something more alert. "Give me an hour," Claire said. She did not ask whether it was conclusive. She had learned, somewhere in the years since the coroner's office, that Helen did not call unless it was.

Helen put the phone down.

Edmund, across from her, was looking at the window. The organ in the cathedral had stopped at some point during the call and the close was quiet — the particular quality of afternoon quiet that Wychford had when the rehearsals and the services paused and the community simply existed for a moment in the spaces between its obligations. She had not noticed it, those first weeks. She noticed it now.

"Right," Edmund said. He did not say it in a way that required a response.

"Right," Helen agreed.

He looked at her. For a moment neither of them said anything, which was not an absence of communication. They had, she thought, been communicating this way for several weeks now — in the pauses, in the things not said, in the evidence laid out on tables and the notebook entries that she did not read and he did not show her, and in the shared understanding, accumulated day by day, of what this investigation had actually been.

She had started it alone, she noted. She had looked at the body with her pathologist's eye and known within sixty seconds that it was wrong, and she had begun from that private knowing and had worked in what she supposed she could admit now had been a kind of isolation — the habit of a person who had learned to rely on her own reasoning and not particularly to share it until it was complete.

She was not alone now. She noted this without further comment.

"She'll want us both," Edmund said.

"I expect so."

He nodded and reached for his coat where he'd laid it over the arm of the chair. Outside, the organ started again — the same passage, the same fall-short, the same patient return to the beginning. Someone who was not going to leave it unresolved.

Helen put her tea cup on the tray and went to get her coat and her notebook, and called Claire back to say they would come to her.

She had named it out loud now, in this room, to Edmund, and in a moment she would name it to Claire, and she was quite clear-headed about what that meant and did not require herself to feel anything in particular about it. She was a pathologist. Evidence was evidence. The name was Rosalind Ferris, and she had told DS Claire Marsh that she believed Rosalind Ferris had killed Marcus Vayne, and the investigation had turned.
