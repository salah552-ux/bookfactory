# CHAPTER TEN
## No Rehearsal

Edmund went up to his office after the memorial and locked the door behind him.

He did not, in twenty-two years, often lock the door of his office. The door had a lock, of the heavy iron kind that had been put in in 1893, and the key was kept in the keyhole on the inside; but the cathedral working day did not, ordinarily, require that an officer of the Chapter make himself unavailable to a Chapter colleague who needed five minutes.

He locked it.

He stood with his hand on the key for a moment after the bolt had gone over. He registered, with a small dry detachment, that he was a man who had begun, in the last seventy-two hours, to take small physical precautions against being interrupted. He registered that this was not nothing.

He went to the desk.

—

The cathedral schedule for the choir term was kept, in his office, in a buff-coloured A4 binder labelled *MUSIC — TERM PLAN — JANUARY TO JULY*, a copy of which Margaret Holt printed up for him in January and updated in pencil on a rolling weekly basis. He kept it because he liked, when there was a Chapter meeting on a Tuesday, to be able to say without having to ring anyone whether choir was due to rehearse on the Thursday.

He took it from the shelf and opened it.

He turned to the relevant week.

Thursday.

The Thursday slot for choir practice had a black diagonal line drawn through it, in marker pen, in a hand he recognised at once as Rosalind's. In the margin, in the same marker pen, in the same hand, was the small careful note: *cancelled — Confirmation rehearsals taking precedence*.

He did not, immediately, do anything.

He stood at the open binder with his finger on the line.

The cancellation had been made, by the dating in pencil at the top of the page, on the third of April — three weeks before the death. He had been at the Chapter meeting at which it had been formally noted. He could remember, now that he was reminded, that Rosalind had given the substitution arrangements; that the Confirmation rehearsals would use the choir's Thursday slot for three consecutive weeks; that the choir would not, in this period, rehearse on Thursday at all. He could remember saying *thank you, Rosalind* in the way one says *thank you, Rosalind* at a Tuesday meeting at a quarter past five.

He stood at the binder for some time.

He thought, with the small clean precision he had been keeping for thirty-six hours: *there is no Thursday rehearsal this week. There has not been one for three weeks. There will not be one next week. Rosalind has told three people that she went round to Marcus's loft on the night he was killed in order to remind him of it.*

He sat down very slowly in his chair.

—

He had Margaret bring him the police evidence schedule at four. Margaret brought it without asking why, which was one of the reasons Margaret was Margaret. The schedule had come to him as cathedral liaison.

He read it through.

The police lab had returned the preliminary forensic on the items recovered from the chancel and the loft. Edmund read it twice — once at the speed of a cathedral lawyer, once at the speed of a man who had become, in the last two days, a different kind of man.

The items were listed numerically.

*Item 17. Coffee mug, white china, Wychford Cathedral kitchen issue. Recovered from the second tread of the north transept stair at 08.42 hrs. Examined. Wiped clean of all biological and chemical residues consistent with use, with the exception of a faint residue ring detectable only on chemical analysis at the inner base of the mug. The residue, on testing, confirms the presence of a soluble compound consistent with digoxin in solution in coffee. Concentration not measurable due to the wiping but inconsistent with the trace quantities one would expect from prescription-dose ingestion.*

He read it a third time.

The mug had been wiped. Wiped of fingerprints and of all visible residue. But not, in the end, wiped well enough — there had been enough digoxin in the coffee that a faint film had remained at the very bottom of the cup, where the wiping cloth had passed and missed.

He laid the page flat on his desk.

He thought: *Helen knew*.

Helen had, on the morning of the body, looked at the mug and known that the mug had been wiped, and had said so to him in the cathedral, and he had not, at the time, fully heard what she had said. He had heard *the mug is wiped* and he had filed it as one of the small forensic notings that Helen produced on the morning of bodies. He had not, until this moment, joined it to anything else.

He joined it now.

He picked up the schedule and read on.

*Item 24. Pharmacy bottle, brown plastic, Wychford Pharmacy, prescription label Marcus Vayne, dated 28 January, contents two tablets digoxin 0.125mg. Recovered from the archive corridor (north stack, 1830s parish registers). Cap loose. Two surfaces tested for prints. The label surface bears a single partial print of the deceased. The cap and body bear no prints, having been wiped down.*

Wiped down.

The killer had wiped the mug. The killer had wiped the bottle. The killer had used a cloth, twice, on two separate objects, in two separate locations, in two separate parts of the cathedral, on the same night.

He sat back.

—

He did not, for a long time, do anything.

He stood up at one point. He went to the cherrywood table where his audit printouts were still spread, and he stood at the table without sitting at it, and he counted again. Forty-seven payments. Three Bristol PO boxes. Twelve years.

He went back to the desk.

He picked up the police schedule and turned to the third item that he had noticed and had not, on the first read, allowed himself to attend to.

*Item 31. Personal medication: digoxin batch number reference XR-0184/22. Trace from coffee mug residue: matches prescription bottle (Item 24). Note: enquiry as to whether deceased held any second prescription of digoxin remains open; pharmacy records being requested.*

He read it twice.

The trace in the coffee matched the bottle in the archive. Marcus's own prescription. His killer had used Marcus's own bottle. The killer had taken it from the office, dissolved a fatal dose into his coffee, brought him the cup, watched, and then carried the bottle round the back of the cathedral and tucked it behind a row of parish registers in the archive corridor. Then wiped the cup. Then wiped the bottle. Then walked out of the north transept stair door into the close at four minutes past eleven.

He thought: *the killer has access to the archive*.

He thought: *the killer has access to the Song School corridor*.

He thought, with a stillness in him: *the killer wears a pale blue cardigan*.

—

He did, then, a thing he had not done in twenty-two years in this office.

He stood up, and he went to the rosewood bookcase, and he took down the small grey ring-binder labelled *MUSIC — TERM PLAN — JANUARY TO JULY* — the second copy of the binder, the spare he had not consulted in four months — and he opened it, and he turned to the relevant Thursday, and he checked the cancellation in the second copy. The diagonal line was there. Margaret's pencil tick was there, dated the third of April. The cancellation was as it ought to have been.

He closed the binder.

He went to the cherrywood table and gathered the audit pages into a stack and put them in the deep drawer of his desk and locked the drawer with the key that lived in his cassock pocket.

He put the police schedule on top of the desk.

He sat with it.

It was now five o'clock. Outside the office window the cloister was lit by the very low light of an April evening. Father Reeve would be processing into evensong in eleven minutes. Rosalind would be in her stall. Edmund would, on most days, by this hour, be putting on his cassock at the back of the south aisle.

He did not put on his cassock.

He did not go down to evensong.

He sat at the desk with his hands flat on the schedule, and he thought through, in order, the small things.

The mug had been wiped — and in a way that had only just failed.
The bottle had been wiped — and had been hidden in a corridor accessed by a Song School key.
The cardigan had been pale blue, soft, hip-length, walking briskly out of the north transept stair at four past eleven.
The Thursday rehearsal had not existed for three weeks.
The music fund had been disbursed on a single signature for twelve years, and the single signature had been Rosalind's.

He counted them. There were five.

He thought: *each of these is, on its own, explicable.*

He thought: *all of them together, on one mantelpiece, in one woman, are not.*

He took out his battered notebook and turned to a fresh page, and he wrote the date, and beneath the date he wrote, in his small careful hand, three lines.

The last line was: *I wonder if it has been Rosalind*.

He underlined the verb *wonder* twice.

He sat with the notebook open in front of him and he looked at it for what felt like a long time, and at last he closed the notebook and put it in the cassock pocket, and he straightened the police schedule on the desk, and he stood up.

He did not go down to evensong.

He did not ring Helen.

He had, in the cathedral acoustic of his head, the question: had Helen seen all of these things, and arrived at the same place, and held the same name in her own private silence.

He thought, with absolute clarity: yes.

He said nothing to Helen.

He needed to be certain first.
