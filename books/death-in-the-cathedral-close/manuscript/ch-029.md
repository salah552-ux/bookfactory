# CHAPTER TWENTY-NINE

The close did not know about Webb's release until Tuesday morning. This was unusual — information moved quickly in the close — but Monday evening had been wet, and the cathedral evening prayers were poorly attended, and several houses that would normally have had their front room lights on had them off. Helen had gone to bed early without being sleepy and had lain listening to the rain on the roof valley outside her window, which made a variable sound depending on how hard it was raining, and had thought about what Claire would be doing.

Claire would be building the file. She was methodical and she was good at this — Helen had watched her become good at it, fifteen years ago in a different context, in the coroner's office when Claire was still a trainee and still making the mistakes that trainees made and still correcting them. She was thorough and she was not impatient; the file would be assembled carefully, each piece of evidence supported and cross-referenced, the timeline reconstructed in a form that would survive examination by a barrister who would want to find holes in it.

It would take time. Helen had asked, before she left the station, how long. Claire had said: "Not long." Which, from Claire, meant what it always meant — she was being precise and the question she had been asked was the wrong one.

Helen went to the archive on Tuesday morning.

The Tudor land grant was finished now. She had completed the transcription over the past three weeks in the small hours between other things, and it lay on the table under its cotton square, waiting to be photographed and filed. She photographed it. She opened the next box — fifteenth-century bishop's correspondence, much of it in a hand that required patience rather than expertise, the patience of someone willing to look at a form until the form became legible. She opened her notebook to a clean page.

She had been sitting for twenty minutes when she became aware that what she was doing was the archival equivalent of making tea without drinking it.

She sat with her pen above the blank page and thought about the arrest warrant.

She had never been on this side of the process. She had spent thirty-five years on the side that received the conclusions — the court, the inquest, the coroner's hearing, the legal machinery that processed what the police and pathologist and toxicologist had produced. She knew that machinery well. She did not know the rhythm of the process by which one person came to be formally named as a suspect in the preliminary documentation, the moment when a specific name and a specific charge became the thing that would be put before a court. She knew it happened. She had assumed she would know when it had.

She packed her work at eleven o'clock and climbed the cellar stairs and went out into the close.

The rain had cleared overnight. The close grass was very green after it, and the cathedral's west front had darkened from the water and was drying in patches — the stone shifting from nearly-black to its ordinary grey in uneven shapes that gave the surface a layered quality, like geology. A pair of jackdaws were in the cloister garth, doing something purposeful with a piece of wrapper. The close gate was open; a delivery van was reversing in, the driver navigating the width of the medieval gate with the total confidence of someone who did it every week.

Helen walked north along the close path. She had no particular destination. She was, she understood, walking in order not to be inside, which was a simpler reason than she usually gave herself.

She was passing No. 4 when she noticed the curtains were open in the front room.

She had been aware of No. 4 every time she walked past it for six weeks. This was not unusual — she was a thorough person and she had been thinking about the house for six weeks — but she had trained herself not to look at it in any way that would be visible. The house was the house. The front room was the front room. The piano was behind the window.

This morning, Rosalind was at the window.

She was doing something with a plant on the internal sill — a trailing thing in a terracotta pot that needed attention, a tendril out of place, and Rosalind was working at it with both hands, rearranging the stems with the deliberate patience of a person who tends things, who has always tended things, who finds this particular rhythm steadying. She had not yet looked up.

Helen did not slow her pace. She could not slow her pace. Any change in the ordinary rhythm of walking past would itself be a signal, and the situation did not want more signals. She walked at the pace she had been walking.

As she drew level with the window, Rosalind looked up.

It was a coincidence of timing — not more than that; Rosalind had finished with the plant and was simply looking out, the way a person did, the way anyone did when they had been looking down at something and then looked up because the middle distance was what the eyes wanted next. She did not arrange her expression. She had not seen Helen coming. The look was simply: a face, a moment, the close outside the window.

Helen held her pace.

Their eyes met. Four, five steps — enough for it to register on both sides that it had happened, that the meeting was real, that neither of them was going to look away first, and neither of them was going to accelerate it.

Then Helen was past the window and walking north toward the cathedral's south door, and the moment was behind her.

She stopped in the porch. She stood for a moment in the cold smell of the stone — candle wax and something underneath it, the remnant of the Easter lilies finally composting into something darker and sweeter — and she breathed it.

She thought about what she had seen in Rosalind's face in those four or five steps. She had a vocabulary for faces; she had spent thirty-five years developing it. What she had seen was — not fear. Not guilt, in the performed sense. Something more precise than either. The specific expression of a person who has been carrying a thing and who has, in the moment of the eyes meeting, calculated exactly how much of the thing the other person also carries.

A reckoning. A mutual one.

Neither of them spoke. Then Rosalind moved away from the window, and was gone, and Helen walked on.
