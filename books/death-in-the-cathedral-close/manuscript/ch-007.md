# CHAPTER SEVEN
## Cardigan

Thomas Oakes had a small office behind the south porch in which he had spent forty-three years and which he had decorated with the cumulative inattention of a man who needed only the desk and the kettle and the keys.

Helen, who had been there once before in three years — to ask after a parish register — sat in the visitor's chair with her tweed coat folded across her knees and watched Thomas brewing tea. He brewed tea, she noted, with the seriousness of a man for whom the brewing was the conversation. She did not interrupt.

"It was the cuff that I noticed," Thomas said, when he had set the cup in front of her.

"Of the cardigan."

"Of the cardigan. You asked me yesterday morning about the cardigan, Mrs Marsh, and I've thought about it in the night."

"Tell me."

Thomas sat down. He turned his own cup in its saucer.

"I was late," he said. "I was late on the rounds. I'd had a — well. My niece had rung at quarter to ten. Family thing. I'd taken the call in this room and then I'd had to sit a moment, and so when I went out for the rounds I was late by — twenty-five minutes, perhaps. Twenty. I'd ordinarily be at the south end at twenty-to. I was at the south end at five past."

Helen waited.

"I came round the south porch and across the cloister edge — the way I always go — and I came up by the north transept door, on the outside. There's a flagged path there. The cathedral wall is on my right hand. I was about to come round the corner. I heard the door open. The north transept stair door."

"Heard it."

"It's stiff, you know. It scrapes on the threshold. Marcus used to complain about it. I knew the sound of it because — well — because it always wanted oiling, and I used to oil it, and Marcus used to comment on whether I had."

"Yes."

"So I heard it scrape, and I came round the corner — perhaps three seconds, four seconds — and there was a woman walking away from me, going up the close. Towards the deanery, near as. She was perhaps twenty paces ahead by then. She didn't look round."

"Walking pace."

"Brisk. Not running. The walk of a person who didn't want to be slow. But not running."

"Describe the cardigan."

Thomas considered. He took up his cup, drank, set it down.

"Pale blue," he said. "I'd say the colour they call powder. Not bright. Not pale grey-blue, the other kind. Powder. I said cashmere yesterday — I don't know I'd swear cashmere. It was soft. The way it sat on her. The way the cuff hung at her wrist. Soft. Like a Sunday cardigan, if you take my meaning. Not a working one."

"Length?"

"To the hip. About. Buttons down. A long one. The collar — there was a collar. A small one. Folded over."

"Hood?"

"No hood, Mrs Marsh."

"Hair?"

"Couldn't see. She — she had something over her head, I think. A scarf, possibly. Or her hair was up. I thought at the time it was a scarf, but I thought that quickly. I haven't thought about that part since. But there was nothing falling at the back. No — no length of hair."

"Height?"

He squinted at the wall behind her, as if there were a measure on it.

"Five-six, I'd say. Five-seven. Average. She wasn't a small woman. She wasn't tall. She moved — she moved comfortably. As if the path were known to her."

"As if the close were known to her."

"As if the close were known to her, Mrs Marsh, yes."

"Time?"

"Five past eleven. Four past. I looked at my watch when I'd come round into the close because I knew I was late. Four past."

Helen made a single line in her notebook. She did not look up while writing it.

"You assumed," she said, "that it was Rosalind."

"I did, Mrs Marsh."

"Why."

"She has one. The pale blue. Cashmere — you'd know about cashmere, I wouldn't. I've seen it on her at evensong twice this week. It's her — her cardigan, if you take my meaning. The one she puts on when there's a chill in the cathedral. I thought — I thought, the precentor's been to check on Marcus. The precentor's gone home. That was the whole of it. I didn't think about it again until the canon rang me."

"You didn't see her face."

"I did not, Mrs Marsh."

"You only saw her back."

"I only saw her back. From twenty paces, getting further. For perhaps four seconds before she went round the gate end."

"Could it have been someone else's cardigan."

Thomas weighed it. Helen approved of the weighing.

"It could," he said. "It could have been any pale blue cardigan. I'm not — I'm not the man to swear to a cardigan, Mrs Marsh. I'm sorry. I am only the man to say what I saw. What I saw was a pale blue cardigan, soft, powder, hip-length, buttons, collar, no hood, on a woman five-foot-six or seven, walking briskly up the close at four past eleven. I assumed it was the precentor because I was not in a state of mind to think it was anyone else."

"Thomas. That is exactly what I needed."

"I'm sorry I closed the door yesterday morning."

"You said yesterday."

"I keep saying it. I shouldn't have. I am sorry, though."

"Don't be sorry. Be precise. You are being precise. Thank you."

She stood. Thomas stood. He did not come round the desk, which was a small kindness she registered and which she would not have known how to thank him for.

—

She walked out into the close in the slant noon light and saw Julian sitting on the bench by the lych-gate.

He was looking at his phone. He did not, at first, look up. She walked across the grass at a pace that did not signal anything. When she reached him she sat down on the bench at the other end and folded her hands in her lap, as though they had agreed to meet for tea.

"Mum."

"Julian."

He put the phone away.

"You said you came to see Marcus," she said.

"I did."

"At nine in the morning, the day after he died. Without telling me."

"I didn't know he'd died. Mum. I came down on the seven o'clock from Bristol. I bought the ticket on Tuesday. I didn't — I came to talk to him. I had no idea anything had happened. I walked into a closed cathedral and a police van."

She did not look at him while he spoke. She looked at the cathedral. She had found, over forty years, that people told the truth more often when they were not being looked at.

"You knew Marcus."

"Yes."

"How."

There was a long pause.

"He lent me money," Julian said. "Nine years ago. Twelve thousand. When you and I had — when I was —"

"I know when."

"I didn't repay him on the timetable I'd agreed. I haven't, ever. Two weeks ago he wrote and asked for it back with interest. I came down to talk to him. That is everything. I have not been in this place in two years. I came to ask Marcus to give me another twelve months."

She was silent.

"I argued with him," Julian said, "yesterday afternoon. In the close. By the gate. He told me he'd had enough. He said he was sorry. He said he was not, in the end, sorry. I went to the King's Head. I had two pints. I went back to the room I'd booked. I came over this morning." He paused. "I didn't kill him, Mum. I want to say that out loud. I didn't kill him."

She looked at him then.

"Have you told the police any of this."

"Not yet."

"You will. This afternoon. Before they come and ask."

"Yes."

She nodded, once. Then she stood.

"Mum."

She turned.

"I'm sorry," he said. "About all of it."

"Tell the police, Julian. We will talk after that."

—

Edmund was crossing the close when she came back, and he changed his line and met her on the grass.

He looked at her face.

"Helen."

"Edmund. The cardigan was Rosalind's, by Thomas's eye. Pale blue, powder, cashmere by feel, hip-length, four past eleven. From the back, twenty paces, four seconds." She paused. "He was clear. He was also clear that any pale blue cardigan would have looked the same."

Edmund said, slowly, "She owns one. So does half the close."

He said it the way a man closes a door he is not yet ready to open.

She said, "I know."

They walked across the grass together towards the cathedral, and neither of them said any of the four other things they were each, separately, thinking.
