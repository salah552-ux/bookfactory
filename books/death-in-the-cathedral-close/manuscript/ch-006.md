# CHAPTER SIX
## The Policy

"I'd like to make this formal, Canon," DS Claire Marsh said.

Edmund's pen was on the desk. He had been, for the last forty minutes, composing a list of correspondents that needed to be informed of the death within the working day, and he had reached the bishop's secretary, whom he wished he could have skipped. The light through the cloister window was the colour of weak tea. Claire's reflection sat doubled and faintly green in the glazed door of the rosewood bookcase, which was the kind of thing Edmund noticed at the wrong moment.

"Make what formal."

"The arrangement. You as cathedral liaison. Me as SIO."

He set his pen down. He had been making, for the last forty minutes, a list of cathedral correspondents that ought to be informed of the death within the working day, and he had reached the bishop's secretary, whom he wished he could have skipped.

"I am perfectly content," he said, "to be the liaison. I should say that the Dean ought to make the formal nomination. It is not technically my decision."

"The Dean has already made it. She rang my superintendent at half eight."

"Of course she has."

"You'll be the channel. Anything we need from the cathedral comes through you. Anything we give the cathedral goes through you. Helen is, in this construction —"

"In this construction Helen is what."

"I haven't decided yet, Canon."

He looked at her. She looked at him with the steady careful gaze of a woman whose judgement was excellent and whose deference was not. He thought: she has been Helen's, in the past. She is not Helen's now. She is the law's. She would like to be both.

"Helen is a witness," he said. "She heard music in the dark at two a.m. She is also a forensic pathologist of forty years' standing. Those are two facts. They are not the same fact."

"They are not."

"You will use her or you won't."

"I will use her informally," Claire said. "I will not put her in a witness box if I can help it. I cannot make any other promise. Are we agreed."

"We are agreed."

She nodded once. The nod had the quality of a piece of paperwork being set aside.

—

She had brought him a list.

The list was in the careful printout font that Wychford CID used for its preliminary investigations, and it had three columns: persons of interest, relationship to Marcus Vayne, status. Claire put it on his desk and turned it round so that he could read it without leaning.

He read it twice.

"You have not put the Dean on this list," he said.

"Should I."

"It depends on whether the property negotiation has reached you. That is a question, not an accusation."

"It has reached me," Claire said. "I have not yet established its relevance. I am not putting names on this list that the press could obtain by accident. The Dean is on a list elsewhere."

"That is correct procedure."

"Thank you, Canon."

He read the names again. Webb. Father Reeve. Margaret. Thomas. Rose Hartley. The two organ scholars. The tenor who had been making his mother's life difficult. Julian Marsh, with a small careful asterisk and the word *unknown*.

Rosalind was on the list.

She was on it as: *Canon Rosalind Ferris, Precentor — last person known to have spoken with deceased*.

Edmund put his finger on her name and lifted it again.

He said, "She is on the list because she went round."

"She is on the list," Claire said, "because she has told three separate people this morning that she went round at half-past eight. Including you. Including me. Including, I believe, Mrs Marsh. She went round on a small errand, she stayed ten minutes, she went home. That is a perfectly explicable visit. She is on the list because she was the last person known to have seen him, by her own account."

"It is also possible that the eight thirty visit is true."

"It is also possible that the eight thirty visit is true," Claire said, "and that he was alive at eight forty-five. Yes. Canon, I am putting her on the list. I am not yet treating her differently from anyone else on the list."

"All right."

"The list will narrow," she said.

"They always do."

—

When Claire had gone, Edmund stood at the window and looked across the cloister garth.

The yew was in shadow. A blackbird was on the lawn. The cloister was empty of its mid-morning canon-and-clerk traffic because everyone had been told quietly to keep clear of the cathedral until the SIO had finished, and so the close in its grass-and-stone equilibrium looked, briefly, as it had looked in 1374.

Edmund was very fond of the close.

This was not a sentimental fondness. He had come to it at forty-six, an unmarried Anglican with a passable understanding of canon law, and in twenty-two years it had become the architecture of his interior life as surely as it was the architecture of his outer one. The cloister and the cellar and the library and the Chapter House had taken him into the long, slow, layered habit of paying attention, which he believed, in the part of himself he did not articulate, to be the most underrated of the moral virtues.

He was about to perform an act of attention now that he did not want to perform.

He came back to the desk and opened the deep drawer. From the deep drawer he took the buff cardboard folder marked, in his own younger handwriting, *FINANCIAL POLICY 2014 — DRAFT 3 — APPROVED*.

He laid it on his blotter.

He had not opened this folder in ten years, possibly more. The reason he had not opened it was that he had drafted it at a point in his career when he had been, for once, ahead of the cathedral's institutional habits; he had got the Chapter to agree, at his urging, that all cathedral funds — including the smaller and older funds, of which Wychford had perhaps forty — should require two signatories on every disbursement above one hundred pounds. This was perfectly ordinary practice in any modern church; but Wychford had, until 2014, run a number of its smaller funds on the personal authority of the canon responsible. The reform had passed by one vote. Edmund had been pleased.

He had been pleased, and then — six months later — the cathedral had had a difficult Easter, and the administrator had complained about cheque countersigning, and Edmund had quietly, in his function as Chapter clerk's overseer, ceased enforcing the second-signatory clause on the funds where it had been most awkward.

He had told himself it was a small matter. He had told himself the canons in question were beyond reproach. He had told himself, in the quiet and only sometimes self-aware part of himself, that the reform had been the principle and the principle was on record.

He had forgotten about it.

He opened the folder.

—

It took him forty minutes to confirm what he already suspected. He went through the music fund disbursement records for the last three financial years — the cathedral office had emailed them to him within fifteen minutes when he asked, which was itself a small unkindness — and what he saw on the screen and on the printed return forms was very plain and entirely consistent.

The music fund, since 2014, had been disbursed on a single signature.

That signature was Rosalind's.

The countersignature box, on every form, was either blank or initialled by Rosalind herself with the abbreviation *p.p. RFK*, where RFK was the Chapter clerk before Margaret Holt — a man who had retired in 2017 and died in 2022. Margaret had never countersigned the music fund. Margaret had not, evidently, been asked to.

It was not, on its own, evidence of anything.

It was evidence of a procedural lapse.

The procedural lapse was Edmund's.

He sat for a long time with his palm flat on the open folder. The cloister bell rang the three-quarters. He did not move. He was thinking — and this was the worst of it — of the Easter of 2014, and of the small kindness he had thought he was doing the precentor and the treasurer and the dean by waving the second signature through, and of what it must look like, now, on a list where Rosalind's name appeared, that she had been administering a fund without oversight, with the active consent of the cathedral's lawyer, for twelve years.

He thought: I gave her this.

He thought: I do not yet know I gave her anything except the absence of an inconvenience.

He thought: that may turn out to be the same thing.

He closed the folder.

He put it in the centre of his desk. He took his battered leather notebook from his cassock pocket and wrote, in the small careful hand he had used since law school, three lines.

The third line was: *I wonder if Rosalind has known the policy lapsed*.

He underlined the verb *wonder* twice, very lightly, because in his private orthography it was the verb that meant: I already know.

He sat with the notebook open on the closed folder for what felt like a long time.

Then the bell rang ten, and he went down to evensong rehearsal, because he had told Claire he would be the liaison and the liaison did not, on the first morning, fail to attend to the cathedral.

He went down the cloister with the folder under his arm.

He had not yet decided whom he would show it to.
