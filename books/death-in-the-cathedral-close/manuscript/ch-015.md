# CHAPTER FIFTEEN
## The Second Bottle

The pharmacy records came on a Thursday, eleven days into the inquiry, in a folder that Claire slid across Helen's kitchen table with the expression of someone who had already read the contents twice and had been, both times, disappointed.

Helen made tea first. This was, Claire had long ago concluded, non-negotiable — Helen made tea at the opening of every significant conversation, and the act was not hospitality but preparation: it gave her approximately three minutes in which to organise what she was about to think. Claire, who had known Helen for seventeen years, had learned to use the three minutes.

When Helen sat down, Claire opened the folder without preamble.

Wychford Pharmacy. Marcus's prescription record. Digoxin, 0.125mg, monthly dispensing, the same Wychford Pharmacy on the market square that had been dispensing to the cathedral close residents since 1987. His records went back six years — the full period of his relationship with the pharmacy. Regular. Every twenty-eight days, precisely. No gaps, no interruptions, no anomalous refills.

Helen looked at the prescription label on the archive bottle in the evidence photograph: Wychford Pharmacy, *R. Vayne*, dispensed 28 January.

"The archive bottle is consistent with his prescription record," she said.

"Yes," said Claire. "Which was what we expected." She turned over the next page.

This was a second document, and it was shorter, and it was from a different pharmacy entirely.

Avon Private Dispensary, Clifton, Bristol. A private pharmacy — the kind that served private prescriptions, which were written by private physicians and which did not feed into the NHS dispensing records system. Cash payment, which was how private prescriptions were sometimes paid for, and which was also, Helen noted, how you arranged a purchase that would not appear in any record connected to you by name unless someone specifically asked the pharmacy for its sales records from a particular time period.

The dispensed medication: digoxin. Dosage: 0.25mg tablets. Quantity: sixty tablets. Dispensed: the eleventh of March.

Helen looked at the dispensing date. The eleventh of March was three days before Marcus had filed his formal complaint letter to the Bishop. It was also — she worked this backwards — approximately six weeks before the murder.

"The batch number," she said.

Claire turned to the third page. Avon Private Dispensary's batch records, requested under the pharmacy's legal obligation to maintain them for five years. The batch number for the sixty digoxin tablets dispensed on the eleventh of March was —

She looked at it.

It was not XR-0184/22.

It was a different batch entirely.

Helen sat very still. The kettle was still warm on the counter behind her. The window over the sink showed the close wall and a strip of April sky and the top of the lime tree that had been growing in the close for ninety years and which the gardener threatened to take down every autumn and which survived each winter on what appeared to be pure institutional stubbornness.

"Two bottles," Helen said.

"Two bottles," Claire confirmed. "Marcus's own prescription — in the archive corridor, planted. And a second bottle, obtained privately from a Bristol pharmacy, sixty tablets of twice the dose, on the eleventh of March."

Helen thought through the arithmetic. Sixty tablets at 0.25mg. A prescribed therapeutic dose for someone without Marcus's existing blood levels would have been perhaps one tablet daily. In someone who was already carrying 0.125mg in his system daily, administered additionally — an extra 0.25mg dissolved in an evening drink, or several, over the course of the hour or so between the visit and the loss of consciousness — the cumulative blood level would have entered fatal range without producing acute symptoms he would have recognised as poisoning rather than the ordinary unwellness of a man with a heart condition who had been working late.

This was, she thought, not improvised. This required knowing his medication, knowing his dosage, knowing his condition, knowing his schedule, knowing when he would be alone and how long he would stay.

"The prescription," Helen said. "Who wrote it?"

"We're tracing that. The pharmacy says a private physician — they have a name and a GMC number. We've passed it to the Medical Practitioners Tribunal." Claire paused. "It may be fraudulent. The name given may be false." She paused again. "There are ways to obtain a private prescription in a false name from a private practice."

Helen understood this. She had seen it, over thirty-five years. Not common. Not impossible. Requiring, at minimum, a plausible story and a convincing patient presentation.

"Cash," she said.

"Cash. No card record. No name other than the physician's reference."

Helen picked up her tea. It was slightly cooler than she preferred, but she drank it anyway, because the warmth was useful. She thought about someone travelling to a private pharmacy in Clifton, Bristol, on the eleventh of March. Private. Cash. A medication prescribed under a false name or a false physician's reference. Sixty tablets of twice the dose.

The eleventh of March. Three weeks before the choir rehearsal was cancelled. Four weeks before the complaint letter was filed. Six weeks before the murder.

This was not panic. This was not a decision made over the course of one afternoon.

She said: "The person who bought this had decided to use it before Marcus filed the complaint."

Claire looked at her. "Yes."

"Which means," Helen said carefully, "that it was not a response to the complaint. The complaint made it urgent. But the decision was made before the complaint was filed."

Claire was quiet for a moment. Outside, the lime tree top moved in the light easterly that had been running all day. The close was very still otherwise — no bell, no organ, just the wind and the distant sound of a door somewhere in the south range.

She said: "The archive bottle. Someone put it there to be found. To explain the missing digoxin. To suggest Marcus left his own medication in the archive corridor — careless, perhaps, the kind of thing a man with a lot on his mind might do."

"Yes," Helen said.

"But it's not the bottle that killed him."

"No. The archive bottle is the original prescription. The bottle that killed him — or the medication in it — is in Bristol. Obtained privately. Cash." Helen set down her tea. "Access to the archive corridor requires a Song School key."

Claire said: "We're aware of that."

"I note that three people hold Song School keys." Helen paused. "Rosalind Ferris. Marcus Vayne. And Rose Hartley."

Claire looked at her with the expression of someone who had just had a conversation confirmed from two directions at once. She did not say: and at the two o'clock meeting with you and Canon Hale I was told that Marcus Vayne was seen leaving Rosalind Ferris's house at nine-thirty on the night of the murder. She did not need to say it. The two o'clock meeting had happened, and both of them had sat in Edmund's office and looked at the blank forwarding column and had said the things that needed to be said. She was here now with the pharmacy records because those records were the next thing, and because she trusted Helen to make the connection rather than having to describe it to her.

"We need to establish her movements on the eleventh of March," Claire said.

Helen thought: there will be nothing easily traceable. If the person had the nerve to go to Bristol for the medication, they went on a day that could be explained. A free Tuesday. A personal appointment already in the diary. Something that looked like the ordinary movement of a person with a life that extended beyond these walls. "I'll need the music fund supplier invoices from March," she said. "There may be a visit that coincides."

"I'll request them."

They sat for a moment. The folder lay between them on the table. Helen's kitchen was quiet — the grandfather clock in the front room marking the half-hour with its soft decisive double-knock, the kind of sound that had been the background to seventeen years of thinking in this close.

She thought about what it meant that the decision had been made before the complaint. She thought about Rosalind, who had managed the music fund for twelve years on a single signature, who had signed for post and managed choir schedules and conducted anthems with steady hands and brought shortbread to Helen's kitchen and asked, in the cloister, in the April sun, whether it was normal for the same questions to be asked many times.

She thought about what it meant to decide something and then to wait six weeks for the right moment.

Someone had obtained digoxin specifically in order to kill Marcus Vayne. The question was no longer whether. The question was when they had decided to.
