# CHAPTER THIRTY-FOUR
## The Killer's Truth

Claire had arranged it for the Friday afternoon — one conversation, permitted before formal proceedings, in an interview room at the station. She had been precise about the conditions: not a statement, not under caution, nothing that would be admissible. Rosalind's solicitor would be present. Edmund and Helen were there by Rosalind's specific request, not Claire's. "She asked for familiar faces," Claire had told them, on the phone Thursday evening, with something careful in her voice that was not quite reluctance and not quite approval.

The interview room was the one Edmund had sat in with Webb — the same chipped table corner, the same fluorescent strip buzzing at irregular intervals, the same high window facing into the car park. Rosalind was already seated when they came in, in a clean grey dress Edmund did not recognise, her hair up. The pale blue cardigan was not there — presumably with the evidence. She had a glass of water on the table in front of her, untouched.

Her solicitor — a careful young man Edmund had not met before — sat at the corner of the table with a yellow legal pad and the particular stillness of someone who intends to say very little.

Rosalind looked at Edmund when he sat down. She looked at Helen, beside him. She breathed in slowly and then said, without any preamble: "I want to tell you what happened. Not what you've deduced. What happened."

He said: "All right."

Helen said nothing. He had an impression that she was there in the capacity she had occupied for thirty-five years in rooms like this — the professional witness, not there to participate but to hear accurately. He was grateful for it.

"The money," Rosalind said. "That's where it starts. That's where it always starts — not the money itself, but what the money was for." She paused. "You'll have found that by now."

"We have," Edmund said.

"She had been deteriorating for — years, before she stopped knowing who I was." Her voice was entirely even. Edmund had heard Rosalind conduct two funerals, a Good Friday service, and a memorial she had organised within forty-eight hours of learning about Marcus's death, and her voice had the same quality in each: level, genuine, making no extra claim on the listener's sympathy. "The council assessment said residential. The residential home was — you can imagine. She had been a very intelligent woman. She couldn't tell you where she was, but she could tell when a place wasn't kind. I could see it every visit." She lifted the glass of water and put it down without drinking. "Private care is four thousand pounds a month. My salary at the time was thirty-two thousand a year. The numbers don't work."

"They don't," Edmund agreed.

"The music fund had reserves. Every year there were reserves — concerts cancelled, events underspent. The first withdrawal was small. Sixty-eight pounds. I told myself I would replace it. I believed that." She said this without emphasis, not performing self-awareness. It was simply a report. "I didn't replace it. I made another withdrawal the following month. And then it was the shape my life had taken, and I kept its shape. For twelve years."

He had known this. He had known it since Helen had found the Clifton Convalescent Home account. Hearing it said aloud was different in a way he had not expected — the knowing had been a kind of structure, and the saying made it human, and the human version was harder to hold.

"Marcus found it three months before." Her hands were on the table, folded. The gold ring she wore on the right hand — some kind of family piece, he had always assumed — caught the fluorescent light without warmth. "He called me in to his office. He showed me the paper trail. He had been — methodical. Marcus was always methodical. He said he would give me three months to repay quietly, to write the Chapter a private explanation and make it right, and that he would then consider the matter resolved."

"But he changed his mind," Edmund said.

"He had always intended to change his mind." She said this without bitterness, and he believed her. "I see that now. He needed me to have the opportunity, so that when he went to the Chapter he could say he had given me every chance. Which was what he did." She turned to look at the high window, at the strip of car park sky. "He called me back six weeks before — six weeks before April. He said he had spoken to a lawyer. He said the discrepancy was too substantial for a private resolution. He said he was going to raise it at the April Chapter meeting." She came back from the window. "I would have lost — everything. The position. The pension. The close. Thirty years." Her voice had not changed. "I said yes, I understood. I went home."

"The afternoon," Edmund said. "You made the decision that afternoon."

"Yes."

The solicitor's pen moved once on the yellow pad and stopped.

"I had his prescriptions from — an occasion three months before, when he was unwell and asked me to collect them. I still had the spare. I knew what a digoxin overdose would do — I had looked it up, after Marcus showed me the paper trail. I don't know precisely when I looked it up. At some point in those three months I had thought about what might be done, and then I had — not thought about it anymore. The way you put something in a drawer. You know it's in the drawer. You don't open the drawer." She stopped. "On the afternoon he told me, I opened the drawer."

Edmund thought of the music fund policy he had written twelve years ago and stopped enforcing six months later. He thought of the decision he had made fifteen years ago — the minor cover-up, the thing he still understood and still regretted. He thought about the drawer.

"The Clifton pharmacy," he said. "March eleventh."

"I drove to Bristol. I had her identity documents still — I hadn't returned them after the estate was settled. I hadn't thought about why. Perhaps I had always known, at some level, that I might need them." A pause. "Might need something. I don't know."

"And the evening," Helen said. It was the first time she had spoken. Her voice was quiet and precise — the forensic voice, the coroner's-office voice. "You brought the coffee."

"I brought the coffee," Rosalind said. "I had prepared it at home. I told myself, walking across the close, that I could still decide not to. That it was only coffee until I handed it to him." She was silent for a moment. "He was grateful. It was a cold evening. He thanked me."

The fluorescent light buzzed and steadied.

"He thanked me," she said again. Not emphasis. Simply the fact, arriving each time it was said.

Helen said nothing. Edmund said nothing.

"He was unconscious by quarter past ten. I don't believe he was in pain. I would like that to be true, and I believe — the pharmacist I consulted, online, said —" She stopped. "I carried him to the gallery rail. It took longer than I had expected. He was — Marcus was not a small man." She breathed. "I pushed him over. I heard him land. Then I went down the stair."

"The mug," Edmund said.

"I wiped it. I thought I'd done it properly. I know now I hadn't." The ghost of something crossed her face — not irony exactly, but the recognition of irony at a distance. "Thirty years in the cathedral and I still don't know how to be careful enough." She looked at Helen. "You knew. From that morning."

Helen said: "I noted it."

"Yes." Rosalind absorbed this. "And then the archive. I had to put the prescription bottle somewhere it would look as though he'd left it himself. The archive was where he'd have no reason to go, so the absence would be plausible." She looked at Edmund. "I hadn't thought about the CCTV."

"Very few people think about the Song School corridor CCTV."

"No. I imagine not." She looked down at her folded hands. "I want to say — I want to say that I think about him. Every day since April fifteenth. Not as a — not in the way of someone performing remorse. I simply do. He was difficult and he was brilliant and he loved this place. I loved it too. I still do. I am aware of how that sounds."

Edmund said: "Did you think about what Marcus gave his life to?"

The silence that followed was the longest of the conversation.

"Every day since," Rosalind said.

She lifted the glass of water and this time she drank from it. Her hands, around the glass, were steady. They had always been steady. He had remarked on it in his notebook, on the occasion he had brought tea while he was working on the audit, and she had asked him gentle questions about the fund. The same hands that had held the coffee mug on a cold April evening in an organ loft. The same hands that had conducted the memorial anthem without breaking.

He felt something complicated in that room. Not betrayal. He had spent enough of his professional life on betrayal — institution covering individual, individual covering institution — to know its specific weight. This was not that. This was closer to grief, and not quite grief, and possibly there was no exact word in English for the feeling of comprehending completely why a person did a terrible thing and finding that comprehension does not resolve into either forgiveness or condemnation but simply into the knowledge of what happened.

He looked at Helen. She was looking at Rosalind.

"Thank you," Edmund said, because it was true, and because it was what the moment required.

Rosalind nodded, once.

He closed his notebook. He had not remembered opening it.
