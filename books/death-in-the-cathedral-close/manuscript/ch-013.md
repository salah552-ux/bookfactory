# CHAPTER THIRTEEN
## Webb

Claire had decided to conduct the interview in the vestry, which was in Helen's view the wrong room — too resonant, the acoustic of a stone box, so that every silence was amplified to significance in a way that would make everyone uncomfortable and nobody honest — but Claire had not asked Helen's opinion and Helen had not offered it.

She sat in the north transept, on a rush-seated chair that one of the vergers had brought out for her, and waited. The stone floor breathed cold upward even through the soles of good shoes. The stained glass on the north side was mediaeval and rather dark — a martyrdom she had never quite been able to identify, which she had always attributed to her ignorance of iconography and which she now wondered might simply be bad medieval design. The figure at the centre of the composition had an expression of faint reproach that she found, in her present state of mind, entirely appropriate.

The vestry was ten feet away. The door was solid oak, three inches thick, and she could hear nothing through it. She had not been invited in. She was, in Claire's working arrangement, the informal expert witness — which was to say: present, useful, and out of the procedural frame when the frame was needed.

She had been in this position, in various configurations, for thirty-five years. She was used to the geometry of it.

Webb had been called at nine-thirty. He had arrived at nine-twenty-eight, which she noted; punctuality in the presence of authority was either the habit of the innocent or the strategy of the guilty, and she had long since given up using it as a diagnostic. He had arrived with Anne — they had come in together, close but not touching, Anne's hand near his arm but not on it, the specific posture of a couple who had been having a difficult conversation and had agreed to pause it for external company. Helen had seen this before too. She read it as: he has told her something. Not everything. Something.

They were separated in the vestry. Anne waited in Edmund's office. Edmund was — she did not know. He had sent her a note that morning, on the small square notepaper he used for internal cathedral correspondence — *Could you be available this afternoon? Something to show you. — E.H.* — and she had sent back: *Yes. Also something to tell you. — H.M.* She had put it in his letterbox at eight o'clock. She considered this a significant piece of communication for two people who had not yet said aloud what they both already knew.

The vestry door opened at eleven forty-three.

Webb came out first, walking quickly, the colour in his face doing something complicated. Claire came out second, making the expression of a woman who had learned something she already knew but which now had additional texture.

Helen stood.

Claire said, quietly, when Webb was out of earshot: "He admits he was in the close that night. From about nine to eleven. He says he was walking. He cannot tell me exactly where." She paused. "He says he didn't go near the organ loft. He says he doesn't know anything about Marcus's medication. He says—" She stopped. "He's lying about something. I can't yet tell you what."

Helen said: "I note that a person can be lying about something that is not murder."

Claire looked at her. "Yes." She did not say: and I note that you know something you aren't telling me yet. She was too professional for that. But the understanding sat between them.

"His alibi?" Helen asked.

"Inconsistent. He says he was walking. His wife says he came home at eleven. He says eleven-fifteen, maybe eleven-thirty." Claire made a small movement. "The inconsistency is ten to thirty minutes. In a murder investigation, that is not nothing."

Helen looked at the martyrdom window. The reproachful central figure. "No," she said. "It is not nothing."

She found Rosalind in the cloister at half-past twelve, which was where Rosalind could often be found at midday when the weather permitted — she had a particular stone bench on the east walk, sheltered from the wind, in the sun from about eleven until two, and she sat there when she needed to think. Helen knew this because she had sat there herself, before the investigation had rearranged her sense of where she could be without difficulty.

Rosalind was holding a clipboard. She looked up when Helen appeared, and the warmth that moved across her face was immediate and — Helen could not escape the word — genuine.

"Helen." She shifted along the bench. "Come and sit. You look chilled through."

Helen sat. The stone was cold through her coat, but the sun on the cloister garth was real enough — low and April-bright, catching the new grass and the daffodils along the east walk that someone had planted and no one had removed.

"I've been putting together a proposal," Rosalind said, holding up the clipboard, "for how we manage the music provision through the rest of the summer term. The choir is going to need to understand the situation clearly — what we can do, what we can't, who covers what. I thought if I drafted something for the Chapter it would at least give people something practical to focus on." She paused, her pen hovering. "Does that seem—? I don't want to be seen to be rushing past the grief of it. But I also don't think grief is served by an institution that loses its bearings entirely."

Helen looked at the clipboard. A neat grid: dates, services, personnel, alternatives. Rosalind's handwriting — the same forward-leaning hand she had last seen in the correspondence ledger, though she had not seen the ledger, only heard of it from Edmund's note. The same small upward flick on double letters.

"I think it seems very sensible," Helen said.

"I've also offered to speak with the choir personally. Some of them are quite young. The choristers especially." Rosalind looked at the garth. A pigeon was working its way across the south walk, unhurried, ignoring everything. "Marcus could be— he was not always easy with the children. But he was their teacher. They will grieve for him in the way children grieve — obliquely, and then all at once."

"You were fond of him," Helen said.

Rosalind was quiet for a moment. "Yes," she said, at last. "I think I was. We were very different people." She set the clipboard in her lap. "He was not easy to be fond of. He was not easy to work with. But he cared about the music in a way that was — absolute. You can respect that in a person even when they make everything around them quite difficult." She paused. "I think I will find it hard to remember only the difficulties. I think I would rather remember the music."

Helen said: "Yes."

The pigeon reached the west walk and turned, with great dignity, north.

"There is a question I want to ask you," Rosalind said, turning to look at Helen with an expression of — what? Not anxiety, exactly. Openness. The expression of someone who has arranged themselves into helpfulness and genuinely means it. "The detective sergeant — Claire — has asked me again about the evening. I told her everything I could. But I wanted to ask you, because you understand these things better than I do — is it normal, at this stage, for the same questions to be asked many times?"

Helen noted the question underneath the question: what do they know? What has my account done?

She said: "It's procedure. The same questions, different contexts. Sometimes the answers shift slightly." She paused. "It's nothing to be concerned about."

"Good," said Rosalind. "I want to be helpful. More than anything else, I want — I think what I want is for this to be over. For whoever did this to Marcus to be named. And then—" She stopped. "Do you think it will damage the close? The long term?"

Helen thought: you are the damage. "Institutions survive the exposure of their failures more often than they survive the concealment," she said. "In my experience."

Rosalind considered this, turning her pen end over end in a slow, regular rotation. "Yes," she said. "I expect that's true."

She offered lunch. Helen declined, citing work. She walked back through the cloister, out of the east walk, across the garth, into the Chapter House and up the stairs to Edmund's office.

He was at the cherrywood table, the ledger open in front of him. He looked up when she came in, and there was something in his face — a controlled relief, as though a question he had been holding for several days had just been given the beginning of an answer.

She sat down.

"Julian told me," she said. "He saw Marcus at nine-thirty. Leaving Rosalind's house. Walking toward the north transept."

Edmund said nothing for a moment. His hand rested on the open ledger. "And Rosalind," he said, carefully, "told you she left at half past eight."

"Yes."

He looked at the ledger. She looked at the ledger. She could see, even upside down, the blank column and the date and the neat, forward-leaning signature.

He said: "I have a meeting with Claire at two o'clock."

"I know," said Helen. "Tell her."

"Both of us," said Edmund. "We tell her together."

Helen looked at the martyrdom scene through the window — the wrong window, she realised; it wasn't martyrdom at all. She had been looking at it for four days in the wrong light. It was an Annunciation.

"'Webb is our most likely suspect,'" she said, quoting Claire's assessment from earlier.

Edmund said, very quietly: "Probably."

"Probably," said Helen. And said nothing else.
