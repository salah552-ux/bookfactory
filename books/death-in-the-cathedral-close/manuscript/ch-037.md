# CHAPTER THIRTY-SEVEN
## Julian

He came back on the first weekend of May, which surprised me.

He had gone back to Bristol the week after the arrest — not dramatically, not with any particular finality, simply with the ordinary logistics of a man who had been away from his flat and his work for longer than he had planned. He had knocked on the door of No. 7 that morning and said he was going. I had said all right. He had stood in the hall for perhaps four seconds longer than necessary, and then he had gone. I had stood in the kitchen and listened to the gate.

That was all we had managed, then. Two years of estrangement and six weeks of proximity and we had managed that much: he had knocked, I had said all right, there was a pause neither of us knew how to fill.

He came back on the Friday evening of the first May weekend, because, he said, he had some things to pick up from the King's Head — he had left a jacket. This was transparently an excuse, which was also progress of a kind. He had the jacket under his arm when he came to the door of No. 7, which meant he had been to the King's Head already, which meant the jacket was not why he was here.

"Tea," I said. It was not a question.

"If you're making it anyway."

I was not making it anyway. I made it.

We sat in the kitchen — he took the chair across the table, not the one beside it, not the one that would have been too close. Julian had always known, without being taught, where the correct distance was between himself and a difficult conversation. He had learned this from me. I note this without pride.

He said: "Are you all right?"

I said: "Yes."

He looked at me the way he looks when he is deciding whether to accept what I've said or to ask something more specific. He is thirty-five years old and he has had forty years of experience with me, if you include the period before he was old enough to form intentions about me, and he knows the difference between "yes" that means yes and "yes" that means something more complicated. He has always known this. It is one of the things we have not talked about.

"She was —" He stopped. He had been in the close for six weeks, on and off, long enough to have tea with Rosalind once at a neighbour's gathering, long enough to have heard her conduct Evensong from the nave. "She was not what I expected."

"No," I said. "She never is."

He turned his mug around on the table, a habit he has had since he was eight years old and I could never convince him it didn't scratch the wood. "Why did you stay?" he said. "After Dad died. Here. Why did you stay in pathology?"

This was not about Rosalind. I understood that. This was the question that had been waiting for two years, or twenty, the question that had been on the table between us every time we sat across from each other, which in the last two years had been never, and in the years before that had been not enough and too far apart.

"Because it was what I was good at," I said. "And because stopping felt —" I found the word. "It felt like agreeing with something I wasn't ready to agree with."

"With what?"

"That the work was finished," I said. "That there was nothing left worth being exact about."

He was silent for a moment. The clock on the sideboard marked the half. The kitchen smelled of the basil I'd planted on the windowsill in March and never quite managed to keep at the right temperature, and of tea, and of the close outside the window in the particular way the close smelled in early May: cooling stone, the last of the lime blossom from the tree in the cloister garth.

"I didn't think you were doing it for Dad," Julian said. "I thought you were doing it instead of him."

I note that this was, in its way, accurate. I also note that I had not said it to myself in those words, and that the words, said aloud by someone else, produced a sensation I had no professional term for.

"That may also be true," I said.

He turned his mug around again. He has a small scar on the back of his right hand, from a bicycle accident when he was eleven. He had been braver about it than I had. I had cried in the A&E while the nurse cleaned it, which was not characteristic, and he had told me he was fine in the careful way children use when they are trying to manage the adults around them. I had not forgotten this. I had not known, until now, that he had remembered it.

"You're good at this," he said. "The — what you did here. The thing with Marcus."

I put down my mug. "I know," I said, because denying it would have been false modesty, and because the correct response to Julian when he says a true thing is to receive it without dismantling it.

He looked at me. He was about to say something and then he looked away, at the window, at the lime tree just visible over the close wall. He said: "I'm going to be in Bristol until — I don't know. Work is busy."

"Yes," I said.

"But I could — if you rang. I don't always pick up." He stopped. "I mean, I would pick up. If it was you."

I note that he is thirty-five years old and has his father's eyes and his grandmother's stubbornness, and that this is not forgiveness, and it is not resolution, and the estrangement is not over, and it is also not what it was two years ago. There is a door that has not been opened. There is a door that is no longer locked.

"Yes," I said. "All right."

He drank his tea. I drank mine. The clock marked the three-quarter. Outside, the close was going about its evening business — a door opening and closing at No. 8, footsteps on the stone path, the distant sound of the television in what I thought was probably the deanery. Small, continuing sounds of a community that had absorbed something large and was still here.

When he left, he said: "Take care of yourself, Mum."

I said: "I always do."

He gave me a look that suggested he was revising his assessment of my self-knowledge. This was also, I thought, accurate.

I watched his car leave the close gate. Then I turned back to the cathedral. It was standing there, as it always was, taking the early May evening light on its limestone and returning it in the particular colour that limestone returns at this hour: a warm grey, a colour that had no name I'd found for it. The gate was closed. The close was quiet.

I went back inside. There was still half a pot of tea.
