# CHAPTER ONE
## The Organ at Two in the Morning

The first wrong note woke her.

Helen lay on her back, listening, before she had decided to be awake. The bedroom was the particular dark of the close at this hour — moonless, without streetlamps, only the faint silver of the cathedral's west front bleeding through the curtain gap. Her clock said two minutes past two.

The note came again. Then a fragment — three bars, the opening of something she half-recognised, and then the music broke off mid-phrase, as if the player had forgotten what came next or had been interrupted by a thought.

It started up again. Different piece. Bach, possibly. She was not musical, but thirty-five years of weddings and funerals had given her a serviceable ear.

The Bach broke off too.

Helen sat up and put her bare feet on the floorboards. Cold. April was not yet warm in the close, even with the daffodils up. She pulled on her dressing gown — the navy one, the one she had owned since David was alive, the wool worn soft at the cuffs — and crossed to the window.

The cathedral rose against the sky like a darker shape of dark. She could see the great west front and the south transept and, just visible, the slit windows of the organ loft above the rood screen.

The slit windows were unlit.

She stood for a long moment, holding the curtain, looking. The music began again — Mendelssohn, this time, she thought, though she would have struggled to swear to it in court. Wedding March. A few bars. Then silence again.

She noted, in the precise, flat way she had learned to note things during postmortems: the music was being played from the loft. The light in the loft was off. Someone was practising the organ in the dark.

There were innocent explanations. Marcus Vayne had been the organist at Wychford Cathedral for twenty-five years. He kept difficult hours. He had been heard before at strange times — Easter rehearsals, recital preparation, the occasional crisis with the pedals that needed addressing before someone else used the instrument. The close was used to him.

But Marcus did not play in the dark. Marcus played with the gallery lamp on, with his music open, with his half-rim spectacles low on his nose. Helen had walked past the cathedral at midnight in February and seen the warm yellow square of the loft light and, distantly, the white of his shirt cuff moving against the manual.

She watched the slit windows. They stayed black.

The Mendelssohn started a fourth time. This time it ran longer — eight or ten bars — and then it did not stop so much as falter. A wrong note. A pause. And then nothing.

The silence that followed was the silence of a building that knew it was being listened to.

Helen drew the curtain back across the window and went downstairs.

She did not turn on the hall light. The close at night had its own quiet, and she had lived in No. 7 long enough to walk it without falling. The flagstones in the hall were cold through her slippers. The grandfather clock in the front room — David's, inherited from his father, which she had never moved — ticked twice while she stood at the front door listening. Then twice more.

She opened the door an inch.

The smell of the close at this hour: cold stone, the last of the night's wood-smoke from somewhere in the row, the faint sweetness of the Daphne by the gate that had been flowering since February. Familiar. Reassuring. The smell of home, if she was honest with herself, in a way her old house in Bristol had stopped being years ago.

She stepped out onto the path in her dressing gown and slippers and stood.

The cathedral was perhaps forty yards from her front door, across the lawn that the close residents shared and that the gardener treated with terrifying respect. The west front was lit, faintly, by the security lamps on the perimeter wall. The body of the building above that was a deeper black against the sky.

The north transept door — the door at the foot of the stone stair that led up to the organ loft — was an inset oak door, studded, ten feet tall, and famously stiff. Marcus had complained about the hinge for years. It was always closed. It was the sort of door that, when it stood open, made you doubt your eyes.

It was open.

Not wide. Open the width of a hand, perhaps. Black inside the black of the wall, but distinguishable, if you were standing where Helen was standing, looking at it.

She felt the cold on her ankles and made herself look properly.

The door was open. The organ loft light was off. The music had stopped.

Three things.

She catalogued them and then asked herself the question she had been trained for forty years to ask: what is the simplest explanation that accounts for all of these.

Marcus had come over late, as he sometimes did. He had let himself in at the north transept door — he had a key — and he had gone up to the loft. He had played in the dark because he was trying to memorise. He had left the door ajar behind him, because the door was stiff and pulling it fully closed required a deliberate effort and he had been preoccupied.

That accounted for the door and the dark and the music.

It did not account for the silence that had now lasted four minutes.

Or for the fact that Marcus had played four pieces in the space of eight minutes, none of them for longer than ten bars, and each of them broken off mid-phrase as though he had been listening for something else.

She stood in her slippers at the edge of her path and looked at the dark slit of the open door, and she thought, with great clarity: Helen, you are seventy years old, and you are not going to walk across the lawn in your dressing gown in the middle of the night to investigate something that is almost certainly nothing.

She thought: ring Edmund.

She thought: it is two in the morning, and Edmund is asleep, and what would you tell him? That an organist played the organ?

The thought that came after that, she did not finish.

She stepped back inside her own door and closed it. She turned the deadbolt. Then she stood with her hand on the chain and did not put the chain across, because she could not have explained, even to herself, why she would put a chain across the door of her house in the close.

She went upstairs.

She did not sleep again. She lay on her back in the cold bedroom and looked at the ceiling and listened. The cathedral did not produce any further music. The bell marked half past two, and three, and half past three, and four. Somewhere between four and five she became aware that the wrongness she had felt at the window had not gone away. It had simply settled. It was now the texture of the room.

At a little after five, she got up and made tea. She drank it standing at the kitchen window, looking at the cathedral going slowly grey in the first of the light.

The north transept door, she could not see from the kitchen.

She took her tea back to bed and pretended to read, and at six-fifty-eight the telephone in the hall began to ring.

She knew, before she put her feet on the floor, that it was Edmund.

She knew, before she lifted the receiver, what he was going to say.

The organ loft light had been off. The music had been playing in the dark.
