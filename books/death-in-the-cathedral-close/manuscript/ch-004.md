# CHAPTER FOUR
## Medication

Marcus's office occupied the small wood-panelled room on the first floor of the Song School. It had the long horizontal window that faced into the cloister and the green gloom of the yew, and it had the particular cold of a south-facing room that had been heated only when the man inside it remembered to turn the radiator on, which was not often.

Edmund stood in the doorway for a moment before going in. He had been into this room perhaps a dozen times in twenty-two years — Marcus had been a man who came to your office, not the other way round — and the room itself had the quality of a place that had not expected a witness.

The desk had a music stand at one end and a typewriter — yes, a typewriter — at the other. Marcus had loathed email and had managed, through sheer obstinacy, to retain the right to a typewritten memorandum at Chapter meetings. The typewriter was a black 1962 Olivetti. The keys still smelt faintly of oil.

Edmund went in.

DS Claire Marsh came after him. She had asked him, with great courtesy and only slight hesitation, whether he would mind walking her through the room before her own people came up; she wanted, she said, the sense of what was usually here from someone who had known Marcus. Edmund had said yes, of course. He had not added that he had not, in fact, known Marcus very well — Marcus had not let himself be known very well — but that he had known the room.

"Talk me through it," Claire said. "What's normal."

Edmund stood in the middle of the floor and looked.

"The desk lamp is on," he said. "That is unusual. He turned things off when he left. He was an obsessive — he disliked waste and disliked the sound of fluorescent lights."

He moved to the desk.

"The typewriter has paper in it. He left paper in only when he was mid-letter. The current sheet is — " he leaned to read it without touching, " — a draft to the music suppliers about a tenor passage in the *Salve Regina*. It is half finished. He went out, intending to come back."

"What time would he have stopped working at the typewriter, normally?"

"Seven," Edmund said. "Half past. Something like that. He would do paperwork up here in the late afternoon and then go down to the loft to play. Ate at the loft sometimes — there's a sort of shelf, by the console."

He passed the music stand. He did not touch it.

"His diary is here." A leather pocket diary, on the corner of the desk, where it always was. "Open at last week. Nothing for last night except — nothing for last night. Just 'practice'. He wrote that on every blank evening."

Claire wrote a line in her own notebook.

Edmund came round to the row of medication bottles.

There were three small brown plastic pharmacy bottles standing in a tidy row beside the desk lamp. They were lined up by height, descending. This was so characteristic of Marcus that Edmund felt a small clean stab in the chest, the kind that comes when one notices, after the death, the irreplaceable smallness of a habit.

Beta-blockers.

Statins.

And then a third, on the right.

"Vitamins," Edmund read. "He took these. Dean's wife sent him to the GP about his colour, three years ago. He resented it." He paused. "There should be one more."

Claire said, "Should there?"

Edmund frowned. He bent slightly, did not touch.

"He had a heart condition. He took digoxin. Daily. He kept it here — he kept all of them here. Three or four bottles, in this row. Tidy." He gestured at the gap. "His digoxin seems to be missing."

Claire wrote a longer line, this time.

"He could have taken it home," she said.

"He could," Edmund said. "He didn't, normally. But he could have."

She looked at the row again. Then she looked at the desk lamp, on. Then at the typewriter, mid-letter. Then back at the row.

"Anything else, Canon?"

Edmund moved his eyes carefully along the desk, the bookshelves, the small armchair by the window with a folded cardigan over the back, the wastepaper basket — empty — and the row of music files on top of the bookcase.

"Nothing," he said, and then, "no, nothing — yes, this is as I would expect, except for the bottle."

Claire stood still for several seconds.

"All right," she said. "Thank you, Canon. We'll seal it. I'll have a CSI here within the hour."

—

Edmund went back down the wood stair into the cloister and stood for a moment looking at the green of the yew. The cloister was fully cold now, and entirely empty. He thought about the row of medication bottles and about the gap on the right, where a man like Marcus would have set the missing one without thinking; and about the typewriter, mid-letter, and the desk lamp on, and the practice diary that said only the one word.

He thought: he meant to come back. He went down to the loft and he meant to come back.

He thought: who has been in this office.

He registered the thought as a thing to write down in due course.

He went down the cloister.

—

In the south transept, when he came back through, the close had largely been moved on by Claire's officers. The body remained, behind a screen now, awaiting the SIO. Helen stood at the screen, talking to Claire. Rosalind had been taken to the canon's stall on the other side of the chancel and was there with Father Reeve. Margaret had been taken home.

Edmund went to Rosalind first.

"You don't need to stay here, Rosalind," he said.

"I do, though." She turned her face up to him. Her eyes were red, which was a thing Edmund had never previously observed in her in eleven years of close colleagueship. "I do. Until somebody who is not me will run the music."

"There's no music to run today, Rosalind."

She made a sound that was almost a laugh, and entirely without warmth, and entirely without bitterness.

"Edmund. There is the eight o'clock tomorrow. There is the evensong tonight. There is the pattern of services that Marcus has run for twenty-five years and that does not pause because — that does not pause." Her hand went out and touched the edge of the stall. "Let me sit, and look useful, and when you can spare somebody to send me away with something to do, send me away with something to do. Please."

He looked at her. He thought: she is asking, in her way, to be employed by the catastrophe. He thought: I have known this woman eleven years and I have never seen her ask for anything.

He said, "All right, Rosalind. The music schedule for the next fortnight. Could you put something on paper. Something simple. Substitute organist if Roger can come over from St Mary's. Cancellations for choir services where we cannot manage. Bring it to my office at eleven."

"Yes." She pressed the back of her hand briefly to her mouth and lowered it. "Thank you, Edmund. Yes."

He turned to go, and as he turned she said, "Edmund."

"Yes."

"Was it — was it quick."

He looked at her. He looked at her properly. He saw the tear ducts and the faint flush along the collarbone and the way her hands were knotted in her lap, and he thought: this is grief. He thought: I should not catalogue this. He thought: but I am cataloguing it anyway, because Helen has just told me he did not fall, and now I am cataloguing everyone.

He hated himself, briefly, for the cataloguing.

He said, "Yes. It will have been quick."

"Good," she whispered. "Good. Good."

—

Helen was crossing the south aisle to him when the south door opened and a man came in whom Edmund did not know, but whom Helen, by the fractional change of her face, plainly did.

He was a man of about thirty-five, wearing a dark jacket with the collar up, carrying nothing. He stopped at the back of the nave when he saw the screen and the figures in high-vis and the police tape. Then he saw Helen, and his face did a strange small thing — not surprise, exactly. Something more particular. Something closer to bracing.

Helen stopped where she was.

She said, very quietly, only to Edmund, "That is my son."

Edmund waited.

"He has not been in the close in two years," she said. "He did not tell me he was coming."

Then, after a moment: "Edmund. He knew Marcus."

The young man came up the aisle towards them, slowly, with the wary forward motion of a person walking into something he had not known he was walking into. When he reached his mother he did not touch her. He looked at the screen and at Edmund and at his mother in turn.

He said, "Mum. What's happened."

Helen said, "Why are you here, Julian."

He said, "I came to see Marcus."
