# CHAPTER THIRTY-SIX
## Morning Prayer

The first of May, and Morning Prayer at seven.

He was in his stall at six-fifty-five, which was earlier than necessary and had been, for twenty-two years, approximately when he arrived. The cathedral in the few minutes before Morning Prayer had a quality he had never successfully described — a held quality, as if the building were drawing a breath before it was asked to hold people's small urgencies again. The stone was cold at the back of the stall; his coat was on the peg to his left; the candles on the altar had been lit by someone who had come in before him. He did not know who lit the early candles. He had been here twenty-two years and had never once asked.

Father Reeve led the responses this morning.

He was not Rosalind. Edmund did not require him to be Rosalind. What Simon Reeve was, at seven in the morning in an old wooden stall, was present and earnest and young enough to feel the weight of the office more visibly than his predecessor had. Rosalind had worn thirty years of these services lightly, the way expertise becomes ease when it has been long enough practised. Reeve wore his with care, which was the correct thing at this stage in a career, and there was something in the care that Edmund found more consoling than the expertise would have been. The expertise had been, it turned out, a form of concealment.

Three in the congregation this morning. Margaret in her usual place, third row on the north side. Thomas Oakes in the back, which was where Thomas always stood or sat — the post he had occupied for forty-three years, the verger's position along the south transept wall long since become simply his. Canon Webb, on the far side, not looking at anything in particular, his large hands resting on his knees in the posture of a man who is not comfortable and is not trying to perform comfort.

Edmund was also not comfortable. He found this appropriate.

He had been turning over, in the two weeks since the arrest, a question that he had not put into his notebook because he had not yet found the formulation. The question was whether he had always known, and if he had always known, what exactly it was that had prevented him from knowing until the evidence made it unavoidable. He was not given to excessive self-examination as a rule — the ecclesiastical lawyer's habit was to examine the facts and let the conclusions follow. But this was a case in which the facts had been, for a significant portion of the investigation, in his possession, and he had looked at them and looked away.

The morning, he thought, walking in. The first morning, when the close was still gathering itself and no one yet knew what word they would use for what had happened. The music fund, which he had audited and found short and filed under *I wonder if* without being willing to name what came after *if*. The tea she had brought while he worked. The questions she had asked about the investigation's progress, delivered with such warm concern that he had answered them honestly, which was to say he had told the killer, on three separate occasions, exactly how close they were.

*Stultissimus,* he thought. *Fools' errands.* He immediately translated this, as was his habit, and found he had no adequate English equivalent.

He was not asking for absolution from himself, or anything like it. He was simply noting that the decision to look away, when looking would have been too costly, is a kind of decision — the most common kind, perhaps, in institutions, where looking away is often called patience, or prudence, or awaiting further evidence. He had confused looking away with each of these, in turn, across twelve years. He did not think he would make that particular confusion again.

This was, he supposed, what the two weeks had settled in him. Not resolution in the sense of relief, but resolution in the sense of clarity. *Resolutio* — the loosening of what was bound, the releasing of what had been held under tension. He had held, for twenty-two years, a form of institutional loyalty that contained, as a necessary component, the willingness not to see certain things too clearly. He had thought of this as discretion. He understood it now as a habit of avoidance with a polite name.

He had chosen to see, when the evidence made seeing unavoidable. He had done what the situation required. The institution had survived it — the cathedral was here, the services continued, the choir was imperfect and genuine and Morning Prayer was at seven as it had been at seven for three hundred and forty years, through plague and commonwealth and two world wars and the slow institutional crises that never make the history books but are in some ways the more accurate record of what an institution actually is.

He had not expected the institution to survive it. He had thought, in the darkest weeks of the investigation — when the evidence had assembled itself into the shape of someone he had worked beside for twenty years and trusted with the close's internal knowledge — that what he was looking at was a crack through which the whole structure might fall. Institutions felt, when you loved them, as though they depended on specific people, specific arrangements, the loyalty of particular persons in particular roles. They do not. They depend on the thing they were built for, and when the specific arrangements fail, other arrangements take their place, and the services continue at seven.

He had not expected to find this consoling. He found it consoling.

Reeve finished the responses. The collect for the day, May the first: *O God, who hast taught us that all our doings without charity are nothing worth; send thy Holy Ghost, and pour into our hearts that most excellent gift of charity, the very bond of peace and of all virtues, without which whosoever liveth is counted dead before thee: grant this for thine only Son Jesus Christ's sake, our Lord.* The language was four centuries old and had been asked in this room, or near it, since the building's first stone was placed. Edmund said Amen.

He sat for a moment in the quiet after the service had ended and before people began to move.

The light from the east window came down the nave in long coloured bars — blue, amber, a thin stripe of red — and fell across the choir stalls and across his hands on the prayer book and across the stone floor that seven centuries of feet had not noticeably worn. He had been sitting in this stall for twenty-two years. He thought of the first morning he had sat here, aged forty-six, with the newness of the close still on him and the cathedral feeling, as new things do, slightly too large and slightly too quiet. He thought of what he understood now that he had not understood then.

He opened his notebook.

He wrote: *May 1. Morning Prayer. Reeve, collect. Three in the congregation.* He thought for a moment. He added: *I chose truth. The institution survived it. I had not expected that.*

He underlined the last sentence once.

Then he closed the notebook and went to the vestry to speak to Thomas about the May schedules, because there was always something to be seen to, and the something continued to be true whether or not you were a man who had recently discovered what it felt like to be exact about things he had spent twenty-two years approximating.

The coloured light was still there when he left. It always was.
