# CHAPTER TWENTY-SIX
## The Arrest

They came for Webb at seven forty in the morning.

Edmund did not witness the arrival — he heard it from his kitchen, a car on the cobbles at an hour when the only people moving in the close were Thomas beginning his day-rounds and whoever was setting up Morning Prayer. The particular sound of a car engine that has been turned off deliberately rather than running its way down told him something. He looked at his kitchen clock. He pulled on his coat.

By the time he reached the gate at No. 3, there were two uniformed officers at Webb's front door and DS Claire Marsh on the step. Anne had opened the door. She was in a dressing gown, which she held closed at the neck with both hands — the specific stance of a person who has had no time to prepare. Edmund could see her face and it was not the face of a person who was entirely surprised.

Webb appeared behind her. He was dressed — fully dressed, as if he had known or guessed or simply not slept. He stood in the doorway of his own house looking at DS Claire Marsh and he looked, Edmund thought, like a man who had been running a very long time and had now simply stopped.

Claire said something. Webb nodded. Anne reached for his arm — an involuntary reach, immediately checked, so that her hand stopped in the air between them. Webb turned to her and said something very brief, something close enough that Edmund could not hear it, and then he went.

Edmund stood in the close and watched the car leave.

---

The police station in Wychford was a red-brick building from the 1970s that sat at the corner of Minster Lane and the inner ring road, generating the particular institutional sadness of architecture designed without reference to the architecture around it. Edmund had been here six times in the course of the inquiry. He knew the desk sergeant's first name and the location of the waiting room chairs that did not have a broken rear leg.

Claire met him in the corridor outside the duty room. She looked as she always looked — no more tired than usual, no more pressured — but Edmund had come to read her face with a certain precision, and what he read this morning was the controlled expression of a person managing two different things simultaneously.

"He asked for you," she said. "He hasn't asked for a solicitor yet. I've told him he should." A pause. "He specifically asked for you, and then he asked for Helen."

"I'll ring Helen."

"She's already here. She drove herself."

Edmund found Helen in the waiting room, sitting very straight with her coat still on and her hands in her lap. She had her pearls on. She always had her pearls on, but there was something about them this morning that read to Edmund as armour rather than habit.

They were given ten minutes in the interview room, together, with Webb. The formal caution had been administered. He had not been charged.

The room was small, with a table that had a chip out of one corner and a plastic chair on either side and a fluorescent light that buzzed at irregular intervals. The window was high and faced nothing. Webb was sitting with his hands flat on the table in a way that suggested he had been told to keep them visible and had complied with the instruction more thoroughly than required.

He looked at Edmund when they came in. Then at Helen.

"I didn't," he said. It was not a plea and it was not a denial in any formal sense. It was a statement addressed to two specific people whose opinion of him, Edmund understood, was the thing that mattered most in this moment.

"I know," Edmund said. He sat down. It was not, strictly speaking, something he should have said. He said it because it was true and because the situation required it.

Helen sat beside Edmund. She looked at Webb without any expression that could be described as warm or cold — she looked at him with the focused attention she brought to things she was assessing. She had seen fear many times, she had told Edmund once — genuine fear, performed fear, the specific fear of a guilty person watching the evidence assemble against them, the specific fear of an innocent person who does not understand how the world has arrived at this point. She had said: *Frightened people don't look like that unless they're innocent.*

Webb's hands were flat on the table and they were trembling finely. His face was the face of a man who had been stripped of something that he had never thought about precisely because he had never needed to, and who now understood what it was: the assumption that the ordinary mechanisms of daily life would continue to function correctly.

"The affair," Edmund said quietly. "Sarah Clifton's statement is on record. Your alibi is confirmed from nine until approximately ten forty-five."

"Then why—"

"There are other elements of the evidence that are still being assessed." Edmund chose each word with care. "The timeline has a gap between ten forty-five and the established time of Marcus's death. You were in the close. There are other witnesses."

"I was walking," Webb said. He said it with the flat simplicity of someone who has said it several times and has run out of a better version. "I was just walking. I do that — I've always done that. When the evening is bad, I walk." His voice had changed slightly on the word "bad" — the particular modulation of a man who will not, in front of these two specific people, name what kind of bad it had been.

"I know," Edmund said again.

"There are things Claire's team are waiting for," Helen said. She did not look at Edmund. "Results that are due shortly. Within hours. Not days."

Webb looked at her. Something shifted in his face — not hope, exactly; more like the recalibration of a man who has adjusted to a disaster and is now being told the disaster may not be the final fact.

"Within hours," he said.

"Yes," Helen said.

They left him there. In the corridor, Claire was speaking to one of her constables and did not acknowledge them as they passed, which was itself an acknowledgement.

Outside, the light was thin and cold, the kind of April morning that had forgotten spring was supposed to be happening. Edmund stood on the pavement of Minster Lane and felt the ring road traffic two blocks away in the air around him.

He thought: noon.

He thought: forty-eight hours before the charging decision.

He thought about Webb's hands on the table.

Edmund drove back to the close alone. The enhancement, Patel had told him, would be ready by noon. It was nine forty-three.
