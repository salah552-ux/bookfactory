# CHAPTER THIRTY-EIGHT
## The Case File

DS Claire Marsh came to his office on the Tuesday of the second week of May, which was the first Chapter meeting she had been able to attend in something other than a professional investigative capacity. She arrived ten minutes before the meeting was due to begin, knocked precisely twice, came in when he said come, and sat in the chair across the desk that was not the chair for comfort — that chair was Helen's, or had become Helen's across the course of the investigation, and Claire had never sat in it. She sat in the upright one, the one with no arms, the one he used when he needed to be precise.

"The case file," she said, and placed it on the edge of his desk.

He looked at it. It was a green lever-arch file with his name on the cover tab in Claire's own handwriting, which was very small and very legible. There were perhaps two hundred pages inside, and he had contributed approximately forty of those pages himself, in the form of document copies, official statements, and two typescript summaries that he had written in the third person, as he preferred, because the third person maintains a useful and professional distance from the events it describes.

"She's entered a guilty plea," Claire said.

"Yes," Edmund said. "I had heard."

"Her solicitor came in yesterday. I thought you should know formally." She paused. "The charge stands as put. Murder. The embezzlement runs concurrent — separate proceedings."

He nodded. He had expected this. The guilty plea was, in some ways, another form of Rosalind being honest — having decided to tell the truth, she had continued in that direction. He could not quite decide whether this made him feel better or worse about things, and had tentatively concluded that the question was not well-formed.

"The sentencing hearing will be in the autumn," Claire said. "You may be asked to provide a character statement. As cathedral legal officer. About the embezzlement period and her conduct in the investigation."

"I'll provide one," he said.

"Yes," said Claire, as if she had expected this and found it appropriate. "I thought you would."

They sat for a moment with the case file between them and the morning coming in from the cloister garth through the window. A Tuesday morning in May: the cloister garth had the lime tree in its full first green, and the jackdaws were on the grass below it, and somewhere in the cathedral the college practice had already started — a different organist from before, not Rosalind's style, something warmer and less precise.

"There's one thing," Edmund said.

He had been considering whether to say this. He had written it in his notebook, the previous Wednesday evening, and then looked at it for some time without coming to a conclusion. In the end the conclusion was that it was true, and that Claire ought to know it, and that the fact that it was not evidence of anything in a legal sense did not diminish its accuracy.

"Before Marcus raised the embezzlement formally," Edmund said, "before he went to the Bishop — which, as we know, he also went to Rosalind about first, and then she intercepted the letter, so he never actually completed either course of action — before all of that, there was a period of three months in which he held the information and did not use it. He gave her the opportunity. Three months."

"Yes," said Claire.

"And she obtained the Bristol digoxin on March eleventh. Three days before the letter. Three months into those three months." He looked at the case file. "Which means that during the period in which he was being generous — in which he was giving her time and a private exit — she was already planning."

Claire looked at him.

"I'm not suggesting it changes the charge," Edmund said. "I'm suggesting it changes the picture of Marcus. He was being patient with someone who was simultaneously obtaining the means to kill him." He paused. "He was, in his way, a better person than the circumstance allowed him to be."

Claire took out a small notebook — a black cover, cheap spiral, nothing like Edmund's — and wrote something in it. She looked at what she had written and did not say anything.

"I'll note that," she said, at last.

"Thank you," Edmund said.

She stood. She did not take the case file. He realised, after a moment, that she had left it for him — that it was his copy, the cathedral's copy, and that the transfer had been done precisely, in the manner of a person who understands that documentation is a form of respect.

"She asked about the close," Claire said, at the door. "Rosalind. She asked if the services were continuing."

"They are."

"I told her." She was looking at the door frame rather than at him. "She said, 'Good. That's the right thing.' As if she'd had a say in it."

"She had thirty years of say in it," Edmund said. "The momentum doesn't stop the week she leaves."

Claire looked at him. She absorbed this in the way she absorbed Edmund's more compressed formulations — not visibly, but he could tell by the slight stillness that followed that it had landed.

"All right," she said. "I'll be in touch about the character statement."

She knocked twice on the door frame as she left, the same knock as when she'd arrived — a habit, he supposed, or a courtesy, and not a contradiction. Then she was gone and he heard her on the stairs.

He sat for a moment with the case file. The cloister garth outside was doing what it always did on a Tuesday in May: existing, quietly and without urgency, in the full first green of the lime tree. The jackdaws were there, and the practice organ was playing something he didn't recognise and found he was listening to anyway.

He opened the case file. He read the first page, which was the formal summary, and then he turned to the back, where his own statements were, and read the first line of his first statement: *I am Canon Edmund Hale, cathedral legal officer of Wychford Cathedral, and I was present when the body of Marcus Vayne was discovered on the morning of 15th April.*

He had been there. He had been in his stall at seven in the morning and had heard Thomas's voice from the north transept with something in it that he had known, before understanding, was wrong. And he had called the police, and he had called Helen, and it had taken six weeks and fifty miles of evidence chain and one afternoon in a Bristol interview room to arrive at the truth that had been available, in principle, from the beginning.

He had done what the situation required. This was all any of them had done.

He closed the case file. He put it on the bookcase under the glazed door, in the space that had been accumulating new material for twenty-two years. It was, in terms of volume, no different from the others. In terms of what it had cost to compile it, it was.

The Chapter meeting was in ten minutes. He picked up his notebook — the new one, amber cover, bought from the same shop in the cathedral precinct where he had bought the last sixteen — and went downstairs.

At the foot of the stairs, he saw the archive light through the garth window — the small frosted casement, still lit. He had not yet found the formulation for something he wanted to say to Helen. He thought: afterwards. Afterwards would do.

He went into the meeting room.
