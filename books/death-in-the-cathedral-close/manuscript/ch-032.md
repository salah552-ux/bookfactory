# CHAPTER THIRTY-TWO
## No. 4

Helen noted that No. 4 had a blue front door.

She had been in the close eight months — she had caught herself beginning to think *twenty-two years*, which was Edmund's number, not hers — and she had not thought to observe the colour of Rosalind's front door until she was standing at her own sitting room window watching someone ring the bell. Oxford blue. The colour of someone who had opinions. The paint was fresh enough to take the morning light without flinching.

Claire rang the bell at eleven forty-one. Helen had been watching from the moment she saw the car turn in through the close gate.

She had two uniformed officers with her. They stood at the base of the step — one on the path, one on the narrow strip of lawn — and the arrangement had, from across the close, an almost courteous quality. As if they were there to help with something. In a sense, they were. Edmund stood at his office window, first floor of the Chapter House, looking down into the close at an angle to Helen's. She could see the shape of him, still. He had been standing at that window when she crossed the close at eleven twenty to take up position at hers. Neither of them had arranged this. It had simply happened.

The door opened.

Rosalind was dressed. She was in the grey wool dress, the collarless jacket, the silver treble clef at the collar. Her hair was up. Nothing informal in any of it. Helen had been a pathologist for thirty-five years and had stood at the bedsides of people who were waiting to die and she knew, by the set of their bodies, the difference between someone taken by surprise and someone who had been expecting this for some time and had made certain decisions about how it would go. Rosalind had made her decisions. She was in the doorway of No. 4 exactly as she intended to be.

She looked at Edmund's window. Helen did not know what she saw there. She saw Edmund, still and pale, watching.

Then Rosalind looked at Claire, and then at the two officers, and said something Helen could not hear.

She stepped back from the door. They went in.

Helen stood at her window and waited.

The close was quiet. A Tuesday-morning quality of quiet — though this was a Thursday; this was not ordinary Thursday morning quiet, this was the held breath of an enclosure that did not yet know what it was holding. The jackdaws were in the cloister garth. Someone's terrace door banged shut on the far side. The quarter-bell had rung twenty minutes ago.

She did not go over.

She had thought, arriving back from the archive at eleven-fifteen, that she might. That proximity might be what the moment asked of her. She had known Rosalind Ferris for eight months. She had shared an afternoon and shortbread and the particular intimacy that comes from sitting in someone's kitchen while grief does what grief does. It was not nothing. She had also spent six weeks understanding precisely what Rosalind had done and why she had done it, and what it had cost Marcus Vayne, who was difficult and brilliant and who had kept thirty years of love letters locked in a box where no one could find them, who had tried to give her a way out and been killed for trying.

She did not go over.

She stood at the window and watched the close be still.

After eleven minutes — she noticed the time without meaning to, an occupational habit — the blue door opened again. Rosalind came out first, then Claire, then the two officers arranging themselves at a distance that was professionally courteous without being personal. Rosalind had her pale blue cardigan on. Over the jacket, over the grey dress: the pale blue cardigan, both arms, the small collar turned down, the buttons done. She had not come to the door in it. She had put it on inside.

She walked toward the close gate. She did not look up at Helen's window. She did not look at Edmund's. She walked with the posture she always walked with — upright, purposeful, the posture of a woman who for thirty years had led cathedral worship and understood that how you carry yourself in a public space is itself a form of meaning. She was still the Precentor of Wychford Cathedral, walking through the close on a Thursday morning in her pale blue cardigan.

Then she was through the gate, and the gate was closed, and she was not.

Helen looked up at Edmund's window. He was still there. They did not wave or gesture. They simply registered that the other was present, and then she turned away from the window, and she thought he did too.

The close outside was quiet in the way it had been quiet the morning Marcus was found, but different — that quiet had been the close not knowing. This quiet was the close knowing and not having found the words for it yet. They would find them. Communities always find words. Some of the words would be wrong, and some would be slightly-right, and some — Margaret's, probably, or Father Reeve's — would land on the thing accurately without meaning to.

Helen sat down in David's armchair. She had not meant to. She sat there for a few minutes without doing anything in particular, which was not something she did often or well. The clock on the mantle marked the quarter. The organ had not started up again after the college organist's morning practice session. The cathedral was simply there, outside the window, as it had been there yesterday and the day before and for seven hundred years before either of them had arrived.

She thought about the coffee mug. Plain white china. One word in her notebook. She had been right from that first morning and she had known it and it had taken six weeks of evidence to get them to this particular quiet Thursday morning. She had needed all of it — not because the conclusion was in doubt, but because in this place, with these people, certainty was not enough. You needed the kind of certainty that could be written down and handed to someone else and remain true in their hands.

She picked up her phone. She thought about calling Julian, and then she thought about what she would say, and then she put the phone down.

The narcissi in Rosalind's front window would need water by the weekend, if no one else thought of it.

Helen noted this. She sat in David's armchair and held it, the way she had learned to hold the things that had nowhere else to go. It was a small thing. It was not nothing.
