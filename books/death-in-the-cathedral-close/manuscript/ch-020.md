# CHAPTER TWENTY
## The Dispensing Record

Edmund and Helen were still at the station — three hours in, the interview room's fluorescent light doing nothing for either of them — when Claire's phone went and she looked at it and said, without expression, "One moment." She stepped out and was gone for four minutes, which Edmund counted without looking at his watch because four minutes in a small room with a cooling radiator and the distant sound of a printer somewhere in the station's administrative depths is a countable thing regardless of intent.

When Claire came back she sat down and placed her phone face-up on the table, which was not her habit.

"The pharmacy chain," she said. "The Avon Private Dispensary in Clifton. They've traced the dispensing record for the batch."

Edmund looked at Helen. Helen was looking at Claire.

"The second bottle," Claire said. "Batch number DA-6650/11. 60 tablets of digoxin 0.25 milligrams. Dispensed 11 March. We've been trying to verify the prescribing physician — the GMC number on the prescription turned out to be a retired Somerset GP whose details are still in the database but who hasn't practised since 2019 and who categorically did not write this prescription. Which is the fraud element."

"The patient name," Edmund said.

Claire looked at him. She had a way, when she was about to say something she considered significant, of becoming very still — it was a quality he associated with Helen, actually, and wondered if Helen had taught it to her or if Claire had arrived at it independently. "Ferris," she said. "Mrs. B. Ferris. Address given as the Clifton Convalescent Home, Clifton, Bristol." She paused. "She died eighteen months ago."

Edmund sat with the precise weight of what she had just said. He had known, on some level — had known since Helen had called him that morning with the sort code, since he had driven to the station with Helen beside him in the car and they had spent the first hour walking Claire through the care home account, the withdrawal dates, the correlation — he had known that this was a convergence point, that the investigation was gathering itself into a shape that was no longer inferential. He had known it in the theoretical way that a person knows a thing before it is confirmed.

Confirmed was different.

"Rosalind used her mother's name," he said. It was not a question. He said it in the way he recorded things in his notebook — for the fact of having said it aloud, for the discipline of naming what was in front of them.

"The address would have been in her mother's medical records," Helen said. She was looking at the table. "The Clifton home had Bridget Ferris in the national prescription database as a dementia patient — primarily on other medications, but the category of patient who might legitimately receive a digoxin prescription for cardiac management. An elderly woman in a care home. An existing patient of record. The forged prescription would have looked entirely plausible to a private dispensary that didn't look too closely."

"The GMC number was a guess," Claire said. "Or a search. She would have needed a plausible number. She found one that was real but inactive — someone who stopped practising quietly."

Edmund thought about this. He thought about Rosalind at her desk in the close, with the choir schedule and the correspondence files and the faculty application for the new music stands she had been circling back to for the last four months. He thought about her composing, with the same systematic patience she brought to everything, a false prescription for sixty tablets of digoxin in her dead mother's name. Using the home address. The registered patient details. The name of an old GP whose licence had lapsed somewhere in Somerset.

He thought: she is extremely good at this. And immediately: she has had twelve years of practice at being extremely good at this.

"The dispensary," he said. "Did they have any record of who collected it? Physically."

"Cash purchase. They remember a woman. They think middle-aged — they said 'not young, not elderly.' Smart dress. The description is not precise enough to be useful on its own, but it is not inconsistent." Claire picked up her phone. "The dispensary CCTV is being requested. It's possible the system has been overwritten — eleven March is six weeks ago. If it hasn't, we may get an image."

"You may not need it," Helen said. "You have the name on the prescription. You have the connection to the music fund account. You have the key ledger. You have the cardigan — Thomas's account of the eleven o'clock figure. The CCTV from the Song School corridor, which is still being processed." She looked at Claire. "You have enough to formally redirect this to Rosalind."

"I've been formally redirected since you walked in this afternoon," Claire said. She said it with the precision of a person who is being very careful about what she puts on the official record and what she leaves in the informal register of this particular relationship. "What I have this evening is the first piece of documentary evidence that is not circumstantial inference. Which is different."

Edmund understood this distinction. He had spent twenty-two years navigating the space between what was obvious and what was provable, and he had sometimes found it a comfortable distinction to hide behind, and he had sometimes found it a necessary one, and he knew the difference well enough to see that Claire was not hiding.

She was building. There was a difference.

"The Song School CCTV," Edmund said. "When can we expect the enhancement?"

"Two days. Possibly three." Claire looked at him. "I don't intend to wait for it. The enhancement will confirm what the dispensing record and the key ledger and the music fund accounts already confirm by triangulation. If I wait for perfect evidence, the investigation becomes slower than it needs to be." She picked up her pen. "I'm going to need formal statements from both of you. Tonight if possible, or first thing tomorrow."

Edmund nodded. He looked at his watch: nearly six o'clock. Through the narrow window of the interview room — high and frosted, designed for no particular view — he could see the colour of the sky going from grey to grey-blue, the particular quality of an April evening that could not decide between its moods.

He thought about the morning: Webb in his office with the coat collar up and the face of a man putting down a weight. The lichen smell in the garth. The kettle's percussion. All of that had happened this morning, in the same day that was ending here in this room with a dispensing record bearing the name of a dead woman. The day had the density of a thing that had been waiting to resolve.

He thought about Rosalind's mother — the three lines in the Bristol Evening Post, the private funeral arrangements, the absence of Wychford from the notice. Bridget Mary Ferris, aged 87. *Much loved mother.* He thought about the small dishonesties that compound, as Helen had put it in her flat, careful way in one of their conversations: the thing that begins as a temporary measure and becomes, through repetition, a way of living.

Mrs. B. Ferris, address the Clifton Convalescent Home.

The name on the prescription was the name of the person whose care Rosalind had been funding for twelve years with the money the prescription had helped kill Marcus Vayne. He noted this without particular elaboration. The ironies of criminal investigation were a known occupational hazard and he did not find that they became easier to look at with familiarity.

"Edmund." Helen was looking at him. She said his name in the way she said things when she had decided to say them and not when she had considered whether to.

"Yes," he said.

"It's enough." She was not offering reassurance. She was stating a fact. The distinction was, by now, one he could hear without difficulty.

"I know," he said.

He did know. That was, perhaps, the thing that the dispensing record had actually changed — not his certainty, which had been complete for some days, but the shape of the work. They were no longer, he thought, gathering evidence to form a belief. They were gathering evidence to build a case that would survive examination by people who had not been present for any of it — a jury, a barrister, a judge in a court where what he knew in his bones would be required to become what was on the record. The investigation had changed shape. It was no longer about finding Rosalind's guilt. It was about constructing the proof of it with enough care that no element of it could be unpicked.

He opened his notebook to a fresh page. The green pen was in his jacket pocket. He wrote the date at the top and then, below it, in his small careful hand: *Dispensing record — DA-6650/11 — Mrs. B. Ferris — Clifton Convalescent Home — 11 March.* He underlined the name once. Then he underlined the date.

"Statements," he said to Claire. "First thing tomorrow — earlier if you need us tonight."

"Tomorrow," Claire said. She looked at the phone still lying face-up on the table. "I need to make some calls this evening."

Edmund closed the notebook. He looked at Helen, who was buttoning her coat with the precise efficiency she brought to small physical tasks. He looked at Claire, who was already thinking past the room.

He thought: there is still work to do. The Song School CCTV. The dispensary footage, if it had survived. The formal statements. The arrest warrant process, which was its own careful architecture. There was no shortcut.

But the net had closed by a step. The step had a name on it: Mrs. B. Ferris, address the Clifton Convalescent Home. A name Rosalind had reached for in a private dispensary in Clifton with sixty tablets and cash, and which had just found its way back to this table.

He stood, picked up his coat, and went with Helen out into the April evening, which was cold now and smelled of rain on stone and the particular damp exhalation of a city settling into its night. They walked to the car without speaking. The silence was not uncomfortable. It had, by now, the quality of a shared professional understanding between two people who had worked on the same problem long enough to know when a day's work was complete.

He drove. The cathedral came into view as he turned into Wychford's lower approach — the tower lit from below, the colour of amber in the dark, steady and indifferent as seven centuries of steady indifference.

He thought: she is still in the close. She is still at her desk, or at her piano, or making her careful dinner at No. 4. She does not yet know the dispensing record has been found.

He thought: she probably knows it was a matter of time.

He thought: it no longer matters what she knows.
