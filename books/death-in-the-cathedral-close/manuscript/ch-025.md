# CHAPTER TWENTY-FIVE
## Convergence

"He's not eating," Margaret said, on Saturday morning, coming into Edmund's office with shortbread and finding only Helen. She set the plate down with the air of a person depositing evidence. "I took biscuits round on Thursday. He came to the door in his coat and boots. In his own house."

Margaret Holt had opinions. This was well-established. She delivered them not as opinions but as statements of atmospheric fact, the way a barometer delivered pressure readings.

"He's concerned about something," Helen said, which was accurate and not specific.

"He's concerned about something," Margaret said. She adjusted the shortbread. "He's been concerned about something for six weeks, which is roughly how long all of us have been concerned about something. What I want to know is whether that something is going to be sorted before we get to another Monday."

Helen recognised in Margaret's "another Monday" a reference to the forthcoming Chapter meeting. She had not thought about the Chapter meeting. She thought about it now.

"Presumably the meeting will be brief," she said.

"Presumably," said Margaret, in a tone that indicated she thought nothing of the kind. "Canon Webb is not well. He hasn't been at Evensong in four days. Mrs. Webb came for the Saturday collect and sat in the back."

Helen took a piece of shortbread. It was good shortbread — buttery, fine-crumbed, the kind that requires more precision than it appears to. She ate it and thought about Webb in his house not eating, with his wife sitting in the back of the cathedral at the Saturday morning service, and the Chapter meeting on Monday, and Hargreaves's call to Edmund.

She thought about the enhancement, which she had asked Patel about that morning by a text message to the number Edmund had given her: *Status update on the Song School enhancement if you're able to say.* Patel had responded: *Noon Monday at the earliest. Could be Tuesday.*

Noon Monday. The Chapter meeting was at two.

"Margaret," she said. "You've been here forty years."

"Forty-one," Margaret said, mildly corrective.

"How many times has there been something the Chapter hasn't been able to resolve before the meeting started?"

Margaret considered. "1989," she said. "That was the organ scholar. And then 2003, which I won't go into, and 2011 when the south tower figures had ice damage and the insurers weren't being helpful. And whatever this is." She picked up her own piece of shortbread. "The difference, if you're interested in what the difference is, is that in '89 and '03 and '11 we all knew more or less what we were dealing with. This time we don't." She looked at Helen with eyes that were considerably sharper than her usual manner suggested. "Or rather," she said, "some of us might. And some of us haven't been told."

Helen said nothing.

"Quite," said Margaret, and left.

The six o'clock Evensong on Saturday was better attended than it had any right to be. Word travelled in the close by processes that Helen had never been able to fully account for — not gossip exactly, not deliberate communication, just the kind of knowing that small communities developed like weather systems, self-generating and mostly accurate. People came on Saturday evening because something was in the air, and sitting in the cathedral felt, in the way it had done for seven centuries, like the appropriate response to a thing in the air.

The choir was in the stalls. Rosalind was at the lectern before her score, which she had open at the selected psalms, though she was not consulting it. She knew the psalms. She had known them for thirty years.

Helen sat in the nave on the north side, where she always sat. Beside her on the bench: Margaret, who had apparently forgiven the morning's non-answer. Behind her, a woman with a young child who was managing to be respectful of the silence, mostly. On the far side of the central aisle, three members of the choir committee who had come as congregation rather than as choir, which was itself a statement. In the south transept, Thomas Oakes, standing against the pillar in the way he always stood, his arms folded, his expression that of a man professionally attending to the building's safety while personally considering something else entirely.

Not Webb. Webb was not there.

Rosalind did not look at the congregation. She rarely did during the service; her attention was entirely on the choir when she was conducting and entirely on the words when she was not. She had worn, this evening, a dark grey dress with a collarless jacket — not the pale blue cardigan, which Helen noted with the involuntary precision of someone who has been noting it for weeks. Her hair was pinned as usual. The silver treble clef brooch at her lapel.

The psalms were 84 and 85. Helen knew them both.

*How lovely is thy dwelling place, O Lord of hosts.* She had sung it as a girl, in a smaller cathedral in a colder county, with a choir that had none of Wychford's technical precision but twice its emotional weight. *My soul hath a desire and longing to enter into the courts of the Lord.* She wondered sometimes whether Marcus had chosen his profession for that reason — the daily permission to be in those courts, to have the architecture as your working environment, to move through seven centuries of accumulated human longing on a Tuesday morning and call it Tuesday.

*Yea, the sparrow hath found her a house.*

Rosalind raised her right hand. The choir came in, sixteen voices, and the sound went up into the vaulting and spread there the way it always did — the particular acoustic of the nave that gathered high harmonics and held them a fraction longer than the lower frequencies, so that a chord, after four beats, still had its top note living in the stone. Helen had heard it described by someone who knew about these things as a vertical reverb, the building itself functioning as instrument.

She watched Rosalind's hands. The right hand marked the beat with a clarity that required no effort — it was the precision of a person who had been doing this long enough that the motion lived below consciousness. The left hand shaped the phrase, lifted a soprano line, touched the tenors into pianissimo on the second verse. There was no hesitation in any of it. There was no tremor, no catch, no moment where the hands failed to do what she intended.

Helen had once testified in a court case about a surgeon who had operated under stress so acute that his colleagues had questioned his fitness to continue. The argument she'd been asked to address was whether stress affected surgical precision. Her answer, which the barrister had been somewhat put out by, was that it depended entirely on the person. Some people became clumsy under pressure. Some people became very, very still.

Rosalind's hands described the final bar of the psalm. The choir came to rest on the last chord — the tenors leaning slightly into it, the way tenors always did — and she held them there for two beats longer than the score required.

It was exactly right. The stone held the chord.

She closed her score and turned to the choir, her hands describing the final bar, and Helen watched the hands, and knew.
