# CHAPTER TWENTY-ONE
## The Footage

The Song School's security office was a converted cupboard that smelled of old carpet and photocopier toner, and whoever had installed the monitor had done so at a height intended for a person considerably shorter than Helen. She stood with her chin too high, which made her neck ache, and watched the grey rectangle in the upper-right quadrant of the screen.

The timestamp read 22:14:52. The corridor was empty.

At 22:15:03, a shape appeared at the left edge of the frame.

The image was not clear. It was the particular unclarity of CCTV footage that has been stored in compressed format on hardware that was installed a decade ago and last serviced six years before that — a shimmer at the edges, a slight softening of the centre, every dark shade pulling toward black. The fluorescent strip lighting in the corridor flickered at intervals, which the camera had faithfully recorded as periodic wash-outs of the frame.

Still.

The shape moved along the corridor with a brisk, contained walk — not hurrying exactly, but a person who knew the space, who had walked it many times, who did not need to look where the walls were. Hip-length garment, pale against the darker corridor wall. Below that, dark trousers or a skirt — impossible to determine. Hair up, or a scarf. No bag visible. The figure paused at the archive storeroom door for six seconds, opened it — Helen noted the ease of that, the unlocked certainty of it — and went inside.

The corridor was empty again at 22:15:37.

At 22:26:14, the figure re-emerged. Same walk. Pulled the door closed without testing it. Moved back to the left edge of the frame and was gone.

Eleven minutes in the storeroom.

Helen noted the time: eleven minutes. Long enough to place something. Long enough to wipe something. Not long enough to search. A person who went in knowing what they meant to do.

Edmund was standing at her right shoulder. He said nothing. The technical officer from Claire's team — a young man named Patel with an abrupt manner and good shoes — advanced the footage at Helen's request, then rewound it, then paused it on the best frame they had of the figure mid-stride.

"The cardigan," Helen said.

"Resolution's degraded," Patel said. "I can tell you it's a light-coloured garment. Hip-length. Buttons visible at the front — possibly — but that could be artefact." He pointed at a section of the paused frame where the compression had turned three pixels into a smear. "Enhancement will take two days minimum. Possibly three."

"The walk," Edmund said. His voice was very even.

Patel looked at him. "What about it?"

"I note," Helen said, "that the figure walks without reference to the walls."

Patel turned back to the screen. He considered this. "Familiar with the space," he said, after a moment.

"Yes."

He typed something. "I'll request the enhancement this afternoon. Forensic imaging contractor — they're backed up. Two days if I flag it urgent. Three if I don't."

"Flag it urgent," Edmund said.

On the way out, they walked along the same corridor, the same fluorescent strip above them, the same dark threshold of the archive storeroom door on the left. Helen stopped. She counted the steps from the storeroom to the edge of the frame. She measured the ceiling height with her eye. The figure on the screen had stood approximately here — she adjusted her position slightly — and had moved approximately at this pace.

Five foot six, she thought. Five foot seven at the most. Not heavy. Not young.

Edmund had stopped beside her. They both looked at the storeroom door.

"The enhancement will show what it shows," Helen said. "We will wait for the enhancement."

"Yes," said Edmund.

They walked back through the Song School and out into the close. It was a Wednesday, early afternoon, the kind of April day that cannot decide whether it is spring or is still practising. The close grass was very green. From the far side of the chapter house, the cathedral choir was rehearsing something with brass — a young tenor voice carrying over the wall, climbing a phrase and then stopping. Someone said something in a register too low to hear. The voice tried again.

Rosalind would be taking that rehearsal, Helen thought. She had been taking the Wednesday afternoon rehearsal for thirty years. The choir knew her patterns better than she knew them herself — the pause she left before a tempo instruction, the particular way she lifted her right hand on the downbeat.

They walked past No. 4. The curtains in the front room were open. The piano was visible, dark and still, with the lid down. A small stack of music sheets on the bench beside it.

Helen did not slow her pace.

She and Edmund let themselves into his office through the south passage door and sat at the cherrywood table. Edmund opened his notebook to a blank page. He uncapped his green pen and wrote the timestamp at the top: 22:15:03. Then the exit timestamp: 22:26:14. Then: 11 minutes.

He underlined "11 minutes" once.

"She came in," Helen said, "through the Song School door. She had her key. She went to the archive storeroom — she had that key also, it's on the same ring as the Song School master. She placed the bottle behind the 1830s registers. Then she left."

"The archive storeroom has no separate camera," Edmund said. "We have her entering and leaving. We do not have the placement itself."

"We do not need it. The bottle was found there. The batch number from the bottle matches Marcus's prescription." Helen paused. "What we need the enhancement for is the cardigan."

Edmund was writing. He wrote carefully, his pen moving across the page without any hurry.

"Two to three days," he said.

"Yes."

Outside, the tenor voice found the top of the phrase and held it. It was a good voice — not fully formed yet, but real. The note hung over the close like weather.

Helen looked at the window. The light through the medieval glass in the chapter house passage had always coloured the floor in the late afternoon — amber, a strip of faded green — but at two o'clock it was too early for that. The room was ordinary, the light was ordinary, and what they were sitting with was not ordinary at all.

"We need to be certain," she said.

Edmund set down his pen. He looked at the notebook, at the two timestamps and the eleven minutes between them.

"Yes," he said. "We do."

Neither of them mentioned Rosalind's name. They had not needed to for some time now. The name was there in the room with them, in the timestamps, in the walk without reference to the walls, in the music score on the piano bench at No. 4 and the brass rehearsal carrying over the close wall, and in the way both of them had not looked at the curtained window as they passed.

They sat with it for a moment. The tenor voice in the close had gone quiet. Somewhere in the cathedral, a door closed — the north transept stair door, probably, by the sound of it. Neither of them moved.

"We need to be certain," Helen said. "And then we will not need to be uncertain any longer."
