# CHAPTER EIGHTEEN
## What Alistair Webb Was Hiding

It was Webb who came to Edmund, which Edmund had not expected, and it was early on a Thursday morning when the close was still damp from overnight rain and the garth smelled of wet stone and something green and almost medicinal that Edmund suspected was the lichen on the north cloister wall reacting to the moisture. Edmund had just unlocked his office and was waiting for the kettle — an elderly and enthusiastic device that expressed its readiness with a sound like distant percussion — when he heard the knock on the outer door, two steps down and through the panelled corridor.

He opened it and found Webb in the doorway with his coat collar turned up and his face doing something complicated that Edmund had not seen on it before. It took him a moment to identify the expression. It was, he thought, the face of a man who has decided to stop carrying something.

"Have you got a minute," Webb said. It was not quite a question.

Edmund stood aside.

He had known Alistair Webb for twenty-two years, which was the length of his own tenure at Wychford. They had never been close — Webb was prickly in the way that men are prickly when they feel they have been passed over, and the long shadow of the Director of Music appointment fifteen years ago had never quite lifted from his manner around the cathedral music department. But Edmund respected him in the way that Edmund respected most people who were genuinely good at something: Webb's pastoral work was careful and real, and the people he visited in hospital and in grief seemed to find in him a quality of presence that Edmund acknowledged he himself did not possess.

He poured two cups of coffee from the pot he had been nursing since seven and set one in front of Webb without asking. Webb wrapped both hands around it and stared at the surface of the coffee with the intensity of a man reading an augury.

"I wasn't walking," Webb said. "On the night. That's what I told Claire. I said I was walking."

Edmund waited.

"I was with Sarah Clifton." Webb looked up. His eyes were red at the rims, the particular red of a sleepless night rather than tears. "Her husband is in Manchester most weeks. She lives in Vicars' Row — do you know which house?" Edmund did. "I was there from nine until half past ten. Maybe quarter to eleven. Then I came back through the gate." He set his coffee down. "I have been trying to find a way to say it that doesn't sound like what it is."

"It sounds like what it is," Edmund said.

"Yes." Webb passed a hand over his face. "Anne doesn't know. I've been — I have been going to tell her. I keep not telling her." He looked at the cherrywood table, the papers Edmund had laid out the previous evening, the green pen sitting crosswise on the open audit notebook. "Is it relevant? To Marcus? To what they think about me?"

"Your alibi has been inconsistent," Edmund said carefully. "That is a fact which has been noted."

Webb made a sound that was not quite a laugh. "I couldn't say I was in bed with a married woman while her husband was in Manchester. I couldn't — Anne is in the next pew every single Sunday. Anne has been in the next pew for thirty-one years." He looked at his hands. "I know what I've done. I don't need you to explain it to me."

Edmund did not explain it to him. He thought about the afternoon three weeks ago in the cloister when he had asked Webb whether he had told them everything, and Webb had said *I will speak to Anne first*, and Edmund had read that as the code of a man with a real secret, a secret whose nature he had not known but whose existence had been plain. He had been right about the existence. He had spent some portion of the intervening weeks wondering, in a way he preferred not to examine too closely, whether he had also been right that it was not murder.

He now believed he had been right. But believing was not the same as being certain, and certain was what Claire needed him to be.

"I'm going to need to tell Claire," Edmund said. "Not now, this morning — but today. She will need Sarah Clifton to confirm the account, and she will need the specific times. She will be discreet about it as long as she possibly can. That is Claire's nature as well as her professional obligation."

"And Anne."

"That is a separate matter," Edmund said, "and not mine to navigate."

Webb sat with this for a moment. The kettle, unprompted, produced a brief clatter of cooling metal. The rain had started again outside the window overlooking the garth; Edmund could hear it in the gutters, a variable, intermittent sound like a conversation about something that couldn't quite be resolved.

"Did you suspect me?" Webb asked. He was looking at the table rather than at Edmund.

Edmund considered several versions of an honest answer. "You gave us reason to," he said at last.

"I know." Webb picked up his coffee again. "I knew you would. I thought I could — manage it. Stay on the right side of it without actually saying the thing. I've been managing things for thirty years and I thought I could manage this." He looked up. "Marcus knew, actually. About Sarah." He said it quietly, without drama, as if he'd decided to empty his pockets entirely since he'd already started. "He said nothing. Three years he knew and he said nothing. That's the kind of man he was, underneath the difficulty."

Edmund did not write this in his notebook. It did not need to be written down; he would not forget it. He would add it to the private file of things he had learned about Marcus Vayne over the last several weeks — the locked box, the thirty years of loving a woman called Catherine, the complaint letter he had tried to send through proper channels because improper ones were not in his nature. The picture of a difficult man who had, underneath the difficulty, kept secrets that were not his to tell, and been killed for the one secret he had decided, finally, not to keep.

"I'll call Claire after Morning Prayer," Edmund said. "You should be there when I do."

Webb nodded. He finished the coffee standing up, which was not how he had sat down. He left his cup on the edge of the table and went to the door and then stopped with his hand on the frame. "Edmund." He said it without turning round. "I'm sorry I wasn't — I should have said it straight away. It would have been cleaner."

"Yes," Edmund said. "It would have been."

Webb left. Edmund stood for a moment at the window, watching the rain make brief mirrors on the cloister flagstones. He thought about clean and unclean and the degrees between them: Marcus's complaint letter sent through proper channels. Rosalind intercepting it through proprietary ones. Webb covering an affair with a lie of omission that looked, from a sufficient distance, like murder. These were different orders of failing, though they existed on a spectrum that was, in the end, the same spectrum.

He called Claire at nine-thirty. She was professional, quick, and asked four questions in the correct order. He heard her writing.

He went to Sarah Clifton's house in Vicars' Row himself at half past ten, because Claire had asked him to — familiar face, less formal — and he stood in the small front room with the rain now properly steady against the window and the smell of a gas fire recently extinguished, and listened to Sarah Clifton confirm the account in a voice that was quiet and steady and did not crack, which Edmund respected, because the situation she was in was not easy and she was managing it with more composure than he suspected he would have managed himself.

She confirmed nine o'clock. She confirmed half past ten. She confirmed the conversation they'd had — she had mentioned, she said, that her husband would be home on Friday, and Webb had said he was going to sort things out. He hadn't said what things. She looked at the fireplace when she said this and did not look back up for a moment.

Edmund thanked her and walked back through the rain, which had the particular quality of April rain — not quite cold, not quite warm, more determined than its temperature warranted — and thought about Webb's "I will speak to Anne first" on the day Edmund had asked him in the cloister whether he had told them everything. He had been telling the truth. He had been telling a truth that was not about murder.

Red herrings, Edmund thought, without particular satisfaction. The investigation's false consolations. They existed not to deceive but because human life was genuinely complicated, and complicated people under pressure looked guilty when they weren't. This was not the detective's failure. It was simply the condition of the work.

He sat in his office for a long time after, with the rain running down the garth window and the coffee gone cold and his notebook open to the page that now held three words: *Webb — alibi confirmed.* He underlined "confirmed" once. Then he wrote underneath it, in smaller letters: *He knew about Clifton and said nothing. Three years. That was Marcus.*

He closed the notebook.

He was relieved, which he had expected. He was weary in a way that went beyond the morning's work, which he had also expected. One less thing to be uncertain about. One more human failing in the close, explicable if not excusable, resolved without catastrophe for the investigation if not for several other things.

He opened the notebook again and looked at the words he had just written. He thought about the work still left: the CCTV footage Claire had requested from the Song School corridor; the pharmacy records coming through official channels; the music fund connection that Helen was, he suspected, already several steps ahead of him on. The net was drawing in. Slowly. With the particular patience that evidence required.

He wondered, briefly, if Rosalind knew how close it was. He thought: she is probably very good at not thinking about that directly.

He did not wonder whether he had been wrong about her. That door had been closed for some time now. He wondered instead about something smaller and more persistent: how long she had been carrying it, and what it had felt like to carry it, walking into Chapter meetings and choir rehearsals and the corridors of a cathedral she had devoted her life to, with a weight she could not put down and could not speak of.

He decided he would probably never know. He closed the notebook again, for the last time this morning, and went to find something to eat.

Marcus had known about Webb and Clifton for three years and had said nothing. The investigation had just become two things simpler and one thing harder.

He had kept other people's secrets, Edmund thought. Until the secret he would not keep was his own.
