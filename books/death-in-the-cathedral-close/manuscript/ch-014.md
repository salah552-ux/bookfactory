# CHAPTER FOURTEEN
## Love Letters

The organ bench was walnut, and old — the kind of old that meant it had been in the organ loft before the organ itself, that it had outlived three previous instruments and the men who played them and would outlive several more. It was the length of two men lying end to end, the bench itself hinged along the top, the cavity below used by successive organists to store everything that the Song School office was too public for. Marcus had used it for music scores, a spare waistcoat, a hip flask that turned out to contain cold tea, a jar of boiled sweets that had fused together in the heat of previous Augusts into a single amber mass, and a locked mahogany box the size of a shoebox.

Claire's sergeant had flagged the box on Wednesday. Claire had waited for Edmund.

He did not know, precisely, why she had waited for him. He suspected she was giving him the opportunity to be present for the discovery of something that would either matter or not matter, and that she thought his presence was the variable that would tell the difference. He appreciated this more than he would have said.

The key was in an envelope in the evidence bags from Marcus's Study. His keychain: eleven keys, the smallest a flat-cut brass the right size for a small domestic lock. It turned the first time.

The box contained letters. Not many — eight, perhaps ten. Edmund did not count them immediately; he registered the paper first: pale cream, good quality, the kind of quality that was not used for casual correspondence. A faint scent, which was the smell of paper stored carefully for many years in a small enclosed space — cedar and something else that might have been lavender or might simply have been the paper's own ageing. A rubber band, red, dried to brittleness, that came off in two pieces.

He picked up the top letter. Unfolded it. The hand was a woman's — or he made the assumption, and then immediately caught himself making it and substituted: the handwriting was fine and regular, with certain characteristically forward flourishes on the capitals. *My dear Marcus.* The date was June, nineteen years before.

He read the first paragraph. Then he refolded the letter and placed it back in the box.

Claire was watching him. She did not rush him.

He said: "They are love letters. A woman named Catherine. The date on the first one suggests nineteen years ago." He looked at the box. "The name on the envelope, if there is one."

Claire's sergeant checked the envelope at the bottom of the stack. "Catherine Vane. No address."

Edmund sat back on the side of the organ bench. The loft was cold — the heating in the nave did not extend to the gallery, and the cold air came in from the high north windows in the way cold air came into old churches, as though it had a right to be there. He could smell, faintly, the rosin dust from the organ pipes, and beneath it the stone and the darkness of a space that had not been a comfortable space for a long time.

He said: "Catherine Vane died." He knew this only because Margaret had mentioned, in an unrelated context some four years ago, that Marcus had been distracted in the weeks after an old friend of his had died — a woman from his Bristol days, before Wychford. Margaret had, with the particular economy of institutional memory, provided the name and the manner of death (cancer, swift) and the fact that Marcus had attended the funeral and had come back looking, in Margaret's phrase, "as though someone had removed a piece of him." Edmund had, at the time, made a sound of sympathy and had not asked further. He regretted, briefly, that he had not asked further.

"When?" Claire asked.

"About nineteen years ago," Edmund said. "Which aligns with the date."

Claire was quiet for a moment. Then she said, in a tone that was professionally neutral and personally careful: "So these are not relevant to the murder."

"No," said Edmund. He looked at the stack of letters. Ten of them, or perhaps twelve — he still had not counted. Written over what period, he did not know. Kept locked, away from the Song School office with its semi-public access, kept in a walnut box that was older than Marcus, in a loft where Marcus had spent the better part of twenty-five years alone.

He said: "He kept them in the only place he was entirely private."

Claire nodded. She had the expression of a police officer who had seen the locked boxes of the dead before and who had made peace with the fact that the dead were entitled to their privacies even when those privacies temporarily disrupted an investigation.

She said: "We'll need to confirm no blackmail angle."

"Of course." Edmund looked at the box again. "I don't believe you'll find one. But we should look." He paused. "Was there a return address anywhere in the box? A second name?"

The sergeant went through the remaining items: a pressed flower, dried flat and brown, the species unidentifiable after two decades of compression; a stub of concert ticket (Bristol Philharmonia, February, nineteen years ago); a small photograph, black-and-white, of a woman looking sideways at something outside the frame, laughing. The back of the photograph: *Catherine, Tintern, 1999.* Nothing further.

Edmund looked at the photograph for a moment. A woman laughing, in sunlight, at something she found genuinely funny. Her face in profile. She would have been in her late thirties or early forties then. She was dead now, and the man who had kept her photograph in a locked box for nineteen years was also dead, and the box was being handled by the cathedral lawyer and a detective sergeant who were looking for blackmail angles and finding none.

He said: "May I put the photograph back?"

Claire looked at him. "After we've processed it."

"Of course." He put the photograph face-up on the evidence sheet. "I would like it noted that these items are of significant personal value and no probative value. I will write to his sister."

They locked the box again and placed it with the evidence team. Claire's sergeant took away the key.

Edmund went down the narrow stone stair from the loft — that cramped descent, the wall pressing close on both sides, the scrape of the door at the base — and came out into the north transept, where Margaret Holt was waiting with an expression of barely contained curiosity and the offer of tea.

"You've been up there a long time," she said. "I brought tea in case."

"You are an institution," Edmund said. It was not entirely a compliment, but Margaret received it as one.

They went, by unspoken agreement, to the small room beside the vestry that had been used since 1987 as a place to store chairs and which Margaret had quietly furnished with a table and two proper chairs and a small electric ring for exactly this purpose. The tea was strong. The room smelled of cold stone and old fabric and, faintly, of whatever Margaret put in her hair.

"Was it anything?" she asked.

"No," said Edmund. "Nothing for the case." He held the mug. The warmth was welcome. "He kept letters from a woman who died many years ago. He had a private grief, evidently. One that no one here knew about."

Margaret was quiet for a moment. She was seventy-five and she had been in this close for forty years, which meant she had seen fourteen members of the cathedral Chapter move through, half of them complicated in ways that only became clear after they were gone. She said: "He wasn't always easy."

"No."

"But he played the Tallis Fantasia at Evensong last November and I thought — " She stopped. "Well. I thought I wouldn't hear it played like that again." She sipped her tea. "He loved someone named Catherine for thirty years without anyone knowing. Edmund closed the box and put it back."

Edmund looked at her.

She blinked, and for a moment he was confused — and then he understood. She had not read his thought. She had simply said, in the way that she said things, the truth that was available to both of them in the room.

He said: "Quite right, Margaret."

He walked back to his office in the early afternoon, through the cloister, through the arch that led to the Chapter House, and he thought about the concert ticket stub. February, nineteen years ago. The Philharmonia. He thought about Marcus Vayne at twenty-nine or thirty, keeping a ticket stub from the night he first sat beside a woman who would later write him letters on pale cream paper. He thought about the Olivetti on the office desk with the unfinished letter about the tenor passage in the *Salve Regina*, and about the fact that Marcus's last professional act had been to argue for a musical interpretation that he believed in absolutely.

It did not change what Marcus was. It did not change what had been done to him. It did, quietly, change how Edmund held the weight of it.

He opened his notebook and wrote, in the margin beside the page of five things: *He loved someone named Catherine for thirty years without anyone knowing.*

He underlined it once.
