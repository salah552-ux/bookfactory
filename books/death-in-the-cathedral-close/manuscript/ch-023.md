# CHAPTER TWENTY-THREE
## Doubt

Helen sat in the archive on Thursday morning and made herself do the other thing first. She pulled out the Tudor land grant she had been cataloguing since February — a long vellum roll, fragile at the creases, the ink faded to the colour of old tea — and she set it under the lamp and continued the transcription she had left off three weeks ago. The archive was cold. The stone walls sweated faintly; she had put a folded paper between her notebook and the table surface so the damp would not come through. She could hear the heating system in the chapter house above her, ticking and settling.

She had been sitting here for twenty-five years, on and off. Before retirement, between court dates, when she needed to think about something that wasn't death. Now she sat here because it was the one room in the cathedral where nobody came to ask her anything.

She had been reviewing possibilities since four in the morning, which was when she had woken and found it was no longer possible to persuade herself she was asleep. In her years at the coroner's office, she had trained herself to construct alternatives. A good pathologist did not decide what the body was telling you and then look for confirmation — she decided what all the possible explanations were and eliminated them one by one until only one remained. This was not a natural habit of mind. It was an acquired discipline, and it was discipline she brought now to the archive cellar at seven in the morning with the Tudor roll on the table and the alternative explanations written in small columns in the margin of her notebook.

She tested each one in turn.

*Could the CCTV figure be Rose Hartley?* Rose had a key. Rose was approximately the right height. Helen ran through the counterarguments: Rose's walk, which was slower, with a characteristic slight roll on the left foot from an old ankle injury — notable at the Song School Christmas concert, notable at morning prayer. The figure on the CCTV moved without that roll. And Rose had no access to the pharmacy records, no account from which to make cash withdrawals that matched the dispensary cost, no knowledge of Marcus's medication schedule. The music fund had a single signatory. Rose had never been that signatory.

She drew a line through Rose Hartley.

*Could Thomas's identification of the cardigan figure be wrong?* Could what he saw at 11pm have been an entirely different person — someone Helen hadn't accounted for? She had run through the residents of the close. She had run through the visitor logs from the week. There was no one — no one with a Song School key, no one whose walk Thomas would have associated with pale blue cashmere, no one who could have known where the medication was kept and how the system worked. The CCTV figure knew the space. Knew the door, knew the lock, knew how long eleven minutes would take.

She drew another line.

She sat with the remaining name for some time and then returned to the Tudor roll.

At ten past eleven, her doorbell rang. She climbed the cellar stairs, went through the archive anteroom, and opened the front door of No. 7 to find Rosalind standing on the step with a lidded pot and the expression of a woman who has prepared a visit in advance and is not going to be talked out of it.

"Leek and potato," Rosalind said. "I made two portions. You're going to tell me you ate already. I know that you haven't."

Helen held the door open.

They sat in Helen's kitchen, which was warmer than the rest of the flat and smelled of the coffee Helen had made at six and not quite finished. Rosalind put the soup pot on the hob and found a ladle — she had been in Helen's kitchen enough times to know where things were — and served it without ceremony. She sat down across from Helen and wrapped both hands around her own bowl.

"You look tired," she said.

"I've been in the archive since seven."

"I know. I saw your light on my way back from Matins." Rosalind's soup was very good — Helen noted the slight bitterness of leek properly cooked, the way the potato had thickened the broth without being pushed too far. She noticed she was cataloguing the soup the way she catalogued evidence: systematically, because it was easier than being present in the room. "How is the investigation going? You needn't tell me if you'd rather not. I just — you and Edmund have been carrying something that isn't yours to carry alone. I see that."

"The inquiry is in Claire's hands," Helen said. "It moves at the pace it moves."

Rosalind nodded. She turned her bowl slightly on the table — a small, unconscious habit of her hands that Helen had noticed before. She wanted to stop noticing things. It was useful and she wanted to stop.

"I heard Webb's name again this morning," Rosalind said. "From Margaret. I don't know how Margaret hears things as quickly as she does, but she heard it, so presumably everyone will." She looked at her soup. "I keep thinking — an innocent man, living under suspicion in a community this small. Whatever his other failings." She paused. "Is there anything to be done for him? Practically speaking."

"Claire is thorough," Helen said. "She doesn't make arrests on insufficient evidence."

Rosalind looked up briefly. "No," she said. "No, I suppose she doesn't."

They finished the soup. Rosalind talked about the choir — a boy soprano in the top line who had come to his break early and was managing the transition with more grace than she had expected. She talked about the cathedral for thirty minutes the way she always talked about it: with the easy specificity of a person for whom it was simply the shape of her life. She knew which pillar had the oldest repair mortar. She had watched six organists come and go before Marcus, and Marcus for twenty-five years, and she was now facing, she said, the question of what she would recommend to the Chapter for an interim appointment. "Someone who won't try to change too much too quickly," she said. "The choir needs steadiness right now."

Helen listened. She refilled her coffee. She did not, she noted, find Rosalind's warmth unconvincing. That was the difficulty. The warmth was not a performance. It was real. Helen had been a forensic pathologist for thirty-five years, and she had looked at real and performed grief in a dozen coroner's courts, and she knew the difference. What she was sitting across from was something more complicated than a performance. It was a person who was entirely real in this kitchen at this moment, who had also done what she had done.

After Rosalind left — touching Helen's arm at the door, the same dry, brief contact she had offered in the first days of the investigation, saying "Thank you for having me, again" — Helen washed the soup bowls and stood at her kitchen window with the cloth in her hands and looked at nothing in particular.

She thought about what David had said, the time she'd come home from the Pellerine case: the family had been devastated, the killer had been a man everyone in the village considered a good person. She had told him this and he had been quiet for some time. *The trouble with understanding why someone did something is that it doesn't change what they did.* She had written it in her notebook. She had looked at it often in the years since.

She thought about the care home. She thought about twelve years of small payments, made every month without fail, for a woman with dementia who could not know they were being paid, and who died three lines in the Bristol Evening Post. She thought about what Rosalind had said about her mother in this same kitchen, over shortbread: *long and mannerless death*. She thought about the moment — one afternoon, one decision — when the letters from Marcus had stopped being something she could manage and started being something she could not.

She is a good person who did a terrible thing. That is not a contradiction. That is the hardest kind of case.
