# CHAPTER NINE
## Murder

Claire rang Helen at twenty-past nine on the third morning.

Helen took the call standing at her kitchen window, with the second cup of tea of the morning in her left hand and the cold of the flagstone going up through her slippers, and she registered Claire's voice and then the very small change in Claire's voice — a flattening, a pre-formal flattening — and she set the cup down before Claire said the next sentence, because she knew what the sentence was.

"Helen. The preliminary toxicology came in."

"Yes."

"It's digoxin. The level is — it is well above his prescribed dose. His prescription, on his own bottles, was point-one-two-five milligrams a day. The blood level is consistent with several times that. I don't have the final figures. I have the shape."

"It is murder."

"It is."

There was a silence.

"Helen."

"Yes."

"I'd like you to come down to the cathedral. We've found something else, in the meantime. The forensic team did a sweep of the back of the cathedral. The crypt corridor and the archive corridor. A bottle has come up. I'd like you to look at it before they bag it."

"I'm coming now."

"Helen."

"Yes."

"Wear your coat. It is colder than it has been all week."

She put the phone down. She stood at the kitchen window and finished the tea. She did not, as she sometimes did at the kitchen window, look at the cathedral. She looked at the gravel of the path and the stone of the wall and the small steady fact of a robin on the trough.

She was, she registered, a great distance from upset. She was, she registered, very level indeed. She had been like this in the early days of the coroner's office, when the work had been new and large; she had been like this at David's funeral; she had been like this on the morning Julian had told her, at twenty-three, that he was leaving the country and she had said, *all right*, and put the kettle on.

She found her coat.

She stepped out into the cold of a Wychford April morning that was, as Claire had said, sharper than it had been; the sky was a hard pale blue, and the daffodils on the lawn looked yellow in the way things look yellow when one is paying attention.

—

The archive corridor was the long stone passage that ran behind the chancel and gave onto the cellar stair. Helen had walked it three afternoons a week for three years as a volunteer. She knew every flagstone of it by foot, in the particular way one comes to know the flagstones of a place where one is permitted to be useful.

Claire was at the top of the cellar stair with two of her people, and a small folding table had been set up under the archive window. On the folding table was a transparent evidence bag, and inside the bag was a small brown plastic pharmacy bottle. The label faced up.

Helen approached. She did not touch.

"Marcus Vayne," she read aloud. "Wychford Pharmacy. Digoxin point-one-two-five milligrams. Take one tablet daily. Twenty-eighth of January."

"His prescription," Claire said.

"His prescription."

"Found behind a row of leather bound parish registers. Eighteen-thirties. The bottle is —" Claire glanced at her sergeant, who nodded, "— substantively empty. Two tablets remaining. The cap was loose."

Helen looked at it.

"The lab will tell us," Claire said, "in due course, whether the pills in the bottle came from the same batch as the digoxin in his blood. They will also tell us whether the bottle has prints. They will also tell us — they will tell us a great many things."

"Yes," Helen said.

She looked at the bottle in its bag and felt, somewhere distantly down at the level of the floor, a small tremor pass through her hands. She had not been expecting this. She had not, in fact, expected anything quite as solid as this for several days.

She closed her hands round each other at her front.

"Do you want a moment, Helen."

"No."

"Are you sure."

"Yes."

Claire nodded.

"Who," Helen said, after a moment, "has access to this corridor."

"In a working sense — anyone with a Song School key. The corridor connects to the Song School at the end. There is also internal access from the cathedral office. So, technically, anyone with a cathedral office key. We are mapping it."

"A Song School key."

"Yes."

Helen turned the phrase over in her hand without speaking.

"Helen."

"Yes."

"Walk with me to the office. I want to walk."

—

They walked the cloister.

The cloister at half past nine was empty, and the colour of the light coming through the bays was the very pale yellow of a cold morning. Their footsteps doubled in the vault. Claire stopped halfway along, in the bay where the lay-clerk's bench was, and put her hands behind her back, and looked at the lawn.

"Helen."

"Yes."

"I am going to ask you a question. I am going to ask it as a friend, before I ask it as anything else."

"Go on."

"Do you know."

Helen waited a moment.

"I am being asked," she said, "by my friend, whether I know who killed Marcus Vayne."

"You are."

"My friend is asking that question because she has worked with me for six years and she has noticed that I have, since the morning of the body, become quieter in a particular way."

"Yes," Claire said.

"I do not know," Helen said.

"All right."

"I have a number of small things in my possession. They are not any of them, by themselves, anything. They may be a pattern. I am not prepared to make them a pattern by saying them out loud yet."

"Helen."

"Yes."

"You will tell me when you can."

"I will tell you when I can."

Claire nodded once. She did not press. Helen registered the discipline of the not-pressing, and the cost of it, and the fact that Claire had probably rehearsed both the question and the not-pressing on the way in this morning. She thought: *you have grown into yourself, Claire*. She did not say it.

"Walk on," Claire said.

They walked on.

—

The memorial service, hastily arranged, was at midday in the lady chapel. Father Reeve led it because the Dean had said, with what was on reflection a small political tact, that she would prefer to sit in the body of the chapel like the rest of the cathedral.

Helen sat at the back. Edmund sat beside her. Neither of them had needed to discuss this.

The chapel was full. Half the close was there. Most of the choir was there. Webb was there, beside Anne, and Edmund had registered, when they came in, that Anne was holding Webb's hand in a way she did not, that Helen could remember, ordinarily hold it.

Rosalind was at the front.

She was at the lectern, in the place she would stand to read the lesson at evensong — pale blue cardigan over a charcoal dress, her hair drawn back, her hands folded in front of her on the lectern's wooden ledge. Father Reeve had asked her to read. She had agreed. She had said, Helen knew, at the meeting in the morning, that it was the only thing she could think to do that would be of use.

She read from First Corinthians.

She read it well.

She read with the faintly clipped, exact diction she used when she conducted; she read as one would read for a person who had cared, more than perhaps anyone, about how things were sounded. She did not speed up or slow down. She did not crack. Her hands lay still on the lectern.

"And now abide faith, hope, love, these three; but the greatest of these is love."

She closed the book.

She stood for one moment longer at the lectern, looking at the cover of the lectionary, and then she lifted her face, and she did not look at the congregation, and a single tear came down the side of her nose, and she did not, with any sleight or apology, wipe it away. She stepped down. She walked to her stall. She sat.

Helen watched her cry.

The crying was not loud. The crying was decorous, and exact, and contained, and continuous. Rosalind cried in her stall for the rest of the service, with her hands folded in her lap, and she did not move, and she did not look at anyone, and Helen, three rows back, watched her do it.

Helen registered the watching. Helen registered, with a flat clean precision that was its own form of pain, that she could not tell, and would not be able to tell on the basis of the watching, whether what Rosalind was crying for was Marcus, or for what she had done to Marcus, or for what Marcus had been about to do to her, or for all three at once.

She thought: *the close has changed*.

She thought: *it changed at twenty-past nine this morning*.

The bell rang the hour above them. Father Reeve gave the blessing. The cathedral went out into the cloister into the cold, and the close stood about on the grass in small, low-voiced groups, and the word *murder* went round it like a draught under a door.
