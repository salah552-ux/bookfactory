It's worth saying plainly what you changed in seven days, because it is more than behaviour. You altered your gut environment. Three days of reduced fermentable load shifted your bacterial population — the gut responds to dietary change within roughly a day and consolidates it over three to four (David et al., *Nature*, 2014). Less fermentation meant less gas, less stretch on the gut wall, lower background reactivity. Four minutes a day of slow-exhale breathing nudged your autonomic nervous system toward its calming branch, an effect demonstrated in controlled research (Balban et al., *Cell Reports Medicine*, 2023). This is not a metaphor and it is not willpower. It is a real, early, physiological change in how the system runs.

Early is the honest word. Seven days changes the trajectory; it does not finish the work. The gut lining renews continuously, the bacterial population keeps responding to what you feed it, and a stress pattern built over years keeps retraining well past day seven. What you have is not a cured gut. What you have is a gut that is moving in the right direction, and — more importantly — a map for keeping it there.

The seven days are finished. The knowledge isn't. The trigger profile you named on Day 2, the tolerables list you started on Day 6, the reset meals you found on Day 3 — those belong to you now. They don't expire when the book ends.

Hold onto that. Because the next time your gut tells you something is off, you won't start from zero. You'll go back to the map.
