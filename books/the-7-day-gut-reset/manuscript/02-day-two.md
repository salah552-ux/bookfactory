# Day Two
## Reading the Log — Three Trigger Profiles

You have 24 hours of data now. Let's read it.

Pull out your log — notebook, phone note, whatever you used. Spend ten minutes with what you wrote yesterday before you do anything else. You're not looking for a verdict. You're looking for a pattern. And the pattern you find today will tell you more about your gut than any protocol that starts with a list of foods to eliminate.

There are three profiles. Most people recognise themselves in one clearly, by the end of this chapter. Read each one slowly. Then go back to your log.

Before you do, one idea to hold onto. These three profiles are not three different diseases. They are three different *mechanisms* — three different things that can go wrong in the same digestive system. That distinction matters, because a mechanism can be worked with. Each profile points to a specific part of the gut behaving in a specific way, and once you can name which part, you can stop treating your gut as a single mysterious organ and start treating the actual problem.

---

## Profile 1: Food-Triggered

Your log shows symptoms that follow meals — not always, but consistently when certain types of food are involved. The timing is the tell. Symptoms appear within roughly twenty minutes to ninety minutes of eating, and they correlate with what was on the plate more than with the stress score logged at that time.

Consider someone who has a calm, unhurried lunch — stress score: 2 — and still spends the afternoon bloated. Then notice three meals in the log where the stress score was 7 or 8 but symptoms were absent. The food column tells the story the stress column doesn't. Certain categories — highly fermentable foods, very fatty meals, heavily processed items — produce a stronger response every time.

Here's what's physically happening. Certain carbohydrates aren't fully absorbed in the small intestine. They travel onward to the large intestine, where the bacteria that live there ferment them — and fermentation produces gas. That gas stretches the gut wall. In a sensitive gut, that stretch registers as bloating, pressure, and pain at a level a less reactive gut wouldn't notice. The twenty-to-ninety-minute delay is simply the transit time: how long the food takes to reach the bacteria that react to it. Your symptom isn't random. It's a fermentation timeline.

If your log looks like this, your primary driver is likely food-triggered. The Calm Plate for you focuses on temporarily reducing the food categories most likely to create symptom load.

## Profile 2: Stress-Triggered

Your log doesn't show a clear food pattern. It shows something else: the hours when your stress score was highest are the same hours when your gut symptoms were strongest — regardless of what you ate.

Think about someone who eats an ordinary breakfast at a stress level of 3 and feels fine, then eats nearly the same meal before a difficult meeting at a stress level of 8 and spends two hours with cramping and urgency. The food didn't change. The stress did.

This is the gut-brain axis at work, and it's not a metaphor. Your gut and your brain are wired together by a direct neural pathway. When the brain registers stress, it activates a hormonal cascade — the HPA axis — that releases cortisol and other stress chemicals into the body. Those chemicals act directly on the gut. Research using corticotropin-releasing hormone, the signal that starts the stress cascade, has shown that people with IBS-type symptoms tend to produce a larger stress-hormone response than people without them, and that this hormone increases gut motility and changes how the gut wall behaves (study on brain and gut responses to corticotropin-releasing hormone in IBS, *Scientific Reports*, 2017). Sustained cortisol is known to alter gut motility, the permeability of the gut lining, and the gut's immune activity. The gut doesn't distinguish between a genuine physical threat and a difficult Tuesday afternoon. It responds to the chemical signal the nervous system sends.

If symptoms track stress scores more reliably than food in your log, your primary driver is likely stress-triggered. The Stress Bridge on Day 5 is the most important part of your protocol.

## Profile 3: Motility-Triggered

Your log doesn't show a clear food pattern and doesn't show a clear stress pattern. It shows something more diffuse: symptoms at irregular intervals, not clearly event-linked, better described as a chronic background.

Think about a gut that's reliably slow — bloating that builds through the day, stools that are infrequent, a fullness that persists long after eating. Or the opposite: urgency that arrives unpredictably, inconsistent stools, a gut that moves things through faster than feels manageable. In both cases, the log doesn't point to a specific meal or stressful moment. It points to pace.

Motility is the speed at which your gut moves food through the digestive tract — the rhythmic muscular contractions that push everything along. Too slow: contents linger, ferment longer, and produce bloating, sluggishness, and constipation. Too fast: contents move through before water and nutrients are properly reabsorbed, producing urgency and loose stools. The problem isn't what you're eating or feeling. It's the rate. The Calm Plate for you focuses on fibre type and fluid timing rather than elimination.

## Reading Your Own Log

Ask three questions.

First: do symptoms follow meals within roughly ninety minutes, and does the food column correlate with symptom intensity more than the stress column does? If yes — food-triggered pattern.

Second: do symptoms track stress scores more than food choices? If a high stress score consistently precedes a gut episode regardless of what was eaten — stress-triggered pattern.

Third: are symptoms diffuse, irregular, and not clearly linked to food or stress? If the log looks like background noise rather than event-triggered spikes — motility is the most likely primary driver.

Most people are primarily one profile with elements of another. Focus on the dominant pattern. This is a working hypothesis, not a medical diagnosis.

One more thing worth knowing, because it explains a lot of frustrating histories. Gut symptoms sometimes begin after a single, identifiable event — a bout of food poisoning, a stomach bug. A meta-analysis of cohort studies found that roughly one in ten people develop ongoing IBS-type symptoms after an episode of infectious gastroenteritis (Thabane and Marshall, pooled analysis of post-infectious IBS). For those readers, the gut genuinely was working fine, and then it wasn't — which makes "it's probably just stress" land especially badly. If your symptoms started after an infection, your log may point to motility, because that's often the part of the system a past infection disrupts.

Here's what your doctor probably didn't tell you. The reason previous advice failed you isn't that the advice was wrong — it's that it was aimed at the wrong mechanism. A food-elimination diet is a precise tool, and it does very little for a stress-triggered gut. A breathing practice is a precise tool, and it does very little for a fermentation problem driven by food. When a protocol "doesn't work for you," that is not a verdict on your gut or your willpower. It is a sign the protocol was built for a different mechanism than yours. Nobody told you there were different mechanisms. So when the protocol missed, you had nowhere to put the failure except onto yourself.

> **Take this to your doctor**
>
> If you want to raise your trigger profile with a GP or gastroenterologist, frame it as an observation, not a self-diagnosis. Say: "I've tracked a week of meals and symptoms. The pattern I'm seeing is [symptoms consistently follow specific foods within about an hour / symptoms track my stress levels rather than my meals / symptoms are irregular and not clearly linked to either]. Based on that, I think the main driver may be [food sensitivity / a stress-gut response / a motility problem]. Does that match what you'd expect, and is there a test or referral that would help confirm or rule it out?" This gives the clinician a structured starting point instead of an open-ended complaint — and it signals you've done observable work, not guesswork.

You now know something no generic gut health book has ever asked you to know: which type of gut problem is actually yours.

If your symptoms feel worse before they feel better over the next few days, that is not a bad sign. A gut that has been compensating for a long time often registers the change when the background load lifts — a kind of recalibration. Rebound symptoms in the first two or three days of a reset are common and frequently precede improvement, not the reverse.

That identification changes what every day from here does. Not because the protocol is complicated — but because it was built for the answer you just found, not for an imaginary average gut.
