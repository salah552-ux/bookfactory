# Day Seven
## Your Personal Maintenance Map

Seven days ago you had a gut that wasn't responding. Today you have a name for why, a protocol calibrated to that reason, and data you built yourself. That gap is not small.

You know your trigger profile. You've followed a protocol built for it. You've started reintroduction. That is more actionable information about your own digestive system than most people accumulate in years of trial and error.

Now you build the map.

Before you do, it's worth being clear about what actually changed this week — because it is more than a set of habits. In seven days you have measurably altered your gut environment. The bacterial population shifted in response to three days of reduced fermentable load; gut bacteria respond to dietary change within roughly a day, and the change consolidates over three to four (David et al., *Nature*, 2014). Lower fermentation meant less gas, less stretch on the gut wall, and a calmer baseline reactivity. The daily breathing practice has been nudging your autonomic nervous system toward parasympathetic — calming — dominance, and slow-exhale breathing has demonstrated measurable effects on physiological arousal (Balban et al., *Cell Reports Medicine*, 2023). This is not "developing good habits." It is a real, if early, change in how the system runs.

It is early, though — and that honesty matters. The gut lining renews continuously, the bacterial population keeps responding to what you feed it, and a nervous system pattern built over years takes more than a week to fully retrain. Seven days is enough to change the trajectory and to give you a readable map. It is not enough to call anything finished. What makes an improvement hold, rather than fade, is not intensity — it's consistency in a few specific places. The map is how you keep those few places consistent without living inside a protocol.

## What the Map Is

The Personal Maintenance Map is a one-page reference you create from your week's data. Not a template you fill in from scratch — a set of prompts you answer from your own log. A notebook page or a phone note is enough. It has seven fields.

**1. My trigger profile.** Write one word: Food-triggered, Stress-triggered, or Motility-triggered. If you found a dominant pattern with a secondary element, note both: "Primarily food-triggered, with stress as a secondary amplifier."

**2. My confirmed tolerables.** The food categories — or eating contexts, if you're stress-triggered — you reintroduced on Day 6 without a significant response. These go back on your plate. You're no longer uncertain about them.

**3. My confirmed sensitivities.** The categories or contexts that produced a clear response: symptom intensity up three or more points within 48 hours. Write them down with one note: "approach with care." Not "never again." The gut changes over time. These findings are worth revisiting in 8–12 weeks, not treating as a permanent verdict.

**4. My Calm Plate defaults.** Two or three meal formats from Days 3–5 that reliably produced fewer symptoms. These become your reset meals — the default choices you reach for when symptoms start building again. You don't follow the Calm Plate permanently. You reach for it for a day or two when you need to reset, because you already know it works.

**5. My Stress Bridge.** The time of day that worked best for the practice this week — morning, afternoon, or both — and the duration: four minutes. If one component was more effective than the other, note that too. Make it yours.

**6. My warning signals.** Look back through your log. What was the first symptom to appear before a more significant episode? Mild bloating two hours after lunch? Low-grade urgency in the morning? That first signal is your early warning. When you notice it, a one-day Calm Plate reset is usually enough to prevent it from escalating.

**7. When to go further.** If symptoms remained complex or severe across the entire week — if the trigger profile didn't become visible, or if the Calm Plate produced no reduction — this reset isn't the right tool in isolation. The next step is a gut health specialist, your GP, or the full-length series guide, *Fix Your Gut for Good*, which goes deeper into the mechanisms and long-term repair of gut function. Be honest with yourself here. This book has a specific scope.

## How the Map Maps Onto Your Gut

Each field of the map corresponds to a real part of the system you've been working with this week.

Your trigger profile names the dominant mechanism — fermentation, the gut-brain axis, or motility. Your tolerables and sensitivities are a record of how your gut's bacterial population responds to specific fuels. Your Calm Plate defaults are a known way to drop fermentable load fast, which is why a one-day reset can interrupt a building flare. Your Stress Bridge is a direct lever on the autonomic nervous system. And your warning signals are your gut telling you, early, that one of those systems is drifting — while a small correction is still enough.

That early-warning point is the one that decides whether improvements hold. A gut flare is far easier to interrupt at a symptom intensity of 2 than at an 8. The reader who catches the first signal and reaches for a one-day reset stays in control. The reader who waits until a 7 is back to firefighting. The map's job is to make you the first reader.

Here's what your doctor probably didn't tell you. Gut healing is not a finish line — it is a slope you can keep walking up or slide back down, and which direction you go depends on a handful of recurring inputs: the fermentable load you carry day to day, the diversity of what feeds your bacteria, and how regulated your stress response stays. Nobody framed it that way for you. You were given a protocol and, implicitly, the idea that you'd either be "fixed" or not. So when symptoms came back, it felt like failure. It wasn't. It was a slope behaving like a slope. The map exists because the work is ongoing by nature — and ongoing work needs a reference you can actually live with, not a regime you'll abandon.

> **Take this to your doctor**
>
> At a routine appointment, you can fold gut health into the conversation rather than waiting for a crisis. Say: "I manage a long-term digestive pattern and I keep a symptom log. I'd like gut health to be something we review periodically. Specifically — given my history, are there baseline checks worth repeating from time to time, such as ferritin and iron studies, coeliac screening, or inflammatory markers? And at what point would my symptom pattern justify a gastroenterology referral?" This turns gut health into something monitored on a schedule, not something raised only when it becomes unbearable. Your clinician sets the actual intervals — but asking puts it on the record.

## What the Map Does

The map runs indefinitely. Not as a diet to follow, but as a reference to return to. When symptoms flare after a period of irregular eating, travel, or high stress, you open the map, reach for a Calm Plate default, and do the Stress Bridge. When something new appears in your gut's behaviour, you log it the way you did on Day 1.

The difference between where you started and where you are now isn't that your gut is cured. It's that you have a framework. You know which question to ask. You know how to find the answer. You built the tool you'll use to answer it — from your own data, not from someone else's protocol.

The map you've just built belongs to you — not to this book, not to the seven days. Take it with you.
