# Day Four
## The Repair Kit

Restriction was yesterday's work. Today you add — and adding feels different to most people than taking away does.

The Calm Plate continues. These five additions run alongside it, not instead of it. They are not a substitute for reducing your specific trigger — they are tools your gut recognises as support, and introducing them during the calm-down phase means the gut is better positioned to use them.

None of these require a trip to a specialist shop. None of them carry a brand name. They are categories — widely available, ordinarily priced — and your job is simply to add them deliberately rather than waiting to see if your gut improves on its own.

First, a quick word on what "support" actually means here, because the wellness industry has made the phrase nearly meaningless. The lining of your gut is a single layer of cells, renewed constantly, and it does two jobs at once: it lets nutrients through and it keeps everything else out. The cells of that lining have specific fuel needs and the gaps between them — the tight junctions — can loosen under stress, inflammation, and a disrupted bacterial population. "Support" means giving that lining the conditions it needs to do its two jobs well. It does not mean a miracle repair. Some of what follows has solid evidence; some has promising but preliminary evidence. Where that's the case, this chapter says so — because the gap between "shown to help" and "plausibly helps" is exactly the gap most gut books paper over.

---

**1. Fermented foods — a small amount**

Plain live-culture yoghurt, kefir, or fermented vegetables — a tablespoon or two of sauerkraut or kimchi — introduce a diverse population of live bacteria through food rather than through a supplement. The reasoning is straightforward: a more diverse bacterial population is generally a more stable one, and food is a low-cost way to widen the input. Small portions matter. If you're food-triggered, fermented foods may temporarily increase bloating as your gut adjusts to the bacterial influx — and because fermented foods can themselves be higher in fermentable compounds. Start with a tablespoon at a meal and observe. If symptoms increase, reduce or skip for now and return to it after the reset. If you tolerate it, keep it in.

For stress-triggered and motility-triggered readers, fermented foods are generally well-tolerated and worth including from today.

**2. Bone broth or slow-cooked stock**

A cup of bone broth or a slow-cooked stock — homemade or a plain, low-sodium version — is easily digestible, warm, and traditionally used across cultures for gut comfort. Here is the honest state of the evidence. Bone broth contains gelatine and the amino acids glycine and glutamine. Glutamine is a genuine fuel source for the cells that line the gut, and in animal studies gelatine-derived peptides have shown anti-inflammatory effects and a positive impact on tight-junction proteins (animal-model research on gelatine peptides and the intestinal barrier, summarised in recent reviews of bone broth and gut-barrier function). What there is *not* is strong human evidence that drinking broth directly "repairs" or "seals" the gut lining — that claim runs ahead of the data. So include broth because it is gentle, warm, hydrating, and easy to digest during a reset. Treat the deeper repair claims as plausible but unproven. Don't overthink it: a mug in the afternoon or alongside a meal is enough.

**3. Soluble fibre from food**

Oats, cooked root vegetables, and plain psyllium husk (if you tolerate it) feed the beneficial bacterial population in your gut. This matters because those bacteria, when fed soluble fibre, produce short-chain fatty acids — including butyrate, the preferred fuel of the cells lining the large intestine. In other words, the right fibre doesn't just pass through; it gets converted by your own bacteria into fuel for your own gut lining. The bacterial community needs fibre to do this — cutting all fibre during a reset is not the goal. The goal is choosing the type your gut handles more easily right now: soluble, not insoluble. Increase it gradually if you're motility-triggered.

**4. Water timing**

This isn't about drinking more water. It's about when you drink it relative to meals. Many people find that spacing their main fluid intake away from meals — roughly thirty minutes before eating or sixty minutes after — produces more comfortable digestion. The mechanism is modest and worth stating plainly: very large volumes of fluid taken with a meal can leave the stomach feeling overfull and may briefly dilute digestive secretions. This is a comfort adjustment, not a cure, and the evidence is more practical than clinical. Try it and notice whether it makes a difference for you. It's a small behavioural change with no cost.

**5. A short walk after your two largest meals**

Ten to fifteen minutes of gentle walking after lunch and dinner is the best-evidenced item in this kit. Light post-meal movement has been shown to improve gastric emptying and reduce the post-meal glucose response compared with sitting (research on postprandial walking and gastric emptying, including data published in the *American Journal of Gastroenterology* and later trials in *Nutrients*). For the gut specifically, gentle walking gives the digestive system a physical prompt to move contents along — useful for anyone, and particularly useful for motility-triggered readers whose gut runs slow. You don't need to raise your heart rate. A calm, unhurried walk — around the block, along a corridor — is enough.

---

For some readers, Day 4 is the day things feel worse, not better — and that needs saying directly, because an unexplained dip here is where people quit. There are two common reasons. One: you've added several new inputs at once, and a sensitive gut is reacting to the additions, not failing the reset. Two: a gut that has been compensating for a long time can register the change in load as it recalibrates. Either way, a temporary increase in symptoms in the first days of a reset is common and is not evidence the reset isn't working. If something specific you added clearly worsens things, remove that one thing and note it. You're still in diagnostic mode.

Here's what your doctor probably didn't tell you. The supplement aisle sells gut "repair" in a bottle — glutamine powders, collagen, branded probiotics — and a lot of it is built on exactly the animal-study evidence described above, stretched into a human promise it hasn't earned. You were never told that distinction. You were left to assume that if it's sold for gut health, it's proven for gut health. Most of it isn't. The genuinely well-evidenced items in this chapter are the cheapest and least glamorous: the right fibre, and a short walk. That is not a coincidence. It's what the evidence actually supports.

> **Take this to your doctor**
>
> If your symptoms include anything that warrants investigation, ask for specifics rather than a general "gut health" conversation. Say: "I've had persistent digestive symptoms. Could we check the basics with blood tests — a full blood count, ferritin and iron studies, coeliac screening, and inflammatory markers such as CRP? And is a stool calprotectin test appropriate to help distinguish inflammation from a non-inflammatory cause?" Naming these tests turns a vague appointment into a concrete one. Your clinician decides what's warranted — but asking by name makes it far harder for the conversation to end at "it's probably IBS."

Keep the log going today. The Repair Kit may produce a gradual quieting — less symptom intensity, slower onset, or fewer events.

Tomorrow introduces the Stress Bridge — the element most gut health books mention but never give you something specific to do with.

Five additions. No products to buy. No prescriptions. Just inputs your gut recognises as support.
