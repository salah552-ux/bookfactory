# Day Six
## Reintroduction

Everything you reduced on Day 3 is not banned forever. Today you start finding out what you can bring back — and what your gut is actually asking you to avoid.

This is the step every elimination-based protocol skips. Most guides tell you what to remove and then leave you there, indefinitely restricted, with no mechanism for finding out what you can actually tolerate. That's not a reset. That's a permanent diet with no exit. Reintroduction done systematically converts a temporary reset into real, lasting knowledge about your own gut — knowledge that belongs to you permanently, not to the protocol.

The principle is simple: one food category at a time.

Reintroducing everything at once — all the foods you reduced in one day — is the most common mistake. It produces a symptom spike with no identifiable cause, which leaves you back at the beginning. One category, one test, one observation window.

Here's what's actually happening inside your gut when you reintroduce a food, because the mechanism tells you how to read the result. After three days of reduced fermentable load, your gut has a quieter baseline: less gas, less stretch on the gut wall, lower background reactivity. When you reintroduce a single food category, you reload one specific input into that quiet system. If that food is highly fermentable, the bacteria in your large intestine ferment it and produce gas — and because the baseline is now quiet, the response stands out clearly instead of being lost in constant noise. That clarity is the entire reason the three-day reduction came first. You weren't depriving yourself. You were building a clean test.

This is exactly how the structured reintroduction phase of a clinical low-fermentation diet works. Each food group is challenged on its own, over roughly three days, while the rest of the diet stays low-fermentation as a stable background — the method registered dietitians and the Monash University FODMAP framework use, and the method tested in a blinded reintroduction study published in *Gastroenterology* in 2024. This book borrows that structure in a shorter, simpler form. The principle is identical: change one variable, hold everything else steady, and the result becomes readable.

It also helps to know the difference between two things that get confused. A food *intolerance* is, broadly, a digestive or fermentation problem — the food isn't fully broken down or absorbed, and the result is gas, bloating, or altered bowel habit, usually dose-related (a little is fine, a lot is not). A food *allergy* is an immune-system reaction, can be triggered by tiny amounts, and can be serious. Reintroduction in this book tests for the first kind — tolerance and digestive response. It is not an allergy test. If you have ever had a rapid, severe, or breathing-related reaction to a food, do not test that food here; that belongs with a doctor.

## How Reintroduction Works

**Step 1.** Choose one food category to test today. Start with the one least likely to produce a strong response — this builds confidence and gives you a clear baseline. For most people following a food-triggered protocol: mildly fermentable vegetables come first (something like regular broccoli or onion in a small amount), then dairy, then wheat-based products. Test the categories you most suspect last. Sequence matters — early, low-risk wins teach you to read your own responses before you reach the harder tests.

**Step 2.** Eat a normal portion of that food category at one meal today. Not a large test portion — a normal portion, the size you'd ordinarily eat. Continue the Calm Plate at your other meals today and tomorrow.

**Step 3.** Log the response over the next 48 hours using the same five columns from Day 1. The gut's response to a new input can take up to 24 hours to fully manifest. Forty-eight hours gives you a clear observation window with no ambiguity.

**Step 4.** At 48 hours, ask one question: is my symptom intensity 3 or more points higher on the 1–10 scale than it was during Days 3 through 5?

**If yes:** This food category is telling you something. It doesn't mean it's a permanent trigger — gut responses shift over time — but for now, flag it in your log as "approach with care" and don't reintroduce it again during this reset week.

**If no:** This food category is likely tolerable for your gut right now. Note it as a "tolerates" finding. It goes back on your plate.

One reading tip the mechanism gives you for free. Symptoms that appear within a few hours and centre on gas, bloating, and pressure point toward a fermentation response — an intolerance-type pattern. Symptoms that are slower, or involve more than the gut, are worth noting separately and raising with a clinician rather than self-interpreting.

## For Stress-Triggered Readers

Reintroduction for you isn't about food categories. It's about eating contexts.

The contexts you may have reduced or avoided during Days 3–5 — eating while working, eating socially in a loud or pressured environment, eating quickly between commitments — those are what you test now. One context per 48-hour window, using the same response scale. If eating at your desk while working produces symptoms that eating at a table without your phone doesn't, that's your data.

## On Reacting Badly

A reaction during reintroduction is not a failure. It's the most useful information the reset can produce.

If you eat something and your gut responds strongly, that's not a crisis and it's not a setback. Stop the reintroduction for that category, note the finding, and continue the Calm Plate. A single meal of something your gut doesn't tolerate right now doesn't harm you. It informs you.

The anxiety about reintroduction — "what if I react?" — usually dissolves once you remember that you're collecting information, not making permanent decisions. One meal, one observation, one data point. That's all reintroduction day is.

Here's what your doctor probably didn't tell you. If you've ever tried an elimination diet, you were almost certainly told what to take out — and almost never given a structured way to put it back. That is not a small omission. It is the omission that turns a diagnostic tool into a slowly shrinking diet. Without a reintroduction method, every food you remove "to be safe" stays removed, because you have no way to clear it. People end up eating fifteen foods, malnourished and anxious, having been handed only the first half of a two-part process. Reintroduction is the half you were missing. It is how restriction ends.

> **Take this to your doctor or dietitian**
>
> If you want to take your reintroduction findings to a professional, bring the data in structured form. Say: "I completed a short low-fermentation period and then reintroduced foods one category at a time, logging symptom type and intensity over 48 hours for each. These categories produced a clear response: [list]. These did not: [list]. I'd like help interpreting this and building a sustainable long-term diet that isn't more restrictive than it needs to be." A registered dietitian is the right professional for this. Presenting it as one-variable-at-a-time data — not "I think I'm intolerant to everything" — gets you a far more useful consultation.

Formal reintroduction continues beyond Day 6 at your own pace. The seven-day reset provides the framework. The process belongs to you indefinitely.

By the end of Day 7 you'll have begun to build the list that matters most — not what everyone else's gut reacts to, but what yours does.
