# Day One
## The 24-Hour Trigger Log

Today you observe. That's it. You don't change anything.

You don't cut anything out, add anything in, or try to make today a "good gut day." You eat whatever you'd normally eat. You go through your normal day. The only difference is that you write things down — specifically, five things — within about fifteen to thirty minutes of each meal.

That window matters. Logging from memory at 9pm means losing the detail that makes this exercise useful. Symptom timing is the diagnostic signal. When you capture it live, you capture the pattern. When you reconstruct it from memory, you capture a rough sketch at best.

Here's the part most people skip past, and it's the reason this works. Your gut sends its signal in the gap between eating and feeling something. That gap — twenty minutes, ninety minutes, six hours — is not noise. It is the single most informative number your body produces. A food sensitivity, a stress response, and a motility problem each leave a different fingerprint in that gap. But the fingerprint only exists if you record the start time and the symptom time separately. Lose the timing and you lose the diagnosis.

This is not a wellness habit. It is data collection. And written tracking genuinely outperforms memory for one concrete reason: your brain edits. It rates the meal you remember as "bad" and quietly drops the three earlier meals that were fine. It links the symptom to the food you already suspect and ignores the one you don't. A written log can't do that. It records what happened, in order, including the days that don't fit your theory — and those are the days that break the case open.

The research backs this up. Surveys collated by the International Foundation for Gastrointestinal Disorders find that up to 65 per cent of people with IBS-type symptoms report food intolerances — yet most can't reliably name which foods. When researchers ran statistical analysis on detailed food-and-symptom journals from people with chronic digestive problems, the journals revealed a unique set of trigger foods for most participants, and using those findings to guide a personalised approach reduced symptoms for around 75 per cent of them (research on food-symptom journal analysis, summarised in clinical literature on IBS trigger identification). The journal worked where guessing had not. That is the entire premise of today.

The trigger log has five columns. You can draw this on a piece of paper, open a note on your phone, or use a basic spreadsheet — whatever you'll actually do. No app required.

---

**Column 1 — Time**

The time you started eating. Not when you finished, not when you felt something — when the meal or snack began. Precision here is what gives you the data you need tomorrow.

**Column 2 — Food or drink consumed**

What you ate and drank. Keep it brief but specific: "coffee with milk, two slices of toast with butter and peanut butter" is useful. "Breakfast" is not. You don't need nutritional details. You need enough information to recognise food categories tomorrow when you're reading the log.

**Column 3 — Stress level (1–10)**

Rate how you feel emotionally and physically in the window around the meal — not globally for the day, but right then. A 1 is calm and rested. A 10 is acute stress or anxiety. A 5 is the background hum of a busy, slightly pressured afternoon. Be honest. This column is the data that distinguishes a food-triggered pattern from a stress-triggered one, and it only works if you're accurate.

**Column 4 — Symptom type**

Describe what you feel, in plain language: bloating, reflux, urgency, a dull ache, sluggishness, cramping, loose stools, constipation, or nothing at all. Log symptoms even when they're mild. A 2 out of 10 symptom that appears after every meal containing a particular food category is a pattern. "Nothing dramatic" is also worth writing — absence of symptoms is data too.

**Column 5 — Symptom intensity (1–10)**

Rate the intensity of any symptom at the point you notice it. A 1 is barely noticeable. A 10 is the kind of discomfort that changes your plans. Log this alongside the time so you capture how long after eating the symptom appeared.

---

That's the complete log. Five columns. It takes about two minutes to fill in after each meal.

A few things to keep in mind as you do this. First: you're not looking for anything today. That's tomorrow's job. Today you're collecting raw data, which means your observations are most useful when they're neutral. Don't pre-interpret. Don't decide that a symptom was definitely because of the cheese or definitely not because of the coffee. Just write what happened and when.

Second: log snacks too. A symptom that appears two hours after a snack, not a meal, is still a symptom. Some of the clearest patterns appear in the gaps between meals, not during them.

Third: every body has bad days. One log entry that looks alarming doesn't mean much on its own. You're looking for patterns across the full 24 hours, not a verdict from a single meal. One data point is noise. A pattern across six or eight entries is signal.

One more thing: if you already know one or two foods that always cause problems, eat them today anyway. The point is baseline data in your normal life — not a best-case snapshot. A log that reflects your actual eating is more useful than a carefully curated one. The trigger log works because it captures your real gut behaviour, not your aspirational gut behaviour.

Here's what your doctor probably didn't tell you. When a ten-minute appointment ends with "it's probably IBS, try to manage your stress," what's missing isn't your doctor's competence — it's time. There is no slot in a standard appointment to sit with you and map symptom timing across a week of meals. So the one tool most likely to actually identify your pattern is the one tool the system isn't built to hand you. You've been told to "watch what you eat" without ever being shown how to watch. That phrase did nothing for you because it was never an instruction. It was a shrug. The log is the instruction. It is the appointment the system didn't have time to give you, and you can run it yourself, tonight, for free.

> **Take this to your doctor**
>
> When you bring a symptom log to an appointment, don't hand over the raw pages and hope. Say this: "I've kept a structured food and symptom log. I recorded the time I ate, what I ate, my stress level, and the type and intensity of any symptom on a 1-to-10 scale. I'd like to go through the timing pattern with you." Then point to the gap: "Symptoms consistently appear [X] minutes after meals containing [category]." A specific, timed log changes the conversation from a vague complaint into reviewable data — and it is far harder to dismiss. This log is an observational record, not a diagnosis; a clinician's assessment still matters, especially if symptoms are severe or new.

Tomorrow you'll read what you logged. That's when the three trigger profiles become visible, and for the first time, you'll be looking at your gut through a framework built around what you actually experience rather than what a generic protocol assumes.

Tonight, just collect the data.

The most useful thing you'll do all week happens in the next 24 hours — and it doesn't require changing a single thing about your day.

## RESEARCH NOTES

- International Foundation for Gastrointestinal Disorders (IFFGD) — patient survey data indicating up to ~65% of people with IBS report food intolerances. Used for: the scale of unidentified food triggers.
- Research on statistical analysis of food-and-symptom journals for chronic digestive problems — identification of personalised trigger foods, with symptom reduction reported for ~75% of participants when journal findings guided dietary change. Used for: written-log effectiveness vs. guessing.
- Chey WD et al. and related clinical literature on IBS food and symptom journals — validated food-symptom diary instruments (e.g. the Food and Symptom Times diary) confirm that timed written records have construct and predictive validity for relating diet to gastrointestinal symptoms. Used for: why timed, written tracking outperforms memory.
- General gastrointestinal physiology — symptom-onset latency after eating differs by mechanism (food sensitivity, gut-brain/stress response, motility). Used for: why the time gap is the diagnostic signal. Mechanism described, not a single cited trial.
