# Day Three
## The Calm Plate

Three days of a quieter plate. That's what stands between where you are now and the first clear signal your gut has sent in months.

Before anything else: this is not a diet. The Calm Plate is a temporary eating framework for Days 3, 4, and 5. Three days. Its purpose is to reduce the specific inputs most likely to be creating symptom load for your trigger type, and give your gut enough quiet to begin responding differently. A gut in persistent reactivity benefits from a deliberate reduction in that cycle — even briefly — because it changes what's possible in the days that follow.

No food is permanently banned here. Nothing is forbidden. The language of this framework is "reduce for now" and "try not to" — not "never again." If you eat something outside the Calm Plate, log it and continue. This is not a punishment protocol.

Here's why three days is enough to matter, and why it isn't an arbitrary number. Your gut is not a fixed machine. It is a living environment, and the bacterial population inside it responds to what you feed it remarkably fast. A landmark study published in *Nature* tracked people through a sudden change in diet and found the gut bacterial community shifted measurably within a single day, with the change clearly established within three to four days (David et al., "Diet rapidly and reproducibly alters the human gut microbiome," *Nature*, 2014). Three days is enough. Three days of reduced fermentable load is enough to change what the bacteria produce, how much gas is generated, and how stretched and reactive the gut wall becomes. The Calm Plate works on the same timeline your gut actually runs on.

That is also why this is a reset and not a forever plan. You are not trying to permanently exile foods. You are turning the volume down long enough for the signal — your gut's real response — to become readable. On Day 6 you start turning it back up, deliberately, one input at a time.

## The Calm Plate for Food-Triggered Readers

Your log showed that symptoms track what you eat more than when or how stressed you are. For three days, reduce the food categories most likely to produce a strong response: legumes, certain raw fruits (apples, pears, stone fruits), wheat-based products in large quantities, and dairy if it featured near symptom spikes in your log.

These categories have something in common. They tend to be higher in fermentable carbohydrates — the carbohydrates that aren't fully absorbed in the small intestine and instead become fuel for bacterial fermentation further down. Reducing them lowers gas production, which lowers the stretch on the gut wall, which lowers the symptom. This is the same principle clinicians use when they trial a lower-fermentation diet for IBS. Across randomised trials and meta-analyses, reducing fermentable carbohydrates produces meaningful symptom relief for a large share of people with IBS-type symptoms — one elimination-phase study reported around 80 per cent of participants responding (research on low-fermentation diets for IBS, including elimination-phase responder data published in *Gut* and subsequent meta-analyses).

One important difference, though. A clinical low-fermentation diet is a long, strict, carefully staged programme — and that is exactly the kind of plan readers of this book have abandoned before. The Calm Plate borrows the principle, not the severity. Three days. Reduced, not eliminated. A reset, not a regime.

Replace the reduced categories with foods your gut processes more easily. Cooked vegetables rather than raw. Easily digestible proteins: eggs, white fish, grilled chicken. Lower-fermentable grains: white rice, oats. Bananas or blueberries if you want fruit.

A Day 3 meal might look like grilled fish with cooked courgette and white rice. Scrambled eggs with spinach. A small bowl of oats with blueberries. Simple, recognisable food that doesn't ask your gut to work harder than it needs to right now.

## The Calm Plate for Stress-Triggered Readers

Symptoms in your log tracked stress scores more than food choices. For you, the Calm Plate is less about removing ingredients and more about removing the conditions that amplify the gut's stress response.

Eat sitting down. Not at a desk, not while scrolling. The act of giving a meal your full attention is a direct instruction to your nervous system that this is a safe moment. Digestion is a parasympathetic function — it runs properly when the body is in "rest-and-digest" mode, and it is suppressed when the body is in a stress state. Eating while stressed asks the gut to do a calm job in an alarmed body. The gut's stress response often begins before the first bite, when the body is already in low-level alarm.

Avoid eating during your highest-stress windows if possible. If midday is when your cortisol is loudest, eat slightly before or after that peak. A short walk before a meal — five minutes — may help shift the nervous system toward a calmer baseline before eating.

Your food choices still matter at the margins. Eat warm, cooked, recognisable foods. Avoid very heavily processed meals, not because they're banned, but because they require more digestive effort than a stress-reactive gut has capacity for right now.

## The Calm Plate for Motility-Triggered Readers

What the log showed isn't a food pattern or a stress pattern — it's something more diffuse: a background issue of pace. The Calm Plate for you is about fibre type and fluid timing.

The two main types of fibre do opposite jobs, which is why generic "eat more fibre" advice fails a motility gut. Soluble fibre absorbs water and forms a gel, slowing things down and adding form. Insoluble fibre adds bulk and speeds transit. Telling a fast gut to eat more fibre without saying which kind can make it worse.

If your pattern is slow motility — bloating that builds, infrequent stools, persistent sluggishness — increase soluble fibre: oats, cooked carrots, sweet potato, plain psyllium husk in water, increasing gradually. Drink water between meals rather than during them.

If your pattern is fast motility — urgency, loose stools — reduce insoluble fibre for now. Fewer raw vegetables, fewer bran-heavy grains. Cooked vegetables, white rice, oats, and easily digested proteins keep the gut working without pushing it faster.

Here's what your doctor probably didn't tell you. "Try cutting things out" is advice almost every reader of this book has received — and it is incomplete in a way that sets you up to fail. It doesn't tell you what to cut, for how long, or how to know whether it worked. So people cut everything, can't sustain it, and conclude that diet "doesn't make a difference" for them. The Calm Plate is the missing half of that sentence: a defined set of categories, a defined window of three days, and a defined endpoint where the food comes back. Restriction without a return date isn't a treatment. It's just a smaller life. This is not that.

> **Take this to your doctor**
>
> If you want clinical input on the dietary side, say: "I've done a short, structured reduction of fermentable foods for a few days and I'm planning to reintroduce them one category at a time to identify specific triggers. Would a referral to a registered dietitian be appropriate to do this more thoroughly?" A dietitian — not a generic elimination diet found online — is the right professional for staged, evidence-based food testing, and asking by name makes the referral request concrete. If you have a diagnosed condition or take medication that interacts with diet, confirm any change with your clinician first.

The Calm Plate runs today, tomorrow, and Day 5. You're not perfecting it — you're applying it consistently enough to create a quieter baseline.

Tomorrow, the Repair Kit arrives: five additions that work alongside the Calm Plate, each one chosen because it supports gut function rather than restricts it.

Your gut has been working against a constant background load. Three days of a quieter plate removes that load long enough for the pattern to become visible — and for what follows to actually land.
