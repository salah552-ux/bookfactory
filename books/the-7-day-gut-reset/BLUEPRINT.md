**Key beats the writer must hit:**

### Section 3 — Day 2: Reading the Log — Three Trigger Profiles
**Word target:** 700
**Manuscript file:** `02-day-two.md`

**Purpose:** Teach the reader to interpret their own 24-hour log. Introduce and define the three trigger profiles. The reader identifies which profile is theirs by end of this chapter. This is the diagnostic payoff — the structural difference between this book and every competitor.

**Opening hook type:** Return to the log. "You have 24 hours of data now. Let's read it." Transition from observer to analyst.

**Core argument:** Three profiles account for the majority of non-pathological gut symptoms in otherwise healthy adults. Identifying which one is dominant changes everything about what the protocol needs to do.

**The three profiles — define precisely (see also FACTS.md):**

**Profile 1 — Food-Triggered:**
The log shows a consistent time gap of 20–90 minutes between eating specific foods and the onset of symptoms. Symptom intensity correlates with what was eaten (certain categories — high-FODMAP, fatty, or highly processed foods — produce stronger responses). Stress score does not correlate with symptom peaks. Symptoms are primarily digestive in location: bloating, reflux, urgency, or altered bowel habit following food.

**Profile 2 — Stress-Triggered:**
The log shows that symptoms spike in the hours following high-stress events, regardless of what was eaten. Symptom timing aligns with stress scores more than food timing. The gut-brain axis is the primary driver: the enteric nervous system responds to cortisol and adrenaline by accelerating or slowing transit, increasing sensitivity, or triggering the muscles of the gut wall to spasm. Physical symptoms (bloating, urgency, cramping) follow emotional events, not meals.

**Profile 3 — Motility-Triggered:**
The log shows symptoms that are not clearly food-linked or stress-linked. Timing is irregular. The reader may notice that the problem is not *what* triggers symptoms but *how long it takes* for food to transit (too slow → bloating, constipation; too fast → urgency, loose stools). Motility issues are often chronic background patterns rather than event-triggered responses.

**Key beats the writer must hit:**
1. Open by walking the reader through the log — what to look for: do symptoms follow food within 90 minutes? Do they follow stress events? Are they random and timing-independent?
2. Introduce each profile clearly, in plain language, with examples of how the pattern appears in a log. Keep examples general — a person, not "an IBS patient."
3. Acknowledge overlap: "Most people are primarily one profile with elements of another. Focus on the dominant pattern — that is where the protocol starts."
4. Give the reader a simple decision: based on their log, which profile is most consistent? Stress that this is not a medical diagnosis — it is a working hypothesis. "This is your starting point, not a verdict."
5. End with a direct next-step: "In Day 3, you will see what the Calm Plate looks like for your profile. The protocol from here is built around what you just identified."

**Closing hook:** "You now know something no generic gut health book has ever asked you to know: which type of gut problem is actually yours."

**Tone notes:** This is the pivot chapter — the emotional intensity is MEDIUM-HIGH. This is the moment the book earns trust. The writer should let this land. Do not rush through the profiles. Give each one enough space for the reader to genuinely recognise themselves. Use white space. Short paragraphs. Avoid clinical terminology — "motility" can be explained as "how fast or slow your gut moves food through."

**Pacing note:** MEDIUM-HIGH intensity — the diagnostic payoff. This chapter is the structural centrepiece. Do not let it feel rushed.

**Compliance:** No brand names. No diagnosis language — "this may suggest" not "this means you have." No FODMAP lists by name — refer to "high-fermentable foods" or describe the category in plain language.

### Section 4 — Day 3: The Calm Plate
**Word target:** 700
**Manuscript file:** `03-day-three.md`

**Purpose:** Deliver the dietary protocol tailored to the reader's identified trigger profile. This is prescriptive — but for the first time in the book, the prescription is earned because the reader knows their profile. The Calm Plate is not a diet. It is a temporary eating framework to reduce symptom load while the gut recovers baseline function.

**Opening hook type:** Confirmation and transition. "You know your profile. Now the protocol starts — and it starts with what you eat."

**Core argument:** You do not need to eliminate everything. You need to reduce the specific inputs that are most likely to be creating load for your trigger type. Three days of reduced load gives the gut enough quiet to begin responding differently.

**Key beats the writer must hit:**
1. Explain what "the Calm Plate" is: a temporary, three-day eating framework (Days 3–5) designed to reduce symptom-driving inputs without extreme restriction. This is not a permanent diet. It is a deliberate reduction in load.
2. Describe the Calm Plate for each profile separately:
   - **Food-triggered profile:** Reduce high-fermentable foods (legumes, certain fruits, wheat products, dairy-based items) for three days. Replace with easily digestible proteins, cooked vegetables, and lower-fermentable grains. The goal is quiet — not perfection.
   - **Stress-triggered profile:** The Calm Plate is less about what you remove and more about *how* you eat: meals eaten sitting down, without multitasking, in a calm environment. Avoid eating during high-stress windows if possible. The food choices matter less than the eating context.
   - **Motility-triggered profile:** Adjust fibre type and fluid intake. Slow-motility pattern: increase soluble fibre (oats, cooked carrots, psyllium husk) and water intake. Fast-motility pattern: reduce insoluble fibre temporarily and avoid known gut accelerators (caffeine in large amounts, very fatty meals).
3. Give two or three concrete meal examples for each profile — general categories, not recipes, no brand names. (e.g., "a piece of grilled fish with cooked courgette and white rice" — not a named product.)
4. Reassure the reader: "If you make a choice outside the Calm Plate framework, it is not a failure. The log on Day 1 is your data — this is not a punishment protocol."
5. Introduce what Day 4 adds: "Tomorrow the Repair Kit arrives — five specific add-ins that work alongside the Calm Plate."

**Closing hook:** "Three days of a quieter plate gives your gut the signal it cannot get while everything is turned up to full volume."

**Tone notes:** Practical and reassuring. This is the chapter where readers most fear failure — they are changing something. Keep the language permission-giving, not restrictive. Never use the word "forbidden" or "banned." Use "reduce," "avoid for now," "try not to." The Calm Plate should feel achievable.

**Pacing note:** MEDIUM intensity — instructional but emotionally important. The reader is taking action for the first time. Keep it concrete and unambiguous.

**Compliance:** No brand names anywhere. No named supplements. No named diets (do not say "low-FODMAP" by name — use "reducing high-fermentable foods"). No medical claims. Do not say "this will heal your gut lining" — say "this may reduce the symptom load while your gut settles."

### Section 5 — Day 4: The Repair Kit
**Word target:** 600
**Manuscript file:** `04-day-four.md`

**Purpose:** Introduce five specific add-ins (practices and food categories — no brand names, no supplement products) that support gut recovery during the calm-down phase. These are additions, not restrictions. The tone shifts from "reduce" to "add."

**Opening hook type:** Reframe. "Yesterday was about reducing what's creating noise. Today is about adding what helps the gut do its job."

**Core argument:** Gut recovery is not only about elimination. Several non-branded, widely available inputs actively support the gut environment during a reset. Adding them deliberately, rather than waiting for symptoms to improve on their own, accelerates the timeline.

**The five add-ins (no brand names — describe categories and mechanisms):**
1. **Fermented foods (small amounts):** Plain live-culture yoghurt, kefir, or fermented vegetables (sauerkraut, kimchi — small portions). Why: introduce diverse bacterial input without a supplement. Note: for some food-triggered readers, fermented foods may temporarily increase bloating. Start with a tablespoon and observe.
2. **Bone broth or slow-cooked stock:** Gelatine-rich liquid that coats and soothes the gut lining. No clinical claim — describe it as easily digestible, warm, and traditionally used across cultures for gut support.
3. **Soluble fibre from food:** Oats, psyllium husk (plain), cooked root vegetables. Feeds the beneficial bacterial population without the fermentable load that triggers symptoms in food-sensitive readers. Increase gradually.
4. **Hydration — specifically water timing:** Not total volume but the timing relative to meals. Large volumes of water immediately before a meal can dilute digestive capacity; spacing water away from meals (30 minutes before or 60 minutes after) may improve digestion comfort. This is a simple behavioural shift, not a supplement.
5. **Movement — short walks after eating:** 10–15 minutes of gentle walking after the two largest meals of the day. Stimulates gut motility without the intensity that can trigger symptoms. Particularly useful for motility-triggered readers.

**Key beats the writer must hit:**
1. Introduce the Repair Kit as additions, not substitutions for the Calm Plate. Both run simultaneously on Days 4–6.
2. Present each of the five add-ins clearly — what it is, how to use it in practical terms, which trigger profile benefits most. Keep each add-in to 2–3 short paragraphs.
3. Acknowledge that not all five will suit every profile — give brief guidance on which to prioritise per profile.
4. Maintain the log: "Keep noting symptoms. The Repair Kit should produce a gradual quieting — if something you've added increases symptoms, remove it and note it. You are still in diagnostic mode."
5. Preview Day 5: "Tomorrow introduces the stress bridge — the element most gut health books mention but never operationalise."

**Closing hook:** "Five additions. No products to buy. No prescriptions. Just inputs your gut recognises as support."

**Tone notes:** Positive and practical. This chapter is energetically different from the elimination-adjacent tone of Day 3 — it should feel like good news. Keep it brief and concrete. No long explanations of gut microbiology. The reader wants to know what to do, not a lecture on the enteric nervous system.

**Pacing note:** LIGHT — relief chapter after the protocol begins. The reader is gaining tools, not facing restrictions.

**Compliance:** No brand names anywhere. No supplement product names (no named probiotic brands, no named fibre supplements). No medical claims — "may support," "may help," "traditionally used." Do not claim any add-in will repair gut lining, treat IBS, or address any specific diagnosed condition.

### Section 6 — Day 5: The Stress Bridge
**Word target:** 500
**Manuscript file:** `05-day-five.md`

**Purpose:** Operationalise the gut-brain connection into a specific, timed, daily practice. This is the differentiating chapter — it is what no competitor does. The vagal tone practice is the mechanism; the four-minute duration is the commitment level.

**Opening hook type:** Validation before instruction. "You already know stress affects your gut. What no one has given you is something specific to do about it in the middle of a normal day."

**Core argument:** Vagal tone — the activity level of the vagus nerve, which connects the brain to the gut — is trainable. Brief, consistent practices that activate the parasympathetic nervous system reduce cortisol response time and dampen the enteric nervous system's over-reactivity. Four minutes a day is sufficient to begin shifting baseline tone. This is not meditation. It is a physiological lever.

**The vagal tone practice — define precisely (see also FACTS.md):**
A four-minute daily practice combining two elements:
- **Physiological sigh (2 minutes):** Double inhale through the nose (sharp first inhale, small secondary inhale to fully inflate the lungs), followed by a slow, extended exhale through the mouth. This pattern activates the parasympathetic nervous system faster than standard deep breathing. Repeat for 2 minutes.
- **Humming or low-frequency vocalisation (2 minutes):** Sustained humming on a low comfortable note. The vagus nerve passes through the vocal cords; low-frequency vocalisation stimulates vagal activity directly. No particular technique required — a sustained low "mmm" is sufficient.

**Key beats the writer must hit:**
1. Explain the gut-brain axis in plain language — one paragraph. The vagus nerve carries signals in both directions. Stress signals from the brain reach the gut within seconds via this pathway. Calming the vagal pathway calms the gut's over-reactivity. This is not psychological; it is physiological.
2. Name the practice clearly: "The Stress Bridge." Define it: a four-minute daily practice (2 minutes physiological sigh + 2 minutes low-frequency humming). Give it at a specific time recommendation: once in the morning, once in the afternoon if symptoms are high.
3. Address the likely objection: "This sounds too simple." Answer it directly: "It is simple because the mechanism is direct. You are not trying to think calmer — you are telling your nervous system a different story through your breath and your voice."
4. Explain which profile benefits most (stress-triggered: essential / food-triggered: still useful, stress is a secondary amplifier for most gut patterns / motility-triggered: particularly useful if urgency or spasm is part of the motility pattern).
5. Embed the practice within the day: it does not require a quiet room, a mat, or silence. It can be done in a parked car, in a bathroom, at a desk with headphones on. Duration: exactly four minutes.

**Closing hook:** "Four minutes. Same time tomorrow. The stress bridge does not require you to feel less stressed — it requires you to activate a different system while the stress is still happening."

**Tone notes:** Confident and grounded. The writer must not hedge excessively on this practice — it is a well-understood physiological mechanism (parasympathetic activation via breath and vagal stimulation). Present it as a known tool, not as a tentative suggestion. Do not use words like "might" or "perhaps" when describing the mechanism. Use "may" when making outcome claims about individual results.

**Pacing note:** LIGHT-MEDIUM — conceptually interesting, practically simple. The reader is given a new tool that feels novel. No long science sections. Keep the explanation tight.

**Compliance:** No medical claims. Do not say "this treats anxiety" or "this cures stress-related IBS." Say "this may help reduce the gut's over-reaction to stress signals." No brand names. The practice uses no products.

### Section 7 — Day 6: Reintroduction
**Word target:** 600
**Manuscript file:** `06-day-six.md`

**Purpose:** Teach the reader how to test what was removed — systematically, one item at a time, with a protocol for reading the response. This is the chapter that converts a temporary reset into actionable long-term knowledge. It is the structural answer to "what do I do after this week ends?"

**Opening hook type:** Reframe restriction as information. "Everything you reduced on Day 3 is not banned forever. Today you start finding out what you can bring back — and what your gut is actually asking you to avoid."

**Core argument:** Reintroduction done systematically is the most useful thing a gut-health reset can produce. Without it, the reader has only confirmed that restriction reduces symptoms — which they already suspected. With it, they know specifically which inputs their gut tolerates and which it does not. That knowledge belongs to them permanently.

**The reintroduction mechanic — how it works (define precisely — see also FACTS.md):**
- Reintroduce one removed food category at a time. Not multiple foods simultaneously — that makes it impossible to isolate the cause of any response.
- Allow 48 hours between each reintroduction. The gut's response to a new input can take up to 24 hours to fully manifest; 48 hours provides a clear observation window.
- On reintroduction day: eat a normal portion of the food being tested at one meal. Continue the Calm Plate at other meals. Log the response over the next 48 hours using the same columns as the Day 1 log.
- A clear response (symptom intensity rising 3+ points on the 1–10 scale within 48 hours, or symptom type returning that was absent during Days 3–5) = this food category needs more time out, or may be a consistent trigger. Do not reintroduce it again during this seven-day reset.
- No clear response = this food is likely tolerable for this reader's gut. Bring it back.
- For stress-triggered readers: reintroduction is not about food. It is about reintroducing the contexts or situations that were avoided (eating while working, eating socially in a loud environment). Test one context at a time, same 48-hour window.

**Key beats the writer must hit:**
1. Explain the one-item-at-a-time principle and why it matters. Reintroducing everything at once is the most common mistake — it produces a symptom spike with no identifiable cause, leaving the reader back at the beginning.
2. Walk through the 48-hour observation window clearly. Give the reader the exact question to ask: "Is my symptom intensity 3 or more points higher than it was on Days 3–5?"
3. Address anxiety about "what if I react badly?" — this is not a clinical test. A bad reaction during reintroduction is information, not a failure. The reader stops the reintroduction and notes the finding. They are not harmed by a single meal of something their gut doesn't tolerate.
4. Provide a sequencing suggestion: which categories to reintroduce first and last. (Reintroduce the least likely triggers first — mildly fermentable vegetables before dairy, dairy before wheat — so early wins build confidence.)
5. Explain that formal reintroduction beyond Day 6 continues at the reader's own pace. The seven-day reset provides the framework; reintroduction is an ongoing process the reader owns.

**Closing hook:** "By the end of Day 7 you'll have begun to build the list that matters most — not what everyone else's gut reacts to, but what yours does."

**Tone notes:** Methodical and reassuring. The reader may feel anxious about reintroducing foods they have been avoiding. The tone should normalise the process: this is science, not willpower. They are collecting data, not making permanent decisions.

**Pacing note:** MEDIUM — instructional but important. Keep it structured with clear steps. Use numbered instructions for the reintroduction mechanic.

**Compliance:** No brand names. Do not name specific food products. Use categories (dairy, wheat products, legumes). No medical claims. Do not say "this will identify your food allergies or intolerances" — say "this may help you identify which food categories your gut responds to more strongly."

### Section 8 — Day 7: Your Personal Maintenance Map
**Word target:** 600
**Manuscript file:** `07-day-seven.md`

**Purpose:** Transfer ownership of the reset to the reader. The Personal Maintenance Map is a one-page reference they build from the week's data — what worked, what to avoid, what to watch, when to revisit. The book ends with the reader holding something they made, not a set of instructions to follow.

**Opening hook type:** Arrival. "You made it to Day 7. More than that — you made it through seven days with data, not guesswork."

**Core argument:** The goal of any reset is not to follow the protocol forever — it is to understand your gut well enough to manage it without a protocol. The maintenance map is that understanding, condensed into something usable every day.

**The Personal Maintenance Map — what it contains:**
1. **My trigger profile:** Food / Stress / Motility (the one identified on Day 2)
2. **My confirmed tolerables:** Food categories (or eating contexts) reintroduced on Day 6 without a significant response
3. **My confirmed sensitivities:** Food categories (or contexts) that produced a clear response on Day 6 — flag for long-term awareness, not permanent elimination
4. **My Calm Plate defaults:** 3–4 meal formats that reliably reduced symptoms during Days 3–5 — these become the "reset" meal choices when symptoms flare
5. **My Stress Bridge:** Time of day and duration of the practice that worked best this week
6. **My warning signals:** The first symptom to appear when my gut is moving toward a flare — this is the reader's personal early warning. Catching it early means a one-day calm plate reset is sufficient, rather than waiting until symptoms are severe.
7. **When to go further:** If symptoms remain high or complex after this week, the next step is the full-length guide (*Fix Your Gut for Good*) or a gut health specialist. Be direct about limits — this is a seven-day reset, not a substitute for clinical care.

**Key beats the writer must hit:**
1. Walk the reader through building each section of the map from their week's data. The map is not a template in the book — it is a set of prompts the reader fills with their own material. (Keep it simple enough to fit on a single page of a notebook or a note on their phone.)
2. Explain the difference between "confirmed sensitivities" and "never again" — the reset establishes which categories to approach with care, not permanent prohibitions. The gut can change over time. Sensitivities identified this week should be revisited in 8–12 weeks.
3. Introduce the "reset meal" concept: 2–3 of the Calm Plate meals that worked best this week become the reader's default when symptoms return. This converts the protocol into a practical tool they already know works.
4. Address the "what do I do now?" question directly. The seven-day reset is complete. The map runs indefinitely. If symptoms remain significant, the next step is more depth — which is where *Fix Your Gut for Good* enters.
5. Acknowledge the reader's effort. This chapter is the emotional close — the writer should let the reader feel the accomplishment of having done something methodical and specific that no previous book gave them the framework for.

**Closing hook:** "The map you've just built belongs to you — not to this book, not to the seven days. Take it with you."

**Tone notes:** Warm and celebratory — but not saccharine. The reader did real work. Honour it without being gushing. The final pages should feel like handover: the book's job is done, the reader's knowledge is theirs.

**Pacing note:** LIGHT-MEDIUM — emotional close. The heavy work is done. This chapter is the reward.

**Compliance:** No medical claims. The map explicitly includes "when to go further" to avoid implying the reset is a substitute for clinical care. No brand names.

## 5. TONE BIBLE

### What this book's voice IS:
- Second person, always. "You" throughout. Never "one" or "the reader."
- Short sentences for instruction. Medium sentences for explanation. Long sentences are rare and used only for emotional moments.
- Contractions always. "You don't" not "you do not." "It's" not "it is" — except where emphasis requires the full form.
- The author is a knowledgeable companion. Not a doctor. Not a lecturer. Not a cheerleader.
- Warmth comes from specificity, not from exclamation marks or affirmations.
- Respect for the reader's intelligence is shown by not explaining things they already know and by stating directly the thing most books dance around.

### Sentence length guidelines:
- Instructions: 8–15 words per sentence. Clear. Unambiguous. No qualifiers.
- Explanations: 15–25 words. One idea per sentence.
- Emotional beats: variable. Some one-word sentences are correct. "Stop." "That's it." "Seven days."

### Paragraph length guidelines:
- Maximum 4 sentences in standard body paragraphs.
- Single-sentence paragraphs are used for emphasis only — maximum 2 per chapter.
- No paragraph should carry more than one main idea.

### NEVER do these things (specific to this book):
1. **Never open a paragraph with "Additionally," "Furthermore," "Moreover," or "It is worth noting that."** These are AI-sounding constructions that the reader's subconscious flags as generic.
2. **Never use "journey" as a noun for the reader's experience.** ("Your health journey," "this healing journey" — both forbidden.) This is the single most overused word in wellness writing.
3. **Never say "groundbreaking," "revolutionary," "game-changing," or "life-changing" about the content.** The book's value is proven by its structure, not claimed by its adjectives.
4. **Never use the phrase "in today's busy world" or any variant of it.** It is the single most tired opener in self-help and health writing.
5. **Never address the reader's potential failure with phrases like "Don't worry if you slip up" or "Be kind to yourself."** These are condescending. If failure is possible, address it directly: "If you eat outside the Calm Plate, log it and continue."
6. **Never use passive voice for instructions.** "The log should be completed" → "Complete the log." "Symptoms may be noticed" → "Notice your symptoms."
7. **Never end a chapter with a rhetorical question.** Chapters close with statements or direct next-step instructions — never "So, are you ready to find your trigger?"

### ALWAYS do these things:
1. Address the reader's likely objection before they think to object. Pre-empt resistance, then answer it in the same paragraph.
2. Name the mechanism before stating the instruction. Tell the reader *why* a thing works (briefly) before telling them to do it.
3. Use plain English for gut physiology. "The vagus nerve connects your brain to your gut" is sufficient. No Latin terms. No clinical abbreviations.
4. Earn every adjective. If a food category is described as "easily digestible," the next sentence must briefly explain why.
5. When introducing each Day's content, begin with one sentence that tells the reader exactly what today does. Not what they'll learn — what they'll *do* or *have* by end of day.

### How to handle jargon:
- FODMAP: never use the acronym or the term. Say "high-fermentable foods" and describe the category in plain language.
- IBS: may be mentioned once in the Introduction as the label readers have been given. After that, describe symptoms directly — do not use the diagnosis term.
- Microbiome: may be used once in Day 4 with a one-sentence plain-language definition. Do not repeat.
- Vagal tone / vagus nerve: introduce once in Day 5 with a plain-language definition. Use "the nerve that connects your brain to your gut" as the working description.
- KENPC, KU, BSR: do not appear in the manuscript — these are publishing terms.

### How to handle emotion:
Emotion is acknowledged, not amplified. When the reader's likely emotional state is frustration, exhaustion, or disappointment (Introduction, early Day 1), name the feeling in plain terms and move quickly to something concrete. Do not dwell in the emotional state — give the reader something to do.

### Example of a perfect paragraph in this book's voice:
*You didn't respond to the last protocol because it wasn't built for your gut. It was built for the average gut — which is not a thing that actually exists. Every person's digestive system has a primary driver: a food sensitivity, a stress response, or a motility pattern. Identifying which one is yours takes 24 hours. Everything from Day 3 onwards depends on that identification.*

## 10. STRUCTURAL RISKS

1. **Day 2 abstraction risk:** The trigger profiles must be made immediately recognisable. If the reader cannot identify their profile with confidence, the entire tailored-protocol structure collapses. The writer must use brief illustrative examples (one scenario per profile) — not clinical descriptions.
2. **Day 3 restriction anxiety:** The Calm Plate section risks triggering the same "another elimination diet" response the book is positioned against. Keep the tone emphatically permission-giving. Emphasise temporariness and the limited scope of what is being reduced.
3. **Day 5 credibility risk:** The vagal tone practice must be presented with enough physiological grounding to avoid sounding like wellness fluff. One solid paragraph on the vagus nerve mechanism before introducing the practice resolves this.
4. **Conclusion CTA balance:** The *Fix Your Gut for Good* CTA must not feel like the seven-day reset was a lead magnet. Frame it clearly as "for readers whose questions this week has opened" — not as "what you really need is the full book."
5. **Total length risk:** At 5,500 words, every section must stay tight. The writer should draft to target and not pad. Day 2 (700 words) and Day 3 (700 words) are the highest-risk sections for over-writing — three trigger profiles × two types of content per profile = dense. Tight paragraph discipline is essential.
