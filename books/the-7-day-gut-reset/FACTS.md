## 1. THE THREE TRIGGER PROFILES

These are the diagnostic categories introduced in Day 2 and referenced throughout the book. They must be defined consistently in every chapter that mentions them. No variation in language.

### Profile 1 — Food-Triggered

**Definition:** The primary driver of gut symptoms is an adverse response to one or more food categories. The log shows a consistent time gap of 20–90 minutes between eating specific foods and the onset of symptoms. Symptom intensity correlates with what was eaten. Stress score does not correlate with symptom peaks. Symptoms are primarily digestive in location: bloating, reflux, urgency, or altered bowel habit following food.

**Plain language description for the manuscript:** "Your log shows that symptoms follow meals — not just sometimes, but consistently, and within roughly an hour or two of eating certain types of food. Stress and other factors may play a secondary role, but the primary driver is what you put on your plate."

**Calm Plate application:** Reduce high-fermentable foods for Days 3–5. Reintroduction on Day 6 tests food categories one at a time.

**Stress Bridge relevance:** Still useful as a secondary tool — stress amplifies food sensitivity in most gut patterns.

---

### Profile 2 — Stress-Triggered

**Definition:** The primary driver of gut symptoms is the gut-brain axis response to psychological or physiological stress. The log shows that symptoms spike in the hours following high-stress events, regardless of what was eaten. Symptom timing aligns with stress scores more than food timing. The enteric nervous system responds to cortisol and adrenaline by accelerating or slowing transit, increasing gut sensitivity, or triggering the muscles of the gut wall to contract.

**Plain language description for the manuscript:** "Your log doesn't show a clear food pattern — but it does show that your worst gut days follow your most stressful hours or days. The connection is your nervous system. Your gut and your brain share a direct line, and when stress activates that line, your gut responds whether or not you've eaten anything unusual."

**Calm Plate application:** The Calm Plate for this profile focuses on *how* the reader eats (sitting, calm, without multitasking) rather than what is removed. Food choices matter less than the eating context.

**Stress Bridge relevance:** Essential. The Day 5 practice is the primary protocol for this profile.

**Reintroduction application (Day 6):** For stress-triggered readers, reintroduction tests eating contexts (eating while working, eating in a loud social environment) rather than food categories.

---

### Profile 3 — Motility-Triggered

**Definition:** The primary driver of gut symptoms is a disruption in the speed at which food moves through the digestive tract, not a specific food category or a stress response. The log shows symptoms that are not clearly food-linked or stress-linked. Timing is irregular. The pattern is a chronic background issue rather than event-triggered: slow motility produces bloating and constipation; fast motility produces urgency and loose stools.

**Plain language description for the manuscript:** "Your log doesn't show a consistent trigger — not a particular food, not a particular stressful event. What it shows is a pattern in the timing of symptoms that suggests your gut moves things through either too slowly or too quickly. The issue isn't what you're eating or what you're feeling — it's the pace."

**Calm Plate application:** Adjust fibre type and fluid intake. Slow-motility: increase soluble fibre (oats, cooked root vegetables) and water intake. Fast-motility: reduce insoluble fibre temporarily and avoid known gut accelerators.

**Stress Bridge relevance:** Useful if urgency or spasm is part of the motility pattern.

## 2. THE VAGAL TONE PRACTICE (DAY 5)

**Name in the manuscript:** "The Stress Bridge"
**Duration:** Four minutes — this is a fixed, non-negotiable specification. Do not describe it as "a few minutes" or "three to five minutes." Always "four minutes."
**Timing recommendation:** Once in the morning. A second practice in the afternoon if symptoms are elevated.

**The practice — two components:**

**Component 1 — Physiological sigh (2 minutes):**
Double inhale through the nose: a sharp first inhale to partially inflate the lungs, followed immediately by a small secondary inhale to fully inflate them. Then a slow, extended exhale through the mouth. The double inhale deflates the alveoli in the lungs that collapse during normal breathing; the extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system faster than standard deep breathing. Repeat for 2 minutes.

**Component 2 — Low-frequency vocalisation / humming (2 minutes):**
Sustained humming on a low comfortable note — any pitch the reader finds natural. The vagus nerve passes through the vocal cords (specifically the superior and recurrent laryngeal branches of the vagus). Low-frequency vocalisation stimulates vagal activity directly via mechanical vibration. No particular technique required. A sustained low "mmm" on an exhale is sufficient. Repeat for 2 minutes.

**Mechanism (for the manuscript — plain language):** The vagus nerve is the primary channel of the parasympathetic nervous system. It connects the brain to the gut, the heart, and the lungs — carrying signals in both directions. When the sympathetic nervous system (stress response) is active, vagal tone is suppressed: gut motility changes, gut sensitivity increases, and the enteric nervous system becomes over-reactive. Practices that directly stimulate the vagus nerve — breath-based and vibration-based — shift the nervous system toward parasympathetic dominance. Four minutes is sufficient to measurably shift heart rate variability (a proxy for vagal tone) in most individuals.

**Compliance note:** Do not claim the vagal tone practice "treats anxiety," "cures stress-related IBS," or "heals the gut-brain axis." The manuscript must use: "may help," "may reduce," "is designed to." The mechanism is described accurately; the individual outcome claim uses "may."

**No brand names:** No named app, no named breathwork system (no Wim Hof, no Box Breathing by name — describe mechanics only).

## 3. THE REINTRODUCTION MECHANIC (DAY 6)

**Core principle:** One food category reintroduced at a time. Multiple simultaneous reintroductions make it impossible to isolate cause and response.

**Observation window:** 48 hours between each reintroduction. The gut's response to a new input can take up to 24 hours to fully manifest. 48 hours provides a clear, unambiguous observation window.

**The reintroduction day protocol:**
- Eat a normal portion of the food category being tested at one meal on Day 6.
- Continue the Calm Plate at all other meals on Day 6 and the following day.
- Use the Day 1 log columns to record response: time / symptom type / symptom intensity 1–10.
- Assess at 48 hours: has symptom intensity risen 3 or more points on the 1–10 scale compared to Days 3–5?

**Response interpretation:**
- Clear response (symptom intensity +3 or higher within 48 hours, or return of symptom type absent during Days 3–5): this food category needs more time out, or may be a consistent trigger. Do not reintroduce it again during the seven-day reset. Flag in the Maintenance Map as "approach with care."
- No clear response: this food category is likely tolerable for this reader's gut. Note it in the Maintenance Map as "tolerates."

**Reintroduction sequence recommendation:** Least likely triggers first. Mildly fermentable vegetables before dairy; dairy before wheat products. Build confidence with early tolerables before testing the most likely suspects.

**For stress-triggered profile:** Reintroduction tests eating *contexts*, not food categories. One context per 48-hour window (eating while working, eating socially in a loud environment). Same response scale applies.

**Compliance note:** Do not say "this will identify your food allergies or intolerances." Say "this may help you identify which food categories your gut responds to more strongly." Reintroduction in this context is a personal observational exercise, not a clinical allergy test.

## 4. THE PERSONAL MAINTENANCE MAP (DAY 7)

**Structure — seven fields:**
1. My trigger profile (Food / Stress / Motility)
2. My confirmed tolerables (food categories or contexts that produced no response on reintroduction)
3. My confirmed sensitivities (categories or contexts that produced a response — flag for awareness, not permanent elimination)
4. My Calm Plate defaults (2–3 meal formats that reliably reduced symptoms during Days 3–5)
5. My Stress Bridge (time of day and practice duration that worked this week)
6. My warning signals (the first symptom to appear when a flare is beginning — the reader's personal early warning)
7. When to go further (if symptoms remain complex or severe after this week: consult a gut health specialist or proceed to *Fix Your Gut for Good*)

**The map belongs to the reader.** It is not a fill-in-the-blank template within the book — it is built by the reader from their week's data. The manuscript prompts the reader to build it; it does not hand them a form.

**Sensitivity note on "confirmed sensitivities":** These are not permanent prohibitions. The gut changes over time. Sensitivities identified this week should be revisited in 8–12 weeks. The Maintenance Map is a working document, not a verdict.

## 5. THE TRIGGER LOG (DAY 1)

**Five columns:**
1. Time
2. Food or drink consumed
3. Stress level (1–10, self-rated)
4. Symptom type (describe: bloating / reflux / urgency / pain / sluggishness — not limited to these)
5. Symptom intensity (1–10)

**Timing discipline:** Log within 15–30 minutes of eating. Not at the end of the day from memory. Retrospective logging loses symptom-to-food timing accuracy and therefore loses the diagnostic value of the exercise.

**Day 1 rule:** No food changes on Day 1. The reader eats normally. The point is baseline data — introducing variables by already changing behaviour defeats the diagnostic purpose.

## 6. COMPLIANCE CONSTRAINTS — HARD RULES FOR THE MANUSCRIPT

These constraints apply to every chapter without exception. The health-writer must not deviate.

### No Brand Names
Zero product names, supplement brands, food brands, supplement company names, or app names anywhere in the manuscript. This includes:
- Named probiotic brands (e.g., Yakult, Align, Culturelle — do not name)
- Named fibre supplements (e.g., Metamucil, Benefibre — do not name)
- Named prebiotic or postbiotic supplements — do not name any
- Named breathwork apps — do not name any
- Named gut health programmes or named dietary systems beyond brief descriptive mention

**If a category is needed:** describe it. "Live-culture yoghurt" not "[Brand] yoghurt." "Plain psyllium husk" not "[Product]."

### No Medical Claims
The manuscript may never:
- Claim to diagnose any condition
- Claim to treat, cure, or reverse any condition
- Use the word "cure," "treat," "heal" (in a medical sense), or "diagnose"
- Reference specific medical tests or prescribe specific supplements for diagnosed conditions
- Suggest the book is a substitute for clinical care

**Required language substitutes:**
- "may help" not "will cure"
- "may reduce" not "will eliminate"
- "may support" not "will repair"
- "this is not a substitute for medical advice" — include once, in the Introduction, as a brief disclaimer sentence

### No Diagnosis Language
The manuscript must not diagnose the reader. It establishes three working profiles (food / stress / motility) as observational frameworks, not diagnoses.
- Never: "If your log shows X, you have Y condition."
- Always: "If your log shows X, your dominant trigger profile may be Y."
- The word "IBS" may appear once in the Introduction (as the label readers have been given by GPs) and not again.
- The word "SIBO," "Crohn's," "colitis," "coeliac," or any specific named GI condition does not appear in this manuscript.

### "Calm" Not "Cure"
The book's promise is to help the reader calm gut symptoms and identify triggers. It is not a cure. This distinction must be maintained in every section.
- "Calm Your Gut" — correct (matches title)
- "Heal Your Gut" — avoid (implicit medical claim)
- "Fix Your Gut" — avoid in this manuscript (that is the title of the series anchor book)
- "Repair Your Gut" — avoid
- "Cure Your IBS" — never

### Series Cross-Reference — Back Matter Only
*Fix Your Gut for Good* (the full-length series anchor) is referenced in the manuscript ONLY in:
- Section 9 (Conclusion + Back Matter) — the CTA paragraph
- Day 7 (the "when to go further" field of the Maintenance Map) — a brief mention

It does not appear in Sections 1–8 beyond these permitted locations. Do not reference it as a resource mid-protocol.

**Exact title to use:** *Fix Your Gut for Good* — use this title precisely. No abbreviation, no variation.
