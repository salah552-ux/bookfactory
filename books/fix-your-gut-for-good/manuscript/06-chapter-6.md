# Chapter Six
## The Diet That Isn't a Diet
### Why What You Eat Matters Less Than When You Stop

You've been eating carefully for months, maybe years. You cut out gluten. Then dairy. Then onion and garlic and apples and most bread and anything that might, in theory, ferment. You carry food in your bag because restaurants are a minefield. You read ingredient lists. You say no to things at parties. And you're still bloated.

The problem was never the food.

That sentence deserves a moment to sit before we keep going, because it runs against everything you've been told, and everything you've tried.

---

## What Low-FODMAP Actually Does

If you've spent any time in the SIBO or IBS world, you've encountered the low-FODMAP diet — a framework developed at Monash University in Australia that restricts fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides, and polyols. FODMAPs are short-chain carbohydrates that ferment rapidly in the gut. When you remove them from your diet, the bacteria in your small intestine have less to eat. Less fermentation. Less gas. Less bloating. Less pain.

It works. That part is real and documented, and if low-FODMAP helped you, that's not a coincidence.

But here's what the diet was designed to do: manage IBS symptoms. It was built as a symptom management tool for a condition characterised by gut hypersensitivity, not bacterial overgrowth. IBS and SIBO are different diagnoses with different mechanisms, and they require different interventions. The low-FODMAP diet addresses neither the overgrowth nor the conditions that created it. It removes the fuel. It doesn't remove the bacteria, and it doesn't address why they're there.

This is not a flaw in the diet. It's a mismatch in application.

Think of it this way: if your kitchen has a gas leak, opening the windows makes the smell better. You can breathe in there again. But the pipe is still leaking, and if you close the windows, the gas comes back. Low-FODMAP is the open window. It's genuinely useful. It was just never designed to fix the pipe.

The reason this matters is that many people with SIBO spend months — sometimes years — on an increasingly strict version of low-FODMAP, cutting more foods with every symptom flare, trying to find the threshold where the bloating stops. The threshold keeps moving because the root problem is untouched. And the stricter the restriction, the more malnourished the beneficial parts of the microbiome become, because many high-FODMAP foods are also the foods that feed the good bacteria in your large intestine.

If you've been on low-FODMAP for longer than the Monash protocol recommends — typically two to six weeks of strict elimination, followed by careful reintroduction — and you're still not better, it's not that you haven't restricted enough. It's that restriction was never going to get you there.

That's not your fault. The gap between what the diet was designed for and what it's been prescribed for is a systemic problem, not a personal failing.

## The One Rule That Changes Everything

We've touched on the migrating motor complex in earlier chapters. You know that it's your gut's housekeeping system. You know that it runs between meals, in the fasted state, sweeping bacteria from the small intestine into the large intestine. You know that frequent eating interrupts it.

Now let's give this the full treatment, because the eating window is the most actionable thing in this entire book.

Current clinical guidance recommends four to five hours of fasted time between meals to support a full MMC cleaning cycle. This is the window that clinical practice and physiological reasoning suggest allows a full sweep to happen and — critically — allows the next cycle to begin before you eat again.

When that window is consistently respected, the MMC runs multiple cycles per day. Bacteria get swept. The small intestine stays cleaner between treatments. Whatever antimicrobial protocol you're running has a fighting chance to hold because you're not continuously reseeding the environment through interrupted mechanics.

When that window isn't respected, the opposite is true. The MMC can't complete a sweep in ninety minutes. That's the cycle duration, not the fasted window needed — the ninety-to-one-hundred-twenty-minute interval is how long a single MMC wave takes to travel from stomach to terminal ileum on average. But for a full cleaning cycle to be useful, you need more than one wave, and you need the gut to be empty when those waves are running. The four-to-five-hour window recommended in clinical practice gives the system enough space to actually work.

Here's what counts as "caloric" for these purposes: anything that triggers a digestive response. Any food. Any caloric beverage — fruit juice, milk, oat milk, protein shakes, bone broth. Anything sweetened, even with zero-calorie sweeteners that still trigger cephalic-phase insulin response in some people.

Here's what doesn't interrupt the MMC: water, plain black coffee, plain tea with no milk and no sweetener. That's the full list.

You can have your coffee in the morning. You can drink water freely. But the protein bar at ten-thirty, the handful of almonds at three, the "just a little" of something between meals — each of those stops the clock. The MMC pauses, begins responding to the incoming food, and won't restart its cleaning sweep until the gut is clear again. Which, if you ate something again an hour later, isn't happening.

Most gut-health advice recommends grazing — small meals frequently, don't let your stomach get too empty. For people with acid reflux or certain types of gastroparesis, that advice has merit. For people with SIBO, it's working directly against the mechanism that keeps bacterial populations in check. It is the opposite of helpful. Not slightly less optimal. The opposite.

Three meals a day, with four to five hours between each, is not an extreme protocol. It aligns with how the gut's cleaning mechanism is understood to function and predates the modern pattern of near-continuous eating.

## What to Eat During Active Treatment

Now that we've reframed the priority, we can talk about food itself — not as a diet, but as a practical tool.

During active antimicrobial treatment, reducing fermentable substrate makes clinical sense. If you're trying to reduce a bacterial population, giving those bacteria less food to ferment makes the treatment more effective. This is the same logic that supports low-FODMAP — less fuel, less advantage for the bacteria. The difference here is that we're applying it temporarily and specifically, not indefinitely.

This is not the same as going full low-FODMAP. That protocol restricts aggressively across multiple food categories and requires careful tracking. What we're looking for during treatment is a moderate, pragmatic middle position: not zero fermentable carbohydrates (that would starve your microbiome long-term and isn't sustainable), and not an unrestricted diet that pours fermentable substrate onto an active overgrowth. Something in between, chosen for simplicity and short-term effectiveness.

Well-cooked vegetables are generally lower in fermentable fibre than raw vegetables. Cooking can break down cellular structure and reduce fermentable substrate in many vegetables, though the degree varies by food and cooking method. A bowl of roasted carrots is a very different fermentative event than a raw carrot. Steam things. Roast things. Avoid salads during treatment — not because vegetables are the enemy, but because raw plant fibre is significantly more fermentable than cooked.

Proteins are clean in this framework. Eggs, meat, fish, and poultry don't ferment. They don't feed bacterial overgrowth. They're also some of the most nutrient-dense options available, which matters when your gut has been compromised and absorption has been impaired.

Certain starches ferment very slowly and are generally well-tolerated during treatment: white rice, white potatoes, cooked sweet potato, cooked carrots. These are not perfect — no food is — but they produce minimal fermentation in most people and give you something substantive to build meals around.

Here's a practical framework for the treatment phase:

---

### Treatment-Phase Meal Framework

| Category | Include | Limit During Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Protein** | Eggs, beef, chicken, turkey, fish, lamb, pork | Legumes (lentils, chickpeas, black beans — high fermentation) |
| **Cooked vegetables** | Zucchini, carrots, green beans, spinach, kale, bok choy, cucumber (cooked), bell pepper | Raw salads, cruciferous vegetables raw (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage) |
| **Starches** | White rice, white potato, sweet potato, cooked parsnip | Wheat, rye, barley, most commercial breads |
| **Fats** | Olive oil, butter, coconut oil, avocado oil | — |
| **Fruit** | Unripe banana (low-FODMAP), berries in small amounts (blueberries, strawberries) | Apples, pears, mango, stone fruits, dried fruit |
| **Dairy** | Hard aged cheese (low-lactose), lactose-free milk if needed | Regular milk, soft cheeses, yogurt, kefir |
| **Beverages** | Water, plain black coffee, plain tea | Fruit juice, oat milk, almond milk with additives, kombucha |

This is not a permanent list. It's a treatment-phase tool. Chapter 7 covers reintroduction — when to start adding foods back, what order to do it in, how to read your own responses, and why rushing that step is the most common and most expensive mistake in SIBO recovery.

## Five Things People Think Are Safe

This is worth its own section, because these foods come up constantly, they're often marketed as healthy or gut-supportive, and during active SIBO treatment, they work against you.

> **Kombucha and fermented foods.** Kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut, kombucha — these are popular gut health tools, and in a healthy microbiome context, they have a real role. During active SIBO treatment, you're trying to reduce microbial populations in the small intestine. Adding live bacteria and yeast to that environment is the opposite of what you need. Fermented foods aren't dangerous. The timing is wrong.
>
> **Protein bars and energy bars.** Read the ingredients. Many commercial protein bars contain chicory root or inulin — high-FODMAP prebiotic fibres added to boost the fibre count on the label. Inulin ferments aggressively. A single bar can produce more fermentation than a full meal of the problem foods people are carefully avoiding. The packaging says "prebiotic fibre" as if that's a selling point. During SIBO treatment, it isn't.
>
> **Apples, pears, and stone fruits.** These are high in both fructose and sorbitol — two fermentable sugars that cause significant bacterial activity even in small amounts. Apples in particular are among the highest-fructose fruits available. "But it's just fruit" is a phrase that has caused a lot of confused symptom flares. Unripe banana and small amounts of berries are different. An apple is not.
>
> **Garlic and onion in any form.** Fructans — the fermentable fibre class in garlic and onion — survive cooking exceptionally well. Garlic-infused oil is often recommended as a workaround on the grounds that fructans don't transfer into oil. This is true for some people and not for others, depending on sensitivity level and bacterial load. During active treatment, the safest position is to avoid both and revisit them during reintroduction, where you can assess your individual response clearly.
>
> **Probiotics during active antimicrobial treatment.** We'll cover this properly in the next section. The short version: you're running an antimicrobial protocol. You're trying to reduce bacterial populations. Adding bacteria at the same time creates a biological conflict, and the research on probiotic use during active SIBO treatment doesn't support it. Timing is everything here, and the right time is not now.

## Probiotics: What the Timing Question Actually Means

Probiotics have a legitimate and evidence-supported role in gut recovery. This is not a chapter about dismissing them. But the timing question that was flagged briefly in Chapter 5 deserves a proper answer.

During active antimicrobial treatment — whether pharmaceutical or herbal — the goal is to reduce bacterial populations in the small intestine. Most commercial probiotic supplements contain live bacteria: Lactobacillus strains, Bifidobacterium strains, mixed combinations. Introducing live bacteria into an environment you're trying to depopulate doesn't make biological sense, and the clinical evidence for probiotic use during active SIBO eradication is not strong.

After treatment, the picture changes. Specific strains at specific times have good supporting evidence. Lactobacillus reuteri has been studied for its effects on gut motility — some evidence suggests it may support MMC function, which is exactly what you need to maintain after treatment. Saccharomyces boulardii is a yeast, not a bacterium, which means it isn't affected by most antimicrobial protocols and survives in the gut environment; some evidence suggests it may help support barrier integrity — the tight junctions between intestinal cells that SIBO tends to loosen.

The principle here is sequence. Eradication first, then support. Running both at the same time doesn't double the benefit — it divides the effectiveness.

When reintroduction is underway and treatment is complete, that's the window to reintroduce probiotic support thoughtfully. Chapter 8, which covers motility and the MMC in depth, will connect this to the longer-term maintenance picture.

## The Part That Isn't Temporary

This chapter has spent a lot of time telling you what to limit during treatment. Before closing, it's worth being precise about what "temporary" means and what it doesn't.

The food restrictions in the treatment-phase framework are genuinely temporary. They exist to reduce bacterial fuel during active eradication. Once treatment is complete and reintroduction begins properly, the goal is to expand your diet — not to stay restricted forever. A permanently restricted diet is not a successful outcome. It's a managed misery that continues to harm your microbiome in different ways.

The eating pattern — three meals, roughly four to five hours apart, no snacking — is not temporary.

This is not a punishment. It's a description of how the gut's cleaning mechanism works. The MMC requires fasted time. You can eat three full, satisfying, unrestricted meals. You can eat more than you've been eating, if SIBO has made you afraid of food. What you're changing is the gap, not the volume.

This is also the most sustainable change in this entire book. It doesn't require a prescription. It doesn't require a test. It doesn't require insurance approval or a doctor who understands SIBO. It requires understanding the mechanism and working with it.

You've spent years looking for the right food.
It was never about the food.
It was about the window — and the window is yours to set.

## What This Means For You

During SIBO treatment, the most important dietary decision you make is not what you eat — it's when you stop eating.

Four to five hours between meals. Nothing caloric in between. Water, black coffee, plain tea are the only exceptions. This gives the migrating motor complex the fasted window it needs to complete cleaning cycles and support whatever antimicrobial protocol you're running.

While in active treatment, reduce fermentable substrate without eliminating it entirely. Cooked vegetables over raw. White rice and potatoes as your working starches. Any protein source. Avoid the foods in the sidebar above — not forever, just for now. Skip the fermented foods, the protein bars with inulin, the apples, and the probiotics until treatment is complete.

Hold the restrictions lightly. They're a treatment tool, not an identity.

Chapter 7 covers reintroduction: when to start, what order matters, how to read your responses, and why going slowly at that stage pays dividends in a way that no other part of this process does.

The restriction is almost over. The pattern of when you eat is something you'll keep — not because it's a rule, but because once you understand the MMC, it stops feeling like restriction and starts feeling like maintenance. There's a difference. You'll recognise it when you get there.
