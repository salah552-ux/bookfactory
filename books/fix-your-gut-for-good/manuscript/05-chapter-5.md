# Chapter Five
## The Treatment Protocol: What Works, What Doesn't, and What Order Matters

You took rifaximin. You took it correctly — fourteen days, three times daily, exactly as prescribed. At the end of two weeks you felt noticeably better. Maybe significantly better. And then, somewhere between week six and week twelve, the bloating came back. The pressure. The urgency. The exact pattern you recognised.

That wasn't the drug failing. That was the drug doing its job — and stopping there. Treatment for SIBO is not a menu. It's a sequence. And every part of that sequence that was skipped is sitting in the gap between why you felt better and why you're back here.

Treatment for SIBO is not a menu. You don't pick the item that appeals to you and call it done. It's a sequence — and the sequence has a logic. Understanding that logic is the difference between a treatment that holds and one that doesn't.

This chapter covers what is currently available for SIBO treatment, what the evidence actually says about each option, and — most importantly — the order in which the pieces fit together. Some of what you read will be familiar. Some will directly contradict what you've been told or tried. That's expected. Let's work through it.

## Before Treatment Starts: The Pre-Condition

There is one thing that must come before the antibiotic, the herbal protocol, or the elemental diet. It is not optional, and skipping it is the single most reliable predictor of treatment failure.

Know your SIBO type.

Chapter 1 laid out why type matters. Here it matters in the most concrete possible way: the treatment for hydrogen-dominant SIBO and the treatment for intestinal methanogen overgrowth are different drugs, different doses, sometimes different durations. Treating methane overgrowth with the hydrogen protocol — rifaximin alone — is a well-documented route to a treatment that appears to fail. The rifaximin isn't failing. It's doing its job on bacteria. Archaea, which produce methane, are not bacteria. Rifaximin alone doesn't adequately clear them. You need a second agent.

If you do not know your SIBO type — if you have never had a breath test that measured all three gases, or if you had a test that only measured hydrogen — you are choosing treatment blind. That may be the position you're in because testing is imperfect and access is limited. Chapter 3 covered the limitations honestly. But if there is any way to establish your dominant gas type before treatment, do it. It changes what you take.

What you observed on your breath test graph matters here. Predominantly rising hydrogen? Predominantly rising methane? Flat hydrogen with sulfurous symptoms? Each points toward a different protocol. Use it.

## The Antibiotic Protocols

### Hydrogen-Dominant SIBO: Rifaximin

Rifaximin is the most studied antibiotic for SIBO treatment, and for hydrogen-dominant SIBO it is the appropriate first-line agent. It has several properties that make it well-suited to gut treatment: it is minimally absorbed into the bloodstream, meaning it acts locally in the small intestine where it's needed and has limited systemic effects; it has a broad enough spectrum to cover the gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria typically involved in hydrogen SIBO; and it has a relatively favorable side effect profile compared to many systemic antibiotics.

The standard dosing is 550mg three times daily for fourteen days. Some clinicians use 400mg three times daily for fourteen days. The evidence base is stronger for the 550mg dose.

Response rates in clinical studies for hydrogen-dominant SIBO run at roughly 60 to 70 percent for breath test normalisation. That means roughly one in three patients does not achieve normalisation with a single rifaximin course. If you are in that group — if you completed rifaximin and felt no meaningful improvement, or felt improvement and then relapsed within weeks — this is not treatment failure in any meaningful sense. It is an incomplete match between the drug and the situation, and there are clear next steps.

Non-response or rapid relapse after rifaximin points toward one of several possibilities: the wrong SIBO type identified (check for methane), inadequate dose or duration, significant biofilm formation reducing antibiotic access, or a root cause so dominant — severe MMC dysfunction, for example — that bacterial recolonisation is outpacing the antibiotic faster than a single course can manage. These are diagnosable and addressable problems, not a signal that treatment is futile.

### Methane-Dominant IMO: The Dual Protocol

Intestinal methanogen overgrowth requires a different approach, and this is where a substantial proportion of "treatment-resistant" cases find the explanation.

Rifaximin alone has limited efficacy against archaea. The current best-evidence protocol combines rifaximin with a second agent that has activity against methanogens — specifically neomycin or metronidazole.

**Rifaximin + Neomycin:** The most studied combination. Neomycin is an aminoglycoside antibiotic that, unlike rifaximin, is absorbed to a small degree, giving it some systemic reach. The standard dosing is rifaximin 550mg three times daily combined with neomycin 500mg twice daily, for fourteen days. Studies comparing this combination to rifaximin alone in methane-dominant patients have found meaningfully higher breath test normalisation rates for the combination.

**Rifaximin + Metronidazole:** Used when neomycin is contraindicated or unavailable, or when clinical preference favors it. Metronidazole has established anti-archaeal activity. It has a more significant side effect profile than neomycin in many patients — nausea, metallic taste, neurological effects with prolonged use — and is typically used at the lowest effective dose for the shortest necessary duration.

The decision between these options belongs to your prescribing clinician, who can weigh your specific history, other medications, and tolerance. What you can bring to that conversation is clarity about your SIBO type and a direct request for the combination protocol if methane is your dominant finding.

### Hydrogen Sulfide SIBO: The Emerging Picture

This is the category where certainty runs out fastest, and honesty requires acknowledging that.

Hydrogen sulfide SIBO is recognised as a distinct clinical entity, but its treatment protocols are less standardised than those for hydrogen or methane overgrowth. Several approaches are used in clinical practice:

**Bismuth-based protocols.** Bismuth subsalicylate — the active ingredient in Pepto-Bismol — binds hydrogen sulfide in the gut and reduces its concentration. Some practitioners use it as part of a SIBO treatment protocol for hydrogen sulfide overgrowth, typically combined with rifaximin. The evidence base is primarily mechanistic and case-based rather than from randomised controlled trials. It is not a proven protocol. It is a rational approach used by clinicians working with a patient population that has limited options.

**Low-sulfur dietary support.** During treatment for hydrogen sulfide overgrowth, reducing dietary sulfur can lower the substrate available for hydrogen sulfide-producing bacteria. This is not a cure. It's a supportive measure that some patients find reduces symptom burden while treatment proceeds. High-sulfur foods include cruciferous vegetables, eggs, onions, garlic, and certain protein sources.

**Molybdenum.** An essential trace mineral that acts as a cofactor for enzymes involved in sulfur metabolism. Some practitioners use it as a supplement alongside hydrogen sulfide SIBO treatment to support sulfur processing. The evidence is preliminary.

If your SIBO type is hydrogen sulfide, your treatment conversation requires a clinician familiar with the current state of this evolving area. Generic SIBO protocols written for hydrogen overgrowth will not be adequate.

## Herbal Antimicrobials: What the Evidence Actually Shows

Herbal antimicrobials are widely used for SIBO treatment, and the evidence is more substantive than their "natural" framing sometimes suggests — though also more limited than their advocates sometimes claim.

A 2014 randomised controlled trial by Chedid and colleagues compared an herbal antimicrobial protocol to rifaximin in patients with abnormal lactulose breath tests. The herbal protocol involved two commercially available products — FC Cidal and Dysbiocide — and the study found similar rates of breath test normalisation between the herbal and rifaximin groups. That is a notable finding, though the study was small and its results cannot be generalized to all herbal approaches without caution. It is a single study with a specific patient population and a specific herbal combination.

Several herbal compounds have documented in vitro antimicrobial activity against bacteria relevant to SIBO. These include:

**Berberine** — an alkaloid found in plants including goldenseal and barberry. Has demonstrated broad-spectrum antibacterial and antifungal activity in laboratory studies. Some evidence from clinical settings for gut dysbiosis management.

**Oregano oil (carvacrol)** — the active compound in oregano has documented antibacterial properties. Used widely in clinical herbal SIBO protocols, often in enteric-coated form to ensure delivery to the small intestine rather than absorption in the stomach.

**Allicin** — the active compound in garlic (formed when garlic is crushed or chopped). Has broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity and is one of the few compounds studied specifically against the archaea involved in methane overgrowth. Some practitioners use allicin as a component of IMO protocols.

**Neem** — used in Ayurvedic medicine with broad-spectrum antimicrobial properties.

Herbal protocols are typically run for four weeks — longer than antibiotic courses — because the antimicrobial activity is generally less potent and requires more time. They may be a reasonable option for patients who cannot access prescription antibiotics, who have had adverse reactions to rifaximin, or who prefer a non-pharmaceutical approach. They are not a superior option to rifaximin for hydrogen SIBO on current evidence. They are an alternative with some supporting data and a long track record of clinical use.

One important note: herbal does not mean without effect on the gut microbiome. These compounds are antimicrobial. They affect bacteria. The use of herbal antimicrobials warrants the same attention to protocol sequencing, prokinetics, and repair as antibiotic-based treatment.

## The Elemental Diet: When All Else Fails — and When It's the Right First Move

The elemental diet is not a diet in any conventional sense. It is a medical nutritional intervention — a complete meal replacement using pre-digested nutrients (amino acids rather than intact proteins, simple sugars rather than complex carbohydrates, and fats in easily absorbed forms) that the body can absorb in the upper small intestine before bacteria can ferment them.

The mechanism is straightforward: by delivering nutrients in their fully broken-down form, the elemental diet starves small intestinal bacteria of the fermentable substrate they require. With no fuel available, bacterial populations decline. Breath test normalisation rates in the available studies are high — a study by Pimentel and colleagues (2004) found that 80% of patients normalized their breath test after a two-week elemental diet course, including some who had not responded to antibiotic treatment.

The practical reality is that the elemental diet is genuinely difficult to maintain. The formulas taste poor. They are expensive. Two to three weeks of consuming nothing but formula — no solid food, no texture, no mealtime normalcy — is a significant life disruption. Compliance in real-world settings is accordingly lower than in controlled studies.

Who is the elemental diet most appropriate for?

- Patients who have failed multiple antibiotic courses
- Patients with known or suspected rifaximin resistance or non-response
- Patients with extremely severe symptoms who need rapid improvement before starting standard protocols
- Patients who need to avoid antibiotics for specific clinical reasons

The elemental diet can also be used as a diagnostic tool — if symptoms improve dramatically during the course, that constitutes meaningful evidence of bacterial fermentation as the driver.

If the elemental diet is being considered, it should be done under clinical supervision, particularly with attention to caloric adequacy, electrolyte balance, and any underlying conditions that might be affected by complete liquid nutrition.

## What Happens During Treatment

The first week of antimicrobial treatment — antibiotic or herbal — is often not a comfortable week. This is worth knowing in advance so that early discomfort is not interpreted as treatment failure.

As bacteria are killed off in large numbers, they release bacterial cell wall components and metabolic byproducts that can temporarily worsen symptoms. Increased bloating, brain fog, headaches, fatigue, skin reactions, and general malaise in the first five to seven days of treatment are common. This is sometimes called a die-off reaction or Herxheimer-like response. It is not a sign that the treatment is wrong. It is, in many cases, evidence that the treatment is working.

Most patients find that symptoms begin to improve meaningfully by the second week. Some see improvement earlier. A minority feel no improvement at any point during the course — that is the group that warrants re-evaluation of type, dose, or protocol.

**During treatment, these things interfere with outcomes:**

- Continuing to eat fermentable carbohydrates at high volumes (feeding the bacteria faster than the antimicrobial can clear them)
- Probiotics during active antimicrobial treatment (adding bacterial organisms while trying to reduce them — timing matters)
- Interrupting the course early because you feel better (the full course is required for complete clearance)
- High-stress periods that suppress motility and allow rapid recolonisation

**During treatment, these things support outcomes:**

- Extending the gap between meals to allow MMC cycles (aim for four to five hours between meals during treatment)
- Adequate hydration
- Adequate sleep — autonomic regulation during sleep supports motility
- Starting prokinetics at the appropriate point

## The Sequencing Principle: Treatment Is Not Just the Antibiotic

This is the single most important structural point in this chapter.

The antibiotic or herbal protocol is phase one of a two-part eradication process. Phase one clears the existing overgrowth. Phase two prevents immediate reseeding — by starting the motility support that keeps bacteria from re-establishing before the gut environment has time to stabilise.

Prokinetics — medications or supplements that improve gut motility and support MMC function — are a core part of SIBO eradication sequencing, not an afterthought. In protocols used by the most experienced SIBO clinicians, prokinetics are typically introduced partway through or immediately after the antimicrobial course, and continued for weeks to months afterward.

The reason for timing matters: starting prokinetics too early in the antimicrobial course can theoretically accelerate transit and reduce antibiotic contact time in the small intestine. Starting them at or near the end of the course — and crucially, continuing them into the post-treatment period — supports the MMC in doing the job it failed to do in the first place.

Chapter 8 covers prokinetics in full — the pharmaceutical options, the evidence-based natural options, the dosing, and the duration. For now, the key point is this: if your treatment plan ends with the antibiotic and doesn't include prokinetics, it is incomplete. Raise this with your prescribing clinician.

| SIBO Type | First-Line Protocol | Duration | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|-------|
| Hydrogen-dominant | Rifaximin 550mg TID | 14 days | Most studied; ~60–70% normalisation |
| Methane-dominant (IMO) | Rifaximin 550mg TID + Neomycin 500mg BID | 14 days | Combination essential; rifaximin alone inadequate |
| Hydrogen sulfide | Rifaximin + Bismuth-based protocol | 14 days (variable) | Less standardised; requires clinician familiar with H2S protocols |

*This table reflects current clinical approaches. Specific drug choices, doses, and durations should be determined in partnership with your prescribing clinician, who can account for your specific history, type, and complicating factors.*

## What to Do When the First Course Doesn't Work

Non-response to a first antibiotic course is not the end of the road. It is a diagnostic signal.

**Check the type again.** If you were treated for hydrogen SIBO and didn't respond, was methane or hydrogen sulfide adequately ruled out? A three-gas breath test is the relevant question here.

**Consider biofilm.** SIBO bacteria can form biofilm — a protective matrix that significantly reduces antibiotic penetration. Some practitioners use biofilm disruptors before or alongside antimicrobial treatment — N-acetylcysteine (NAC), serrapeptase, or specific enzyme combinations. The evidence base is preliminary but mechanistically sound.

**Consider the elemental diet.** If two antibiotic courses have failed, the elemental diet is a rational next step for a different mechanism of action.

**Revisit the root cause.** A course that clears the overgrowth and sees immediate rapid relapse is almost certainly a root cause problem, not a treatment inadequacy. The antibiotic is doing its job. Something else is refilling the tank faster than the antibiotic can empty it.

**Consider a longer or repeated course.** In severe or treatment-resistant cases, some clinicians use extended courses or repeated sequential treatment. This requires careful clinical judgment about benefit versus risk, particularly regarding the gut microbiome impact of repeated antibiotic exposure.

## One Thing That Is Always Within Your Control

The pharmaceutical and clinical dimensions of this chapter depend on access — to testing, to prescribers, to the right protocol. That access is unequally distributed, and if you're in a healthcare system that doesn't recognise SIBO readily, fighting for the right prescription is a real obstacle.

But one thing does not require a prescription. It does not require testing. It requires only understanding the mechanism and adjusting a habit.

The space between your meals matters as much as what you take.

A four-to-five-hour gap between meals — no snacking, nothing caloric between meals — allows the MMC to complete a cycle. This one change, done consistently, is one of the most effective things you can do to support whatever treatment you're running and reduce the speed of recolonisation after that treatment ends.

You were probably told to eat frequently. Many people with gut issues are told this — small meals, little and often, don't let your stomach get empty. For most gut conditions, the reasoning is sound. For SIBO, it works against you at the most fundamental level. The gut's cleaning mechanism requires the gut to be empty. Give it the chance to run.

This is not a treatment. It is a pre-condition for treatment to hold. And it costs nothing.

You have a treatment map now. Not a list of things that might help — an ordered protocol with clinical rationale at every step. Know your type. Match the protocol. Add the second agent if methane is present. Start the prokinetics before the window closes.

The sequence is not arbitrary. Every step is where it is because putting it somewhere else produces a predictable failure. What you've been through wasn't bad luck and it wasn't a broken body. It was the right treatment in the wrong order, at the wrong time, without the piece that holds it all together.

The next chapter tells you the most important thing about eating during SIBO isn't what you eat. It's when you stop. That single shift explains more failed recoveries than almost anything else in this book — and it costs nothing.
