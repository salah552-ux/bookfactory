# Phase-Appropriate Recipes
## What to Eat at Every Stage of SIBO Recovery

You've spent months being careful. Reading labels, asking about ingredients, bringing your own food places, turning down things you used to love without explanation because the explanation takes longer than anyone wants to hear.

These recipes exist for that period. Not to cure anything — food doesn't do that. Not to replace the protocol — the protocol is what you've been building across eleven chapters. These recipes exist because eating during SIBO recovery is genuinely confusing, and having a meal that's already been figured out is one less thing to be confused about.

They're organised around the same three phases you've been following: active treatment, reintroduction, and maintenance. The rules that apply to each phase are already established. The recipes just apply them to dinner.

The logic is straightforward. During active antimicrobial treatment, the goal is to stop feeding the overgrowth while you're trying to eliminate it. That means keeping fermentable substrate low — cooked vegetables over raw, white rice over lentils, no garlic or onion anywhere. Once treatment is complete and you've seen meaningful symptom improvement, you begin reintroduction: first low-fermentation foods if you haven't been eating them, then moderate-FODMAP categories one at a time, with at least three days between each new introduction. In maintenance — once full reintroduction is complete and tolerance is established — the kitchen opens up considerably, though the eating window (4–5 hours between meals, nothing caloric in between) stays in place permanently.

None of these recipes will make your SIBO go away faster. That's not what food does during this process. What food can do is avoid making things worse, keep your symptoms manageable while treatment does its job, and give you a structured path back to normal eating that doesn't turn into a relapse trigger.

Every recipe in this section follows the food guidance established in Chapters 6 and 7. Quantities are given in grams with approximate cup or ounce equivalents where useful. Cooking times assume a standard oven preheated to the stated temperature.

*The recipes in this section are designed to align with the dietary guidance established throughout this book. They are intended as practical starting points, not medical prescriptions. Individual tolerances vary significantly during SIBO recovery. Consult your healthcare provider or a registered dietitian if you have questions about specific foods during your treatment phase.*

## Phase 1 Recipes — During Active Treatment

These six recipes are built for the weeks you're actively taking antimicrobials. Every ingredient is low-fermentation. No garlic, no onion, no apples, no legumes, no fermented foods. No protein bars with chicory root. Flavour comes from herbs, lemon, ginger, and salt — all of which are safe during treatment. All vegetables are cooked, not raw.

The eating window still applies here. Don't graze. Three meals, 4–5 hours apart, nothing caloric between them.

*The recipes in this section are designed to align with the dietary guidance established throughout this book. They are intended as practical starting points, not medical prescriptions. Individual tolerances vary significantly during SIBO recovery. Consult your healthcare provider or a registered dietitian if you have questions about specific foods during your treatment phase.*

### 1. Baked Salmon with White Rice and Roasted Courgette

**SIBO Note:** White rice is one of the lowest-fermentation carbohydrates available — it produces minimal gas during digestion and gives your gut almost nothing to ferment while the antimicrobials are working. Courgette is low-FODMAP in standard portions and tolerated well when roasted. No garlic, no onion. This is a full, balanced meal that won't undermine your treatment.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 25 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 2 salmon fillets (approx. 150g / 5oz each)
- 200g / 1 cup dry white rice
- 2 medium courgettes (approx. 300g / 10oz total), sliced into rounds
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- 1 lemon, halved
- Small bunch of fresh dill or flat-leaf parsley
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

1. Heat the oven to 200°C / 400°F. Line a baking tray with parchment paper.
2. Toss the courgette rounds with 1 tbsp olive oil, a pinch of salt, and black pepper. Spread them in a single layer on the baking tray. Roast for 20–25 minutes until softened and lightly golden at the edges.
3. Cook the rice according to packet instructions. Remove from heat, fluff with a fork, and keep covered.
4. Pat the salmon fillets dry. Rub with the remaining olive oil, season well with salt and pepper. Place on a second lined tray or nestle alongside the courgette for the final 12–15 minutes of oven time, skin-side down.
5. Bake the salmon until the flesh is opaque and flakes easily when pressed — 12 minutes for medium, 15 for well done.
6. Squeeze half the lemon over the salmon. Scatter herbs over everything.
7. Serve the salmon over or alongside the rice, with the courgette on the plate.

### 2. Plain Chicken and Potato Soup

**SIBO Note:** This soup uses no onion and no garlic — both of which are high-FODMAP and would directly feed the overgrowth during treatment. Flavour here comes from fresh ginger, bay leaves, and herbs, all of which are safe during the active eradication phase. The potato contributes resistant starch, but cooked and hot (not cooled), it's digested in the small intestine rather than fermented further down. A warming, filling meal that won't provoke symptoms.

**Serves:** 3–4 | **Prep time:** 15 minutes | **Cook time:** 35 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 2 boneless chicken breasts or 4 bone-in chicken thighs (approx. 500g / 17oz)
- 3 medium potatoes (approx. 400g / 14oz), peeled and cut into 2cm chunks
- 2 medium carrots, peeled and sliced
- 1.2 litres / 5 cups water or plain chicken stock (check the label — no onion, garlic, or inulin)
- 1 thumb-sized piece of fresh ginger (approx. 2cm), peeled and finely grated
- 2 bay leaves
- Small bunch of fresh thyme or rosemary
- Salt and black pepper
- 1 tbsp olive oil

**Method**

1. Heat the olive oil in a large saucepan over medium heat. Add the ginger and stir for 30 seconds until fragrant.
2. Add the chicken to the pan. Brown lightly on both sides — 2–3 minutes per side. You're building flavour, not cooking it through yet.
3. Pour in the stock or water. Add the bay leaves and thyme. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce to a simmer.
4. Add the potatoes and carrots. Season with salt and pepper.
5. Simmer for 25–30 minutes until the chicken is cooked through and the potato chunks are tender.
6. Remove the chicken, shred it roughly with two forks, and return it to the pot.
7. Remove the bay leaves and thyme stalks. Taste and adjust seasoning. Serve hot.

### 3. Turkey and White Rice Bowl with Cooked Carrots

**SIBO Note:** Ground turkey is a lean, easily digested protein with essentially zero fermentable content. Paired with white rice and well-cooked carrots — both low-FODMAP in standard portions — this is one of the most gut-quiet meals you can make during treatment. Season with ginger and fresh herbs rather than any allium-based sauces or stocks.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 20 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 300g / 10oz turkey mince
- 200g / 1 cup dry white rice
- 3 medium carrots, peeled and cut into coins
- 1 tbsp olive oil or butter
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, finely grated
- 1 tsp ground turmeric (optional — low-FODMAP and may be tolerated well)
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh flat-leaf parsley to serve

**Method**

1. Cook the rice according to packet instructions. Keep covered and warm.
2. Steam or boil the carrots until tender — about 8–10 minutes. Drain and set aside.
3. Heat the oil in a frying pan over medium-high heat. Add the grated ginger and stir for 20 seconds.
4. Add the turkey mince. Break it apart with a wooden spoon and cook for 8–10 minutes, stirring regularly, until cooked through and lightly browned. Season with salt, pepper, and turmeric if using.
5. Divide the rice between two bowls. Spoon the turkey over the top.
6. Add the cooked carrots alongside. Scatter parsley over everything and serve.

### 4. Baked Cod with Mashed Potato and Steamed Green Beans

**SIBO Note:** Cod is a white fish with a mild flavour and no fermentable content whatsoever. Mashed potato — made with butter and salt, not garlic or cream-based additives — is one of the safest starchy foods during active treatment. Green beans are low-FODMAP in portions up to approximately 75g (approximately two-thirds of a cup, loosely packed), steamed rather than raw to keep them easy to digest. This is a straightforward, filling dinner.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 30 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 2 cod fillets (approx. 150g / 5oz each)
- 4 medium potatoes (approx. 500g / 17oz), peeled and quartered
- 150g / generous 1 cup green beans, trimmed
- 2 tbsp butter
- 2–3 tbsp whole milk or lactose-free milk
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 1 lemon, halved
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh dill or parsley

**Method**

1. Heat the oven to 190°C / 375°F.
2. Boil the potatoes in salted water for 18–20 minutes until completely tender. Drain well.
3. Mash the potatoes with the butter and enough milk to reach a smooth, creamy consistency. Season with salt and pepper. Keep warm.
4. While the potatoes cook, pat the cod fillets dry. Rub with olive oil, season with salt and pepper.
5. Place the cod on a lined baking tray. Bake for 14–16 minutes until the flesh is opaque and flakes when pressed.
6. Steam the green beans for 4–5 minutes until bright and just tender. Don't overcook them to mush — tender but still with a little give.
7. Squeeze lemon over the cod. Serve over a generous scoop of mash with the green beans alongside. Scatter herbs over the top.

### 5. Scrambled Eggs with Wilted Spinach and White Rice

**SIBO Note:** Eggs are one of the most complete, lowest-fermentation proteins available. Spinach is low-FODMAP and safe during treatment — wilted is better than raw during the active phase to reduce any digestive load. White rice keeps the fermentable substrate as low as possible. This works equally well as breakfast or a light dinner. No onion, no garlic, no seasoning blends that might contain either.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 5 minutes | **Cook time:** 15 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 4 large eggs
- 200g / 1 cup dry white rice
- 100g / 3.5oz fresh baby spinach
- 2 tbsp butter
- 2 tbsp whole milk or lactose-free milk
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh chives or flat-leaf parsley (optional)

**Method**

1. Cook the rice according to packet instructions.
2. While the rice cooks, crack the eggs into a bowl. Add the milk, season with salt and pepper, and beat together until just combined — don't overwork them.
3. Melt half the butter in a non-stick pan over low-medium heat. Add the spinach and stir until it wilts completely — 1–2 minutes. Remove and set aside.
4. Wipe the pan, add the remaining butter, and return to low heat. Pour in the eggs.
5. Cook slowly, stirring with a spatula every 20–30 seconds, pulling the eggs from the edges toward the centre. Remove from heat while they still look slightly underdone — they'll carry on cooking. Soft and just-set is what you're after.
6. Divide the rice between two plates. Pile the scrambled eggs on top. Add the wilted spinach alongside. Scatter herbs if using.

### 6. Herb-Roasted Chicken Thighs with Roasted Baby Potatoes

**SIBO Note:** Bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs roast beautifully with just olive oil, salt, and herbs — no garlic, no onion marinades needed. Baby potatoes are low-FODMAP in standard portions. Roasting both on the same tray keeps this to one pan and about 40 minutes of hands-off time. A reliable dinner for the weeks when treatment already takes enough mental bandwidth.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 40 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 4 bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs (approx. 600g / 21oz total)
- 400g / 14oz baby potatoes, halved
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- 1 tsp dried thyme (or a few fresh thyme sprigs)
- 1 lemon, halved
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh flat-leaf parsley to serve

**Method**

1. Heat the oven to 210°C / 410°F.
2. Toss the halved baby potatoes with 2 tbsp olive oil, the dried herbs, salt, and pepper. Spread them cut-side down across a large roasting tray.
3. Pat the chicken thighs dry with kitchen paper. Rub all over with the remaining 1 tbsp olive oil, season generously with salt, pepper, and a little extra oregano.
4. Nestle the chicken thighs skin-side up among the potatoes.
5. Roast for 35–40 minutes until the chicken skin is deep golden and crispy, and the potatoes are cooked through and slightly caramelised on their cut sides. The internal temperature of the chicken should reach 74°C / 165°F.
6. Squeeze the lemon over everything in the last 2 minutes of cooking, or just before serving.
7. Scatter parsley over the top and serve directly from the tray.

## Phase 2 Recipes — Post-Treatment and Reintroduction

You've completed your antimicrobial course. You've seen meaningful symptom improvement. And you're probably still scared to eat anything that isn't plain white rice — that's normal, and this section is built exactly for that. Now reintroduction begins.

The principle from Chapter 7 applies to everything in this section: one new food category at a time, at least three days minimum before introducing the next. These recipes introduce slightly more variety than Phase 1 — a few moderate-FODMAP ingredients, a broader vegetable range — but they're still structured carefully. The SIBO Note on each recipe flags exactly what's being introduced and why it's appropriate at this stage.

Spring onion greens appear in one recipe here. They're lower-FODMAP than the white bulb — the green tops are safe in moderate amounts and act as a mild flavour bridge. That said, if spring onions haven't been your first reintroduction category, add them separately and give yourself the three days.

*The recipes in this section are designed to align with the dietary guidance established throughout this book. They are intended as practical starting points, not medical prescriptions. Individual tolerances vary significantly during SIBO recovery. Consult your healthcare provider or a registered dietitian if you have questions about specific foods during your treatment phase.*

### 7. Grilled Chicken with Quinoa and Roasted Courgette

**SIBO Note:** Quinoa is a moderate-FODMAP food — appropriate as a deliberate reintroduction category once treatment is complete and baseline symptoms have settled. If you're trialling quinoa for the first time, this is the recipe to use. Keep the portion to around 130g cooked (roughly three-quarters of a cup) to stay within a manageable FODMAP load. Courgette is low-FODMAP in standard portions and should already be tolerated from Phase 1.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 25 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 2 boneless, skinless chicken breasts (approx. 300g / 10oz total)
- 170g / 1 cup dry quinoa
- 2 medium courgettes, sliced into half-moons
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- 1 lemon
- 1 tsp dried oregano or mixed herbs
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh flat-leaf parsley

**Method**

1. Rinse the quinoa under cold water — this removes the saponin coating that can make it taste bitter. Cook in 360ml / 1.5 cups of water: bring to a boil, cover, reduce to a simmer, cook for 15 minutes. Remove from heat and leave covered for 5 minutes, then fluff with a fork. It should look like tiny coiled springs.
2. Heat the oven to 200°C / 400°F. Toss the courgette with 1 tbsp olive oil, salt, and pepper. Spread on a lined tray and roast for 20–22 minutes until softened and catching colour at the edges.
3. Brush the chicken breasts with the remaining olive oil. Season with salt, pepper, and dried herbs.
4. Heat a grill pan or heavy frying pan over medium-high heat. Cook the chicken for 5–6 minutes per side until cooked through and lightly charred at the edges. Let it rest for 3 minutes before you slice — the carry-over heat matters more than it seems.
5. Divide the quinoa between two plates. Top with the sliced chicken. Add the roasted courgette alongside.
6. Squeeze lemon over everything. Scatter parsley. This is a meal worth eating.

### 8. Baked Salmon with Sweet Potato and Steamed Broccoli

**SIBO Note:** Sweet potato's low-FODMAP threshold is approximately 70g per serve — one medium sweet potato (~250g) between two people puts each portion above that threshold and into moderate territory. That's appropriate for Phase 2 as a deliberate reintroduction: treat it as a new category, give yourself three days to assess tolerance, and scale the portion down if you're still early in reintroduction. Broccoli is also moderate-FODMAP — stick to approximately 75g per serving (roughly three-quarters of a cup of florets) and apply the same three-day window before moving to the next category.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 35 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 2 salmon fillets (approx. 150g / 5oz each)
- 1 medium sweet potato (approx. 250g / 9oz), peeled and cut into 2cm cubes
- 150g / 2 cups broccoli florets
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- 1 lemon
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh dill or chives

**Method**

1. Heat the oven to 200°C / 400°F. Toss the sweet potato cubes with 1 tbsp olive oil, salt, and pepper. Spread on a lined baking tray and roast for 20 minutes — they need the head start.
2. After 20 minutes, push the sweet potato to one side of the tray. Add the salmon fillets, rub with remaining olive oil, season with salt and pepper. Return to the oven for 12–15 minutes until the salmon flakes easily when pressed in the thickest part.
3. Steam the broccoli florets for 4–5 minutes while the salmon finishes — just tender, still bright green. Overcooked broccoli has nothing going for it.
4. Plate the salmon with the sweet potato and broccoli alongside. Squeeze lemon over the salmon and scatter dill or chives over everything.

### 9. Beef and Vegetable Stir-Fry with White Rice

**SIBO Note:** This stir-fry uses spring onion greens (the green tops only — not the white bulb, which is higher FODMAP). Green tops are lower-FODMAP in portions up to 75g and make a reasonable flavour bridge for someone who's been avoiding alliums during treatment. Red capsicum and bok choy are both low-FODMAP in standard portions. No garlic, no onion. If you haven't separately confirmed spring onion tolerance yet, leave them out and add a little extra ginger instead.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 15 minutes | **Cook time:** 12 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 300g / 10oz beef sirloin or flank steak, thinly sliced against the grain
- 200g / 1 cup dry white rice
- 1 medium red capsicum / bell pepper, deseeded and sliced
- 150g / 2 cups bok choy, roughly chopped
- 4 spring onion stalks, green tops only, thinly sliced
- 1 thumb-sized piece of fresh ginger, peeled and grated
- 2 tbsp sesame oil or sunflower oil
- 2 tbsp tamari or soy sauce (gluten-free tamari if required — check for additives)
- 1 tsp rice wine vinegar
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

1. Cook the rice according to packet instructions. Keep warm.
2. Mix the tamari, rice wine vinegar, and a pinch of pepper in a small bowl. Set aside — you want the sauce ready before anything hits the pan.
3. Heat a wok or large frying pan over very high heat until it begins to smoke slightly. Add 1 tbsp of the oil. This is the step people skip and then wonder why their stir-fry steams instead of sears.
4. Add the beef in a single layer. Don't stir for 60–90 seconds — you want colour, not grey. Toss once, cook another 30 seconds. Remove and set aside.
5. Add the remaining oil to the pan. Add the ginger and stir 15 seconds. Add the capsicum and stir-fry 2 minutes. Add the bok choy and stir-fry another 2 minutes — the stems need longer than the leaves.
6. Return the beef to the pan. Pour over the sauce. Toss everything together for 30 seconds over high heat.
7. Serve over white rice. Scatter the spring onion greens over the top.

### 10. Turkey Meatballs with Courgette Noodles and Simple Tomato Sauce

**SIBO Note:** Tomatoes are low-FODMAP in moderate amounts — roughly 65g per serving (about three cherry tomatoes or a few tablespoons of passata). This recipe uses passata rather than a jarred sauce, which often contains onion, garlic, or other additives that would be inappropriate during reintroduction. Courgette noodles (spiralised or ribbon-peeled) replace pasta here — low-FODMAP and a straightforward way to introduce more vegetable variety without adding fermentable load. Tomatoes are being introduced as a category if this is new — give it three days before the next addition. Use gluten-free breadcrumbs in the meatballs during Phase 2 — standard wheat breadcrumbs contain fructans (a FODMAP) and would introduce a second untested variable alongside the tomatoes, making it impossible to read your response accurately.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 20 minutes | **Cook time:** 25 minutes

**Ingredients**

For the meatballs:
- 300g / 10oz ground turkey
- 1 egg
- 2 tbsp plain breadcrumbs or gluten-free breadcrumbs
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- 1 tsp dried thyme
- Salt and black pepper
- 1 tbsp olive oil

For the sauce:
- 250g / 1 cup plain passata (tomatoes only — check the label)
- 1 tsp olive oil
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated (optional — adds warmth without alliums)
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- Salt and black pepper

For the courgette noodles:
- 3 medium courgettes (approx. 400g / 14oz), spiralised or peeled into ribbons with a vegetable peeler

**Method**

1. Combine the turkey, egg, breadcrumbs, oregano, thyme, salt, and pepper in a bowl. Mix until just combined — overworking makes them dense. Roll into 14–16 meatballs roughly the size of a golf ball.
2. Heat the olive oil in a large frying pan over medium-high heat. Brown the meatballs in batches — 2–3 minutes per side until golden on the outside. They don't need to be cooked through yet. Transfer to a plate.
3. In the same pan, add 1 tsp olive oil over medium heat. Add ginger if using and stir 20 seconds. Pour in the passata. Add the oregano, season with salt and pepper. The pan drippings from the meatballs will deepen the sauce — that's intentional.
4. Return the meatballs to the pan. Simmer in the sauce for 10–12 minutes over low heat, turning once, until cooked through.
5. While the meatballs finish, heat a dry non-stick pan over medium-high heat. Add the courgette noodles and toss for 2–3 minutes until just softened. Season lightly. Pull them off before they turn watery.
6. Serve the meatballs and sauce over the courgette noodles.

### 11. Prawn and Rice Noodle Bowl with Bok Choy and Ginger

**SIBO Note:** Prawns (shrimp) are a clean protein source with no fermentable content. Rice noodles are low-FODMAP in standard portions. Bok choy is low-FODMAP and well-tolerated by most people during reintroduction. Ginger provides flavour and has some evidence for supporting gastric motility (discussed in Chapter 8). This bowl comes together in under 20 minutes and is suitable for early-to-mid reintroduction once baseline symptoms have settled.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 10 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 250g / 9oz raw prawns, peeled and deveined (or cooked prawns — reduce cook time to 2 minutes)
- 160g / 5.5oz dried rice noodles
- 250g / 3 cups bok choy, halved or quartered depending on size
- 1 thumb-sized piece of fresh ginger, peeled and grated
- 2 tbsp sesame oil
- 2 tbsp tamari or low-sodium soy sauce
- 1 tsp rice wine vinegar
- 1 tsp sesame seeds (optional — low-FODMAP in small quantities)
- Fresh coriander or chives to serve

**Method**

1. Soak or cook the rice noodles according to packet instructions. Drain, rinse under cold water, set aside. Cold water stops them cooking further and keeps them from clumping.
2. Combine the tamari, rice wine vinegar, and 1 tbsp sesame oil in a small bowl. Set aside.
3. Heat the remaining sesame oil in a large pan or wok over high heat. Add the ginger and stir for 15 seconds — you'll smell it immediately.
4. Add the raw prawns in a single layer. Cook 90 seconds per side until pink and curled. Don't crowd the pan or they'll steam. Remove and set aside.
5. Add the bok choy to the pan. Stir-fry for 2–3 minutes until the leaves are wilted and the stems are just tender — they hold up longer than you'd expect.
6. Add the noodles and prawns back to the pan. Pour over the sauce. Toss everything together over high heat for 1 minute.
7. Divide between two bowls. Scatter coriander or chives. Sprinkle sesame seeds if using.

### 12. Omelette with Cheddar and Roasted Red Pepper

**SIBO Note:** Eggs remain as safe in reintroduction as they were in Phase 1. Cheddar is low-FODMAP — aged hard cheeses contain minimal lactose and are generally well-tolerated. Red capsicum is low-FODMAP. Roasting the pepper rather than serving it raw reduces digestive load slightly and improves flavour considerably. This is a fast, solid breakfast or light dinner that fits the eating window approach without requiring much planning.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 20 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 4 large eggs
- 60g / 2oz mature cheddar, grated
- 1 large red capsicum / bell pepper, halved and deseeded
- 2 tbsp butter
- 2 tbsp whole milk or lactose-free milk
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh chives or flat-leaf parsley

**Method**

1. Place the capsicum halves skin-side up under a hot grill / broiler for 10–12 minutes until the skin blisters and blackens. Remove, wrap in foil or cover with a bowl for 5 minutes — the steam loosens the skin. Peel off, slice the flesh into strips.
2. Beat the eggs with the milk, salt, and pepper.
3. Melt 1 tbsp butter in a non-stick frying pan (approx. 22cm / 9in) over medium heat. When the butter foams, pour in half the egg mixture.
4. Tilt the pan to spread the egg. As the edges set, draw them gently toward the centre with a spatula, letting the liquid egg run to the edges. Cook until the top is just barely set — about 2 minutes. You want it a little glossy still.
5. Scatter half the cheddar and half the roasted pepper strips over one half of the omelette. Fold the other half over. Slide onto a plate.
6. Repeat for the second omelette. Scatter herbs and serve immediately.

## Phase 3 Recipes — Maintenance and Long-Term Eating

Full reintroduction is complete. You've tested categories systematically, you know what your gut handles and what it doesn't, and you're eating a broader variety of food again. That's the point this entire protocol has been working toward.

The recipes in this section reflect that broader range. Some introduce foods that weren't appropriate during Phase 1 or early Phase 2 — lentils, avocado, brown rice, a fuller roster of Mediterranean vegetables. The SIBO Note on each flags what's new and why it belongs here rather than earlier.

The eating window remains. This isn't a phase you "complete" and then go back to grazing. The 4–5 hour gap between meals supports MMC function permanently. These recipes are designed to be substantial enough that you're genuinely satisfied for that window — enough protein, enough fat, enough volume.

*The recipes in this section are designed to align with the dietary guidance established throughout this book. They are intended as practical starting points, not medical prescriptions. Individual tolerances vary significantly during SIBO recovery. Consult your healthcare provider or a registered dietitian if you have questions about specific foods during your treatment phase.*

### 13. Sheet-Pan Chicken Thighs with Roasted Mediterranean Vegetables

**SIBO Note:** This recipe uses courgette, red and yellow capsicum, and cherry tomatoes — a broader vegetable roster than Phase 1 permits. All three are low-to-moderate FODMAP in standard portions and appropriate for maintenance once tolerance is established. The result is a full, deeply flavoured dinner that fits the eating window without requiring much active time in the kitchen.

**Serves:** 2–3 | **Prep time:** 15 minutes | **Cook time:** 40 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 4 bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs (approx. 600g / 21oz)
- 2 medium courgettes, cut into 2cm chunks
- 1 red capsicum, deseeded and cut into chunks
- 1 yellow capsicum, deseeded and cut into chunks
- 200g / 7oz cherry tomatoes, left whole
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- 1 tsp dried thyme
- 1 lemon
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh basil or flat-leaf parsley to serve

**Method**

1. Heat the oven to 210°C / 410°F.
2. Combine all the vegetables in a large roasting tray. Drizzle with 2 tbsp olive oil, scatter the dried herbs, season well with salt and pepper. Toss to coat and spread into a rough single layer — they need room to roast, not steam.
3. Pat the chicken thighs dry. Rub with the remaining 1 tbsp olive oil, season generously on both sides. Nestle skin-side up on top of the vegetables.
4. Roast for 38–42 minutes until the chicken skin is deeply golden and crispy, the vegetables are soft and slightly caramelised at the edges, and the cherry tomatoes have burst and released their juice into the pan. That juice is half the point.
5. Squeeze lemon over the tray. Rest for 5 minutes before serving.
6. Scatter fresh herbs and serve directly from the tray.

### 14. Beef Stir-Fry with Brown Rice and Mixed Vegetables

**SIBO Note:** Brown rice is higher in fibre than white rice and contains more fermentable content — which is why it belongs in maintenance rather than earlier phases. By this point, your gut lining has had time to recover, your reintroduction sequence is complete, and brown rice in normal portions is appropriate for most people. If you found brown rice caused symptoms during reintroduction, stay with white rice. This recipe also uses a broader vegetable mix than Phase 1 — broccoli and snap peas are moderate-FODMAP in standard portions; carrots are low-FODMAP and have appeared since Phase 1.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 15 minutes | **Cook time:** 25 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 300g / 10oz beef sirloin, thinly sliced
- 200g / 1 cup dry brown rice
- 100g / 1.5 cups small broccoli florets
- 2 medium carrots, peeled and cut into thin matchsticks
- 100g / 1 cup snap peas (sugar snap or snow peas), trimmed
- 1 thumb-sized piece of fresh ginger, grated
- 2 tbsp sesame oil
- 2 tbsp tamari or soy sauce
- 1 tbsp rice wine vinegar
- Salt and black pepper
- Sesame seeds to serve

**Method**

1. Cook the brown rice according to packet instructions — typically 25–30 minutes. Start this first; everything else will be ready before it is.
2. Mix the tamari, rice wine vinegar, and a pinch of pepper in a small bowl. Set aside.
3. Heat a wok or large frying pan over very high heat. Add 1 tbsp sesame oil. When the oil shimmers, add the beef in a single layer. Don't stir for 90 seconds — let it sear. Toss, cook another 30 seconds. Remove and set aside.
4. Add remaining sesame oil. Add ginger, stir 15 seconds. Add the carrots, stir-fry 2 minutes. Add the broccoli, stir-fry 2 minutes. Add the snap peas last — they only need 1 minute and they'll turn sweet and bright if you don't push them.
5. Return the beef. Pour in the sauce. Toss everything together over high heat for 30–45 seconds.
6. Serve over brown rice, scattered with sesame seeds.

### 15. Baked Sea Bass with Lemon, Capers, and Roasted New Potatoes

**SIBO Note:** Sea bass is a lean, well-flavoured white fish. Capers are low-FODMAP and provide an intensity of flavour that makes a simple piece of fish feel like something more considered than it actually is. New potatoes are low-FODMAP in portions up to roughly 300g cooked. This is a maintenance-phase dinner that requires almost no hands-on time and scales easily if you're cooking for more than two.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 10 minutes | **Cook time:** 35 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 2 sea bass fillets (approx. 150g / 5oz each), skin-on
- 400g / 14oz new potatoes, halved
- 2 tbsp capers, drained
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- 1 lemon, halved
- 1 tbsp butter
- Small bunch of fresh flat-leaf parsley, roughly chopped
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

1. Heat the oven to 200°C / 400°F.
2. Toss the new potatoes with 1.5 tbsp olive oil, salt, and pepper. Spread cut-side down on a lined baking tray. Roast for 28–30 minutes until golden and cooked through. They should be crispy on the bottom and yielding inside.
3. About 5 minutes before the potatoes are done, heat the remaining olive oil and the butter in an ovenproof frying pan over medium-high heat.
4. Score the skin of each sea bass fillet 2–3 times with a sharp knife — this stops them curling and helps the skin crisp evenly. Season all over with salt and pepper.
5. Place the fillets skin-side down in the hot pan. Press gently with a spatula for the first 30 seconds. Cook for 3–4 minutes until the skin is golden and the flesh has cooked about two-thirds of the way up the fillet. Flip carefully and cook another 1–2 minutes.
6. Add the capers to the pan in the last minute. Squeeze lemon over the fish. The pan will spit — that's fine.
7. Serve with the roasted potatoes. Spoon the pan juices and capers over the top. Scatter parsley.

### 16. Lentil Soup

**SIBO Note:** Lentils are a moderate-to-high FODMAP food — which is exactly why they don't appear until Phase 3. They contain galacto-oligosaccharides (GOS), a fermentable carbohydrate that feeds gut bacteria. In maintenance, once your overgrowth has been treated, your root cause addressed, and your gut lining has had time to recover, lentils in moderate portions can be part of a normal diet for most people. The key word is tolerated — if you tested lentils during reintroduction and they caused symptoms, skip this recipe. Canned or well-rinsed lentils reduce the FODMAP load compared to dried lentils, so the recipe lists canned as an alternative worth considering alongside dried red lentils, which are the primary option here. Red lentils also tend to be better tolerated than green.

**Serves:** 3–4 | **Prep time:** 15 minutes | **Cook time:** 30 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 200g / 7oz dry red lentils, rinsed well (or 1 x 400g / 14oz can of lentils, drained and rinsed thoroughly — canned and rinsed reduces FODMAP load)
- 2 medium carrots, peeled and diced
- 2 stalks of celery, diced (celery is low-FODMAP in portions up to roughly 1 stalk per serving)
- 1 x 400g / 14oz can of chopped tomatoes (check label — tomatoes only, no added onion or garlic)
- 1 litre / 4 cups plain vegetable or chicken stock (check the label carefully for onion and garlic)
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp ground coriander
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- Salt and black pepper
- Juice of half a lemon
- Fresh flat-leaf parsley or coriander to serve

**Method**

1. Heat the olive oil in a large saucepan over medium heat. Add the ginger and stir for 30 seconds until fragrant.
2. Add the diced carrots and celery. Cook for 5 minutes, stirring occasionally, until they begin to soften — you're building a base here, not rushing to the next step.
3. Add the cumin and coriander. Stir for 30 seconds until fragrant. Toasting the spices directly in the oil before adding liquid makes a real difference to the depth of the final soup.
4. Add the rinsed lentils, chopped tomatoes, and stock. Stir to combine. Bring to a boil.
5. Reduce to a simmer and cook for 20–25 minutes until the lentils have broken down and the soup has thickened. Red lentils dissolve as they cook — that's not a problem, it's the texture you're after. Stir occasionally so they don't catch on the bottom.
6. Season with salt, pepper, and lemon juice. Taste and adjust — it should be bright and earthy, not flat.
7. Serve with parsley or coriander scattered over. A piece of plain sourdough works well alongside for maintenance-phase eating if bread is tolerated.

### 17. Greek-Style Lamb with Roasted Vegetables and Rice

**SIBO Note:** Lamb is a rich, well-flavoured protein — no fermentable content. The vegetable combination here (courgette, cherry tomatoes, capsicum) is the same broad Mediterranean roster as Recipe 13, which is appropriate for maintenance. The herb marinade uses dried oregano and lemon rather than garlic-heavy Greek seasoning mixes, so it keeps the FODMAP load low while delivering the same flavour profile. A substantial meal — enough fat and protein to keep you comfortable across a 4–5 hour eating window.

**Serves:** 2–3 | **Prep time:** 20 minutes (plus 30 minutes marinating if time allows) | **Cook time:** 35 minutes

**Ingredients**

- 400g / 14oz lamb leg steaks or lamb shoulder, cut into 3–4cm pieces
- 200g / 1 cup dry white or brown rice (depending on tolerance)
- 2 courgettes, cut into chunks
- 150g / 5oz cherry tomatoes
- 1 red capsicum, deseeded and cut into chunks
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 1 lemon (zest and juice)
- 1.5 tsp dried oregano
- 1 tsp dried thyme
- Salt and black pepper
- Fresh mint or flat-leaf parsley to serve

**Method**

1. Combine 2 tbsp olive oil, the lemon zest, juice of half the lemon, oregano, thyme, salt, and pepper in a bowl. Add the lamb pieces and toss to coat. Marinate for at least 30 minutes at room temperature, or up to 4 hours in the fridge. If you only have 10 minutes, it's still worth it.
2. Heat the oven to 210°C / 410°F. Cook the rice according to packet instructions.
3. Spread the courgette, cherry tomatoes, and capsicum on a large roasting tray. Drizzle with remaining 1 tbsp olive oil, season with salt and pepper. Roast for 15 minutes.
4. After 15 minutes, nestle the marinated lamb pieces among the vegetables. Return to the oven for a further 18–20 minutes until the lamb is cooked through and catching colour, and the vegetables are tender and caramelised at the edges. The lamb fat will render into the pan and the vegetables will absorb it. This is why the dish works.
5. Squeeze the remaining lemon half over the tray.
6. Serve over rice, scattered with fresh mint or parsley.

### 18. Smoked Salmon and Cucumber Rice Bowl with Avocado

**SIBO Note:** Avocado is a moderate-FODMAP food — it contains sorbitol, which can cause symptoms in some people even after full treatment. The low-FODMAP threshold is approximately 30g, roughly 1/8 of a medium avocado. A quarter avocado (~50–60g) moves into moderate territory; a third is higher again. Start with 1/8 and work up to a quarter only once that's confirmed well tolerated. If you didn't test avocado separately during reintroduction, use this recipe as the test — go small first. Smoked salmon and cucumber are both low-FODMAP. This is a fast, no-cook assembly — a useful template for a light lunch that stays within the eating window without requiring cooking.

**Serves:** 2 | **Prep time:** 15 minutes | **Cook time:** 15 minutes (rice only)

**Ingredients**

- 150g / 5oz smoked salmon
- 200g / 1 cup dry white rice (or use leftover cooked rice)
- 1 medium cucumber, thinly sliced into rounds or half-moons
- 1 ripe avocado, halved, stoned, and sliced (use 1/8 to 1/4 per serving to start — see SIBO Note)
- 1 tbsp sesame oil
- 1 tbsp tamari or low-sodium soy sauce
- 1 tsp rice wine vinegar
- 1 tsp sesame seeds
- Fresh chives or coriander
- Pickled ginger (check the label — should contain only ginger, vinegar, salt, and sugar; discard if it contains added preservatives or colouring)
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

1. Cook the rice according to packet instructions. Rinse briefly under cold water to cool it slightly — room temperature rice works better in a bowl than steaming hot. Season the cooled rice with sesame oil, tamari, and rice wine vinegar. Toss gently. Taste it — it should be savoury and nutty, not sharp.
2. Divide the seasoned rice between two bowls.
3. Arrange the smoked salmon, cucumber slices, and avocado slices over the rice. Take your time with this — how it looks matters when you've been eating plain food for weeks.
4. Add a small amount of pickled ginger to each bowl.
5. Scatter sesame seeds and herbs over the top.
6. Serve immediately.

## You've Done the Hard Part

These recipes exist because eating during SIBO recovery is one of the more disorienting parts of the whole process. You're making decisions at every meal about what's safe, what's a trigger, what counts as reintroduction and what doesn't. That's a lot of cognitive overhead when you're already tired and probably still symptomatic some days.

The phases give you a structure to stop second-guessing every plate. Phase 1 is not forever. Phase 2 is not permission to rush. Phase 3 is not the finish line — it's just eating, which is what you're working your way back to.

The thing that determines whether any of this holds is not which recipe you picked on Tuesday. It's the protocol underneath: the eating window, the prokinetics, the root cause work, the reintroduction sequence done in order and logged honestly. The food is in service of that — not the other way around.

Most people who complete reintroduction honestly find that the list of foods their gut can't handle is shorter than they feared. Not gone — shorter. The restriction you lived inside during treatment wasn't a sentence about your body's permanent limits. It was a treatment tool. You used it. Now you're on the other side of it.

Cook simply. Eat within the window. When a food causes a reaction, log it and move on — it's information, not a verdict.

You started this book carrying years of treatments that didn't hold and the suspicion that something was being missed. You're finishing it with the full picture. If someone else is still at the beginning — still being handed the same antibiotic, still being told it's IBS, still wondering why nothing holds — a review from you is what gets them here.
