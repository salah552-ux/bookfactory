# Chapter Eight
## Motility, Prokinetics, and the MMC
### The Conversation Your Doctor Probably Skipped

Nobody put the prokinetic in the prescription bag.

You finished the antibiotic course. You waited the right amount of time. You started reintroduction carefully, logged your reactions, kept the eating window. And then the bacteria came back — not because you did anything wrong, but because the mechanism that was supposed to prevent them from returning was running at the same compromised frequency it had been running before treatment. Nobody mentioned that part. Nobody addressed it. And now you're here again.

The migrating motor complex is your gut's housekeeping system. Every 90 minutes in the fasted state, it generates a sweep that moves bacteria from the small intestine down into the large intestine, where they belong. When the treatment cleared your overgrowth, the MMC was still running at the same compromised frequency it was running before. Nothing in your antibiotic course addressed that. Which means the ecological niche you just cleared was warm, quiet, and available — and the bacteria filled it, because that's what bacteria do when conditions are right.

This chapter is about changing those conditions for good.

---

## What Chapter 1 Promised and What This Chapter Delivers

Back in Chapter 1, we said we'd spend an entire chapter on the MMC because it's that important. This is that chapter. Not a recap — an elevation.

You already know the basics: the MMC runs every 90 to 120 minutes on average, in the fasted state, sweeping bacteria and debris from the small intestine into the large intestine where they belong. You know that grazing — healthy snacks every two hours, the way every wellness account tells you to eat — interrupts that cycle every time. You know from Chapter 4 that the MMC can be damaged by post-infectious autoimmune attack, specifically the CdtB-vinculin pathway that leaves the nerve cells coordinating the MMC functionally impaired. You know from Chapter 6 that the eating window matters because the MMC needs the fasted state to work.

What you don't yet know is Phase III.

The MMC is not a single contractile wave. It moves through four phases. Phase I is motor silence — the gut is quiet. Phase II is a period of irregular contractions, preparatory, building in intensity. Phase III is the event: a powerful, coordinated wave of contractions that moves from the stomach through the full length of the small intestine, clearing what's in its path. Phase IV is a brief transition back to silence before the cycle begins again.

Phase III is the cleaning sweep. Phases I and II are the windup. When the MMC is compromised — whether by nerve damage, autonomic disruption, or the structural vulnerability that lets the cycle start but never properly reach Phase III — the clearing work doesn't happen. The bacteria that should have been swept toward the large intestine stay where they are. Or migrate further up. Or proliferate in the environment the disrupted motility created.

This is why it's not enough to clear the bacteria. The MMC must be functional, or the problem rebuilds itself.

## Why the MMC Is So Hard to Restore

The enteric nervous system — the approximately 100 million neurons embedded in the gut wall, which you met in Chapter 2 — initiates the MMC largely independently of the brain. It doesn't need permission from your cortex to run a cleaning sweep. This is exactly why the gut can function during sleep, during stress, during general anaesthesia. The machinery is local.

But "locally operated" doesn't mean "immune to external input." The autonomic nervous system — specifically the balance between sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) signalling — directly modulates the enteric system's activity.

Here's what that means in practical terms: chronic sympathetic dominance suppresses the MMC. Not a little. Measurably. Studies in *Neurogastroenterology and Motility* and related journals have found that acute stress reliably disrupts MMC cycling in healthy subjects, shortening Phase III contractions and reducing their amplitude — a finding replicated across multiple laboratory and clinical contexts. This is the concrete mechanism behind what gets dismissed as "your symptoms are stress-related." Stress doesn't cause SIBO by making you anxious. What the evidence shows is that it runs a specific, measurable interference on the neurological machinery that prevents bacterial overgrowth — and that interference is associated with increased SIBO risk, even if the causal chain hasn't been confirmed end-to-end in a controlled trial.

Sleep is another variable most people never hear about in a gastroenterology context. The parasympathetic nervous system dominates during sleep. That dominance is part of what allows MMC cycling to run overnight — a critical clearing window, especially given that most people's last meal is at least several hours before bed. Sleep that's chronically short or chronically disrupted by apnoea, insomnia, or cortisol dysregulation impairs this window. The overnight cleaning sweep either doesn't happen properly or doesn't happen at all. By morning, the bacterial environment in the small intestine hasn't been adequately cleared.

Post-infectious nerve damage adds a third layer of difficulty. The CdtB-vinculin antibody attack established in Chapter 1 doesn't leave the MMC damaged in the way that damage is immediately obvious. The interstitial cells of Cajal — the pacemaker cells of the gut that coordinate MMC timing — may be structurally present but functionally compromised. The signal initiates. The sweep underdelivers. From the outside, you look like someone with motility issues of unclear origin. From the inside, the MMC is operating below functional capacity — and no amount of dietary change alone is going to push it back to full function.

This is why prokinetics exist. And why this conversation should be happening in every post-treatment appointment. It mostly isn't.

## Pharmaceutical Prokinetics: What's Available, What the Evidence Shows

A prokinetic is any agent — pharmaceutical or otherwise — that accelerates or normalises gut motility. In the context of SIBO post-treatment, the specific goal is MMC support: restoring Phase III contractile activity, improving cycle frequency, and maintaining the clearing function that keeps bacterial populations in check.

The options below require a conversation with a prescribing clinician. None of them are self-prescribable, and none of them are appropriate for everyone. The goal of this section is to put you in that conversation with accurate information rather than walking in blind.

**Low-Dose Naltrexone (LDN)** is the option generating the most active clinical interest in the SIBO community right now, and it's the one most likely to be completely new to you. Naltrexone at full doses (50mg) blocks opioid receptors and is used in addiction medicine. At low doses — typically 1.5 to 4.5mg, administered at bedtime — the pharmacological effect is fundamentally different. At this dose range, LDN causes a brief, transient opioid receptor blockade that is theorised to stimulate an endogenous rebound: the body may produce more endogenous opioid peptides in response, which could drive motilin-like effects in the gut — and motilin is one of the key hormones that triggers Phase III MMC contractions. This mechanism is pharmacologically plausible, though not yet confirmed in controlled trials at the level of gut-specific motilin activity. LDN also appears to have direct anti-inflammatory effects in gut tissue, likely via its action on glial cells and microglia in the enteric nervous system. The SIBO-specific evidence is preliminary — mostly case series and small observational studies — but the mechanistic rationale is solid, the safety profile at low doses is considered favourable relative to full-dose naltrexone, and a growing number of SIBO-literate gastroenterologists are using it as a post-treatment prokinetic. (Some patients report transient sleep disruption or vivid dreams in the first two weeks; these typically resolve without discontinuation.) It requires a prescription, and because it's off-label for this indication, some prescribers will be unfamiliar with the application. DCT Box #3 at the end of this chapter gives you language for that conversation.

**Prucalopride** is an FDA-approved medication for chronic idiopathic constipation, and it works via the same serotonin pathway established back in Chapter 2. It's a 5-HT4 receptor agonist — it activates the serotonin receptors in the gut wall that stimulate propulsive motility, particularly in the colon. The evidence for prucalopride's prokinetic effects on the large intestine is strong and well-documented. For the small intestine specifically, the data is more limited but accumulating. Multiple gastroenterologists working with SIBO patients have incorporated prucalopride as a post-treatment prokinetic specifically because of its 5-HT4 mechanism — the same pathway prucalopride's structural cousin tegaserod works on. It requires a prescription, and because its FDA approval is for constipation rather than SIBO, the prescribing context will again require explanation. It's worth asking about.

**Low-Dose Erythromycin** at sub-antibiotic doses — typically 50 to 100mg, compared to the 250 to 500mg doses used for bacterial infections — works as a motilin receptor agonist. Motilin is the hormone that triggers Phase III MMC contractions, and erythromycin at low doses mimics its action directly. The prokinetic effect is real and documented. Here's what has to be said plainly: even at sub-antibiotic doses, prolonged erythromycin use can promote resistance in gut bacteria. The clinical community is genuinely divided on this. It's not a theoretical worry — it's a real one, and it's why most practitioners who use erythromycin as a prokinetic do so as a bridge, not a permanent fixture. Short-term use — four to eight weeks post-treatment rather than months of maintenance — is generally considered more defensible. Some clinicians use it as a bridge prokinetic early in the post-treatment window while longer-term options are established. Like all prescriptions in this section, this is a conversation to have with your prescriber, not a decision to make alone.

Two other agents come up in this conversation and are worth naming briefly. Tegaserod — another 5-HT4 agonist — was re-approved by the FDA in 2019, but its indication is narrow: IBS-C in women under 65. Outside that context most prescribers won't consider it. Metoclopramide is a prokinetic some of you may already have been prescribed: for long-term SIBO maintenance it's not recommended. The FDA black box warning on prolonged use exists because of documented tardive dyskinesia risk — an involuntary movement disorder — and the risk-benefit profile doesn't hold up against the options already covered here.

## Natural Prokinetics: What the Evidence Actually Supports

There's a wide spectrum of claims made about natural prokinetics online, and most of them aren't calibrated to the evidence. The options below are real, but the evidence behind each one varies considerably. Conflating them — treating Level 2 evidence the same as mechanistic theory — is the mistake most blogs make. We're not doing that here.

**Ginger**, specifically the gingerols that give it its pungency, has documented evidence for accelerating gastric emptying — the movement of food from the stomach into the small intestine. Multiple controlled trials support this effect. For small intestinal transit specifically, the evidence is thinner but present. A 2018 systematic review in *Food Science and Nutrition* found gastric motility benefits across multiple studies, with more modest data on small intestinal effects. The practical implication: ginger is unlikely to fully restore a compromised MMC on its own, but as an adjunct — particularly during the early post-treatment period — it's low-risk and reasonably evidenced. Standard practice doses in this context run to 1 to 2 grams of standardised extract daily, not the amount in a ginger tea bag. Consult your healthcare provider before adding any supplement to your regimen, as individual tolerability and medication interactions vary.

**Iberogast** — sold under the brand STW 5 — is a proprietary herbal combination formula developed in Germany that includes extracts of bitter candytuft, angelica root, milk thistle, celandine, lemon balm, chamomile, caraway, liquorice, and peppermint. It has been studied in properly designed clinical trials, which puts it in a different evidence category from most herbal options. Multiple randomised controlled trials have demonstrated its effectiveness for functional dyspepsia — a condition characterised by impaired gastric motility and associated symptoms. A 2015 meta-analysis in the *American Journal of Gastroenterology* confirmed its efficacy across these studies. Its specific evidence for SIBO-related motility restoration is not extensive, but its general prokinetic mechanism and RCT backing make it one of the better-supported natural options in this space. It's available over the counter in many countries.

**Magnesium** lands in a different category here, because its mechanism isn't primarily prokinetic — it's permissive. As established in Chapter 2, SIBO depletes magnesium through malabsorption, and magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body. Among those: the smooth muscle function that underlies gut motility. Magnesium deficiency impairs smooth muscle contraction directly. Repleting it doesn't supercharge the MMC — it removes a deficiency-driven impairment. This is a meaningful distinction: if your gut smooth muscle is operating under a magnesium deficit, supplementation restores baseline function; it doesn't create enhanced function above baseline. Glycinate or citrate forms are typically better tolerated than oxide forms, which have lower bioavailability and can cause loose stools at doses required for repletion.

**5-HTP** — a serotonin precursor that crosses more readily into gut tissue than raw tryptophan — theoretically supports motility via the 5-HT4 receptor pathway. This is the same pathway that prucalopride and tegaserod activate. The mechanism is plausible, and the serotonin production apparatus established in Chapter 2 makes it mechanistically coherent. But the direct clinical evidence for 5-HTP as a prokinetic in the SIBO context is absent. We have theory, not trial data. Include it with that caveat, and weight it accordingly: possibly useful as part of a broader gut serotonin support strategy, not a substitute for pharmaceutical prokinetics when the latter are clinically indicated.

## The Timing Question

When you start prokinetics matters nearly as much as what you take.

Starting prokinetics early in the antimicrobial course — or before the course begins — is generally not recommended. The reasoning is pharmacokinetic: prokinetics accelerate gut transit, which means they also accelerate the rate at which antimicrobials move through the small intestine. Faster transit equals less contact time between the antimicrobial and the bacterial population you're trying to clear. You'd be undermining the treatment's efficacy while it's running. Don't do this.

The right window is toward the end of the antimicrobial course, or immediately at completion. The bacterial load has been reduced. The active eradication phase is nearly or fully done. Now the question shifts from "how do we clear this?" to "how do we keep it cleared?" That's when the MMC support strategy starts.

The duration is longer than most people expect. Three to six months is the minimum that most SIBO-literate practitioners recommend for prokinetic maintenance post-treatment, and some extend that timeline to twelve months for patients with documented motility dysfunction or a history of multiple relapses. This isn't excessive caution. The MMC's functional cycling patterns take time to re-establish, especially when the disruption has been present for years. Bacterial recolonisation risk is highest in the weeks immediately following treatment — the bacterial population was reduced, but the ecological niche is still warm and available. Sustained MMC support through that window is the mechanism that changes the long-term outcome.

Keep the eating window running alongside prokinetics. This isn't redundant — it's synergistic. The eating window, covered in Chapter 6, gives the MMC the fasted state it needs to run its cycle. Prokinetics strengthen the contractions within that cycle. One is the schedule; the other is the engine. You need both.

The prokinetics don't replace the eating window once treatment is complete. They run alongside it, indefinitely, until your practitioner determines the clinical picture supports stepping down. That determination should be based on symptom status, ideally confirmed by repeat breath testing.

## DCT Box #3: Getting Prokinetics Into Your Treatment Plan

Most people completing SIBO treatment walk out of their last appointment with the prescription finished and no discussion of what comes next for motility. If that's where you are, this conversation is worth having at your follow-up. Here's how to have it.

> **Opening the conversation:**
> "I've completed my antimicrobial course. I'd like to discuss adding a prokinetic to my post-treatment plan to support MMC function and reduce my relapse risk."
>
> **If your clinician is unfamiliar with prokinetics for SIBO:**
> "I've read that low-dose naltrexone and prucalopride are used by gastroenterologists working with SIBO patients specifically for post-treatment motility support. Is either of these something you'd consider in my case?"
>
> **If a prokinetic is prescribed:**
> "What duration are you recommending, and what's the plan for monitoring? Should we repeat breath testing at any point during this period?"
>
> **If your clinician declines:**
> "Can you help me understand the reasoning? I'm asking because my previous treatments haven't held, and I want to understand whether motility is a factor we should be addressing."
>
> **If natural prokinetics are suggested instead of pharmaceutical ones:**
> "I'm interested in natural support options — are there any you'd recommend alongside the pharmaceutical option, or are you suggesting those as a replacement? I want to make sure I understand the evidence level for each."

This conversation has a specific goal: to leave the appointment with a prokinetic protocol in place, or to understand the clinical reasoning for why one isn't indicated in your specific case. Not every patient is the same. A clinician who declines and explains the reasoning clearly is different from one who dismisses the question without engagement.

*The language above is a communication aid, not medical advice. Any decision about prokinetic therapy — pharmaceutical or natural — should be made in consultation with a qualified healthcare provider who knows your full clinical picture. Prokinetics can interact with other medications and are not appropriate for all patients.*

There's a sentence that sits at the centre of most people's experience with SIBO treatment. It goes something like this: *I did everything I was told to do and it still came back.* That sentence has been rolling around in the reader's head since the introduction of this book. Maybe since before they picked the book up.

The mirror for this chapter is quieter than the others. It's not angry. It's just accurate.

*Nobody talked about this part.*

Not the gastroenterologist who prescribed the rifaximin. Not the integrative practitioner who ran the breath test. Not the online SIBO community with its elaborate elimination protocols and supplement stacks. The MMC, the prokinetics, the post-treatment motility maintenance window — this conversation is not happening in most GI appointments. It's not a conspiracy. It's a gap in standard care, the kind that develops when a condition sits in the space between specialties, and nobody owns the full picture.

You have the full picture now.

You understand why the antibiotic alone was never going to be enough. You understand what Phase III of the MMC actually does, and why it's so difficult to restore without targeted intervention. You know what pharmaceutical prokinetics are available, what the evidence says about each one, and which ones are generating the most clinical attention in the SIBO community right now. You have the language to raise this at your next appointment — not defensively, not apologetically, but as someone who has done the work and is asking for a complete treatment plan.

That's what this chapter was for.

## What This Means For You

Start prokinetics at the end of your antimicrobial course or immediately after completion. Not during — too early undermines the treatment. Not months later — the highest-risk window for bacterial recolonisation is right after treatment ends.

Run the prokinetic protocol for a minimum of three to six months. Longer if you have documented motility dysfunction or a history of multiple relapses. This is not a two-week course. The MMC needs sustained support to re-establish functional cycling patterns.

Keep the eating window running alongside prokinetics. Four to five hours between meals, nothing caloric in between. These two interventions are synergistic — the eating window gives the MMC its operating conditions; the prokinetics improve the quality of its contractions within those conditions. Neither replaces the other.

If you have access to a prescribing clinician, LDN and prucalopride are the two options generating the most active clinical interest for post-treatment SIBO motility support. Bring the DCT Box language to your appointment. If pharmaceutical prokinetics aren't appropriate for your situation, Iberogast has the strongest natural evidence, ginger has moderate support, and magnesium repletion removes a deficiency-driven impairment that is extremely common in people with SIBO history.

Don't conflate evidence levels. Iberogast has RCT data behind it. 5-HTP has mechanistic theory. These are not equivalent. Ask your practitioner which options make sense in combination for your clinical picture.

The treatment cleared the overgrowth. The MMC work keeps it cleared. These two things were always meant to run together. The reason they didn't — the reason nobody put them in the same conversation — is a structural gap in how SIBO is managed, not a gap in what's possible.

You have both sides of it now. The clearing and the maintenance. The antibiotic and the mechanism that prevents the antibiotic from being the only thing standing between you and the next relapse.

Nobody talked about this part. Until now. Chapter 9 is where you find out what it looks like when the whole system actually works — and what to do when life tries to knock it off course.
