# Chapter Three
## Testing: The Gap Between What's Available and What's Actually Useful

You took the test. You fasted the night before. You followed the prep diet for a full day. You sat in a clinic or a lab or your kitchen, depending on how you ordered it, and you breathed into a tube every fifteen or twenty minutes for two or three hours. The results came back, and they said negative. And your body, since then, has continued doing what it does.

If that's where you are, this chapter is for you.

Because here's the thing nobody explains at that appointment: a negative breath test does not rule out SIBO. It rules out SIBO that this test, on this day, with this preparation, was capable of detecting. Those are meaningfully different statements. And the difference between them is what you need to understand before you accept that result and go back to square one.

---

## How the Test Actually Works

Start with the mechanics, because the limitations become obvious once you understand what the test is physically measuring.

The hydrogen breath test works by exploiting a very specific biological fact: humans don't produce hydrogen gas. When you exhale hydrogen, it came from bacterial fermentation. Bacteria in your gut encounter a substrate — a sugar they can ferment — and they produce gas as a byproduct. That gas gets absorbed through the gut wall, enters the bloodstream, reaches the lungs, and comes out in your breath. The test captures that signal.

Here's how it happens in practice. You drink a sugar solution — either lactulose or glucose, depending on the protocol. Over the next two to three hours, you breathe into a collection device at regular intervals, typically every fifteen to twenty minutes. Each breath sample gets analyzed for hydrogen, and in three-gas tests, also for methane and hydrogen sulfide. The resulting printout shows you a graph of gas concentration over time. The numbers are measured in parts per million. Rises and falls in that graph tell the story of where in your gut fermentation is happening and how much.

The logic is elegant. The execution is where things get complicated.

---

## The Sensitivity Problem, and What a Negative Actually Means

As we covered in the introduction, lactulose breath tests carry roughly 42% sensitivity. Glucose breath tests come in around 55%. These figures are not contested in the research literature — they're published in meta-analyses, and they don't flatter the test.

Let's sit with what 42% sensitivity actually means. If you have SIBO and you take a lactulose breath test, there's better than even odds the test will miss it. Not because your bacteria aren't there. Not because you did anything wrong. Because the test's ability to detect confirmed SIBO cases is, in the best clinical data, slightly worse than a coin flip.

Glucose fares better — 55% sensitivity means more than half of true SIBO cases are caught. Still, you're looking at roughly four in ten people with real bacterial overgrowth who walk away with a clean report.

A negative result is not a diagnosis of health. It's a statement about detection probability. Hold that distinction. You'll need it later in this chapter when we talk about what to do with a result that doesn't match how you feel.

---

## Lactulose vs. Glucose: Two Different Tools, Two Different Failure Modes

Choosing between these substrates isn't arbitrary. They behave differently in the gut, and their differences determine which type of SIBO they're best positioned to catch — and miss.

Glucose is a sugar that gets absorbed quickly. It's taken up primarily in the upper section of the small intestine — the duodenum and proximal jejunum. If bacteria are overgrowing in that region, glucose reaches them before it's fully absorbed, they ferment it, and you produce hydrogen. The test catches it. The problem is range. If your bacterial overgrowth is located in the mid or distal small intestine — further along the tract — glucose may be completely absorbed before it ever arrives. No fermentation occurs. No gas signal appears. The test reads negative. The bacteria are still there, just out of reach.

Lactulose doesn't have that absorption problem. It's a non-digestible sugar — humans lack the enzyme to break it down. It travels the entire length of the small intestine and reaches the colon fully intact, where large intestine bacteria ferment it heavily. That colonic fermentation always produces a gas spike. Always. This is important, because the lactulose test has a built-in verification that the substrate actually arrived where bacteria live.

But lactulose carries its own failure mode. It speeds up gut transit. The act of taking lactulose can cause the solution to move faster than normal through the small intestine, which means it reaches the colon — and triggers that colonic fermentation spike — earlier than it would under normal transit conditions. If that spike arrives early enough on the readout, it can be mistaken for a small intestine signal. The interpretation becomes muddied. And in some protocols, early spikes get dismissed because the clinician can't distinguish true SIBO signal from accelerated colonic fermentation.

The practical upshot: glucose is better for catching overgrowth in the upper small intestine but misses anything further down. Lactulose has broader anatomical coverage but is harder to interpret precisely and is more vulnerable to transit time confounding. Neither test is wrong. They're both incomplete tools pointed at a complex problem.

If you don't know which substrate your test used, that's information worth getting. It matters for interpreting a negative result.

## The Third Gas Nobody Measured

Here's a gap in standard testing that doesn't get enough attention.

Most breath tests measure two gases: hydrogen and methane. Hydrogen is the primary output of bacterial fermentation. Methane is produced by archaea — specifically Methanobrevibacter smithii — and is the defining marker of what we now call intestinal methanogen overgrowth, or IMO. A test that catches both covers the two most commonly recognized SIBO types.

It doesn't catch the third.

Hydrogen sulfide — the gas associated with hydrogen sulfide SIBO — is not measured by standard two-gas breath tests. Hydrogen sulfide-dominant overgrowth presents differently: it's strongly associated with diarrhea-predominant patterns, a characteristic sulfurous odor, and a pro-inflammatory gut environment. And until recently, there was no commercially available breath test that captured it at all.

Three-gas testing is now available through certain specialty labs. It adds hydrogen sulfide to the measurement panel. The research on interpretation thresholds for hydrogen sulfide is still developing — the clinical community hasn't fully standardized what constitutes a positive result — but having the data is better than not having it. If your symptoms lean toward the diarrhea end, if you experience a particularly sulfurous quality to your digestive output, or if your previous testing came back negative on hydrogen and methane, a three-gas test is worth asking about.

A standard two-gas test can return completely clean results on someone with hydrogen sulfide SIBO. Not a borderline negative. A clean negative. Because the gas that bacterium produces wasn't measured.

## How Your Prep Can Produce a False Negative Before You Even Drink Anything

The preparation protocol for breath testing exists for a reason: it's trying to minimize background fermentation so that the test can detect the signal from the substrate you drink. The standard prep involves a low-fermentation diet for twenty-four hours before testing — typically no high-fiber vegetables, no whole grains, no legumes, no fermented foods — followed by a fast of eight to twelve hours.

When this protocol works, it quiets down the colonic bacteria enough that any hydrogen you produce during the test is more likely to reflect small intestine activity than background noise.

When it doesn't work, you end up with a falsely suppressed baseline. And here's the contradiction built into this: the stricter you follow the prep, the more you're temporarily starving your gut bacteria of fermentable substrate. Some people, especially those who are eating low-FODMAP or low-carbohydrate diets before their test, arrive at testing with bacteria that are already in a reduced-activity state. They produce less gas than they normally would. The test reads quieter than their gut actually is.

The other prep failure mode runs the opposite direction. If you ate something outside the protocol — inadvertently, or because the instructions weren't clear — you arrive with elevated baseline hydrogen. Anything above 10 to 20 parts per million at the start of the test is considered a failed prep in most protocols. The test result is unreliable. Many labs proceed anyway and note the elevated baseline. Whether or not that notation gets explained to you is another matter.

Timing also matters in ways the prep instructions often don't address. Antibiotic or antimicrobial use in the weeks before testing can temporarily suppress bacterial populations, producing a false negative simply because you've knocked back the organisms the test is trying to find. Prokinetics and laxatives affect transit time and change when gases appear in the readout. If you took anything that could affect gut motility or bacterial populations in the weeks before your test, that history belongs in the conversation with whoever interprets your results.

## How to Read Your Own Results

Most people receive their breath test printout with little explanation. Here's what you're actually looking at.

The Y-axis on the graph represents gas concentration in parts per million. The X-axis is time — each point corresponds to one of your breath samples. A baseline reading at time zero gives you the starting level before the substrate hits your gut. What happens after that is the story.

In a negative test, the line stays relatively flat throughout. Gas levels rise by less than 20 parts per million above baseline in the case of hydrogen, or stay below threshold for methane, depending on the protocol and the lab's interpretation criteria. You walk away with a clean result.

In a positive hydrogen test, you'll see a rise — typically at least 20 parts per million above baseline — occurring within the first 90 minutes. The timing matters as much as the absolute number. An early rise, appearing in the first 60 to 90 minutes, suggests fermentation is happening in the small intestine. A rise that appears only at the two-hour mark or later is more consistent with normal colonic fermentation — the substrate made it to the large intestine before being fermented, which is what should happen. A rise that comes early is abnormal.

Methane is interpreted differently. Any methane level at or above 10 parts per million at any point in the test is considered positive by many labs, regardless of timing, because methane-producing archaea are not normally present in meaningful quantities in the small intestine.

Now, the ambiguous results — and there are more of them than the binary positive/negative framing suggests. A small rise in hydrogen that doesn't quite hit the 20-point threshold. A flat hydrogen trace with no methane. A late hydrogen peak that could be small intestine signal or early colonic fermentation depending on transit time. These results are real. They happen frequently. And they are almost never explained to the patient as ambiguous. They're reported as negative, filed, and the conversation ends.

If your result shows any rise in hydrogen — even one that didn't cross the positive threshold — that number is worth discussing. A rise from 6 to 18 ppm is clinically different from a flat trace that sits at 4 ppm throughout. Both would be reported as negative. They are not the same result.

## What Organic Acids Testing Adds to the Picture

Urine-based organic acids testing — OAT, as it's commonly abbreviated — takes a different approach entirely. Instead of measuring gases produced by bacteria in real time, it measures the metabolic byproducts of microbial activity that have been excreted through your kidneys.

The logic: bacteria and yeast produce distinctive organic compounds as they metabolize. Those compounds make their way into your bloodstream, get filtered by the kidneys, and appear in urine. A urine organic acids test captures a snapshot of what your microbial ecosystem has been producing over time — not just on one test day.

This is both its strength and its limitation. OAT gives you a broader metabolic picture than a breath test. It can flag patterns associated with dysbiosis, suggest candida or other yeast overgrowth alongside bacterial issues, and provide information about mitochondrial function, neurotransmitter metabolism, and nutritional status in the same panel. For someone who hasn't been able to get a clear breath test result, it offers a different angle on the same problem.

What it can't do is specifically confirm SIBO. The markers that appear on OAT with bacterial overgrowth — elevated arabinose, elevated HPHPA, others — are suggestive of dysbiosis, not diagnostic of SIBO specifically. The evidence here is preliminary and interpretive rather than definitive. It's a piece of the puzzle, not the answer. Its clinical validity is also an ongoing debate; some practitioners use it routinely, others consider the interpretive thresholds poorly validated. Be aware of both positions before attaching too much certainty to a positive OAT result.

That said, if you've tested negative on breath testing and your symptoms persist, an OAT provides data that the breath test simply doesn't collect. Different information from a different biological window is not nothing.

## Stool Testing: Useful Information, Wrong Location

Comprehensive stool testing has become widely available and, in some circles, widely recommended as a catch-all for gut health assessment. It can tell you quite a lot. It's just not particularly good at confirming SIBO specifically.

Here's the anatomical reason. A stool sample represents what's happening at the end of the digestive process — in the colon and rectum. The large intestine. SIBO is a small intestine problem. By the time the contents of your small intestine have traveled through the colon and become stool, the information about bacterial populations in the upper gut has been fundamentally altered. What you see in a stool test reflects the microbiome of the large intestine, not the small intestine. They are different environments with different organisms in different concentrations.

Stool testing is genuinely useful for detecting large intestinal dysbiosis, assessing inflammatory markers like calprotectin, flagging pathogenic bacteria or parasites, and evaluating digestive enzyme activity. These are real data points with real clinical applications. If your symptoms include rectal bleeding, significant changes in stool form, or if inflammatory bowel disease needs to be ruled out, stool markers are exactly what you want.

But if the question is whether you have bacterial overgrowth in your small intestine, a stool test is the wrong instrument for that question. It's looking in the wrong room.

## The Gold Standard Nobody Uses

If you've done any research into SIBO testing, you may have come across references to small intestinal aspirate and culture — the direct sampling of fluid from the small intestine. This is the actual gold standard for diagnosing SIBO. Not the breath test. Not the stool test. The aspirate.

Here's how it works. During an upper endoscopy — the same procedure used to diagnose celiac disease and visualize the upper GI tract — a physician uses a sterile catheter to aspirate a sample of fluid directly from the jejunum, the middle section of the small intestine. That fluid is then cultured in a laboratory. If bacterial colony counts come back at or above 10^3 colony-forming units per milliliter, SIBO is confirmed. There's no inference from gas measurements, no substrate transit assumptions, no prep diet variables. You're measuring the bacteria directly.

The reason almost no one gets this test is straightforward. It requires an endoscopy — an invasive procedure under sedation, performed by a gastroenterologist, with the associated cost and scheduling delays. The catheter must remain sterile throughout, which introduces technical challenges. And even when everything goes correctly, the sample captures only the section of small intestine reached by the scope. The distal small intestine — the ileum — isn't sampled. So even the gold standard has anatomical blind spots.

In clinical practice, the aspirate is usually reserved for patients with complex presentations, immunocompromised states, or cases where treatment repeatedly fails and the diagnosis is genuinely in question. Most people with suspected SIBO will never receive one, not because their doctors are cutting corners, but because the infrastructure to do it routinely simply doesn't exist outside of academic medical centers.

Knowing this matters. It means that the breath test was never chosen as the diagnostic standard because it's accurate. It was chosen because it's accessible. That's not a critique of any individual clinician. It's a structural fact about how medicine operates when the best option is impractical at scale. The breath test is not the gold standard. It's the available standard. Those are different things, and you needed someone to say it plainly.

## When the Treatment Becomes the Test

Some clinicians — particularly those who specialize in complex gut presentations — use the elemental diet as a diagnostic tool when breath testing has failed to provide a clear answer.

An elemental diet is a pre-digested liquid formula: nutrients broken down to their simplest components — amino acids, simple sugars, fats, vitamins, minerals. Because the gut absorbs these components directly, almost nothing reaches the small intestine as substrate for bacterial fermentation. You essentially starve the bacteria for two to three weeks while continuing to feed yourself.

If symptoms significantly improve on an elemental diet, that's clinically meaningful. It doesn't provide a definitive diagnosis — symptom improvement could reflect many things — but it strongly suggests bacterial fermentation in the small intestine was driving at least some of what you were experiencing. Some practitioners treat a clear clinical response to elemental as sufficient grounds to proceed with treatment, particularly when breath testing has been repeatedly negative but symptoms remain consistent with SIBO.

This approach sits outside standard diagnostic guidelines, and it's worth being clear-eyed about that. It's a clinical inference, not a confirmed diagnosis. But for people who've been caught in the loop of negative breath tests and ongoing symptoms, understanding that this option exists — and that some gastroenterologists consider it a legitimate diagnostic signal — is useful information.

## DCT Box #1: Questions to Ask Before Accepting a Negative Result

The following is a set of exact sentences you can use at your next appointment. Copy them into your phone before you go. You are not challenging your doctor's competence. You are asking for information you're entitled to have.

**About the substrate:**

"I want to make sure I understand my results — was this a lactulose test or a glucose test? I'm asking because I've read that glucose may miss overgrowth in the distal small intestine, and I want to know whether that's a factor here."

**About the gas panel:**

"Was hydrogen sulfide measured in this test? I ask because I understand it's not included in standard two-gas panels, and some of my symptoms — particularly [insert: diarrhea / sulfurous odor / bloating pattern] — are associated with hydrogen sulfide SIBO."

**About the actual numbers:**

"Can you walk me through the raw numbers on my graph? I'd like to know whether hydrogen showed any rise at all — even below the positive threshold — or whether it was completely flat. I understand both are reported as negative, but I want to understand what the data actually showed."

**About prep confounders:**

"I want to flag something about my prep. In the weeks before the test I was [eating very low carb / taking an antimicrobial supplement / on antibiotics]. Could that have suppressed my results?"

**If the answer to all of the above is that the test was technically sound and still negative:**

"Given that I still have symptoms consistent with SIBO, what would you recommend as next steps? I'd like to understand whether a three-gas test, organic acids testing, or a clinical trial of treatment makes sense in my case."

You are not asking your doctor to diagnose you with SIBO. You are asking for the information you need to make a decision together. There's a difference, and most good clinicians will recognize it.

## What This Means For You

Here's the plain version.

Breath testing is the primary diagnostic tool for SIBO, and it is genuinely limited. Not dramatically — it catches a real proportion of true cases — but limited enough that a negative result cannot close the book on the question. The sensitivity numbers we established in the introduction mean exactly what they sound like: a significant number of people with real bacterial overgrowth test negative. Add substrate limitations, prep variables, unmeasured gases, and the anatomical reality that no breath test reaches the entire small intestine, and the margin of error widens further.

If you tested negative and still feel exactly the way you'd expect to feel with SIBO, the test may have missed it. That is a legitimate medical possibility, not a rationalization.

Your job from here is not to self-diagnose. It's to understand what information the test did and didn't collect, and to ask specific questions before accepting a negative as the final word. The DCT box above gives you the exact language. Use it.
