# Chapter Ten
## Your Doctor Relationship: A Practical Guide to Getting What You Need

You've walked out of more appointments than you can count with nothing actionable. Not because the doctor didn't care. Because the system they're operating in wasn't designed to catch what you have — and nobody told you how to navigate around that.

That changes in this chapter.

You're not here to learn how to find the perfect doctor. You're here to learn how to walk into any appointment — with the doctor you have, or the next one — and leave with something you can use. A test ordered. A prokinetic discussed. A root cause conversation that actually happened instead of getting deflected. You have the framework now. This chapter is about how to use it in a room that wasn't built for you.

## Why Doctors Under-Address SIBO

Before we talk about how to work the system, it's worth understanding why the system works the way it does. Not to assign blame. To stop being surprised.

**The time problem.** A standard primary care appointment in the United States typically runs ten to fifteen minutes — a pattern documented consistently in outpatient medicine research, though appointment norms vary across healthcare systems. Diagnosing SIBO properly requires a history that covers years: what your symptoms are, when they started, what treatments you've tried, how you responded, what made things worse, what made things better, and what was happening in your life around the time this began. That history, taken properly, takes twenty minutes on its own. It doesn't fit. So what gets compressed is exactly the nuanced back-and-forth that a condition like SIBO requires.

This isn't your doctor not caring. It's your doctor running out of time before they've assembled enough information to see the pattern.

**The specialty gap.** SIBO sits in an awkward position in medicine. It's technically a gastroenterological condition, but many gastroenterologists focus primarily on structural disease — the kind you find with a colonoscopy or an endoscopy. Polyps. Tumours. Inflammatory bowel disease. Conditions that show up visually. SIBO doesn't show up on a scope. It shows up on a breath test, and breath testing is not universally available and not consistently reimbursed — and it is less standardised than endoscopic procedures.

Primary care physicians, on the other hand, are generalists. They may have encountered SIBO in training, but their day-to-day caseload covers everything from hypertension to anxiety to acute infections. SIBO-specific knowledge — the distinction between hydrogen and IMO, the role of prokinetics post-treatment, the root cause investigation framework — isn't something most primary care physicians maintain at working depth.

The result is a patient who falls into the gap between two specialties. Neither one fully owns SIBO. Neither one has the time or the training framework to manage it end-to-end. You are, through no fault of yours or your doctor's, falling between the cracks of a structural design problem.

**The testing lag.** A doctor who doesn't think to order a breath test will never see a positive result. And a doctor who has never seen a positive breath test result has no personal clinical experience to draw on — which means the condition stays abstract, which means it's less likely to be ordered. It's a closed loop. Breath testing isn't universally available. It isn't universally reimbursed. And in practices where it's rarely used, it rarely gets used.

This is why we spent an entire chapter on breath test mechanics. Understanding the test — its sensitivity limits, its failure modes, its real-world interpretation — is not academic. It's how you advocate for the right testing before someone orders the wrong one, or no test at all.

None of this is a reason to be angry at your doctor. It's a reason to arrive prepared.

## The Case Summary: One Page That Changes Everything

Here's the math on a ten-minute appointment. Your doctor spends roughly eight of those minutes extracting the context they need to understand your situation. That leaves about two minutes for the actual clinical conversation — the one where you ask for what you need and they engage with your request. Two minutes is not enough time to ask for a breath test, explain why you want it, respond to pushback, and walk out with a referral.

The fix is simple. You give them the eight minutes back.

A one-page case summary — brought to the appointment, handed over at the start — converts those first eight minutes into usable time. Your doctor gets context they would have spent the appointment extracting. You get the full ten minutes for the conversation you came to have.

This document is a tool, not a medical record. It's not a substitute for professional assessment, and your doctor may ask clarifying questions or reframe what you've written. That's fine. The goal is orientation, not control.

Here's what belongs on it:

**Your top five current symptoms**, each with an approximate severity on a 0–10 scale and how long you've had it. Not a paragraph — a short list. "Bloating after meals: 7/10, approximately 18 months. Loose stools most mornings: 6/10, approximately 2 years." Specific. Scannable.

**A condensed timeline.** When did symptoms begin. What treatments you've tried, in order. What happened after each one — improved, no change, relapsed after how long. This is the pattern that reveals relapse, and relapse is the signal that root cause was missed.

**Your suspected or confirmed root cause category**, if you know it. You may not know it yet. If you've worked through the root cause map from Chapter 4, you may have a reasonable hypothesis. Write it down. "Possible post-infectious MMC impairment — symptoms began six weeks after a food poisoning episode in 2022." A hypothesis is not a diagnosis. Your doctor knows that. But it tells them where to start looking.

**Your current protocol.** What you're taking, including any prokinetics. What eating window you're maintaining. Where you are in the treatment cycle — active antimicrobial phase, post-treatment, maintenance.

**What you are asking for today.** This is the most important line on the page, and the one most people forget to write down before they walk in. Be specific. "I'm asking for a lactulose breath test referral." Or: "I'm asking whether prucalopride would be appropriate for my case." Or: "I'd like to discuss root cause investigation — specifically whether thyroid function or stomach acid have been adequately ruled out." One ask. Maybe two. Not a list of everything you've read about in the last six months.

The case summary doesn't make you a difficult patient. It makes you an efficient one. Doctors who work with complex, multi-year conditions appreciate patients who arrive organised. It signals that you understand your own history, that you're engaged, and that you're not going to spend ten minutes telling an unfocused story that ends with "so I'm just not sure what's going on."

You know what's going on. Now you're asking for help with a specific next step.

That single shift — from "please help me figure this out" to "here's what I need next" — changes the temperature of the whole room.

## When to Find a New Doctor

This section is not about finding a doctor who agrees with you. That's not the standard. The standard is finding a doctor who can engage with the clinical framework at all — whether or not they land in the same place you do.

There are clear situations where continuing with your current doctor stops being a strategic problem and starts being a structural impossibility.

Your doctor has dismissed SIBO as a diagnosis entirely — not questioned your specific interpretation of it, but categorically denied that the condition warrants investigation — and has refused to order a breath test even after you've made a specific, evidence-grounded request. That's not a disagreement. That's a closed door.

They've prescribed the same antibiotic twice with no conversation about why it keeps failing. You relapsed. They re-prescribed. You relapsed again. That's not a treatment plan. That's a refill.

Your doctor is unfamiliar with the specific treatment approach your SIBO type requires — dual therapy for IMO, post-treatment prokinetics, structural cause investigation for documented motility disorders — and is unwilling either to learn or to refer you to someone who has that knowledge. Unfamiliarity alone isn't disqualifying. Unwillingness to engage with it is.

You have been symptomatic for well over a year with no movement toward a diagnosis or root cause investigation. No new testing. No treatment adjustment. Nothing has changed on their end, and nothing has changed on yours. If the approach has not changed and the condition has not changed, staying in that relationship is not loyalty — it's inertia.

Finding a new doctor in that context isn't giving up on the relationship. It's recognising that the clinical conversation has become structurally impossible, and that your health requires a conversation that can actually happen.

When you do look for a new provider, the question to ask — directly, before the first full appointment — is not "do you treat SIBO?" The question is: "Can you tell me how you approach root cause investigation after a SIBO relapse, and what is your thinking on post-treatment prokinetics for relapse prevention?" The answer tells you everything. A provider who can engage with that question specifically, whether their answer aligns exactly with what you've learned or not, is someone you can work with.

## Conventional Gastroenterology vs. Functional Medicine: The Honest Version

You've probably encountered this debate in the corners of the internet where SIBO patients gather. The conventional gastroenterologist who's never heard of prokinetics. The functional medicine practitioner who claims to have cured forty people this month with a protocol they invented. Neither stereotype is fair or useful. Here's the honest version.

**Conventional gastroenterology** is where you need to go to rule out structural disease. If you haven't had a colonoscopy, an upper endoscopy, or appropriate imaging to rule out Crohn's disease, celiac disease, intestinal obstruction, or malignancy — that work needs to happen before you commit to a SIBO protocol. A gastroenterologist is the right person to do it. Full stop. Conventional GI medicine is also strong on evidence standards. If a gastroenterologist tells you the evidence for a particular intervention is weak, they're probably right, and that's worth hearing even if it's frustrating.

Where conventional gastroenterology can fall short for SIBO patients is in the functional space — conditions where structure looks normal but function is impaired. Many gastroenterologists focus on endoscopic findings. When the scope looks normal, they may not have a strong clinical framework for what to do next. Breath testing is less standardised in conventional GI practice than you might expect. Post-treatment prokinetics aren't always part of the conversation. Root cause investigation — especially for motility dysfunction — may not be a primary area of focus.

**Functional and integrative medicine practitioners** are generally more oriented toward ordering breath testing, discussing root cause, and considering prokinetics post-treatment and herbal antimicrobial protocols — though individual practice varies widely. That's the upside. The downside is significant variability in quality. The functional medicine category is not a credential. It covers practitioners who are evidence-based, rigorous, and genuinely skilled — and it also covers practitioners who operate largely on theory and enthusiasm. Evaluate on specifics. Does this provider know the difference between hydrogen and IMO and why it matters? Do they understand why treatment type needs to match SIBO type? Can they tell you their approach to post-treatment management? If yes, you're talking to someone who knows what they're doing. If no, the category label doesn't protect you.

The practical reality for most SIBO patients is this: the best outcome comes from a practitioner — in either category — who understands the full picture. Breath testing. SIBO type-specific treatment. Treatment sequencing. Post-treatment prokinetics. Root cause investigation. That practitioner exists in both categories. Your job is to find one, not to pick a category and hope.

## The Relationship You Now Know How to Have

You came into this with a stack of symptoms and a list of things that hadn't worked. You're leaving with a framework.

That's not a small shift. It changes the entire shape of every medical appointment you walk into from here. You know what SIBO is, what type you're dealing with, why it comes back, and what a complete treatment plan looks like. You have four scripted conversations ready to go. You have a case summary template that gives your doctor the context they need to actually help you. You have criteria — not feelings, criteria — for knowing when to stay and when to move on.

The doctor relationship used to be something that happened to you. You'd walk in with hope or frustration, describe your symptoms as best you could in whatever time you had, and wait to see if this appointment would be the one that finally moved things forward. Sometimes it was. Often it wasn't.

That's not the relationship you're walking into anymore. You're walking in prepared, specific, and clear about what you need next. What you need from your doctor has narrowed from "help me figure this out" to "here's the next step — can you help me take it." That's a question almost any competent doctor can answer. And when they can't, you now know how to tell the difference between a doctor who's struggling with time constraints and a doctor who's not the right fit for this particular problem.

The work of the next chapter isn't new information. It's closure — a chance to hold the whole arc of what you've learned in one place and carry it forward. Not as a protocol. As a way of being in your own health.

The most useful thing you can do before your next appointment is build your case summary. Spend twenty minutes on it. Five symptoms with severity and duration. A timeline in plain sentences. Your root cause hypothesis if you have one. Your current protocol. One specific ask.

Then decide which DCT Box applies to the conversation you're about to have. Read that section again the night before.

Walk in with both. Hand the summary over at the start.

That picture has changed. You walked in not knowing enough to ask the right question, and you walked out with whatever the appointment produced — which was rarely what you needed and never quite what you came for.
