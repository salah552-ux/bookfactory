# Chapter Four
## Root Causes: Why Treating SIBO Without This Step Is Just Buying Time

The antibiotic course is finished. The breath test came back improved or normalised. You feel better — genuinely, measurably better. And somewhere in the background, something none of your doctors have mentioned is still running. Quietly. Unaddressed. Waiting.

The bacteria came back because they never had a reason not to. This chapter is about finding that reason.

Rifaximin clears bacteria from the small intestine. That's what it does, and in many cases it does it well. What it doesn't do — what no antibiotic does — is fix whatever allowed bacteria to establish there in the first place. The treatment addresses the consequence. The cause continues running in the background, quietly, and when the antibiotic is finished and the gut environment settles, bacteria move back in. Because the door is still open.

This is not a flaw in rifaximin. It was never designed to close the door. It was designed to clear an overgrowth. The flaw is in the protocol that stops there — that hands you the antibiotic and calls the job done, as if the question of why you developed SIBO in the first place is either unanswerable or irrelevant.

It is neither.

There are specific, identifiable reasons the small intestine became hospitable to bacterial overgrowth. Some of them are mechanical. Some are immunological. Some are structural. Most are addressable — not always completely, but enough to shift the odds meaningfully. And every one of them was operating in your gut before your first symptom appeared, before your first prescription was written, before any of this started.

This chapter is about finding yours.

---

## What "Root Cause" Actually Means Here

The term gets used loosely enough that it's worth being precise about what we mean.

The root cause of SIBO is not bad bacteria. Bacteria are everywhere. You ingest them constantly. Your gut deals with them constantly. In a healthy gut, the systems designed to prevent overgrowth do their job well enough that the bacteria don't get a foothold. The root cause is the failure of one or more of those systems.

There are five categories of failure. Most people with SIBO have at least two — which is part of why treating one and ignoring the others produces partial improvement and then relapse. They stack. They interact. A post-infectious injury to the gut's cleaning mechanism, combined with a structural issue that slows transit, combined with a decade of PPI use — that's not three separate problems. That's one compounding problem with three layers.

The goal of this chapter is to help you build your own root cause map. Not a diagnosis — that requires tests and a clinician. A working hypothesis. A set of questions specific enough to take to an appointment and get real answers from.

Here are the five categories. Read all of them. Most people find themselves in more than one.

---

## Category One: The MMC — Your Gut's Cleaning System

This one deserves the most space, because it explains more SIBO cases than any other single factor.

Your small intestine runs a cleaning cycle between meals. Every 90 to 120 minutes on average — when the gut is empty, when you haven't eaten or drunk anything caloric — a wave of muscular contractions moves from the stomach all the way down through the small intestine, sweeping bacteria, debris, and digestive residue into the large intestine, where bacteria belong. This is the migrating motor complex, or MMC. It is the primary defence mechanism against bacterial overgrowth.

When it works, bacteria don't accumulate. They get swept downstream before they can establish. When it fails — when the contractions are too weak, too infrequent, or interrupted constantly — bacteria settle in. They ferment. They multiply. And the conditions that let SIBO take hold are renewed every single day that the MMC isn't running properly.

Understanding the MMC is not academic. It is the mechanical explanation for why so many people cycle through antibiotics and keep ending up in the same place. The antibiotic clears the bacteria. The MMC failure reseeds the problem.

### What Disrupts the MMC

Several things can impair the MMC. Some are acute — they damage the mechanism in a single event and leave lasting effects. Some are chronic — they suppress the MMC on an ongoing basis, every day.

**Post-infectious damage.** Chapter 1 covered the mechanism in full — the CdtB toxin, the vinculin antibodies, the autoimmune attack on the nerve cells that initiate MMC contractions. What's worth emphasising here is the duration. This isn't damage that heals in a few weeks. The antibodies persist. The MMC impairment continues. And every subsequent SIBO treatment that doesn't address this leaves the nerve dysfunction in place, ready to allow recolonisation the moment antibiotic cover is withdrawn.

If your SIBO story begins with a memorable food poisoning event — the kind that put you in the bathroom for two days and left your gut feeling fundamentally different afterward — post-infectious MMC damage is not a possibility you should mention in passing to your doctor. It should be at the centre of the conversation.

**Hypothyroidism.** The thyroid regulates the baseline rate of many physiological processes, including gut motility. An underactive thyroid slows gut movement across the board — including MMC contractions. Hypothyroidism is common, undertreated, and consistently associated with constipation, bloating, and slow transit. If you have SIBO and an untreated or undertreated thyroid condition, the thyroid is not a separate issue. It is a root cause.

**Diabetes and blood sugar dysregulation.** High blood sugar damages the autonomic nerves that regulate gut motility. Diabetic gastroparesis — severely impaired stomach emptying — is the dramatic end of this spectrum, but sub-clinical autonomic nerve damage is possible well before a formal diabetes diagnosis. If you have insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, or consistently elevated fasting glucose, this is relevant to your gut picture.

**Opioid medications.** Opioids bind to receptors throughout the gut and directly suppress motility. This is why opioid-induced constipation is one of the most reliable drug side effects in medicine. Long-term opioid use for chronic pain creates a gut environment where bacteria can accumulate — and where SIBO is both more likely and harder to treat without addressing the opioid effect.

**Eating too frequently.** This is the one that surprises people. And it's the one most likely to be driven by advice from a wellness source that meant well.

The MMC only activates in a fasted state. It requires the gut to be empty. The moment you eat — or drink anything caloric — the digestive cycle restarts and the MMC pauses. This is normal and appropriate. The problem arises when that pause extends across most of the day because eating is continuous.

If you're eating every two to three hours — breakfast, mid-morning snack, lunch, afternoon snack, dinner, evening bite — the MMC never gets a full cycle. It starts, gets interrupted, starts again, gets interrupted again. The bacteria that were about to be swept downstream aren't. They stay. They establish. And when you develop SIBO on this eating pattern and ask your doctor why, the snacking frequency is almost never part of the conversation.

This doesn't mean you need to fast aggressively. It means the gap between your last meal at night and your first meal in the morning matters. It means constant grazing — however "healthy" the individual items — is actively working against your gut's cleaning mechanism. We'll return to this in Chapter 6, which is specifically about eating timing. For now: the frequency of eating is a root cause variable, and it is entirely within your control.

### How to Know If the MMC Is Implicated

There's no standard clinical test for MMC function at the primary care level. Antroduodenal manometry — a pressure-sensor tube down the oesophagus — can measure motility directly, but it's invasive and rarely used outside specialised centres.

What you can observe:
- SIBO that returns rapidly after treatment (within weeks rather than months)
- Symptoms that are consistently worse on days when you've eaten more frequently
- Symptoms that improve meaningfully with fasting or extended gaps between meals
- A SIBO story that begins clearly after a food poisoning event
- Constipation that predates your SIBO diagnosis — this suggests slow motility was operating before the overgrowth

If three or more of these apply, the MMC is almost certainly part of your root cause picture.

## Category Two: Stomach Acid — The Gate That Isn't Guarding

Your stomach is not designed to be a comfortable environment. The gastric acid it produces maintains a pH between 1.5 and 3.5 under normal conditions — acidic enough to kill most bacteria before they reach the small intestine. This is a deliberate defence. Bacteria that enter through food or water don't make it to the small intestine intact. The acid takes them out.

When stomach acid is chronically low, that defence drops. More bacteria survive the journey through the stomach. More bacteria reach the small intestine with the opportunity to establish. The gate is open.

**Proton pump inhibitors.** PPIs — omeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole, esomeprazole — are among the most widely prescribed medications in the world, used to manage acid reflux, heartburn, and gastroesophageal reflux disease. They are effective for those purposes. They are also one of the most clearly established risk factors for SIBO development.

PPIs work by blocking the pumps in the stomach wall that produce acid. They do this reliably and effectively, which is why they work well for reflux. They also reliably reduce the acid that was protecting the small intestine from bacterial colonisation.

Multiple studies have found significantly elevated SIBO rates in PPI users compared to non-users. The relationship is dose-dependent — longer use and higher doses carry higher risk. This does not mean you need to stop your PPI immediately or without medical guidance. It means that if you're on a PPI and you have SIBO, the PPI is not an innocent bystander. It may be a direct contributor. It belongs in the root cause conversation.

**H. pylori infection.** Helicobacter pylori is a bacterium that colonizes the stomach lining and, over time, disrupts acid production. It does this partly through direct damage to acid-producing cells and partly through inflammation that alters the stomach's regulatory signalling. The result: chronic H. pylori infection reduces the acid barrier.

H. pylori is common — estimated in roughly half the global population, though rates vary significantly by geography and socioeconomic conditions. Many people carry it asymptomatically for years. If you've been tested for it, or treated for it and still have ongoing gut symptoms, its role in your stomach acid level — and therefore your SIBO susceptibility — is worth reviewing.

**Age-related changes.** Stomach acid production declines with age. This is a normal process that becomes clinically relevant in some individuals, particularly after 60. Atrophic gastritis — chronic inflammation of the stomach lining that progressively reduces acid-producing cell numbers — is associated with both reduced acid and elevated SIBO risk.

### The Acid-Reflux Trap

Here is a problem that matters to a substantial proportion of SIBO patients: many people with SIBO develop symptoms that resemble acid reflux. The bloating and gas pressure in the abdomen can push upward, causing discomfort in the chest and throat that presents exactly like reflux. The treatment for reflux is a PPI. The PPI reduces stomach acid. The reduced acid worsens the SIBO. The SIBO produces more pressure. The reflux-like symptoms intensify.

If you are on a PPI for reflux that began around the same time as your gut issues — or if the reflux worsened after your gut symptoms began — this cycle is worth discussing with your doctor. The reflux may be a consequence of SIBO-driven pressure, not a separate condition requiring acid suppression.

## Category Three: Post-Infectious Damage

Chapter 1 covered the mechanism in detail — the food poisoning event, the CdtB toxin, the vinculin antibodies, the autoimmune attack on the gut's pacemaker cells. The core point here is what that damage means for root cause treatment.

Post-infectious MMC damage is not reversible through antibiotics. The autoimmune process that attacks the nerve cells coordinating motility continues after the original infection is gone. Clearing the bacteria doesn't stop the antibody attack. The pacemaker cells remain compromised.

This is why post-infectious SIBO has some of the highest relapse rates. The infection is gone. The antibodies are not. The MMC stays impaired. The bacteria return.

Research into treatments that specifically address post-infectious autoimmune damage — including low-dose naltrexone and certain motility agents — is ongoing. The state of the evidence doesn't yet support a single clear protocol. What is established is that identifying post-infectious damage as a root cause changes the treatment priority: addressing motility becomes non-negotiable, not optional. We'll cover the specific interventions in Chapter 8.

## Category Four: Structural Contributors

The small intestine's architecture matters. Anything that creates pockets, narrowings, slow-transit zones, or allows backflow from the large intestine gives bacteria an opportunity to accumulate that a healthy, unobstructed gut wouldn't offer.

**Adhesions from surgery.** Abdominal surgery — appendectomy, C-section, bowel surgery, hysterectomy, gallbladder removal — creates scar tissue as a normal part of healing. That scar tissue can form adhesions: fibrous bands that attach to the intestinal wall and pull it into positions that create kinks, narrow passages, or areas of reduced motility. Bacteria pool in these slow zones. Transit slows. SIBO risk increases.

If you had abdominal surgery before your gut symptoms began — even years before — adhesions are worth considering. The relationship is not inevitable, but it is documented, and it is not visible on standard imaging.

**Endometriosis.** Endometriosis affects structures throughout the pelvic and abdominal cavity and can involve the bowel directly. Intestinal endometriosis creates both structural disruption and chronic inflammation at the gut wall. Motility in affected segments is often impaired. For women with SIBO and a history of endometriosis — or who suspect endometriosis — the two conditions may be more closely linked than they have been treated.

**The ileocecal valve.** The ileocecal valve sits at the junction between the small intestine and the large intestine. Its job is straightforward and essential: open to let digested material pass from small intestine to colon, then close to prevent backflow of colonic bacteria into the clean environment of the small intestine.

When the ileocecal valve doesn't close fully — when it loses tone or becomes chronically loose — colonic bacteria can migrate upward. This is one of the more direct physical mechanisms of SIBO development. Causes include chronic constipation (the prolonged pressure can affect valve tone), previous infection or inflammation, and in some cases connective tissue laxity.

Ileocecal valve dysfunction isn't routinely evaluated in most GI workups. It exists in a diagnostic gap. If you have recurrent SIBO with a pattern that suggests upward migration — symptoms that are worse with constipation, or that began after a period of severe constipation — this is a question worth raising specifically.

## Category Five: Stress, the Nervous System, and the Cortisol Connection

This is the one that most people dismiss as "it's just stress" — the same dismissal they've already received from a doctor. It deserves a more careful treatment.

Chronic psychological stress affects gut motility through concrete physiological mechanisms. This is not a metaphor for "your anxiety is giving you gut symptoms." It is a description of how the autonomic nervous system regulates gut function and what happens when that regulation is chronically impaired.

The autonomic nervous system has two primary modes: sympathetic (the stress response — fight, flight, rapid reaction) and parasympathetic (rest, digest, repair). Gut motility — including MMC activity — is primarily a parasympathetic function. When the stress response is chronically dominant, parasympathetic function is suppressed. The gut slows. The MMC runs less effectively or less frequently.

Cortisol, the body's primary stress hormone, has documented effects on gut motility. Chronic elevation — from persistent psychological stress, from disrupted sleep, from physiological stressors like chronic illness or inadequate caloric intake — is associated with impaired gut motility and reduced MMC function. The relationship is not one-directional. Gut dysfunction also drives cortisol elevation through the gut-brain axis communication described in Chapter 2. The two systems reinforce each other's dysfunction in a loop that has no clean entry point once it's established.

What this means practically:

Stress management in the context of SIBO is not advice to feel less anxious about your symptoms. It is a physiological intervention. Practices that activate the parasympathetic nervous system — slow diaphragmatic breathing, specific vagal nerve stimulation techniques, extended gaps between meals that allow genuine fasted rest — have documented effects on gut motility. They are not supplements. They are not wellness add-ons. They are interventions that directly affect the root cause mechanism.

The person who has been told "your symptoms are probably stress-related" has usually been told this as a way of being dismissed. The statement may nevertheless be partially correct — and rather than rejecting it entirely, it's worth asking which specific mechanisms are involved and what can actually be done about them.

## The Connective Tissue Variable

If you are hypermobile — if you have Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hypermobile type), hypermobility spectrum disorder, or if joints have always bent further than they should — your gut is part of the same picture.

Connective tissue provides the structural matrix for almost every organ in the body, including the gut wall. The smooth muscle of the intestine — the muscle responsible for peristalsis and for generating MMC contractions — relies on healthy connective tissue scaffolding to function properly. In hypermobile connective tissue disorders, that scaffolding is defective. The collagen that should be providing tensile strength and organised structure is instead lax, stretchy, and poorly organised.

The clinical result is GI dysmotility — gut movement that is slower, weaker, or less coordinated than it should be. This dysmotility is structural. It doesn't respond to antibiotics. It doesn't resolve when the SIBO is treated. It is the reason SIBO keeps coming back in a subset of people with hypermobility who never understand why their gut never fully normalizes.

GI dysmotility is consistently documented in hypermobile EDS and HSD. The specific prevalence of confirmed SIBO in this population is not yet well established — the picture is complicated by the fact that some of the gut symptoms that look like SIBO in this population may have other dysmotility origins. What is established is that if you have a connective tissue disorder and significant gut dysfunction, the two are almost certainly related, and treatment that doesn't account for the structural component is addressing only part of the picture.

If you have never been evaluated for hypermobility — if you've been told you're "just flexible" without clinical assessment — the Beighton score is a simple clinical screening tool. It's worth knowing if you have unexplained gut dysfunction alongside joint symptoms.

## How to Build Your Root Cause Map

You now have five categories. The practical question is: which of these applies to you?

Not all of them require clinical testing to identify. Some can be established by history and pattern recognition. Here is a working framework.

**Start with your timeline.**

When did your gut symptoms begin? Is there a clear precipitating event — food poisoning, surgery, a course of antibiotics, a period of extreme stress, a new medication? The starting point often tells you the category. Post-infectious? Structural? Drug-induced?

**Then map your risk factors.**

Go through each category. Which risk factors apply to your history?
- PPI use, current or past? → Category Two
- Food poisoning or viral gastroenteritis that "changed your gut" → Category One, Category Three
- Abdominal surgery, endometriosis, or chronic constipation? → Category Four
- Hypothyroidism, diabetes, or blood sugar issues? → Category One
- Hypermobility or connective tissue diagnosis? → Category Five
- Chronic stress, poor sleep, chronically disrupted eating pattern? → Category One, Category Five

**Then look at your relapse pattern.**

- Rapid relapse (within weeks of treatment): MMC dysfunction, structural issue, or significant ongoing stressor
- Relapse linked to eating patterns: MMC disruption from frequent eating
- Relapse after periods of high stress: Cortisol-motility connection
- Relapse following illness: Post-infectious mechanism or stress response

**Finally, consider your SIBO type.**

Methane-dominant IMO is particularly associated with slow motility. If your SIBO is methane-dominant with constipation-predominant symptoms, the root cause almost certainly involves the MMC or structural slow transit. Hydrogen sulfide SIBO with inflammatory symptoms may involve a different combination of factors. Type is a clue. Use it.

> ## DCT Box #2: Getting Your Root Cause Investigated
>
> Most gastroenterology appointments focus on the overgrowth itself. This box gives you the specific language to redirect that focus toward the cause. Bring it printed, or copy it into your phone before the appointment.
>
> **Opening the conversation:**
>
> *"I'd like to understand what's driving my SIBO, not just treat the bacteria again. I've had [number] rounds of treatment and [number] relapses. I'd like to investigate why it keeps coming back before starting another antibiotic course."*
>
> **If your SIBO followed food poisoning:**
>
> *"My gut symptoms began after a food poisoning episode in [year]. I've read about the CdtB-vinculin autoimmune mechanism. Has that been considered as a root cause in my case? Is there testing available for anti-vinculin or anti-CdtB antibodies?"*
>
> **If you're on a PPI:**
>
> *"I've been on [PPI name] for [duration]. I'd like to discuss whether long-term PPI use may be contributing to my SIBO susceptibility and what my options are for either reducing the dose or addressing the underlying reflux differently."*
>
> **If you suspect motility dysfunction:**
>
> *"I'd like to understand whether my MMC is functioning properly. What motility assessment options are available — either antroduodenal manometry or a clinical assessment based on transit time? And would prokinetics be appropriate given my pattern?"*
>
> **If you have hypermobility:**
>
> *"I have hypermobile EDS / hypermobility spectrum disorder. I understand GI dysmotility is well documented in this population. Can we discuss whether my gut symptoms may have a structural motility component that needs to be addressed alongside the bacterial overgrowth?"*
>
> **Closing:**
>
> *"I'm not looking to skip treatment. I'm asking to understand the cause so that whatever we do next has a better chance of holding."*
>
> *This language is intended to support productive clinical conversations. All diagnostic and treatment decisions should be made in partnership with your healthcare provider.*

## This Is the Turn

There's a moment in most people's SIBO history where the pattern finally becomes visible. Where the relapse stops feeling random and starts feeling inevitable — because it was inevitable, given a mechanism that was never addressed.

This is that moment.

You've been treating the bacteria. The bacteria were never the problem. They were the predictable consequence of a door left open — a motility system that stopped working, an acid barrier that was lowered by a medication, a valve that wasn't closing, a connective tissue structure that couldn't generate the contractions needed to keep the small intestine clean.

The bacteria came back because the conditions that invited them kept running. The treatment was real. The root cause was just never part of it.

This is not a reason for anger, though anger is understandable. It's a reason for clarity. You now know what you're dealing with. Not just the overgrowth. The mechanism behind it. The conditions that created it and sustained it and made every treatment you've tried more temporary than it should have been.

That knowledge changes what happens next. It changes what questions you ask at your next appointment. It changes what you monitor after treatment. It changes the difference between clearing SIBO and being done with SIBO.

You were never going to fix this by treating the bacteria alone. You were always going to need to find the door and close it.

Now you know where to look.
