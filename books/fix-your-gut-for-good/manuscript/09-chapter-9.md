# Chapter Nine
## Relapse Prevention: The Chapter That Should Have Come First

Relapse isn't random. That's the thing nobody tells you after the second time around, when you're standing in the pharmacy again with the same prescription you had fourteen months ago, feeling that particular combination of exhaustion and fury that comes from being back at the beginning. It isn't bad luck. It isn't your body being difficult. It follows a pattern — a predictable, mechanistic sequence of events that, once you understand it, stops being a mystery and starts being a problem you can actually solve.

There are three triggers. Every SIBO relapse traces back to at least one of them, and often two.

The first is an untreated root cause — the door that was never closed. The antibiotic cleared the bacteria; the condition producing them was never touched. This is the most common trigger. You already know it from Chapter 4.

The second is post-treatment window failure — prokinetics not started in time, the eating window abandoned before it had any chance to matter, reintroduction pushed too fast. The bacterial niche was cleared and then immediately made available again. You know this from Chapters 6, 7, and 8.

The third is what I call accumulative stressor loading. Not a single event that broke you, but a period of sustained pressure — chronic work stress running for months, a course of antibiotics for a respiratory infection, a significant illness, surgery, a disrupted travel schedule — enough biological interference to push your gut environment past a threshold it could no longer hold. One of those events alone might not have done it. All of them stacking did.

Knowing which trigger you're dealing with changes everything about how you respond. This chapter gives you the maintenance protocol to prevent relapse, the early warning system to catch it early, the 48-hour rapid response to act before a partial return becomes a full one, and the clinical language to re-enter the doctor conversation when you need to — differently this time.

---

### The Door You Still Haven't Closed

Here's the frustrating arithmetic of SIBO treatment: rifaximin normalises breath test results in roughly 60–70% of hydrogen-dominant SIBO cases. Among the patients who respond, a tracked patient cohort found a relapse rate of 43.7% at nine months. That's not a treatment failure statistic in the sense of a flawed drug — that's a root cause statistic. Bacteria were cleared. The environment that produces them wasn't.

Return to the root cause map from Chapter 4. The five categories are MMC dysfunction, low stomach acid, post-infectious autoimmune damage, structural contributors, and the stress-cortisol-motility axis. In the months since your treatment, how many of those categories were actively addressed? Not mentioned in a conversation — actually addressed.

If your root cause is MMC dysfunction from post-infectious CdtB-vinculin damage, and you never started a prokinetic, the interstitial cells of Cajal are still functionally compromised. The Phase III sweep is still arriving late or not at all. The fasted interval you were supposed to use for bacterial clearance was still partially occupied. You treated the symptom. You left the mechanism running.

If your root cause is hypochlorhydria — low stomach acid — and the conversation never moved past your SIBO diagnosis, the acid barrier in your stomach is still allowing upward bacterial migration from the colon that wouldn't survive in a more acidic environment. The threshold was never addressed. The bacteria came back because the conditions that invited them in the first place were never changed.

This matters because the response to relapse cannot be "same antibiotic again." It has to be "which trigger is active right now — and what is the root cause we didn't finish treating."

That's not a question your doctor will always ask first. It may be the question you have to bring to the table.

### The Maintenance Protocol

Treatment completion is not the finish line. It's the starting gate for the next phase — a maintenance protocol you run indefinitely, not until you feel better, but until the conditions that put you here have been demonstrably addressed.

This isn't an aggressive, restrictive protocol. It doesn't require a radical overhaul of your life. It requires four things done consistently, and one scheduled test.

**The eating window is permanent.**

Four to five hours between meals — not a treatment phase, not a temporary inconvenience. This is the schedule on which your MMC runs. In the fasted state between meals, the Phase III propulsive wave sweeps the small intestine clear of bacterial accumulation. If you eat every two to three hours, or graze through the day on snacks that register as caloric input, you suppress that sweep. Nothing else you do compensates for that failure. No prokinetic performs optimally against a background of constant feeding.

The eating window is the one dietary intervention with genuine mechanistic rationale specific to SIBO. The four-to-five-hour number isn't an arbitrary rule — it's built around the MMC's cycle length, 90 to 120 minutes on average, and the fasted state it needs to run. The mechanism is solid. The window gives the engine time to work.

Water, black coffee, and plain tea are still permitted between meals. They don't trigger a significant cephalic phase or caloric response sufficient to suppress the MMC. Everything else — a handful of nuts, a piece of fruit, a protein shake — counts as a meal. Hold the window.

**Prokinetics: three to six months minimum.**

If you left Chapter 8 with a prokinetic protocol, continue it for a minimum of three to six months after the end of your antibiotic course. For patients with documented motility dysfunction — post-infectious CdtB damage, connective tissue conditions, autonomic nervous system involvement — the duration may extend to twelve months, and the step-down decision should be made with a practitioner, ideally alongside a repeat breath test result rather than symptom intuition alone.

The logic: prokinetics are restoring a function that was compromised. You don't stop a cardiac support medication because you feel better after two weeks. You titrate down when the underlying function is demonstrably recovering. The same principle applies here. Consult your healthcare provider before making any changes to a prescribed prokinetic protocol.

**Stress-motility awareness: the maintenance risk factor you can actually influence.**

Chronic sympathetic nervous system dominance is associated with reduced peristaltic activity in observational and mechanistic data — though direct causation in humans has not been conclusively established. The practical implication is that prolonged sympathetic activation may be one of several factors that affect the gut environment over time. This is not a psychology lecture. It is a mechanism worth taking seriously.

This doesn't mean eliminating stress, which is impossible. It means reducing the frequency and duration of prolonged sympathetic activation. Three things have the most practical impact on this: consistent sleep duration (seven to nine hours — not as a wellness suggestion, but because slow-wave and REM sleep are the parasympathetic dominance window during which the MMC runs its most effective overnight clearing cycle), regular meal spacing that prevents the physiological stress of extreme hunger, and deliberate reduction of chronic stressors where they're within your control to address. Not all of them. Not perfectly. The ones that are taking the most from the nervous system budget at any given time.

**Repeat breath testing: not optional for high-risk patients.**

Schedule a repeat breath test at three months post-treatment. If symptoms have returned, schedule a second at six months. For patients with documented motility dysfunction — and for those with connective tissue conditions such as hEDS and HSD, where GI dysmotility is documented even though SIBO prevalence in this population has not been formally established — annual breath testing is a reasonable clinical practice standard. Discuss with your prescriber whether this applies to your situation. For anyone in whom early warning signs persist beyond 48 hours without improvement, a breath test is the right next step before any treatment decision.

This gives the clinical conversation data. It confirms whether a true relapse is occurring, identifies the current dominant gas type — which may have shifted since the original diagnosis — and prevents the reflexive repetition of a treatment that may no longer match the clinical picture.

Root cause re-evaluation belongs in this same conversation. If relapse occurs, the first question is not which antibiotic. The first question is which of the three triggers is active right now.

### The Early Warning System

Your body gives you a window before a full relapse establishes itself. Five signals tend to appear in the early phase, when the bacterial population is shifting but hasn't yet reached the threshold of your previous worst symptoms. Catching them at this stage gives you options that a full relapse doesn't.

The first signal is bloating returning after meals that were previously well-tolerated — particularly meals with fermentable content that you'd successfully reintroduced. A food that was fine last month produces the familiar pressure and distension. Once could be anything. Two or three times in a row is a signal worth tracking.

The second is the return of brain fog — specifically the post-meal variety. You know what it feels like: a heaviness that arrives thirty to ninety minutes after eating, makes concentration effortful, and sits somewhere between tiredness and confusion. This is a gut-brain axis signal. Hydrogen gas from bacterial fermentation is absorbed into the portal circulation — a documented pathway — and its neurological effects are an active area of research. Some patients report post-meal cognitive symptoms that may reflect this mechanism. When the brain fog comes back before the bloating does, patients often identify it as the clearest early marker they have. Trust that pattern recognition. You've lived in this body through enough cycles to know the difference between ordinary tiredness and that specific, familiar fog.

The third signal is urgency or motility changes after a period of normalised transit. If your bowel pattern stabilised during and after treatment — if you knew roughly what to expect and when — and it's started becoming unpredictable again, that's a signal. This one appears differently depending on your dominant SIBO type: hydrogen-dominant SIBO tends toward looser, more urgent transit; IMO tends toward slowing and constipation; hydrogen sulfide involvement can produce urgency with a distinctive character you'll recognise from before.

The fourth signal is the return of what you personally recognise as your positive-breath-test symptom cluster. You know what your SIBO feels like at its worst. The clinical threshold for a positive breath test is a 20 ppm rise in hydrogen or 10 ppm methane at any point during the test — but your pattern recognition predates the formal number. When you start feeling the way you felt before the last positive test, that knowledge is clinically relevant. It belongs in your symptom log and in your appointment.

Four of those signals show up in your gut. This one shows up in your sleep.

The week before the bloating returned last time — did you sleep badly without knowing why? That's not coincidence. The gut and the nervous system are in constant bidirectional communication via the vagus nerve, with 80 to 90 percent of vagal fibres carrying signals from the gut to the brain. When the gut environment is destabilising, some patients experience a deterioration in sleep architecture and a worsening of baseline fatigue days before the first GI symptom appears. If you've experienced this sequence before, you already know it. Pay attention when the sleep goes bad without an obvious reason.

These five signals are not a diagnosis. They are a reason to act — to tighten the eating window immediately, to reassess what's happened in your life over the past few weeks, and to contact your prescriber about a repeat breath test if they persist.

### The 48-Hour Rapid Response

When early warning signs appear, the worst response is panic — either ignoring the signals and hoping they pass, or immediately self-treating with whatever was in the previous protocol. Both responses cost you information you need.

The 48-hour rapid response is not a treatment. It is a structured information-gathering window that prevents premature decisions and gives your prescriber something to work with.

*This protocol is for educational purposes only. It does not constitute medical advice and is not a substitute for clinical evaluation. Consult your healthcare provider before making any changes to your treatment plan.*

Here's how it runs:

- **Immediately:** tighten the eating window to a minimum of four hours between meals. No exceptions for 48 hours. This removes the feeding interference that suppresses MMC function and gives the fasted sweeping mechanism its best operating conditions. If you've been slightly relaxed about the window — five and a half hours here, a late snack there — correct that now.

- **Prokinetics:** if you're currently taking one, stay on it. Do not stop. If you stepped down from a prokinetic in the past two to four weeks, contact your prescriber now — this timing matters, and the step-down may need to be paused pending the clinical picture.

- **Log symptoms for 48 hours.** Type, severity on a 0-to-10 scale, timing relative to meals, which meals trigger the worst response. If brain fog is present, log its timing relative to eating. This is the data your prescriber needs to make a decision. Don't come to that appointment with a vague sense that things are worse — come with a 48-hour log.

- **Identify the trigger category.** Is there a clear stressor in play — a period of sustained work pressure, a significant emotional event, disrupted sleep over multiple weeks? Did you take a course of systemic antibiotics for another infection in the past three months? Were you significantly ill, had surgery, or travelled across time zones in a way that disrupted your sleep and eating schedule? Identifying the trigger category before the appointment changes the conversation you're able to have.

- **If symptoms worsen or don't improve within 48 hours:** contact your prescriber. Request a repeat breath test before any treatment decision is made. Do not accept a new antibiotic prescription without a confirmed clinical picture. The 48-hour window exists to prevent you from re-entering treatment blind — the same way you entered it last time.

The 48-hour protocol is the difference between reacting and responding. One costs you months. The other gives you data.

### DCT Box #4 — The Relapse Conversation

> **How to talk to your doctor when symptoms return.**
>
> This is the fourth Doctor Communication Toolkit script — the one you're most likely to need, and the one where the quality of the conversation matters most. Use this language when you believe you're experiencing a relapse.
>
> ---
>
> **Opening statement:**
>
> *"My symptoms have returned after treatment. Before we discuss retreatment, I'd like to understand which of three possible triggers is most likely in my case: an untreated root cause, a post-treatment window failure, or an accumulative stressor event."*
>
> This sentence does three things: it names the framework, it signals that you're not coming in for a reflexive repeat prescription, and it invites a diagnostic conversation rather than a prescribing one.
>
> ---
>
> **If you're being pushed toward immediate retreatment without investigation:**
>
> *"I'd like to request a repeat breath test before starting another antibiotic course. I want to confirm whether this is a true relapse, understand my current dominant gas type, and make sure we're treating the right thing this time."*
>
> This is not a refusal. It's a request for information before action. Most practitioners who are managing SIBO carefully will consider this reasonable.
>
> ---
>
> **If your root cause was never fully addressed:**
>
> *"My root cause was identified as [MMC dysfunction / low stomach acid / post-infectious autoimmune damage / structural factor]. I don't think it was fully addressed in my previous treatment plan. Can we add that to the conversation before the next antibiotic course?"*
>
> Fill in the bracket from your root cause map. If you don't have a confirmed root cause, that is itself information — and it belongs in this conversation. "My root cause was never identified" is a legitimate opening.
>
> ---
>
> **If a stressor, illness, or systemic antibiotic course appears to be the trigger:**
>
> *"I believe my relapse may be triggered by [a period of high stress / a systemic antibiotic course for another infection / an illness / surgery]. Can we discuss whether a partial rebalancing protocol — prokinetics and eating window tightening — might stabilise the situation before a full antibiotic course is needed?"*
>
> Not every partial return requires a full antibiotic course. For stressor-triggered destabilisation in patients who still have prokinetic support running, some clinicians will trial a tightening period before retreatment. This conversation gives you access to that option.
>

### The Long-Term Testing Schedule

Breath testing in maintenance is not about anxiety. It's about having objective data before a treatment decision is made, rather than after. Here's the schedule worth building into your calendar.

At three months post-treatment, schedule the first repeat breath test. This is your baseline confirmation that the treatment held, and if symptoms have crept back, it's the earliest point at which a test result becomes clinically useful for a retreatment decision.

At six months post-treatment, schedule a second test if any symptoms have returned since the first. If you're entirely symptom-free at three months and remain so, clinical judgment about whether a six-month test is warranted is a reasonable conversation to have with your practitioner — but if you've had two or more prior relapses, the data matters more than the inconvenience.

For patients with documented motility dysfunction — post-infectious MMC damage, autonomic nervous system involvement, and connective tissue conditions such as hEDS and HSD where GI dysmotility is documented (though SIBO prevalence in this population has not been formally established) — annual breath testing is a defensible maintenance standard. This reflects practitioner consensus rather than controlled trial evidence, but the rationale is clear: these are patients whose gut environment is chronically at elevated risk. Monitoring it annually gives early visibility into what symptom pattern alone might miss.

Any time early warning signs persist beyond 48 hours without improvement, a breath test is warranted before the next clinical decision — regardless of where you are in the testing schedule.

One point worth holding firmly: a repeat breath test serves the clinical conversation, not the other way around. You are not requesting a test to prove to a sceptical physician that your symptoms are real. You are requesting a test to ensure that whatever treatment decision follows is based on the correct clinical picture. That distinction matters for how you hold the conversation.

### The Stressor You Can't Eliminate — and the One You Can

Accumulative stressor loading is the relapse trigger that gets the least attention and causes some of the most confusing relapses — the ones where nothing obvious happened, where the diet was consistent, where the prokinetic was still running, and the symptoms still came back.

The evidence here is associative, not causal — chronic sympathetic activation is linked to reduced GI motility, and cortisol elevation has been observed alongside altered gut barrier function. None of that constitutes a proven chain from stress to SIBO. But it's consistent enough to take seriously as a maintenance variable.

What this means practically: a month of sleeping five hours a night, eating irregularly, running on stress hormones, and then catching a respiratory infection requiring a seven-day course of broad-spectrum antibiotics is not just an unpleasant month. It's a cumulative gut event. Each of those elements adds load to a system that was already managing a compromised baseline. The systemic antibiotic course is particularly relevant — broad-spectrum antibiotics disrupt the entire gastrointestinal microbiome, including the commensal bacteria that compete with opportunistic overgrowth. If your small intestinal environment was already at the edge of stable, that course may have tipped it.

This doesn't mean you can't get sick. It means the conversation with your prescriber when you need systemic antibiotics for another infection should include a line about your SIBO history. It also means the period immediately following that course is a time to tighten the eating window, confirm your prokinetic is still running, and watch for early warning signs.

The stressor you can't eliminate is all of it — illness happens, surgery happens, life has periods of sustained intensity. The stressor you can actually influence is the physiological burden load: sleep duration and consistency, meal spacing, and the deliberate reduction of the chronic stressors that are drawing most heavily from your nervous system's reserves. You don't have to live a stress-free life. You have to stay below the threshold where the cumulative load tips the gut environment into a state it can no longer hold.

### What This Means For You

You've been relying on the right prescription from the right doctor at the right moment. That's not a plan. This is.

The maintenance protocol isn't complicated, but it does require that you run it rather than wait to see if you need it. The eating window doesn't negotiate. The prokinetics don't stop because you feel better. The breath test gets scheduled in advance, not booked in a panic when symptoms return. The 48-hour rapid response runs when the early warning signs appear, not when the bloating is severe enough that you're back to where you were before.

Here is what has changed between your first treatment cycle and this one: you know the three triggers. You know your root cause category, or you know it was never identified and that matters. You know the five early warning signals and which ones have historically appeared first in your pattern. You have clinical language for every conversation you might need to have — diagnostic, treatment, and relapse.

The thesis of this book, from the first page, has been that you didn't fail the treatment. The treatment failed to address the root cause. That sentence is only true when the root cause is left unaddressed. With the maintenance protocol running, with the root cause identified and actively managed, with a prokinetic supporting the MMC sweep and an eating window giving it the fasted window it needs — the conditions that produced that sentence no longer exist.

Relapse is not inevitable. It's predictable — and predictable means preventable.

*This information is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice. Consult a qualified healthcare provider before making any changes to your health regimen.*

Chapter 10 is the final piece: the doctor relationship you're now equipped to have. Not the relationship of a patient waiting for guidance, but the relationship of someone who walks in with a case summary, a scripted conversation, and a clear ask. That's a different appointment than the one you've been having.
