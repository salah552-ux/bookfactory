## THE 5 MOST COMMON GUT TRIGGERS

### TRIGGER 1 — The Eating Frequency Trap

**What it is:** Eating or drinking anything caloric every 2–3 hours prevents the Migrating Motor Complex from completing its housekeeping sweep. The MMC only activates in a fasted state — between meals, not after them.

**How to recognise it:** You graze throughout the day. You have "healthy" snacks scheduled. You drink lattes or protein shakes between meals. You feel better when you eat frequently — which is temporary symptom relief, not healing.

**What to do:** Extend your between-meal window to 4–5 hours minimum. Complete fasting between meals (water only). Start with one clean eating window per day and extend from there. The goal is not calorie restriction — it's giving the MMC time to run.

---

### TRIGGER 2 — The Post-Infectious MMC Disruption

**What it is:** A bout of food poisoning — sometimes years ago — may have triggered antibodies against vinculin, a protein that coordinates the nerve cells responsible for MMC contractions. The gut's housekeeping cycle runs too slowly or too weakly to keep bacteria from accumulating.

**How to recognise it:** You can trace your symptoms to a specific event: a stomach bug, food poisoning, a course of strong antibiotics, gastroenteritis while travelling. Your symptoms didn't start gradually — they started after something.

**What to do:** Mention this timeline to your clinician and ask specifically about post-infectious motility dysfunction. Request assessment of whether prokinetic therapy is appropriate. This mechanism doesn't resolve with diet alone — it requires the MMC to be actively supported.

---

### TRIGGER 3 — The Hypothyroid Signal

**What it is:** Thyroid function directly regulates gut motility. An underactive thyroid slows MMC activity throughout the GI tract. Many people have sub-clinical hypothyroidism that doesn't appear on a standard TSH test but is sufficient to slow motility enough to maintain SIBO.

**How to recognise it:** Other hypothyroid signals alongside gut symptoms: fatigue that isn't explained by sleep, cold sensitivity, hair loss, difficulty losing weight despite normal eating. Gut symptoms that worsen in winter or under high stress.

**What to do:** Ask for a full thyroid panel — TSH, Free T3, Free T4 — not just TSH alone. If thyroid function is at the low end of normal range but not technically out of range, discuss this with a practitioner who understands the relationship between thyroid function and gut motility.

---

### TRIGGER 4 — The Reintroduction Rush

**What it is:** Feeling better after treatment is not the same as being better. The intestinal lining has been damaged. The MMC is still in recovery. The gut bacteria balance is still being re-established. Beginning aggressive reintroduction of high-FODMAP foods before this process completes re-introduces the fermentation substrates that fed the bacteria in the first place.

**How to recognise it:** You follow the protocol. Symptoms improve significantly. You think: "I'm better." You eat normally for a week. Symptoms return. You've done this cycle more than once.

**What to do:** Use the reintroduction sequence from Chapter 7 — low-FODMAP first, one food at a time, 72-hour intervals, track responses. Do not declare victory until you have reintroduced three successive foods without any return of symptoms. Victory is not the absence of current symptoms — it's the stability of the system under reintroduction stress.

---

### TRIGGER 5 — The Wrong-Type Treatment

**What it is:** Hydrogen, methane (IMO), and hydrogen sulfide SIBO respond to different treatments in a specific sequence. A hydrogen protocol applied to methane SIBO will fail — or make methane SIBO worse by clearing competing hydrogen organisms. The treatment worked for someone else because they have a different type.

**How to recognise it:** You completed a full course of rifaximin. It didn't work — or it worked partially and then reversed. You may have methane-dominant or hydrogen sulfide SIBO, which requires different agents.

**What to do:** Ask for a three-gas breath test, not just hydrogen and methane. Confirm your SIBO type before beginning treatment. If methane is present, treatment must include both rifaximin AND neomycin (or allicin as an herbal alternative) — one without the other does not eradicate methane-producing organisms. If hydrogen sulfide is suspected (dark eggs smell to gas, sulphur-sensitivity, negative on standard breath test but all symptoms present), this requires separate investigation.
