## CURRENT AMAZON REVIEW GUIDELINES

### What is permitted (as of April 2026)

- Authors may include a review request in back matter, newsletters, or author communications — no explicit prohibition exists against asking readers for an honest review.
- Authors may provide free or discounted copies without requiring a review in exchange.
- Asking readers for their honest experience — positive or negative — is permitted.
- QR codes and direct links to the Amazon review page are used widely by authors in print back matter and are not prohibited by KDP policy. Amazon's content guidelines prohibit QR codes only when they condition access to something else (e.g., warranty registration) on leaving a review. A passive link or QR code to the review page carries no such condition.
- Directing readers to "search for this book" or "find the review link at [author site]" is an alternative approach for print books where URL access is indirect.

### What is prohibited (non-negotiable constraints)

- Requiring a review in exchange for the book or any other item.
- Offering any incentive beyond a free or discounted copy of the book — no gift cards, no bonuses, no contest entries.
- Attempting to influence the content or sentiment of the review in any direction.
- Selective solicitation — contacting only readers who expressed satisfaction and asking them to review, while not asking others.
- Review trading with other authors.
- Reviews from household members or close personal connections.
- Any language implying the review should be positive, or that a five-star review is expected or preferred.
- Any language framing the review as benefiting the author's sales, rankings, or income (this is author-centred framing and erodes the reader-impact argument).

### Moment 1 — Type A (Insight Moment): The MMC Named and Explained as the Missing Variable

**Location:** Introduction, final section ("This is what structural relapse looks like"), approximately 80% through the introduction.

**Current text:**

> *"The migrating motor complex is the gut's housekeeper. Between meals, when your small intestine is empty, it runs a sweeping contraction that moves bacteria and debris from the small intestine into the large intestine, where bacteria actually belong. The cycle repeats roughly every 90 minutes. It's not optional — it's the primary mechanism your body uses to prevent bacterial overgrowth from taking hold in the first place. When the migrating motor complex works normally, bacteria don't get the chance to accumulate. When it doesn't — when it's too slow, too weak, or too frequently interrupted — the small intestine becomes the kind of quiet, undisturbed environment in which bacteria flourish."*

> *"This is what structural relapse looks like. Not bad luck. Not a weakened immune system. A specific mechanism that isn't running, and a treatment protocol that never addressed it."*

**Score: 26/30**

- **Sharpness: 9/10.** "Not bad luck. Not a weakened immune system." — this is the sharpness the book is built on. The binary between vague causes and named mechanisms is done exactly right. "A specific mechanism that isn't running" is as clean as clinical prose gets.
- **Position: 9/10.** Arriving at the end of the introduction after the reader has been primed by relapse statistics, this lands with maximum force. The timing is structurally correct — the reader has been made uncomfortable by the numbers, and the MMC explanation offers the first credible resolution. Well placed.
- **Follow-through: 8/10.** The sentences that follow move immediately into the four-phase framework preview, which is correct sequentially but slightly undercuts the moment — it pivots to logistics before the MMC revelation has fully settled. One additional beat of stillness here would push follow-through to 10/10. That said, it's not a significant weakness and the chapter handoff notes confirm it lands for the reader state the book was aiming for.

**Assessment:** This is the moment most likely to produce a review from readers who annotate or highlight. The MMC explanation names something they have felt for years — the unexplained return — and gives it a mechanism rather than a story. It reframes self-blame into structural failure in two sentences. That's the conversion event.

## PLACEMENT RECOMMENDATION

Insert the CTA sequence **after** the final line of the conclusion:

> "You know what's wrong, you know why it keeps coming back, and for the first time in a long time, that's enough to start."

Use a section break (`---`) before the CTA. **Do not add a heading.** The CTA should read as a continuation of the author's voice, not as a separate section labelled "A note from the author" or "Leave a review" — both of which create a register shift that distances the reader from the emotional state you need them to be in to convert.

The sequence should appear **before** any appendix material, recipe section, or reference list — back matter that follows the CTA is fine, but the CTA should not be buried after recipes. If the final structure is: Conclusion → CTA → Recipes/Appendices, that is correct. If the structure is: Conclusion → Recipes → CTA, that order should be revised.

## SERIES CROSS-PROMOTION NOTE

The series note (Beat 3) should appear **after** the review ask, not before. Placing the series mention before the ask risks converting the reader's attention toward anticipation of the next book rather than the present action of leaving a review. The sequence should be: acknowledge → ask → look forward. That order is used in the optimized sequence above.

The series note is intentionally brief — one sentence on what the next book covers, one sentence on availability. It does not name a title (no title exists yet) and does not make a release date promise. It is directional, not promotional. This is the correct register for a back-matter series mention in Book 1: "it's coming" rather than "pre-order now."
