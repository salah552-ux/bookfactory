## MARKET ANALYSIS SUMMARY

### Title Pattern Map

**SATURATED PATTERNS — avoid leading with these:**
- "Healing SIBO" — Shivan Sarna owns this exact phrase (Penguin Random House, major distribution)
- "SIBO Solution" — Sylvie McCracken owns this (multiple editions, strong review count)
- "[Number]-Day Plan" — Healing SIBO (21 days), The SIBO Relapse Fix (30 days) both use this structure
- "Guide to Eliminating / Overcoming SIBO" — 3+ titles use this framing
- "SIBO + Cookbook / Recipes" — heavily saturated; not our category
- "Gut Health Protocol" — John Herron title; also generic and often associated with low-quality KDP content
- "SIBO Made Simple" — Phoebe Lapine (4.7 stars, 656 reviews) — strong incumbent in "approachable" lane

**OPEN / UNDERUSED PATTERNS:**
- Relapse as the primary frame (not a sub-topic): only one competitor ("The SIBO Relapse Fix" by Allegria Mackenzie Giraffe, small KDP title) names relapse in the title — and it buries the mechanism
- Root cause language in the title: no top-seller leads with "root cause" despite it being the core reader complaint
- Phase/sequence framing: no major title communicates that treatment order matters
- Doctor relationship / patient advocacy angle: completely absent from all competitor titles
- The MMC / motility as title concept: zero competitor titles reference it
- "Why you keep coming back" framing: present in reader reviews everywhere, absent from titles

**CRITICAL INTELLIGENCE:** "The SIBO Relapse Fix" (B0FNB7TPQM) exists and is a direct competitive concern for relapse-focused titles. It uses "30-Day Natural Protocol" and "Without Expensive Treatments" framing. Our manuscript is significantly more evidence-based and mechanistic — differentiation must signal this. Avoid any title that could be mistaken for that book.

### Emotional Trigger Landscape

The primary reader ("Sarah") has:
1. **Failed 2+ treatment rounds** — dominant emotional state is exhaustion-with-a-tinge-of-rage, not hope
2. **Been dismissed by doctors** — medical gaslighting is a live wound; titles that validate this convert
3. **Self-blame** — she believes she did something wrong; titles that externalize blame to the system resonate
4. **Information overwhelm** — has read too much conflicting advice; craves a clear sequence, not more options
5. **Permanence desperation** — doesn't want "better." Wants "never again."

Emotional triggers that convert in titles:
- "Why you keep..." (validates her recurring experience)
- "For Good" (permanence — the thing she most wants)
- "Root Cause" (she's heard this and wants someone to actually address it)
- "What your doctor didn't tell you" (validates dismissal experience without requiring anger)
- "Stop Relapsing" (direct mirror of her stated goal)
- "The treatment failed" (our book's thesis, in reader language)

Emotional triggers to AVOID in titles:
- "Simple" — she's not new to this and knows it isn't
- "Easy" — same reason; will repel experienced patients
- "Natural" — signals low-evidence content to the research-literate reader
- "Heal your gut" — ambient wellness-speak; she's past this
- "Feel better" — vague and not credible to someone who has failed treatments
