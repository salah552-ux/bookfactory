## THE PREMISE

Dr. Mara Voss does not travel through time. She falls through it.

The ability is called **descent**. A descender touches an artifact, fixes concentration on a temporal coordinate burned into the object's emotional memory, and drops like a stone through centuries to land inside the moment of the object's highest charge — the fire, the grief, the exultation, the last breath. She cannot interact. She can only witness. And when she surfaces, she comes up gasping and smelling of somewhere else, her own hippocampus a little more overwritten than it was before.

Enough descents and a person's memories stop feeling like their own. The community calls it **erosion**. At 34, Mara is already showing early signs.

She works for the **Meridian Archive**, a clandestine organization in Geneva that presents itself as an academic cataloguing body and functions as a quasi-governmental intelligence apparatus. Descenders go into the field. They report what they witnessed. The Archive decides what history gets verified.

For seven years, Mara has trusted the man who decides what gets published: Dr. Callum Morrow, Chief Archivist, her closest colleague, her intellectual equal, and — as she is about to discover — the man who has been selling her memories.

## WORLD RULES: THE DESCENT SYSTEM

1. **Trigger** — Touch plus concentration. The artifact must carry emotional weight. Mass-produced objects rarely work; a soldier's wedding ring, a child's clay tablet, a surgeon's forceps all carry extraordinary charge.

2. **Landing** — The descender cannot choose the precise moment; descent gravitates toward the object's highest emotional event. A skilled descender can nudge within a narrow window of hours, but not choose the day.

3. **Observation only** — Descenders cannot speak, cannot touch objects in the past, cannot alter anything. But they are not entirely invisible. Animals sense them. Children sometimes look directly at them. In very charged fields, other people feel a cold displacement in the air.

4. **Duration** — Limited by artifact charge and the descender's stamina. A weak object: minutes. A powerful one: hours. The descender surfaces involuntarily when the charge expires or they lose consciousness.

5. **The cost** — Every descent overwrites a thin layer of hippocampal tissue with sensory imprints. Mara's own childhood is becoming less vivid than the last hours of Pompeii, which she witnessed at 22.

6. **The economy** — The Archive monetizes verbal debrief recordings. Descenders report; archivists transcribe and classify; the Archive publishes findings that support whatever geopolitical function the organization currently serves.

## THE CHARACTERS

**Dr. Mara Voss, 34** — Senior descender and field archaeologist. Raised on excavation sites by a mother who died in a dig collapse in Syria when Mara was nineteen. Discovered her ability at 21 when she touched Roman glassware in a Florence storage facility. Has been descending for twelve years. Voice: sardonic, precise, anatomical about her own grief. Swears only when genuinely surprised. Cries alone in the field, never in front of anyone.

**Dr. Callum Morrow, 41** — Chief Archivist. Was a descender until an erosion event at 35 left him unable to distinguish his own childhood from a fourteenth-century Mongol court. He can no longer descend. He compensates with bureaucratic genius and a simmering resentment repackaged as protectiveness. For two years he has been selling sanitized versions of Mara's debriefs to a private consortium called **Argent Threshold**, which uses descent-verified history to influence land-rights litigation and political narratives. He tells himself he is making the harder choices so she doesn't have to.

**Yusuf Adesanya, 28** — Mara's research assistant, a junior descender 18 months into his ability. First to notice that Mara's published findings don't match her transcripts. Does not yet understand what he has found.

**Director Helene Bast, 58** — She sanctioned Callum's arrangement. Has never descended. Sees descenders as instruments.
