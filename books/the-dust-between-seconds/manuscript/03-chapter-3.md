## CHAPTER THREE: What the Archive Keeps

The chamber smelled the way all of them did after the first seal was broken — old darkness, dry stone, the faint sweetness of decomposed organic matter that had once been cloth or grain or both. Ephesus in August meant the heat pressed down on everything above ground like a hand on the back of your neck, but fifteen feet below the site grid, inside a Roman-era storage room that had been perfectly sealed since the 263 CE earthquake, the air was cool enough that I could feel my own breath.

I had the artifact on the worktable. A merchant's ledger stylus, bronze, with a wax residue on the writing end that two weeks of painstaking excavation had kept intact. It fit in my palm like it had always been there.

"You're sure about the charge?" Yusuf asked from the doorway. He had learned, in eighteen months, not to come closer than three feet when I was prepping. Not because I'd told him to. Because he had watched the way my face changed.

"Strong enough." I turned the stylus over. The wax was the color of old honey. "The earthquake hit during the afternoon trading session. He was mid-column when the first wave came through."

"How do you know it was a he?"

"I don't." I set my other hand flat on the stone table to ground myself. "Note that down. Working assumption: merchant, adult, using the stylus when the building collapsed. I'll know more in twenty minutes."

Yusuf uncapped his pen. Twenty-three months ago, I had been exactly as earnest as he was, and I found it both endearing and faintly terrifying to watch. "And if the charge runs out before —"

"It won't. Ready?"

He nodded and stepped back another foot.

I closed my fingers around the stylus and went down.

***

The descent is not like falling asleep. Sleep pulls you under slowly, and there is something merciful in the gradual darkening. Descent is a door slamming shut behind you. One moment I was in the cool chamber with the smell of old stone; the next I was standing in blinding August light inside a colonnaded storeroom, and a man three feet away from me was bent over a wooden writing tablet, scratching figures I couldn't yet read.

He was heavyset, maybe fifty, his tunic damp through the back. He did not look up. No one ever looked up, except children and animals and the very occasionally perceptive, and none of those were in the room.

The room was stacked with amphorae — olive oil, I guessed from the residue patterns on the ones that had been opened and re-sealed. Some had been knocked over already, which meant I had landed inside the window, maybe forty minutes before the main collapse. The first tremors had started.

I did what I always do: I stood very still and watched.

His name — I would learn it from a partial inscription on a lead weight recovered in the same chamber — was Atticus. Not a freeborn name in origin, but he moved like a man who had spent forty years forgetting that. He had calloused thumbs and a ring on his right hand that he kept touching with his left, the way you touch something to remind yourself you still have it.

I watched him work. The scratching of the stylus, the dip into the wax, the murmured count under his breath. Eleven amphorae of high-grade Rhodian oil. Transfer complete, awaiting courier, balance in credit.

The second tremor hit.

It was small — a shiver through the flagstones that made the amphorae hum in their shelves. Atticus stopped writing. He looked up at the ceiling the way everyone in seismic country learned to look: assessing, not afraid. Not yet.

He put his hand on the edge of the table. His ring caught the light from the door.

Then he picked up the stylus again and went back to his column.

That was the part that always got me. The way people returned to their work between the tremors, because the work was what made the next hour make sense. Because you couldn't stand still and wait for the ground to decide what it was going to do. You could only keep writing the figures down.

I stayed with him for the next twenty-two minutes. I watched the third tremor take out the upper shelf of amphorae. I watched him throw himself under the table — not panicking, moving with the efficient calm of a man who had drilled for this — and I watched the ceiling hold, barely, while the world outside made a sound like a city being crumpled in a fist.

He survived. I knew from the stratigraphy that the chamber hadn't fully collapsed until a secondary event six hours later. He would have walked out. He would have walked out into a street full of rubble and found his courier and finished the transaction, probably, because he was exactly the kind of man who finished transactions.

The charge broke when the main tremor subsided. I felt it go — a thinning, like the room becoming translucent — and then the door slammed shut from the other side, and I was back.

***

I came up hard, the way I always did now. My hand was flat on the worktable, the stylus rolling away from fingers that had gripped too tight, and Yusuf was already crouching in front of me, which meant I had gone to my knees at some point and hadn't noticed.

"Dr. Voss." His voice was careful. He had learned that tone fast, which was either a natural gift or evidence that he'd grown up around someone who needed it. I'd never asked. "How long do you need?"

"Don't —" My own voice came back to me thick, accented with something that wasn't Turkish or English. After a long descent, I spoke in layers. Callum had thought it was extraordinary the first time he heard it; now it made him politely look away. "Don't touch anything. I'm oriented."

"You're on the floor."

"I know where the floor is. I put myself there." I breathed through my nose and made myself catalog the room: concrete support, generator cable, portable light array, Yusuf's open laptop on the side table, my kit bag, the stylus. Present. Present. Present. "I'm going to need five minutes and then we'll debrief."

He brought me water and retreated to his corner with his notebook, which was exactly correct. I sat on the floor of the sealed storage chamber of a dead man's warehouse and let the twenty-second century CE reestablish its claim on me, and I thought about Atticus going back to his figures between the tremors. The work that made the next hour make sense.

After four minutes I stood up and went to the side table to look at my notes from the pre-descent assessment.

That was when I saw Yusuf's laptop.

He hadn't closed it. The screen was still active — he'd been using it to draft his field report, and the document was open, and it wasn't his field report.

It was a debrief transcript. My debrief transcript. The one I had recorded three days ago in Geneva before flying out to Ephesus, covering the previous descent: a Bronze Age site in Cyprus, a royal treasury, a scribe copying administrative records the day before something significant and violent swept through the palace compound.

The transcript had my name at the top and Callum's access signature at the bottom. Meridian Archive, internal classification, Level 3.

I read it.

I am very good at reading quickly and retaining what I read. It is the most useful thing twelve years of descent have done to my memory — even as the descents erode my own history, they compound my ability to hold other people's words exactly. I read the transcript once and I knew what it said.

Then I read it again, because the second time I was reading for what wasn't there.

Three findings. Gone.

The first: my account of the scribe's administrative notations, which had included what I had clearly described as a reference to tribute payments from a coastal settlement that would significantly complicate the current academic consensus on Bronze Age Aegean trade networks. I had spent eleven minutes in the debrief specifically on this point. I remembered the sensation of speaking into the recorder, the particular effort of translating the visual memory of Linear B notations into spoken English while the notation-shapes were still bright in my mind.

In the transcript, that section of the debrief ended two sentences early. Then it moved to my description of the physical layout of the treasury.

The second: my observation that one of the scribes had been a woman — identifiable from her dress, from the particular social positioning of the other figures in the room. Relevant because it challenged several publications, including two that Callum had personally championed.

Gone.

The third — and this one I had to sit with for a moment, the way you sit with something that could be an honest mistake right up until it isn't — was my description of what I had seen in the doorway of the treasury chamber. A man. He was not a palace official and not a servant. He was watching the scribes and counting something on his fingers, and he was dressed in a way that suggested he was from somewhere the palace did not usually have visitors. I had flagged it in the debrief as an anomaly. I had said: I don't know yet what this means but I want it on record.

It was not on record.

I stood at the side table in the underground chamber in Ephesus and I looked at Yusuf's laptop screen and I thought: this is a redacted document. Someone has edited this before filing it.

Callum has access to every debrief before it is archived.

Callum has access to every debrief, and this transcript has been filed under his signature, and it is missing things I specifically said.

I breathed.

I thought about Atticus under his table between the tremors, waiting to find out what the ground was going to do. The way you couldn't stand still. The way you had to do something with your hands.

I sat down in Yusuf's chair. I opened the Archive's remote server portal. I logged in with my own credentials and navigated to the debrief archive, and I pulled my own file, and it matched the transcript on the screen: the same redactions, the same clean seams where my words had been removed and the remaining text adjusted so the cuts didn't look like cuts.

You had to know what was missing to see it. That was the craft of it.

I navigated up. I was looking for something specific now — not what had been taken, but where it had gone. Callum was meticulous. Everything he did had a destination.

It took me eleven minutes to find the folder. It was nested inside a project subdirectory that I didn't have access to — I could see it existed, but I couldn't open it. The label was three words long, two of which were alphanumeric project codes.

The third word was ARGENT.

I looked at that word for a long time.

Yusuf said, from his corner, "Should I start the verbal when you're ready?"

"Not yet." My voice was level. I was surprised by how level it was. "Give me a few more minutes."

"Of course." A pause. "Is everything —"

"Fine," I said. "Everything's fine."

I closed the server portal. I picked up my field notebook and wrote the word ARGENT at the top of a clean page and underlined it once. Below it I wrote the three findings that had been excised, in full, from memory, as precisely as I could reconstruct them. Below that I wrote the date and time and the name of the artifact.

Then I closed the notebook and I went and sat in the corner with a cup of tea that had gone completely cold while I was under, and I held it in both hands, and I looked at the opposite wall, which was good Roman concrete and had held up for eighteen hundred years.

I thought about the ring on Atticus's finger. The way he'd touched it between the tremors, reassuring himself that what he valued was still there.

Seven years, I thought. Seven years of debriefs. All those hours in the recording room with the good lamp and the bad coffee, translating centuries of other people's lives into language that Callum would receive and classify and — I had always assumed — protect. That was the word I would have used, last week. I would have said Callum was the person who protected the work.

I would have said that without hesitating.

Outside, I could hear the site crew packing up for the night. Equipment cases, someone's music from a phone, the hydraulic exhale of a truck. A perfectly ordinary late August evening in western Turkey, the sun going orange across the ruins above us, and in a chamber beneath the dig I was reconstructing, with the same methodical care I gave to every site, exactly how much of the last seven years I needed to go back and reread.

"All right," I said to Yusuf. He looked up. He had the decency not to show relief. "Let's debrief. And Yusuf —" I paused. "Can you close your laptop?"

He looked at it. He looked at me.

I watched him understand, in the way that sharp young people understand things, that he had left something open that he shouldn't have, and that I had seen it, and that neither of us was going to address this directly.

"Of course," he said. He closed it.

"Start the recorder," I said. "I'll begin with the physical environment."

And I did. I described the storeroom precisely. I described the amphorae and the light and the position of the body at the table. I described Atticus with his stylus and his ring and his careful return to the figures between the tremors. I gave Yusuf everything, held nothing back, delivered my account with the same thoroughness I had given every debrief for twelve years.

But I did not describe the folder. I did not say the word ARGENT. I kept my voice even and my hands in my lap and I let the recorder run, and I thought: whatever you take from this one, you were going to take anyway. But I know it's missing now. I know exactly what you'll cut.

That is the difference between seven years ago and tonight.

The recorder ran. Outside, the light went from orange to dark. And I sat in a sealed Roman chamber that had survived an earthquake, and I started, quietly and without anyone watching, to plan.
