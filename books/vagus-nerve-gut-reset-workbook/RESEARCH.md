## 1. Vagus nerve anatomy and function (established physiology)

- **The vagus nerve is the tenth cranial nerve (cranial nerve X) and the longest of the cranial nerves.** It runs from the brainstem (medulla oblongata) through the neck and thorax into the abdomen, innervating the heart, lungs, and most of the digestive tract down to roughly the splenic flexure of the colon. *(Source: standard neuroanatomy — Gray's Anatomy; Berthoud HR & Neuhuber WL, "Functional and chemical anatomy of the afferent vagal system," Autonomic Neuroscience, 2000.)*
- **The vagus is the principal nerve of the parasympathetic ("rest-and-digest") division of the autonomic nervous system.** It is a mixed nerve. *(Source: Berthoud & Neuhuber, 2000.)*
- **Roughly 80% of vagal nerve fibres are afferent (sensory) — carrying signals FROM the body TO the brain — and about 20% are efferent (motor), carrying signals from brain to organs.** This is why the gut "talks to" the brain more than the brain "talks to" the gut. *(Source: Agostoni E et al., "Functional and histological studies of the vagus nerve and its branches," J Physiol, 1957; widely replicated — see Berthoud & Neuhuber, 2000.)*
- **Vagal tone** = the level of activity of the vagus nerve, commonly indexed in research by heart rate variability (HRV), specifically the high-frequency / respiratory sinus arrhythmia component. Higher vagal tone is associated with better autonomic regulation. *(Source: Laborde S, Mosley E, Thayer JF, "Heart Rate Variability and Cardiac Vagal Tone in Psychophysiological Research," Frontiers in Psychology, 2017.)*

## 2. Gut-brain axis physiology

- **The gut-brain axis is the bidirectional communication network between the central nervous system and the enteric nervous system of the gut**, integrating neural (vagus), endocrine, and immune signalling. *(Source: Mayer EA, "Gut feelings: the emerging biology of gut–brain communication," Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 2011; Cryan JF et al., "The Microbiota-Gut-Brain Axis," Physiological Reviews, 2019.)*
- **The enteric nervous system contains an estimated 200–600 million neurons** — sometimes called the "second brain." *(Source: Furness JB, "The enteric nervous system and neurogastroenterology," Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology, 2012.)*
- **The vagus nerve is the primary neural conduit of the gut-brain axis.** Vagal afferents sense gut distension, nutrients, and microbial metabolites and relay this to the brain; vagal efferents modulate gut motility, secretion, and the gut's immune tone. *(Source: Bonaz B, Bazin T, Pellissier S, "The Vagus Nerve at the Interface of the Microbiota-Gut-Brain Axis," Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2018.)*
- **The cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway:** vagal efferent activity (via acetylcholine) suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokine release (e.g. TNF-α) from macrophages. This is a core mechanism linking vagal tone to lower systemic and gut inflammation. *(Source: Tracey KJ, "The inflammatory reflex," Nature, 2002; Borovikova LV et al., Nature, 2000.)*
- **Gut microbes communicate with the brain partly via the vagus.** In animal models, certain effects of gut bacteria (e.g. Lactobacillus rhamnosus on stress behaviour) are abolished when the vagus nerve is cut (vagotomy), demonstrating the vagus as a necessary channel. *(Source: Bravo JA et al., PNAS, 2011 — animal model; flagged as preclinical.)*

## 3. Vagus nerve and gut conditions (IBS, bloating, motility)

- **IBS is recognised as a disorder of gut-brain interaction (DGBI)** — the formal term adopted by the Rome IV criteria, replacing "functional GI disorder." Altered gut-brain signalling is central to its definition. *(Source: Drossman DA, "Functional Gastrointestinal Disorders: History, Pathophysiology, Clinical Features, and Rome IV," Gastroenterology, 2016.)*
- **Autonomic imbalance (reduced vagal/parasympathetic tone, relative sympathetic dominance) is observed in subsets of IBS patients** and correlates with symptom severity in several studies. The relationship is associative, not proven causal. *(Source: Mazurak N et al., "Heart rate variability in the irritable bowel syndrome: a review," Neurogastroenterology & Motility, 2012 — flagged: heterogeneous evidence.)*
- **The vagus modulates gastric accommodation and the migrating motor complex (MMC)** — the "housekeeping" wave that sweeps the small intestine between meals. Impaired vagal/parasympathetic input is associated with delayed gastric emptying and disordered motility, which can present as bloating and fullness. *(Source: Deloose E et al., "The migrating motor complex," Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology, 2012; Travagli RA & Anselmi L, "Vagal neurocircuitry and its influence on gastric motility," Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology, 2016.)*
- **Stress (sympathetic activation) slows gastric emptying and alters colonic motility and visceral sensitivity** — the physiological basis for why anxiety worsens bloating and IBS symptoms. *(Source: Mayer EA, "The neurobiology of stress and gastrointestinal disease," Gut, 2000.)*
- **Link to H. pylori / inflammation (series tie-in):** chronic gut inflammation activates vagal afferents (visceral hypersensitivity); conversely, vagal anti-inflammatory signalling can dampen mucosal inflammation. This connects nervous-system regulation to recovery from inflammatory gut conditions. *(Source: Bonaz B et al., 2018; Tracey KJ, 2002.)*

## 4. Evidence-based vagal-toning exercises

### Slow breathing / physiological sigh
- **Slow breathing at ~5–6 breaths per minute (resonance/coherence breathing) maximises HRV and increases cardiac vagal tone** by aligning breathing with the baroreflex rhythm. *(Source: Lehrer PM & Gevirtz R, "Heart rate variability biofeedback: how and why does it work?" Frontiers in Psychology, 2014; Russo MA et al., "The physiological effects of slow breathing in the healthy human," Breathe, 2017.)*
- **Extended exhalation (exhale longer than inhale) shifts autonomic balance toward parasympathetic dominance.** Vagal outflow to the heart increases during exhalation (respiratory sinus arrhythmia). *(Source: Russo et al., 2017; Laborde et al., 2017.)*
- **The "physiological sigh" (double inhale through the nose followed by a long exhale) reduced anxiety and improved mood more than mindfulness meditation in a randomised controlled trial.** *(Source: Balban MY et al., "Brief structured respiration practices enhance mood and reduce physiological arousal," Cell Reports Medicine, 2023 — RCT, healthy adults.)*

### Humming, chanting, gargling (laryngeal/pharyngeal vagal branches)
- **Humming and chanting ("OM"/"Mmm") stimulate vibration of the larynx and pharynx, areas innervated by vagal branches (the recurrent laryngeal and pharyngeal nerves), and are associated with deactivation of the limbic/amygdala signal on fMRI.** *(Source: Kalyani BG et al., "Neurohemodynamic correlates of 'OM' chanting: A pilot functional magnetic resonance imaging study," International Journal of Yoga, 2011 — small pilot, flagged preliminary.)*
- **Humming markedly increases nasal nitric oxide output**, a measurable physiological effect of the practice. *(Source: Weitzberg E & Lundberg JON, "Humming greatly increases nasal nitric oxide," American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine, 2002.)*
- **Gargling and the gag reflex engage the pharyngeal muscles innervated by the vagus** and are used clinically as informal vagal-activation cues; direct outcome evidence is limited and mechanistic rather than trial-proven. *(Source: mechanism per Berthoud & Neuhuber, 2000 — flagged: mechanistic, not RCT-backed.)*

### Cold exposure
- **Cold exposure to the face/neck (e.g. cold water on the face) triggers the diving reflex, which raises parasympathetic (vagal) activity and lowers heart rate.** *(Source: review — Jungmann M et al., "Effects of Cold Stimulation on Cardiac-Vagal Activation in Healthy Participants," JMIR Formative Research, 2018.)*
- **Whole-body cold exposure produces an acute sympathetic surge followed by a parasympathetic rebound; HRV responses vary by protocol and individual.** Evidence is mixed; cold should be introduced gradually and avoided by those with cardiovascular conditions without medical clearance. *(Source: Jungmann et al., 2018 — flagged: mixed evidence; safety caveat required.)*

### Movement, posture, social engagement
- **Yoga, slow stretching, and certain neck/diaphragm-engaging movements are associated with increased HRV/vagal tone** in several trials, though effect sizes vary. *(Source: Tyagi A & Cohen M, "Yoga and heart rate variability: A comprehensive review," International Journal of Yoga, 2016 — review, heterogeneous.)*
- **Positive social connection and "safety" cues (warm face-to-face interaction, gentle vocal prosody) are associated with parasympathetic/vagal engagement** — the social-engagement dimension of autonomic regulation. *(Source: Porges SW, "The polyvagal perspective," Biological Psychology, 2007 — theoretical framework; presented in the book as plain-English mechanism, not as settled fact, per blueprint "no jargon" rule.)*
- **Singing, loud expressive vocalisation, and laughter involve controlled exhalation and laryngeal engagement, recruiting the same vagal pathways as humming.** *(Source: Vickhoff B et al., "Music structure determines heart rate variability of singers," Frontiers in Psychology, 2013.)*

## 5. Tracking and measurement (workbook tools)

- **HRV is the most validated non-invasive proxy for cardiac vagal tone** and can be tracked with consumer wearables, though absolute values vary by device and should be read as personal trends, not diagnostic numbers. *(Source: Laborde et al., 2017 — and explicit caveat that consumer HRV is trend-only.)*
- **Resting heart rate, subjective symptom scales, and bowel-symptom diaries are standard, validated self-tracking tools in DGBI management.** *(Source: Rome Foundation / Drossman, 2016.)*

## SAFETY & SCOPE CAVEATS (must appear in front matter and where relevant)
- This is education, not medical treatment. IBS and persistent gut symptoms require a clinician's diagnosis to exclude other conditions.
- Cold exposure carries cardiovascular risk; anyone with heart disease, high blood pressure, or who is pregnant must get medical clearance first.
- Vagal exercises are adjuncts, not replacements, for prescribed treatment (e.g. H. pylori antibiotics, IBD medication).
- Several mechanisms (microbiome-vagus signalling, polyvagal social engagement) rest on preclinical or theoretical evidence and are presented as such.
