## REWRITES APPLIED

### Ch 2 (02-chapter-01.md... → file 03-chapter-02.md) — Opening
**Score:** 34/50 | **Primary weakness:** Specificity (6) and Reader recognition (6)

**Diagnosis:** The original opened on the *book's* cleverness ("something almost no other book bothers to do") rather than on the reader. It was a meta-claim about the program's quality, not the reader's experience — abstract, author-facing, and it made the reader wait for the payoff line "You are going to measure where you start."

**Approach:** Reader-recognition mirror + promise tension. Pull the chapter's own later insight (memory is unreliable; doubt ends programs that were working) up to the front so the reader sees their exact future self before being told what to do. Keep the strong existing second line ("You are going to measure where you start.") intact as the landing.

**Original:**
> Here is something almost no other book in this corner of the shelf bothers to do, and it's the difference between a program that works and a program that just feels nice for a week.

**Rewrite (applied):**
> Four weeks from now, on a good morning, you'll try to remember how rough today actually felt — and you won't be able to. You'll wonder if anything really changed or if you just caught a lucky week. That doubt is exactly what ends programs that were quietly working. So before you do a single exercise, you're going to do the one thing almost no other book on this shelf bothers with.

**Why this works better:** It opens inside the reader's own future head ("you'll try to remember… and you won't be able to") instead of on a claim about the book, which raises both Specificity and Reader-recognition sharply. It also creates a tension the next line resolves ("the one thing almost no other book bothers with" → "You are going to measure where you start"). No new facts: the four-week frame and the memory/doubt argument are both already in the chapter body.

### Ch 3 (04-chapter-03.md) — Opening
**Score:** 32/50 | **Primary weakness:** Immediacy (6) and Promise tension (6)

**Diagnosis:** The original began with soft administrative framing ("This is the chapter you'll come back to… Think of it as the reference shelf") — true and useful, but it warms up rather than starting in the middle of something. There is no concrete image and no tension in the first beat; it reads like a librarian's note.

**Approach:** Precision-fact open (the locked number of exercises) + immediate scope. Lead with the concrete count, which reframes the whole chapter as a finite, masterable kit, then keep the genuinely useful "you'll come back to this / the program picks for you" function.

**Original:**
> This is the chapter you'll come back to. Over the next four weeks, every daily page will send you here for the full instructions on whatever exercise you're doing that day. Think of it as the reference shelf. You don't need to read it all at once or master everything today — skim it, get the shape of it, and return as needed.

**Rewrite (applied):**
> Twelve exercises. That's the entire toolkit — every calming lever you have on the vagus nerve, each one named, timed, and explained on a single page. This is the chapter every daily page will send you back to for the full instructions, so you don't need to master it today. Skim it, get the shape of it, and return as needed; the four-week program will tell you exactly which tool to pick up and when.

**Why this works better:** "Twelve exercises." is a hard, concrete open with immediacy — it lands the chapter's whole promise in two words and frames the toolkit as small and finite (reassuring to an overwhelmed reader), then preserves the original's reference-shelf function. Number is not new: twelve named exercises is locked in FACTS.md and stated later in the same chapter ("Twelve tools, four families").
