# How to Read This Book

## A Two-Minute Orientation Before You Begin

You can read this book straight through, front to back, and it will walk you from your first nervous, bloated day to a calmer gut twenty-eight days later. That is how it is built — as a path, one short day at a time.

But you might not be in a reading mood. You might be bloated right now, or mid-flare, or simply tired of health books that bury one good idea under forty pages of theory. So here is the shortcut.

If your main problem is your gut — bloating, cramping, the unpredictable IBS kind of misery — you can skim Week 1 and Week 2 and go straight to **Week 3**. That week is built for you. It is the reason this book exists.

If you mostly can't switch off at night, **Week 2** is yours.

If stress is the thing that sets everything off, **Week 4** is where you'll live.

But here is my honest advice: do the weeks in order if you can. Each one teaches a skill the next one uses. By the time you reach the gut week, the breathing will already feel like yours.

A word on what this book will and won't do. It will explain the mechanism behind every exercise, briefly, because you deserve to know *why* you're humming at your kitchen sink, not just be told to. It will cite real, published science — you'll find every source listed at the back. It will never sell you a supplement, a tea, a device, or a course. Not once.

Here's the rhythm you'll settle into. Each of the four weeks has a theme — foundations, sleep, the gut itself, then making it stick. Inside each week are seven short daily entries. Each one takes about five minutes to *do*, and that's the whole daily commitment. You don't read ahead, you don't prepare, you don't need anything you don't already own. You turn to today's entry, do what it says, and get on with your life. Tomorrow you read the next one.

You'll notice that each day also shows you what the practice looked like for a reader named Maya — IBS, bloating, the usual stress, the usual scepticism — as she worked through this exact program. That's deliberate. Reading how a real exercise lands for someone like you is far more useful than a blank box telling you to record a feeling you haven't had yet. Watch what Maya notices, then notice the same kind of thing in yourself. The noticing is the point — and it happens in your head, not on the page.

If you're the sort of person who likes numbers on paper — a baseline you can re-score, a weekly grid, a before-and-after you can hold — there's a free printable companion for exactly that, linked in the back of this book. It lives off these pages on purpose, so this stays a book you read and do, not one you write in. Plenty of readers never bother with it and do beautifully. It's there if you want it.

What this book won't do is diagnose you. Persistent gut symptoms need a doctor's eyes to rule out anything serious. This book is what you do *alongside* good medical care, not instead of it. Think of it as five minutes a day that works on the one part of your gut most treatments ignore: the nerve that runs it.

You need five minutes and a willingness to notice what your body has been telling you for years. Let's begin.
