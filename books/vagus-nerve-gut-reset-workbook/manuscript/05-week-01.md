# Week 1

## Foundations — Teaching Your Body the Brake

This week is about one thing: putting the brake back in your hands. You'll learn the breathing that does most of the heavy lifting, then add a little sound and a little cold. Keep it small. Five minutes, once a day, every day this week. Don't chase a feeling — just do the reps. The shifts come quietly, often by Day 5 or 6, and usually in your sleep and your gut before you notice them in your mood.

There's a reason we start with breath rather than anything flashier. Of all the levers you have on the vagus nerve, breathing is the only one you can pull deliberately, any time, for free, with no equipment and no one noticing. It is also the best-studied. Slow breathing has been measured again and again to raise the markers scientists use for vagal tone, and the effect shows up within minutes. Everything else this month — the humming, the cold, the movement — is built on top of the breathing you learn this week.

A word on expectations before Day 1. The first few days can feel like nothing is happening. That's normal, and it's not a sign the program isn't working. You're not trying to force a dramatic state change; you're laying down a new pattern, the way you'd lay down the first few sessions at a gym. The muscle doesn't grow on day two. It grows because day two happened. Treat this week as deposits in an account you'll draw on later.

Pick a time you can repeat — most people do best straight after waking or just before bed. Anchor it to something you already do, like the kettle going on, so you don't have to rely on willpower to remember. Then take a quiet moment to notice how you feel before and after. The noticing isn't busywork; it's part of how your nervous system relearns what calm feels like. Most of us lost the ability to register our own internal state somewhere along the way. This week starts giving it back.

To make that noticing concrete, you'll follow one reader — we'll call her Maya — through her first week. Maya has had IBS and bloating for years, has tried the diets and the probiotics, and is too tired for a lecture. She's not a case study or a statistic; she's an example, the way a worked example in a maths book shows you the method. Watch what she notices each day, then look for the same thing in yourself.

### Day 1 — Meet the sigh
**Today's exercise:** The Physiological Sigh (Library, no. 1)   **Time:** 5 minutes

We start with the fastest tool in the box. The double-inhale-long-exhale pattern is the quickest way to drop yourself out of stress, and it's the one you'll reach for all month when things spike. The reason it works so well is simple physiology: that long, slow exhale is the exact moment your vagus nerve applies the brakes to your heart, and the second little sip of air at the top fully inflates the small air sacs in your lungs, which makes the whole system more efficient. You don't need to understand any of that to feel it. Today, just get familiar with the shape of the breath.

Don't be surprised if a few rounds make you a little emotional or a little yawny. Both are common and both are good signs — they mean your body is shifting gear.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Sit somewhere quiet. Do the Physiological Sigh slowly for five minutes — roughly five or six rounds per minute.
2. Notice the long exhale especially. That's where the calm lives.

On Day 1, Maya sat down on the edge of her bed before her morning coffee and ran the physiological sigh for five minutes. By the third round her shoulders dropped without her telling them to. Her gut, which usually sat at a hard 8 first thing, felt closer to a 6 that morning, and her stress had eased from an 8 to maybe a 6 as well. The thing she noticed was the yawn that arrived out of nowhere around minute three. Her win for the day was small and honest: "did it before coffee, for once."

See if your own shoulders drop before your gut does — that's often the first sign the brake is working.

### Day 2 — Slow it right down
**Today's exercise:** Resonance Breathing (Library, no. 2)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Yesterday was the quick reset. Today is the deep training: breathing at about six breaths a minute, the pace researchers have most consistently found to lift the markers used for vagal tone. There's a tidy reason this exact speed works. Your blood pressure naturally rises and falls in a slow rhythm, and when your breathing lines up with that rhythm — roughly one breath every ten seconds — the two reinforce each other and your nervous system's calming reflex gets amplified. Scientists call this your resonance frequency. You can just call it the sweet spot.

It will feel slow. That slowness is the medicine, not a problem to fix. If a five-count in and a five-count out feels like a stretch at first, start with four and build up over the week. A hand resting on your belly helps you keep the breath low and gentle instead of high and gulping.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Hand on belly. Breathe in for about 5, out for about 5.
2. Keep it gentle for the full five minutes. If your mind wanders, just return to the count.

Day 2 was harder for Maya, and not because of the breathing. A bad night's sleep and an early meeting meant she nearly skipped it, and the slow pace felt almost irritating at first — five counts in, five counts out, with her mind running through her to-do list the whole time. She kept a hand on her belly and kept coming back to the count anyway. Her gut still hovered around a 7 that day, no better than the day before; her stress, oddly, dropped to a 5. The thing she noticed was that the count gave her racing mind something to hold onto. Her win: "kept going even though it felt slow."

If the slowness annoys you, notice the annoyance and breathe through it — the irritation usually fades before the five minutes are up.

### Day 3 — Make the exhale long
**Today's exercise:** Extended Exhale, 4-in / 8-out (Library, no. 3)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Now you'll lean into the half of the breath that does the calming. Here's the key fact: your heart speeds up slightly when you breathe in and slows down when you breathe out, and that slowing on the exhale is your vagus nerve at work. Make the out-breath longer than the in-breath and you're deliberately spending more time in the calming half of the cycle. It's one of the simplest pieces of nervous-system engineering you'll ever use.

Try it tonight if you can, because it doubles as a sleep aid — a longer exhale tells a wired body it's safe to stand down. If eight counts feels too long and you find yourself gasping at the end, that's a sign to shorten it; use four-in and six-out and stretch the exhale gradually as the week goes on. There's no prize for the longest breath, only for the calmest one.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Breathe in for 4, out for 8 (or 4 and 6 if 8 is a stretch).
2. Five minutes. Soft and unforced.

By Day 3 Maya tried the long exhale in bed rather than at her desk, and the difference surprised her. Four counts in, a long eight counts out — and somewhere around the fourth round she felt her stomach, which had been tight all evening, actually loosen. She didn't make it to eight counts at first and shortened it to six without guilt. Her gut came down to about a 6 that night and her stress to a 4. What she noticed was that the out-breath was doing the work; the in-breath barely mattered. Her win: "felt my stomach let go."

Notice which half of the breath you feel most — for most people, the long exhale is where the gut finally unclenches.

### Day 4 — Add a little sound
**Today's exercise:** The Hum (Library, no. 4)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Time for a different lever. The vagus nerve sends branches through your throat and voice box on its way down to your gut, which means you can engage it from the top as well as through the breath. Humming vibrates exactly that region. It feels almost too simple to matter — until you try a few minutes and notice your chest and throat loosen in a way breathing alone doesn't quite reach. There's a measurable side effect too: humming dramatically raises the nitric oxide in your nasal passages, a real, documented physiological change, and nitric oxide is thought to help keep the airways open.

Privacy helps, because you'll want to hum at a volume that genuinely buzzes rather than a polite murmur. The car, the shower, or a room with the door shut are all good spots. Aim to feel the vibration, not to sound musical — there is no wrong note here.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Two minutes of Resonance Breathing to settle.
2. Three minutes of steady humming, feeling the buzz in your throat and chest.

Maya did Day 4 in the car, in the supermarket car park, because it was the only place she felt private enough to actually buzz rather than murmur. She felt faintly ridiculous for the first thirty seconds. Then the vibration settled into her chest and throat in a way the breathing alone never had, and the ridiculousness stopped mattering. Her gut sat at about a 6, her stress at a 4. The thing she noticed was that humming felt active in a way the quiet breaths didn't — easier to stay with when she was wired. Her win: "found a way to do it in a busy day."

Find your private spot and let the hum actually buzz — feel the vibration rather than chasing a tune.

### Day 5 — A splash of cold
**Today's exercise:** Cold Face Splash (Library, no. 7)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Today you meet the cold lever — gentle, brief, and surprisingly strong. When cold water hits your face, especially around the eyes and forehead, your body triggers an ancient automatic response sometimes called the diving reflex. Your heart rate drops and your calming system switches on, all without any effort from you. It's the same reflex that lets seals and divers slow their hearts underwater, and you have it too. A splash of cold tap water is enough to set it off.

*One important note: skip the cold and just repeat Day 2 if you have any heart condition or high blood pressure, or check with your doctor first.* The cold is optional, not essential — the breathing alone will carry the whole program if you'd rather leave the cold out entirely. For everyone else, keep it brief; you're aiming for a quick, refreshing reset, not an endurance test.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Cold Face Splash: 30–60 seconds of cold water on the face, or a cold flannel pressed firmly to it.
2. Then four minutes of Resonance Breathing to feel the settle.

Maya was nervous about the cold, so she started with a cold flannel pressed to her face rather than a full splash, just for thirty seconds. The jolt was smaller than she'd feared, and the after-effect was bigger — a clear, awake calm, like someone had turned the volume of her thoughts down a notch. She has no heart condition, so the cold was fine for her, but she made a mental note to keep it brief either way. Her gut was a 6 again that day; her stress dropped to a 3, the lowest yet. What she noticed: the calm that came *after* the cold, not during. Her win: "tried the cold and it wasn't scary."

If the cold isn't for you, there's no loss in repeating yesterday's breathing — the program stands on the breath alone.

### Day 6 — Release the neck
**Today's exercise:** Neck & Jaw Release (Library, no. 9)   **Time:** 5 minutes

The vagus nerve runs up the side of your neck, sharing that crowded space with the muscles most of us clench all day without realising. When those muscles are locked tight — shoulders up by the ears, jaw set, head jutting toward a screen — they don't help the nerve, but a relaxed, mobile neck gives it room to do its job and the slow movements themselves signal safety to your body. Today you make that room deliberately.

Keep every movement slow and small. This is not a stretch class and you are not trying to crack anything or reach a new range. You're inviting tension to leave, which happens far better with gentleness than with force. Pay special attention to the jaw — it holds more stress than almost anywhere else, and most people don't notice they're clenching until they deliberately let go.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Two minutes of slow neck turns and ear-to-shoulder tilts.
2. One minute unclenching the jaw — tongue soft, teeth apart.
3. Two minutes of any breathing exercise from this week you liked best.

Day 6 caught Maya by surprise. She hadn't realised how high her shoulders had been living until she did the slow neck turns and felt them drop a full inch. When she let her jaw go slack — tongue soft, teeth apart — she actually felt a pop of tension release behind her ears. Her gut crept back up to a 7 that day, a reminder that the line zig-zags, but her stress held at a 4. The thing she noticed was how much she'd been clenching her jaw without knowing. Her win: "didn't know my jaw was that tight."

Let your teeth come apart right now, as you read this — most people are clenching and have no idea.

### Day 7 — Put it together
**Today's exercise:** Your first mini-stack   **Time:** 5 minutes

You've now met breath, sound, cold, and movement — the four families of tools you'll use all month. Today you string a few together into one smooth routine. This is a preview of the personal stack you'll design in Week 4, and it teaches your body that these tools flow naturally into each other: the cold wakes the reflex, the breath deepens the calm, the hum carries it home.

Then you'll score your first week. Don't judge the numbers harshly — Week 1 is about building the habit, not chasing big drops. If you showed up most days, you've already done the hardest part. Consistency in the first week is the single best predictor of whether the whole program works for you.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. One minute Cold Face Splash *(or Cool-Neck Finish, or skip the cold).*
2. Two minutes Resonance Breathing.
3. Two minutes The Hum.

On Day 7 Maya strung it all together for the first time — a quick cold splash, then resonance breathing, then humming — and was struck by how naturally one flowed into the next. The cold woke her up, the breathing settled her, the hum carried the calm home. Her gut finished the week at about a 6, her stress at a 4. What she noticed was that this felt less like a chore now and more like something she'd miss. Her win, and the truest one of the week: "I actually did this most days."

Notice whether the week feels like a chore or a small thing you'd miss — that shift, more than any number, predicts whether this sticks.
