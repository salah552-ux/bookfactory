# Week 2

## Sleep & Evening Down-Regulation

Last week you built the tools. This week you point them at sleep — and not by accident. Poor sleep and a struggling gut feed each other in a loop: a wound-up nervous system keeps you awake, and a short, broken night leaves your gut more reactive and more easily flared the next day. Calm the evening and you ease both ends of that loop at once.

The theme this week is *down-regulation* — a plain word for helping your body climb down from the day. Most of us are very good at climbing up: we ramp into alertness with coffee, screens, deadlines, and worry. We are much worse at the climb back down. We expect to go from a day of low-level stress to sleep by simply lying down and switching off the light, and then we're baffled when the mind races and the gut grumbles. Sleep isn't a switch. It's a descent, and your vagus nerve is what carries you down. This week you'll give it a hand.

Everything here is timed for the last part of your evening, ideally the half-hour before bed. You'll notice the breathing feels different at night — heavier, easier, more like sinking than working. That's not your imagination; that's your nervous system already tipped toward rest and your exercises meeting it there rather than fighting an alert daytime body. Lean into it. The evening is when these tools are at their most powerful, because they're working with the tide instead of against it.

Keep noticing. By the end of this week you'll have a sense of how your gut compares with Week 1, and this is where many people see the first clear dip — often in sleep first, then the gut a day or two behind it, because a rested nervous system is a less reactive one. Maya carries on through this week too, picking up where she left off: her gut sitting around a 6, her sleep still the rough patch. Watch where it starts to give.

### Day 8 — The long exhale, before bed
**Today's exercise:** Extended Exhale, 4-in / 8-out (Library, no. 3)   **Time:** 5 minutes

We open the week with the most reliable sleep tool you own. A longer out-breath than in-breath nudges your whole system toward rest, because the exhale is the calming half of the breath and you're choosing to spend more time there. Done in bed, in the dark, it becomes a kind of runway into sleep — each long exhale carrying you a little further down.

Set it up properly. Lights already low or off, phone already down, lying on your back or your side. The point is that this is the last thing you do, not something you do and then scroll for twenty minutes. If you drift off mid-exercise, that's not failure — that's the exercise working perfectly.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Lights low, lying down. Breathe in for 4, out for 8.
2. Let each exhale sink you a little further into the mattress.

Maya set Day 8 up properly for once — phone across the room, lights already off, lying on her back. She didn't make it to the end of the five minutes. Somewhere in the long exhales she simply fell asleep, which she only realised the next morning. Her gut had been a 6 going to bed; her sleep that night, for the first time in a while, felt closer to a 6 than her usual 4. What she noticed was how much heavier the breathing felt at night than it had in the mornings. Her win: "fell asleep before I finished."

If you drift off mid-exercise, that isn't failure — it's the runway doing exactly what it's for.

### Day 9 — Hum yourself down
**Today's exercise:** The Long OM (Library, no. 5)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Tonight, sound. A long, low "OM" does two things at once: it stretches the exhale, and it adds a throat vibration that early brain-imaging work links to quieter activity in the brain's alarm centres. It is a genuinely strange thing to sit on the edge of your bed and do, and most people feel self-conscious the first time. Do it anyway. The self-consciousness fades within a minute, and what's left is a surprisingly deep settling.

Pitch and volume don't matter — go low and comfortable, wherever your voice naturally sits. What you're after is the feeling of the sound resonating in your throat and chest, not a particular note. Let each OM run until the breath naturally empties, then take an easy breath in and begin the next.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Sitting on the edge of the bed, sound a long, low OM for three minutes.
2. Then two minutes lying down, breathing slowly.

The first OM made Maya laugh out loud at herself, alone on the edge of her bed. She nearly gave up. But she'd read that the self-consciousness fades inside a minute, so she pushed through it, and it did. By the third long, low OM she felt the sound resonate down through her chest, and a settling she hadn't expected from something so silly. Her gut was a 6 again; her sleep held around a 6. The thing she noticed was the chest vibration, lower and deeper than the daytime hum. Her win: "got past feeling daft and it actually worked."

Expect to feel a bit ridiculous for the first minute — then notice how quickly that gives way to something deeper.

### Day 10 — Settle the stomach for the night
**Today's exercise:** Voo Breath (Library, no. 11)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Many people feel the "voo" sound right in the belly, which makes it a quiet two-for-one before sleep: it pairs deep belly breathing with a low vibration you can feel in your gut. If your stomach tends to grumble, gurgle, or tighten just as you lie down — a very common pattern, because lying still is often the first moment all day your gut has your attention — this is the exercise for you.

Keep the breath low. You want your belly rising and falling, not your chest, and the "vooooo" pitched low like a distant foghorn. Don't push for volume; push for depth. The vibration is the active ingredient, and you'll feel it travel down through your chest into your abdomen, which is exactly where you want your nervous system's attention tonight.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Lying down, one hand on the belly. Make a low, foghorn "vooooo" on each long exhale.
2. Continue for five minutes, feeling the vibration low in your body.

This was the one Maya had been quietly waiting for. Her stomach always grumbled and tightened the moment she lay down — the first time all day it had her attention — and the low "vooooo" met it right there. Hand on her belly, pitched low like a far-off foghorn, she felt the vibration travel down into her abdomen. Her gut, a 6 going in, eased to a 5 by the time she finished, the lowest it had been at bedtime. Her sleep that night was a 6. What she noticed: she could feel the sound *in her gut*, not just her chest. Her win: "the foghorn reached my stomach."

If your stomach stirs the moment you lie down, aim the vibration low and let it travel there — that's the spot tonight.

### Day 11 — The hard day
**Today's exercise:** Resonance Breathing (Library, no. 2)   **Time:** 5 minutes

This is the day motivation usually dips. It's not a coincidence and it's not a personal weakness — somewhere around day ten or eleven of any new habit, the novelty wears off before the results have fully arrived, and the whole thing starts to feel like a chore for nothing. Almost everyone hits this wall. The ones who keep going are simply the ones who expected it.

So bring back the one sentence you named as your *why* in Chapter 2 — the thing you most want different in 28 days. Say it to yourself slowly. That sentence is the version of you that started this, talking to the tired version of you reading it now. Then do the simplest, most proven exercise in the book. No heroics today, no stacking, no cold. Just five minutes of gentle breathing and the quiet pride of having shown up on the day you most wanted to skip.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Bring your "why" from Chapter 2 back to mind.
2. Five minutes of gentle Resonance Breathing, in for 5, out for 5.

Day 11 was the day Maya almost quit. The novelty had worn off, the big changes hadn't fully arrived, and the whole thing felt like effort for nothing. She'd been warned this day would come, which was the only reason she recognised it for what it was. So she re-read the one sentence about why she'd started — getting through an afternoon at work without her stomach ruining it — and did five plain minutes of breathing, no heroics. Her gut bumped back up to a 6 and her sleep slipped to a 5; the wall showed up in the numbers too. What she noticed was the quiet pride of having shown up on the day she most wanted to skip. Her win: "did it anyway."

When the day feels like a chore for nothing, that's the wall everyone hits — recognising it is half of getting past it.

### Day 12 — A cool finish
**Today's exercise:** The Cool-Neck Finish (Library, no. 8)   **Time:** 5 minutes

A brief cool rinse on the neck at the end of your evening shower engages the same calming reflex you met last week, without the jolt of a full cold plunge. It might seem backwards that cooling down helps you sleep, but there's sound logic to it: your core temperature naturally falls as you head toward sleep, and a brief cool signal seems to nudge that process along, on top of the vagal calming effect.

*One reminder: skip the cold and repeat Day 8 if you have a heart condition, high blood pressure, or are pregnant — clear it with your doctor first.* For everyone else, keep it comfortably cool rather than shockingly cold, breathe slowly through it instead of tensing, and let the steadiness that follows carry you toward bed.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. End your shower with 30 seconds of comfortably cool water over the back of the neck, breathing slowly.
2. Afterward, three minutes of Extended Exhale in bed.

Maya ended her evening shower with thirty seconds of cool — not cold — water over the back of her neck, breathing slowly through it rather than tensing up. It felt counterintuitive that cooling down would help her sleep, but by the time she was in bed doing the long exhales, she felt unmistakably drowsy. With no heart condition the cool rinse was fine for her, and she kept it brief and comfortable anyway. Her gut settled back to a 5 and her sleep climbed to a 7, the best night yet. What she noticed was the steadiness that followed the cool, not the cool itself. Her win: "slept like I hadn't in weeks."

Notice the calm that arrives *after* the cool, not during — that after-steadiness is what carries you to bed.

### Day 13 — Unclench before sleep
**Today's exercise:** Neck & Jaw Release (Library, no. 9)   **Time:** 5 minutes

We carry the day in the jaw and neck, and we take it to bed with us — which is why so many people wake with a sore jaw or a stiff neck having done nothing to earn it. The clenching happens in your sleep because it never got released before sleep. Tonight you set it down on purpose, in the same region the vagus nerve travels through.

Move slowly and breathe as you go. Let the head turns be gentle and unforced, let the jaw drop open for a moment, let the tongue go soft against the floor of your mouth. You may find a yawn arrives unbidden — let it. A yawn is your body taking the invitation to relax. Finish with a couple of minutes of whatever breathing felt best to you this week, so you go from released to genuinely drowsy.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Three minutes of slow neck turns, ear-to-shoulder tilts, and jaw release.
2. Two minutes of any breathing you like, lying down.

By Day 13 Maya knew her jaw was the place she stored the day, so she gave it real attention — slow head turns, the jaw dropped open, the tongue gone soft against the floor of her mouth. A yawn arrived halfway through, unbidden, and she let it. She finished with two minutes of the long exhale that had become her favourite. Her gut held at a 5; her sleep stayed at a 7. The thing she noticed was that the yawn felt like her body accepting the invitation to stand down. Her win: "let the yawn come instead of fighting it."

If a yawn arrives while you release the jaw, let it — it's your body taking the invitation to relax.

### Day 14 — Lock in your wind-down
**Today's exercise:** Your evening stack   **Time:** 5 minutes

Two weeks in. Tonight you'll build a wind-down routine you can repeat for life — the one that felt best to *you* this week, not the one a book told you to like. By now you'll have a favourite among the evening tools: maybe it's the Voo Breath, maybe the OM, maybe just the long slow exhale in the dark. Trust that preference. The exercise you'll actually keep doing beats the one that scores best on paper.

Then you'll score the week and compare it to Week 1. Look honestly at the sleep column especially — for many people it's the first thing to shift, and seeing it on the page is what makes the rest of the month feel worth it.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Two minutes Voo Breath *or* The Long OM (your pick).
2. Three minutes Extended Exhale, lights out, lying down.

On Day 14 Maya built the wind-down she actually wanted to keep, not the one she thought she ought to. Her pick was easy: two minutes of Voo Breath, because the foghorn reached her gut like nothing else, then the long exhale in the dark. She'd stopped feeling daft about any of it. Her gut finished the fortnight at a 5 and her sleep at a 7, and laying the two weeks side by side, the sleep column was clearly the first thing to move. What she noticed was that she had, almost without deciding to, built a routine she'd miss if she dropped it. Her win: "this is mine now."

Trust the exercise you'd actually keep over the one that looks best on paper — the one you'll do beats the one you won't.
