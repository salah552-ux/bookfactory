# Chapter 4

## Make It Stick

### The part most programs skip — what happens after Day 28

Most books would end at graduation. That's exactly the gap reviewers complain about across this whole shelf: a strong four weeks, then nothing — no plan for the day after, no help when progress stalls, no map for the bad weeks. You've done the work. Let's protect it.

---

### Build your personal 10-minute stack

You don't need all twelve exercises forever. You need the three or four that work best in *your* body, run on most days. Here's the menu. Pick one from each row that you liked, and you've got a balanced daily stack.

- **A breathing tool** (the engine): Resonance Breathing, Extended Exhale, or the Physiological Sigh.
- **A sound tool** (optional but powerful): The Hum, The Long OM, or Voo Breath.
- **A cold or movement finisher** (optional): Cold Face Splash, the Cool-Neck Finish, or Neck & Jaw Release.
- **A gut-timing rule**: do your breathing tool *before your biggest meal* on days your gut is touchy.

Five to ten minutes, most days, attached to your daily anchor. That's the whole maintenance plan. Simpler than you expected, isn't it? That's the point — a plan you'll actually keep beats a perfect plan you abandon by February.

A few people thrive on more structure, so here's an optional way to think about the week if you like a little variety. Keep your breathing engine every day, then rotate the extras: sound-focused days, a cold finisher on the days you want a jolt, a gut-timing focus on the days you're eating something that usually troubles you. None of this is required. If your honest answer is "I'll just do five minutes of slow breathing after my morning coffee and call it done," that is a completely valid, completely effective plan. The best routine is the one that's still happening six months from now.

One more thing about the stack: let it change. The exercises that serve you in a calm season aren't always the ones you'll need in a stressful one. When life gets harder, you might lean more on the fast-acting tools — the sigh, the cold splash — and less on the leisurely ones. When things settle, you might drift back to the longer, gentler practices. That flexibility is a feature, not a sign you're doing it wrong. You're not following a prescription. You're using a toolkit, and a good craftsman picks the tool the job needs.

### When you hit a plateau

Around week three or four of maintenance, the dramatic early gains usually level off. This is normal, and it is not failure. Your nervous system has done its fast learning; now it's consolidating. Three things help when the numbers stop moving:

First, check you haven't quietly drifted to two minutes of rushed breathing. Quality matters more than length. A genuinely slow, soft five minutes beats a distracted ten.

Second, change the lever. If you've been all breath, add sound or cold for a week. Novelty re-engages a system that's gone on autopilot.

Third, look at sleep and stress upstream. A plateau in your gut is often a plateau in your rest. The gut sits downstream of your nervous system as a whole, so if your sleep has slipped or your stress has crept up, the gut will stall no matter how diligently you breathe before meals. Go back to the Week 2 evening tools for a week and watch the gut follow a few days behind.

And here's a reframe worth holding onto: a plateau is not the program failing. It's the program having already worked. You climbed from where you started to a better place, and now you're being asked to maintain it rather than keep climbing forever — which was always the realistic goal. Nobody's gut improves in a straight line indefinitely. The aim was never perfection. It was a reliably calmer, more predictable gut that you can keep with a few minutes a day, and a plateau at a better level than you started is exactly that. Don't let a good result get talked down into a disappointment by the part of you that always wants more.

### Your flare-day emergency protocol

Bad days will still come — that's the nature of a sensitive gut, and a single rough day is not a relapse. Have a plan so a flare doesn't spiral into fear, which only tightens the gut further. Here it is, on one line you can photograph:

> **Flare protocol:** Three Physiological Sighs right away. Then, if you can, two minutes of slow belly breathing and a short, slow walk. Skip the meal-stacking pressure today — just calm and gentle movement. Tomorrow, return to your normal stack.

The aim on a flare day isn't to fix everything. It's to keep the panic-pain loop from taking hold. Calm the alarm, and the gut has room to settle on its own.

### Your weekly maintenance schedule

To keep this effortless, here's a rhythm that holds the gains without taking over your life:

- **Daily:** your 5–10 minute stack, on your anchor.
- **Before touchy meals:** two minutes of breathing.
- **Weekly:** a quick glance at how your week went — gut, sleep, stress. Thirty seconds of honesty.
- **Monthly:** re-score the six baseline questions. Watch the trend, not any single day.

### How this fits with everything else you're doing for your gut

This book is deliberately narrow. It works on one thing — the nerve that runs your gut — and it doesn't pretend to be a complete gut-health plan on its own. That's a feature, not a gap. The most common reason gut programs fail is that they try to be everything, and end up being too much to follow. This one does its one job well and leaves room for the rest.

So how does it sit alongside the other pieces? If you're following a particular way of eating that genuinely helps you — a low-FODMAP approach, say, or simply avoiding the few foods you've learned set you off — keep doing it. Calming your vagus nerve doesn't replace that; it makes it work better, because a calmer gut handles food more gracefully. If you're being treated for a specific condition, such as clearing an H. pylori infection or working through a SIBO protocol, these exercises sit comfortably beside that treatment, supporting it by keeping inflammation's brake engaged while the medicine does its job. Nothing here interferes with anything your doctor has prescribed.

What this program adds is the layer almost everyone misses: the nervous-system layer. You can eat perfectly and still have a gut stuck in alarm. You can take every supplement on the shelf and still be sending a constant distress signal up the nerve. The five minutes a day in this book is the piece that addresses that — and for a lot of people, it's the piece that finally lets everything else they've been doing actually take hold. Think of it as the foundation the other interventions stand on, not a competitor to them.

If you've read the other books in the *Fix Your Gut for Good* series, you'll recognise the philosophy. Find the mechanism. Work with it directly. Don't sell the reader anything. The vagus nerve is simply one more mechanism — arguably the most overlooked one — and now it's in your toolkit alongside the rest.

### A realistic picture of what changes

Let me be honest with you about results, because honesty is rarer in this corner of the bookshelf than it should be.

For most people who actually do this — five minutes a day, most days, for four weeks — something shifts. Often it's sleep first, then a steadier baseline of stress, then a gut that flares less often and recovers faster when it does. The change is usually quieter than the marketing on competing books would have you believe. You probably won't wake up on Day 29 transformed. You're more likely to notice, looking back at your tracker, that the rough days got fewer and the good days got more ordinary.

For some people the change is dramatic, and if that's you, wonderful. For others it's modest but real and genuinely worth keeping. And for a few, the gut barely moves but sleep and stress improve noticeably — which is still a win, and often a sign that the gut piece needs more time or that something else is going on that deserves a doctor's attention.

What I won't tell you is that this fixes everyone or cures anything. A sensitive gut is rarely about one single thing, and a nerve is one important part of a larger picture. But it's a part you can train, for free, starting today, with no risk and no one selling you anything — and that makes it very nearly always worth doing. Set your expectations on "steadily better and sustainable," not "miraculously fixed," and you'll be both more accurate and more likely to stick with it long enough to feel the benefit.

### When to see a doctor

This program calms a nervous system and supports a struggling gut. It does not diagnose disease, and some symptoms need real medical attention. See your doctor — promptly — if you have any of these: blood in your stool, unexplained weight loss, persistent vomiting, difficulty swallowing, a fever alongside gut pain, symptoms that are new after age fifty, or any symptom that is severe, worsening, or simply frightening you.

None of that is a failure of this program. It's the responsible use of it. Vagal exercises are a powerful adjunct to good medical care — never a substitute for it.
