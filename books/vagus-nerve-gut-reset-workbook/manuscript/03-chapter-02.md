# Chapter 2

## Your Baseline

### Score yourself today, so in 28 days you can see what changed

Here is something almost no other book in this corner of the shelf bothers to do, and it's the difference between a program that works and a program that just feels nice for a week.

You are going to measure where you start.

Not because numbers are magic. Because memory is a liar. Four weeks from now, on a good day, you will struggle to recall how rough things actually felt today. You'll wonder whether anything really changed, or whether you just got lucky. A baseline takes the guesswork out. It gives you a number to beat, and proof, in your own handwriting, when you beat it.

This takes about five minutes. Do it now, before Week 1. Be honest — nobody else sees this page, and a flattering baseline only cheats you out of seeing your own progress later. If anything, round toward the rougher number; you want the genuine starting point, warts and all.

There's a second, quieter benefit to measuring that's worth naming. The simple act of rating your symptoms turns a vague, overwhelming "my gut is a mess" into specific, separate numbers — bloating here, sleep there, stress over here. Vague problems feel unsolvable. Specific ones feel like something you can work on, one at a time. You're not just collecting data. You're breaking a fog into pieces you can actually move.

### Part 2 — Two simple body numbers

These are optional, but they're worth ninety seconds because they track your nervous system directly.

**Resting heart rate.** Sit quietly for two minutes. Then find your pulse — at your wrist, below the thumb, or at the side of your neck. Count the beats for thirty seconds and double it. That's your resting heart rate in beats per minute. As your vagal tone improves over the weeks, this number often drifts down a little. Write it here: ______ bpm.

**Heart rate variability (only if you have a wearable).** Some smartwatches and rings report HRV — heart rate variability. It is the most validated everyday proxy for vagal tone we have, which is why researchers lean on it. But two honest warnings. First, the absolute number is meaningless to compare against anyone else; only *your* trend over weeks matters. Second, consumer devices are rough. Treat HRV as a direction, never a diagnosis. If you have it, note today's number: ______. If you don't, skip it entirely — you don't need a gadget to do this program.

### Part 3 — Your "why"

This one matters more than it looks. In one sentence, write what you most want to be different in twenty-eight days.

*What I want to change: ____________________________________________*

When motivation dips around Day 11 — and it will, it always does — you'll come back to this line. Make it specific. Not "feel better." Something like "get through an afternoon at work without my stomach ruining it."

### How to track from here

Every daily page in this book has a few quick fill-in lines: did you do the exercise, your gut and stress right now, one thing you noticed, and a one-line win. They take twenty seconds. Don't skip them — they're not busywork. The act of noticing is part of how your nervous system learns. You are teaching your attention to register calm, which is a skill most of us lost somewhere along the way, buried under decades of pushing through and ignoring the body's quieter signals.

Let me say a word about that "one thing I noticed" line, because it's the one people are most tempted to leave blank. It doesn't have to be profound. "My shoulders dropped." "I yawned." "My stomach gurgled halfway through." "Felt silly but did it anyway." Those small observations are exactly the point. Each one is your attention turning, just for a second, toward your own internal state instead of away from it — and that turning-toward is itself part of how vagal tone rebuilds. A body whose owner is paying gentle attention settles more easily than a body being ignored.

And the "one-line win"? Keep it tiny and true. "Did it before coffee." "Remembered without the alarm." "Felt a bit calmer at lunch." Stacking up small, honest wins is how a four-week program survives the day your motivation goes missing — and that day will come, usually around Day 11. When it does, a column of little wins in your own handwriting is a surprisingly strong argument for carrying on.

At the end of each week there's a tracker table to pull it together, and at the very end, a 30-day recap where you'll score yourself again and compare. That comparison is the moment this all pays off — the day you get to look back at this page and see, in plain numbers, exactly how far you've come.
