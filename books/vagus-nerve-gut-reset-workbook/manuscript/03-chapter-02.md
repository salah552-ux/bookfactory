# Chapter 2

## Your Baseline

### See where you start, so in 28 days you can see what changed

Four weeks from now, on a good morning, you'll try to remember how rough today actually felt — and you won't be able to. You'll wonder if anything really changed, or if you just caught a lucky week. That doubt is exactly what ends programs that were quietly working. So before you do a single exercise, you're going to do the one thing almost no other book on this shelf bothers with.

You are going to notice where you start.

Not because numbers are magic. Because memory is a liar. A baseline takes the guesswork out. It gives you a starting point to compare against later, and a way to tell real change from a hopeful mood.

It takes about five minutes to size up. The trick is honesty. A flattering picture of today only cheats you out of seeing your own progress later. If anything, lean toward the rougher read; you want the genuine starting point, warts and all.

There's a second, quieter benefit to sizing things up that's worth naming. The simple act of rating your symptoms turns a vague, overwhelming "my gut is a mess" into specific, separate numbers — bloating here, sleep there, stress over here. Vague problems feel unsolvable. Specific ones feel like something you can work on, one at a time. You're not collecting data for its own sake. You're breaking a fog into pieces you can actually move.

To make this concrete, let me walk you through it the way it went for Maya — IBS, years of bloating, every diet and probiotic going, and not much patience left for health books. Watch how she reads her own starting point. Then read yours the same way, in your head.

---

### Part 1 — How you feel right now

Here's the heart of it. You take six everyday symptoms and rate each one for the last week or so, where **0 means none at all** and **10 means as bad as it gets.** Don't overthink it. Your first gut answer is usually the true one.

On her first morning, before Week 1, Maya sat with her coffee and rated each one honestly. This is what she came up with:

| What Maya rated | Her score (0–10) |
|---|---|
| Bloating / fullness | 8 |
| Gut pain or cramping | 6 |
| Unpredictable bowel habits (urgency, loose, or constipated) | 7 |
| Trouble falling or staying asleep | 6 |
| Stress / feeling wound-up | 7 |
| Daytime energy *(here 0 = great energy, 10 = exhausted)* | 6 |

The three rows that matter most are the first three — bloating, cramping, and unpredictable bowels. Add those together and you get your **Gut Score** out of 30. For Maya, that's 8 plus 6 plus 7 — a Gut Score of 21 out of 30. Rough, but specific. Now it's a number with a shape, not just a bad feeling.

That single Gut Score is the number this program is built to move. So as you read your own six symptoms in your head, total your own first three the same way. That's your starting line.

---

### Part 2 — Two simple body numbers

These two are optional, but they're worth ninety seconds because they track your nervous system directly.

**Resting heart rate.** Sit quietly for two minutes. Then find your pulse — at your wrist, below the thumb, or at the side of your neck. Count the beats for thirty seconds and double it. That's your resting heart rate in beats per minute. As your vagal tone improves over the weeks, this number often drifts down a little. Maya counted thirty-seven beats in thirty seconds, doubled it, and got a resting heart rate of 74.

**Heart rate variability (only if you have a wearable).** Some smartwatches and rings report HRV — heart rate variability. It is the most validated everyday proxy for vagal tone we have, which is why researchers lean on it (Laborde et al., 2017). But two honest warnings. First, the absolute number is meaningless to compare against anyone else; only *your* trend over weeks matters. Second, consumer devices are rough. Treat HRV as a direction, never a diagnosis. Maya's ring did show a morning HRV figure, but she did the sensible thing and refused to read anything into the single number — she filed it away as a personal trend to watch, nothing more. If you have a device, do the same. If you don't, skip it entirely. You don't need a gadget to do this program.

---

### Part 3 — Your "why"

This one matters more than it looks. In one honest sentence, name what you most want to be different in twenty-eight days.

Maya's sentence wasn't grand. She wanted, in her own words, to "get through an afternoon at work without my stomach ruining it." That's it. Specific, true, and small enough to actually picture.

When motivation dips around Day 11 — and it will, it always does — that one sentence is what you come back to. So make yours specific too. Not "feel better." Something you could picture happening, the way Maya could picture that one untroubled afternoon.

### How to notice from here

From here on, each daily entry shows you the exercise, why it works, and a short look at how the day went for Maya — whether she did it, how her gut and stress sat that morning, the one thing she noticed, and her small win. That's not decoration. It's there so you can do the same noticing in your own head, in real time, without stopping to record anything.

The noticing itself is part of how your nervous system learns. You are teaching your attention to register calm, which is a skill most of us lost somewhere along the way, buried under decades of pushing through and ignoring the body's quieter signals.

Take that "one thing I noticed" beat seriously, because it's the one people skip. It doesn't have to be profound. "My shoulders dropped." "I yawned." "My stomach gurgled halfway through." "Felt silly but did it anyway." Those small observations are exactly the point. Each one is your attention turning, just for a second, toward your own internal state instead of away from it — and that turning-toward is itself part of how vagal tone rebuilds. A body whose owner is paying gentle attention settles more easily than a body being ignored.

The small win matters too. Keep it tiny and true — "did it before coffee," "remembered without the alarm," "felt a bit calmer at lunch." Stacking up small, honest wins is how a four-week program survives the day your motivation goes missing. And that day will come, usually around Day 11. When it does, the memory of a week's worth of little wins is a surprisingly strong argument for carrying on.
