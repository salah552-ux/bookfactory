# Week 3

## The Gut Reset — Calming the Gut-Brain Axis

This is the week you came for.

Everything so far has been building your skill with the brake. Now you'll aim it directly at the part of your body that's been struggling: your gut. This week is built around how digestion actually works and where the vagus nerve fits into it — and around the two moments that matter most for a reactive gut, the moment before you eat and the moment a flare begins.

A quick, honest framing first. IBS — the most common label for an unpredictable, painful, bloated gut — is now officially classified as a *disorder of gut-brain interaction*. That's the formal term doctors adopted in the Rome IV criteria in 2016, and the change of wording matters. For decades, conditions like IBS were called "functional," a word that too often got heard as "it's all in your head." The new term says something truer and more useful: the problem isn't only in the gut, and it isn't only in the head. It's in the wiring between them — the very wire you've spent two weeks learning to tune. This week you put that to work.

It helps to understand what your vagus nerve is actually doing for your digestion, because then the exercises stop feeling random. When you're calm, your vagus nerve switches the gut into action: it gets the stomach releasing acid and enzymes, the gallbladder squeezing, and the long muscular waves moving food through you in an orderly procession. Between meals, it drives a slow cleaning wave — a kind of housekeeping sweep that clears leftover residue from the small intestine so it doesn't sit and ferment. When you're stressed, all of that downshifts. The stomach empties more slowly. The cleaning wave stalls. Food and gas linger, and lingering food and gas is, quite literally, what bloating is. None of this means your symptoms are imaginary. It means they have a mechanism — and a mechanism is something you can work with.

There's a second loop worth naming, because it traps a lot of people. A sensitive gut sends strong distress signals up the vagus nerve to the brain. The brain reads them as threat and ramps up stress chemistry, which makes the gut more sensitive still, which sends a louder signal, and around it goes. This is why anxiety about your symptoms genuinely makes the symptoms worse — not as a character flaw, but as plumbing. The good news hidden in that loop is that you can break it from the calm side. Lower the alarm and the gut gets room to settle. That is the whole strategy of this week.

One reminder before we start. This program sits *alongside* your medical care, never instead of it. If your symptoms are new, severe, or changing — and especially if you notice any of the warning signs listed in Chapter 4 — see your doctor. These exercises calm a gut. They don't diagnose one.

### Day 15 — Before you eat
**Today's exercise:** Resonance Breathing, before a meal (Library, no. 2)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Here's a habit that can change your digestion more than most diets: calm yourself *before* you eat, not after. Your gut digests properly only when your body feels safe — that's the literal meaning of "rest-and-digest." Eat braced, rushed, standing at the counter, or scrolling through bad news, and your digestion runs in low gear before the first bite even lands. The food never had a fair chance. So today, the exercise comes right before a meal, as the on-ramp into eating.

This is one of the few changes in this book that can produce a noticeable difference the very first time you try it. Five quiet minutes of slow breathing tells your nervous system the meal ahead is a safe one, and a gut that's been told it's safe handles food very differently from a gut on alert. Pay attention to how the meal sits afterward compared to your usual.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Before your biggest meal, sit for five minutes of slow Resonance Breathing — in for 5, out for 5.
2. Only then, eat. Notice if the meal sits differently.

By Day 15, Maya had two weeks of breathing behind her, and her gut had crept down from its usual angry 8 to about a 6 on a steady day. Tonight she tried something new. She sat at the table five minutes before dinner instead of eating standing at the counter, and ran the slow in-for-5, out-for-5 until her stomach stopped feeling clenched. The meal landed softer than usual — less of the tight, bloated heaviness she'd come to expect an hour later. She rated her gut a 5 that evening and wrote her win in her head: *ate one meal without bracing for it.* Her quiet surprise was that the work happened before the first bite, not after.

Try this yourself: notice whether a meal you breathed into sits any differently from one you rushed.

### Day 16 — The post-meal bloat routine
**Today's exercise:** The Diaphragm Wake-Up + a gentle walk (Library, no. 10)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Bloating often peaks an hour or so after eating, as food sits in a slow-moving gut and gas builds behind it. Deep belly breathing gently massages the whole digestive area from above — your diaphragm moves down with each breath and presses lightly on the organs below — and that movement supports the natural muscular waves that push everything along. Pair it with a slow walk, which can help encourage the gut to empty and move gas along, and you've got a simple anti-bloat routine you'll keep for years.

The timing matters. Do this about forty-five to sixty minutes after the meal, when the bloat is building rather than right as you finish. The walk doesn't need to be far — up and down a hallway, around the garden, once around the block. You're not exercising; you're encouraging movement in a system that stalls when you sit still.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. About 45–60 minutes after a meal, do three minutes of slow belly breathing (belly rises, chest still).
2. Follow with a slow two-minute walk if you can — even up and down a hallway.

About an hour after lunch, Maya felt the familiar swell start — the waistband-tightening, drum-skin fullness that used to write off her whole afternoon. This time she didn't sit through it. She did three minutes of belly breathing at her desk, hand on her stomach feeling it rise, then walked two slow laps of the office corridor. The pressure didn't vanish, but it eased to something she could ignore, and a small pocket of gas shifted on the second lap. She marked her gut a 5 instead of the 7 that kind of afternoon usually earned. Her win: *didn't lose the afternoon to the bloat.*

Try this yourself: see whether moving the bloat is easier than waiting it out.

### Day 17 — Breathing for a sluggish gut
**Today's exercise:** Voo Breath (Library, no. 11)   **Time:** 5 minutes

The vagus nerve helps drive the slow waves of muscle movement that keep things moving through you — including that between-meal cleaning sweep we talked about. When vagal tone is low, those waves weaken and stall, and you're left feeling full, gassy, heavy, and stuck even hours after eating. The "voo" breath is built for exactly this. It combines deep belly movement with a low vibration that travels down into the abdomen, and many people feel it act directly on the gut, sometimes producing an audible gurgle as things start to move.

If you do hear your stomach rumble during or after this exercise, take it as a good sign rather than an embarrassing one. That sound is often the cleaning wave waking up — the very thing a sluggish gut needs more of. Keep the breath low and the sound low, and give it the full five minutes; this is one that rewards a little patience.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Sitting or lying, hand on belly, make a low "vooooo" on each long exhale for five minutes.
2. Keep the breath low — you want the belly moving, not the chest.

Lying on the sofa that evening, Maya made the low "voo" sound for five minutes and felt faintly silly for the first two. Then her stomach gurgled — a long, rolling complaint she'd normally have been mortified by. She'd read that morning that the gurgle is often the cleaning wave waking up, so this time she grinned at it instead. Her gut, heavy and stuck all day, felt looser by the end; she logged it a 5, down from the morning's 7. Her win was simple: *heard my gut actually move for once.*

Try this yourself: if you hear a rumble, treat it as the machinery switching on, not an embarrassment.

### Day 18 — Your flare calmer
**Today's exercise:** The Physiological Sigh (Library, no. 1)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Every reactive gut has bad moments — the cramp that grabs you out of nowhere, the sudden churn, the urgent need to find a bathroom. Today you build your emergency tool *before* you need it, so it's loaded and ready. The Physiological Sigh works in under a minute, and it's perfect for a flare because it interrupts the panic-pain loop: the moment a symptom hits, fear about the symptom floods in, the fear tightens the gut, and the tightening makes the symptom worse. Two or three sighs cut straight across that cascade.

The trick is to practise it while you're calm, today, so the pattern is automatic when you're not. You don't want to be reading instructions mid-cramp. Do five unhurried minutes of it now, and mentally label it: *this is my flare button.* Then, when a bad moment comes this week or next, you'll reach for it without thinking — which is exactly when it works best.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Practise the Physiological Sigh for five minutes while calm, so the pattern is automatic.
2. Make a mental note: *this is my flare button.*

Maya practised the double-inhale, long-exhale sigh for five calm minutes and, in her head, gave it a name: *my flare button.* The test came the next afternoon, unplanned. A sharp cramp grabbed her low and left, the kind that used to spiral into panic about where the nearest bathroom was. Before the fear could take hold, she did three sighs on the spot — and the cramp, while it didn't disappear, stopped escalating. That, she realised, was the whole point: cut the panic and the pain has less to feed on. Her gut held at a 5 that day; her win was *caught a cramp before it caught me.*

Try this yourself: load the tool now, while you're calm, so your hands know it before a bad moment arrives.

### Day 19 — Map your gut
**Today's exercise:** Gut-symptom mapping + Resonance Breathing   **Time:** 5 minutes

Today is part detective work. For one day, notice *when* your gut acts up — after which meals, in which moods, at which moments. Patterns you've lived with for years often jump out the moment you actually start watching for them. Maybe your worst bloating always follows lunch eaten at your desk. Maybe the cramps cluster on the days a particular person is around. The point isn't to find a food to ban; it's to see the link between your state and your symptoms with your own eyes.

There's a calming effect built into the act of mapping itself. So much gut distress is fed by unpredictability — the sense that it could strike any time, for no reason, which keeps you braced all day. Replacing "it's random" with "it tends to happen here" takes a surprising amount of the fear out, and less fear means a quieter signal up the nerve.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Through the day, notice each time your gut flares — roughly when it happened, what you'd just eaten, and how stressed you were. You're collecting impressions in your head, not keeping records; if you'd rather write them down, the free companion has a simple flare log.
2. In the evening, do three minutes of Resonance Breathing and let the day's flares come back to mind — you'll often find the pattern surfaces on its own.

Maya spent the day quietly watching, and three flares stood out. The first hit around 1:15, just after a sandwich eaten at her desk mid-deadline, with her stress sitting at an 8. The second came at 3:40, after coffee on an empty stomach during a tense call, stress again high. The third was milder, after dinner, stress low. Reading them back over three minutes of breathing that evening, the pattern almost wrote itself: her gut wasn't reacting to the food so much as to the *state she ate it in.* Desk-and-deadline lunches were the real trigger, not sandwiches. Naming that took some of the dread out — a quieter signal up the nerve. She marked her gut a 5 and counted the map itself as the day's win.

Try this yourself: look for the link between your state and your symptoms, not just the food on the plate.

### Day 20 — The gargle and the gut
**Today's exercise:** Gargle Reset + The Hum (Library, nos. 6 and 4)   **Time:** 5 minutes

Two throat-based tools today, and a genuinely different angle on the same goal. Remember that the vagus nerve runs through the back of your throat long before it ever reaches your gut. By working that upper end — gargling vigorously enough to make your eyes water, then humming until your chest buzzes — you engage the same nerve from the top down. It's a refreshing change from a week heavy on breathing, and for some people the throat-based tools land even better than the breath.

Gargle with real commitment; a polite swish doesn't do much. You want to feel the muscles at the back of your throat working hard, because those are the ones the vagus nerve supplies. Then let the humming carry the engagement downward, feeling the vibration travel from your throat into your chest and toward your belly.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Two minutes of vigorous Gargle Reset with your morning water.
2. Three minutes of steady humming, feeling the buzz down into your chest.

A week of breathing had left Maya a little bored of it, so the throat tools were a relief. She gargled her morning water hard enough to make her eyes water, then hummed until her chest buzzed and the buzz seemed to travel down toward her belly. It felt different from the breath work — more physical, more immediate — and oddly, it was the one her on-edge mornings responded to best. Her gut, often grumbly first thing, sat at a 4 by the time she left the bathroom. Her win: *found the tool that fits my mornings.*

Try this yourself: notice whether the throat-based tools land harder for you than the breathing did.

### Day 21 — Your gut-day stack
**Today's exercise:** The gut stack   **Time:** 5 minutes

Three weeks in, and you now have a small kit of gut-specific tools that work at different moments: the calm-before-eating routine, the move-it-along routine after, and the emergency button for flares. Today you combine the best of them into a single approach you can run on any rough gut day for the rest of your life. Think of it less as a fixed five minutes and more as a pattern for the whole day — breathe before you eat, breathe and move after, and sigh whenever it flares.

Then comes the week's reckoning. This is the week the gut number usually moves most, because you've spent seven days aiming directly at the problem rather than circling it. Whatever it does, read it honestly — a small drop you can sustain is worth more than a big one you can't repeat.

**Your 5-minute routine today:**
1. Before your main meal: two minutes Resonance Breathing.
2. After it: two minutes belly breathing.
3. Anytime it flares: the Physiological Sigh.

Maya ran the full pattern across the day — breathe before the main meal, breathe and move after, sigh when anything twinged — and it no longer felt like homework. It felt like a way of handling her own gut. By evening she rated it a 4, her lowest of the whole month so far, and let herself notice how far that was from the 8s of three weeks ago. Her win was bigger than a single day: *I have a kit now, not just hope.* A small drop she could repeat, she decided, beat a dramatic one she couldn't.

Try this yourself: think of these three not as separate exercises but as one all-day pattern for a reactive gut.
