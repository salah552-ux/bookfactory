## 2. READER AVATAR (detailed)

**Name:** Rachel.
**Age:** 41.
**Situation:** Two weeks ago her GP ran a test for the burning, gnawing pain that's worse between meals and told her she has H. pylori. She was handed a week of antibiotics (clarithromycin triple therapy — the old standard her GP hasn't updated from) and told "don't worry." She finished the course. The metallic taste and nausea from the antibiotics were grim. Then — for some patients like her — the re-test (or her returning symptoms) suggests it's **still there.** Or she hasn't been re-tested at all and nobody told her she should be.
**The moment the book meets her:** Standing in her kitchen at 11pm, phone in hand, having just typed "H. pylori cancer" into Google and read that it causes about three out of four stomach cancers. Her heart dropped.

**Her fears (in priority order, from MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md §4b):**
1. **"Am I going to get cancer?"** — the dominant, controlling anxiety. (Stat #12: H. pylori-positive patients measurably more anxious — her fear is normal.)
2. "It's still positive after antibiotics — what now?"
3. "Will it come back?" (reinfection)
4. "Did I give it to my family? Is it contagious?"

**Her frustrations:** "The doctor gave me antibiotics and I'm still positive." "My doctor didn't re-test me / told me not to worry." "I don't know what I'm allowed to eat." Deep distrust of sellers — "is the Matula tea / Manuka honey thing a scam?"

**What she wants:** Honest reassurance that isn't dismissive. To actually clear it and confirm it's gone. To know what to eat. To understand *why*, not just be handed instructions. A real answer about the cancer risk that lets her stop spiralling.

**What she types into Google (verbatim — drives copy and metadata):** "h pylori cancer" · "h pylori still positive after antibiotics" · "h pylori treatment failed" · "gnawing burning stomach pain" · "what can I eat with h pylori" · "how do I know if h pylori is gone" · "h pylori natural treatment."

**What will lose her instantly:** Being sold a supplement. A "natural cure in 60 days" promise. Fear-mongering about cancer. Vague reassurance ("don't worry") with no numbers. Talking down to her — she has already done the reading.

**Where she starts (pain state):** Frightened, post-failed-treatment or post-diagnosis, cancer-anxious, distrustful, physically uncomfortable (burning/gnawing), and unsure who to believe.

**Turning points (mindset shifts):**

### Chapter 1 — You Tested Positive. Then You Googled It.
**Target:** ~3,500 words · **Intensity:** HIGH (emotional open) · **Hook:** scenario (the 11pm kitchen, the Google search).
**Summary:** Open inside the reader's exact moment — the test result, the search, the fear. Validate it without amplifying it. Promise the calm, sourced answer and the one thing no other book offers: what to do when antibiotics fail. State the no-supplement-sales stance up front as the trust signal.
**Key facts/claims:** ~50% of the world carries H. pylori (stat #1) — she is not unusual; ~28.7% of treatments fail (stat #5) — "still positive" is common, not personal failure; H. pylori patients are measurably more anxious (stat #12) — her fear is normal.
**Reader takeaway:** "I'm in calm, honest hands. This book will explain, not sell, and it has answers for the part everyone else skips."

### Chapter 2 — What H. Pylori Actually Is (and What It's Doing to Your Stomach)
**Target:** ~3,800 words · **Intensity:** MEDIUM · **Hook:** mechanism, plain English.
**Summary:** The biology in companion-voice: a spiral bacterium that survives stomach acid, burrows into the mucus lining, and drives inflammation (gastritis), ulcers, and — over long timeframes — the changes linked to cancer. Map symptom to mechanism so the burning/gnawing/nausea finally makes sense.
**Key facts/claims:** prevalence (#1); mechanism of mucosal colonisation and inflammation (mechanism explanation — PERMITTED with sourcing); symptom vocabulary mirrored from §4b ("burning," "gnawing," "empty-stomach pain," "metallic taste").
**Reader takeaway:** "Now I understand what's happening inside me and why I feel what I feel."

### Chapter 3 — The Cancer Question, Answered with the Real Numbers
**Target:** ~4,200 words · **Intensity:** HIGH (the pivotal de-panic chapter) · **Hook:** name the fear out loud.
**Summary:** The chapter the whole book is built to get exactly right. Acknowledge the headline she read is essentially true — and then give her the numbers that make it bearable. Hold the dual message in every paragraph. This is also the legitimate basis for the Cancer-category crossover (Decision 1).
**Key facts/claims:** ~76% of projected gastric cancers attributable to H. pylori (#7); ~15.6M projected lifetime cases (#8); Group 1 carcinogen (#9); **eradication RR 0.61, NNT ≈ 332 (#10) — always paired with the scary numbers, never standalone.** The dual message (FACTS.md §0).
**Reader takeaway:** "Real enough to treat. Low enough not to panic. I can stop spiralling and start acting."
**Compliance flag:** BLOCK-risk chapter. Every fear stat immediately defused. No standalone scare line. Short disclaimer at chapter head.

### Chapter 4 — The Treatment That Actually Works in 2024
**Target:** ~4,500 words · **Intensity:** MEDIUM-HIGH · **Hook:** bold reframe ("the antibiotics you were probably given are no longer first choice").
**Summary:** The 2024 ACG standard explained: optimised bismuth quadruple therapy (PPI + bismuth + nitroimidazole + tetracycline). Why clarithromycin triple therapy was dropped (resistance). What good treatment looks like, how long it runs, and why finishing it exactly matters.
**Key facts/claims:** BQT as preferred first-line (FACTS.md §2); clarithromycin resistance 22.2–31.5% (#2); guideline removed triple/concomitant therapy; avoid clarithromycin/levofloxacin without susceptibility testing.
**Reader takeaway:** "I know what the current best treatment is and what to ask my doctor for."
**Compliance flag:** "your doctor prescribes this, not this book" line. Short disclaimer at chapter head.

### Chapter 5 — Surviving Treatment: Side Effects, Adherence, and the Mistakes That Sabotage It
**Target:** ~3,800 words · **Intensity:** MEDIUM · **Hook:** validation ("the side effects are awful — here's how to get through them").
**Summary:** The practical survival chapter. The metallic taste, nausea, diarrhoea. Why adherence and PPI timing are make-or-break. How to actually finish the course. Where S. boulardii fits for antibiotic-associated diarrhoea (tolerability, not eradication).
**Key facts/claims:** 27–41% fail even when not resistant — adherence/PPI dosing/timing decisive (#6); S. boulardii reduces antibiotic-associated diarrhoea (FACTS.md §4).
**Reader takeaway:** "I can get through the course without quitting — and finishing it properly is half the battle."

### Chapter 6 — When Antibiotics Fail: The Rescue Pathway
**Target:** ~4,500 words · **Intensity:** HIGH (the differentiator — her #2 fear) · **Hook:** direct ("still positive? you are not out of options").
**Summary:** The chapter no consumer book writes. Second-line logic, rifabutin-based rescue, furazolidone for multi-resistant strains. Why failure isn't the end and isn't her fault. The role of susceptibility testing.
**Key facts/claims:** bismuth quadruple as second-line; rifabutin 150mg + amoxicillin 1g + PPI BID ×14d (PMC6406425); furazolidone rescue even after 5 failures (PMC8467492); regional resistance figures labelled (#3, #4); failure-despite-susceptibility (#6).
**Reader takeaway:** "There is a clear, evidence-based path even after multiple failures — and a specific conversation to have with a gastroenterologist."
**Compliance flag:** every named regimen paired with "prescribed and supervised by your doctor." Disclaimer at chapter head.

### Chapter 7 — The Stomach-Lining Diet
**Target:** ~4,200 words · **Intensity:** MEDIUM · **Hook:** the verbatim question ("what am I allowed to eat?").
**Summary:** Symptom-load-reduction and lining-support framework — not a cure diet. What to reduce (salt, cured/smoked meats, excess coffee, alcohol, acidic/spicy triggers), what to support (digestible proteins, cooked veg, soluble fibre, hydration). Mechanism before rules.
**Key facts/claims:** FACTS.md §7 diet framework; salt/cured-meat independent gastric-cancer association stated factually, not as fear.
**Reader takeaway:** "I finally know what to eat and why — without a punishing elimination diet."
**Compliance flag:** "may reduce symptom load," never "heals your ulcer."

### Chapter 8 — The Adjuncts That Actually Have Evidence (and the Ones That Don't)
**Target:** ~4,200 words · **Intensity:** MEDIUM · **Hook:** the trust play ("nobody here is selling you anything").
**Summary:** Honest tour of mastic gum, broccoli-sprout sulforaphane, L. reuteri — every one framed ALONGSIDE, never INSTEAD OF. The headline-honesty anchor: natural alone ~10–40% vs ~80%+ for guideline antibiotics. Explicitly defuses the "natural cure / 60 days / Matula tea" cluster the reader distrusts.
**Key facts/claims:** mastic gum ~92.2% vs ~63.3% with the acid/PPI-timing caveat; sulforaphane reduces colonisation but doesn't fully clear; L. reuteri raises eradication + tolerability; ~10–40% vs ~80%+ headline (FACTS.md §4). Strain-to-trial matches confirmed by fact-checker.
**Reader takeaway:** "I know which helpers are real, how to use them alongside treatment, and that I was right to distrust the sellers."
**Compliance flag:** no brand names, no buy language, generic compounds/strains only.

### Chapter 9 — How to Know It's Actually Gone
**Target:** ~3,500 words · **Intensity:** MEDIUM-HIGH (closure) · **Hook:** the verbatim question ("how do I make sure it's gone?").
**Summary:** Test of cure. Stool antigen or urea breath test ≥4 weeks after the last antibiotic; stop PPIs ~2 weeks before; why blood serology is NOT a test of cure. The practical payoff of the "still positive" thread.
**Key facts/claims:** FACTS.md §5 / stat #11 — exact timing and the serology caveat.
**Reader takeaway:** "I know exactly how and when to confirm I'm clear — and not to trust symptom relief alone."

### Chapter 10 — Reinfection, Your Family, and Staying Clear
**Target:** ~3,500 words · **Intensity:** MEDIUM · **Hook:** her #4 fear ("did I give it to my family?").

**Key facts/claims:** FACTS.md §6 — uncommon-in-adults qualitative framing; household clustering; "discuss with your doctor," not directives.

### Chapter 11 — Rebuilding Your Gut After the Antibiotics
**Target:** ~3,800 words · **Intensity:** MEDIUM (warm, forward-looking) · **Hook:** reframe ("treatment ended — now you recover").
**Summary:** Post-antibiotic gut rebuild: diet, fermented foods, fibre, patience. Modest claims only. The natural bridge to the series — *The 7-Day Gut Reset* for symptom triage, *Fix Your Gut for Good* for deeper repair.
**Key facts/claims:** FACTS.md §6 rebuild framing ("may support recovery"); series cross-reference (exact titles, no pricing).
**Reader takeaway:** "Treatment was the start; here's how I help my gut recover — and where to go next."

## 6. TONE BIBLE (voice direction — match the series)

**Voice IS:** second person, always "you"; contractions always; short sentences for instruction, medium for explanation, long only for emotional beats; warmth from specificity, not exclamation marks; the author is a knowledgeable companion, never a doctor or a cheerleader; respect the reader's intelligence — she has already done the reading.

**ALWAYS:** name the mechanism before the instruction (explain *why* before *what*); pre-empt the reader's objection and answer it in the same paragraph; use plain English for clinical terms (define BQT, PPI, NNT once, plainly); pair every scary number with its de-panic counterpart; state the doctor-supervises-treatment line wherever a drug is named.

**NEVER (book-specific, ≥5):**
1. Never open a paragraph with "Additionally," "Furthermore," "Moreover," "It is worth noting that," or "Interestingly,".
2. Never use "journey" as a noun for the reader's experience.
3. Never say "miracle," "magic bullet," "groundbreaking," "revolutionary," "game-changing," or promise a "cure."
4. Never present a cancer statistic without immediately defusing it with the proportionate number.
5. Never name a brand, supplement product, tea, or vendor — or use any "buy this" framing.
6. Never tell the reader which drug to take as if the book prescribes it — always route through the doctor.
7. Never close a chapter on a rhetorical question; close on a statement or a clear next step.
8. Never use vague reassurance ("don't worry") — reassure with a number or a mechanism, or not at all.

**Forbidden phrases:** see FACTS.md §9 (full list, series-consistent).

**Example of a perfect paragraph in this book's voice:**
> *Here is the part the headline left out. Yes, H. pylori is behind roughly three out of four stomach cancers — that number is real, and it is why your doctor wants it gone. But for any one person carrying it, the lifetime odds of that outcome stay low: clearing the infection lowers the risk meaningfully, yet it takes treating hundreds of people to prevent a single cancer. Both things are true at once. The risk is real enough to treat. It is low enough that you do not need to lie awake.*
