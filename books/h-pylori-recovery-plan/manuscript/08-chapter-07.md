# Chapter 7

## The Adjuncts That Actually Have Evidence (and the Ones That Don't)

Nobody here is selling you anything. I want to open this chapter with that sentence, because this is the chapter where, in almost every other book and on almost every other website, the selling begins.

You know the pattern by now, because you have been swimming in it since the diagnosis. The teas that "eradicate H. pylori naturally." The honey with the special rating. The forty-billion-CFU probiotic blend with the glossy label and the urgent reviews. The "natural protocol" that, it turns out, you can only really follow by buying the practitioner's branded supplements. The whole apparatus is built to convert your fear into a purchase, and you were right to be suspicious of it. That suspicion is one of the healthiest instincts you have brought to this.

So this chapter does the thing none of the sellers will do. It tells you, honestly, which natural adjuncts have real evidence behind them and which do not — and it does so without a single product to buy, because the moment a book about natural remedies has something to sell you, you can no longer trust a word of it. There is nothing to buy here. That is what lets me tell you the truth.

---

### The one sentence that anchors this entire chapter

Before any individual remedy, the single most important fact about natural approaches to H. pylori is this, and everything else in the chapter hangs off it.

Natural approaches *alone* typically clear the infection in only around 10 to 40 percent of people. The guideline antibiotic regimens clear it in upwards of 80 percent. That is the gap, and it is not a small one.

Read what that means carefully. It does not mean natural approaches are worthless. It means they are not a *replacement*. If you rely on a tea or a supplement instead of proper treatment, you are trading an 80-percent-plus chance of clearing the infection for something between a one-in-ten and a four-in-ten chance — and leaving the rest of the time an active infection inflaming your stomach and carrying the very risk you are trying to remove. That is a terrible trade, and it is precisely the trade the "natural cure" industry quietly asks you to make.

So here is the rule for the whole chapter, the rule that separates honest adjuncts from dangerous substitutes: every natural approach worth considering is something you might use *alongside* proper treatment, never *instead of* it. Alongside, not instead. If you hold onto nothing else from this chapter, hold onto that.

It helps to understand why the gap between natural approaches and antibiotics is so wide, because it is not arbitrary and it is not a conspiracy by the medical establishment to keep you on drugs. Think back to what you learned about this bacterium. It burrows beneath the protective mucus, it shelters against the stomach wall, it wraps itself in a cloud of ammonia, it is exquisitely adapted to survive in one of the harshest environments in the body. Clearing something that well-defended takes a concentrated, sustained, multi-pronged chemical assault — which is exactly what a combination antibiotic regimen is. A compound in a food or a supplement, taken in the amounts a person can realistically consume, is rarely going to muster that kind of force. It might weaken the bacteria, reduce their numbers, make the antibiotics' job easier. What it almost never does on its own is finish a deeply entrenched, well-defended infection. The 10-to-40-percent figure is not a slur on natural compounds. It is a measure of how tough the target is.

Now, with that understood, let's look at the few adjuncts that actually have evidence — because some genuinely do.

---

### Mastic gum: real evidence, one crucial catch

Mastic gum is a resin from a tree grown on the Greek island of Chios, and it is one of the natural adjuncts with the most interesting evidence behind it. In studies where it was combined with standard triple therapy, eradication rates have been reported around 92 percent, compared with around 63 percent for the antibiotics used alone in those same comparisons. On the face of it, that is a striking boost.

But notice exactly what that evidence shows, because the detail is everything. The impressive figure is for mastic gum *combined with* antibiotic therapy — added to it, working alongside it. It is not a figure for mastic gum on its own as a replacement. This is the "alongside, not instead" rule made concrete: the value appears when it is added to proper treatment, not when it stands in for it.

And there is a crucial catch that the sellers never mention. Mastic gum appears to need an acidic environment to do its work — and the acid-suppressing tablets that are part of your treatment, the proton pump inhibitors, deliberately lower stomach acid. So a PPI can undermine the very thing that makes mastic gum work. This is exactly the kind of interaction that matters, and exactly why even a promising adjunct is not something to bolt onto your treatment on your own initiative. The timing and the interactions are real, and they are a conversation to have with the doctor supervising your treatment, not a decision to make off the back of a hopeful review.

This catch is a small masterclass in why "natural" is never the same as "simple" or "automatically safe to add." Here is a compound with genuinely interesting evidence, and yet bolting it onto your treatment naively could mean taking it in conditions where it cannot work — or, worse, making decisions about your acid-suppressing tablets, which are themselves load-bearing for the antibiotics, in order to accommodate it. The interactions between a "natural" adjunct and a carefully balanced drug regimen are exactly as real as interactions between two drugs, and they require exactly the same care. The sellers flatten all of this into "take our gum, it's natural and powerful," because the complexity is bad for business. The honest version is that even the good adjuncts have to be fitted into your treatment thoughtfully, by someone who can see the whole picture. That is not a reason to avoid them. It is a reason not to freelance with them.

### Broccoli-sprout sulforaphane: promising, but not a clearer

Broccoli sprouts contain a compound called sulforaphane, and it has attracted serious scientific attention — including a 2024 systematic review in the journal *Frontiers in Medicine* that found real potential for it to reduce H. pylori colonisation. "Reduce colonisation" is a genuine, measured effect, and it is why this compound keeps appearing in honest discussions of adjuncts rather than being dismissed.

But here is the line the headlines blur and you need kept sharp. Reducing colonisation is not the same as clearing the infection. In key trials, sulforaphane did *not* fully clear H. pylori when judged by the proper standard — a breath test confirming eradication. It lowered the bacterial load; it did not finish the job. So it sits firmly in the adjunct category: something that may help knock the infection back as part of a broader approach, not something that eradicates it on its own. Promising, real, worth knowing about — and not a substitute for treatment. The same rule, again.

### Lactobacillus reuteri: the probiotic with the best case

Of all the probiotics marketed at people with H. pylori — and they are marketed at you relentlessly — one strain family has a genuinely reasonable evidence base, and it is worth understanding precisely what that evidence does and does not say.

A 2025 meta-analysis looked at adding *Lactobacillus reuteri* to standard triple therapy and found two things. First, it modestly raised the eradication rate compared with the antibiotics alone. Second, and at least as importantly, it improved tolerability — people had an easier time with the side effects. That second finding matters more than it might sound, because, as we have seen, the side effects are a major reason people quit, and quitting is a major reason treatment fails. Anything that helps you tolerate and complete the course is quietly improving your odds.

I want to be careful and honest about the strains here, because this is exactly where marketing plays loose with the science. The evidence in this area centres on particular, named strains of L. reuteri studied in particular trials. A supplement on a shelf labelled "L. reuteri" is not automatically the same strain that was studied, and a result demonstrated for one strain cannot simply be assumed for another. So the honest framing is this: L. reuteri as a category has reasonable evidence as an adjunct, especially for tolerability, but if the specific strain matters to you, the trial that earned the result used a specific strain, and matching the two is not something to eyeball off a label. It is, once again, a conversation to have with someone who can check the detail — not a reason to grab the nearest bottle.

And to be clear: this is a probiotic that may help you tolerate and slightly improve your treatment. It is not a probiotic that replaces it. Alongside, not instead.

The strain point is worth lingering on, because it is the single most common way probiotic marketing misleads people, and now that you have seen it once you will see it everywhere. "Probiotic" is not one thing. It is a vast category containing many different species, and within each species many different strains, and the differences between strains can be the difference between a real, measured effect and nothing at all. A trial that showed a benefit showed it for a specific strain, grown and tested under specific conditions. A product on a shelf bearing the same species name on the front may or may not contain that exact strain, in a viable amount, in a form that survives the journey to where it needs to act. The label tells you the species; it often does not let you verify the strain or the dose that the evidence actually rested on. This is not a reason to dismiss probiotics — the evidence for L. reuteri here is real. It is a reason to treat the gap between "the studied strain" and "a bottle with a similar name" as exactly the kind of detail worth checking with someone qualified, rather than assumed away because the front of the box looked reassuring.

### Why "some evidence" is not the same as "proven"

A fair question hangs over this whole chapter: if mastic gum, sulforaphane, and L. reuteri all have *some* evidence, why am I so cautious about them? Why not just tell you to take all three? The answer is worth understanding, because it is the difference between how science talks and how marketing talks.

"Some evidence" covers an enormous range. At one end sits a single small study with a promising result that has never been repeated. At the other sits a large, well-designed body of work that has been confirmed again and again. Both can be described, truthfully, as "supported by evidence" — which is exactly why that phrase, on its own, tells you so little, and why marketing loves it. The adjuncts in this chapter sit somewhere in the middle of that range: real, genuine, worth knowing about, but not at the level of certainty that the guideline antibiotic regimens have earned through far more extensive testing. That is why they are adjuncts and not treatments, and why "discuss with your doctor" rather than "definitely take this" is the honest recommendation. The evidence is real enough to mention and not strong enough to prescribe — and a book that respects you will tell you exactly where on that spectrum each thing sits, rather than flattening everything into a confident "this works."

This is also why I keep routing you back to your doctor rather than just handing you a regimen. A doctor can weigh the modest evidence for an adjunct against your specific situation — your other medications, your treatment, the timing — in a way that a blanket recommendation in a book never could. "Some evidence" is an invitation to a careful conversation, not a green light to start swallowing things. The sellers turn "some evidence" into "miracle cure." The honest move is to keep it exactly the size it actually is.

### The ones that don't have the evidence

Honesty cuts both ways, so let's name what does not earn a place — described as categories, because the whole point of this book is that it names no products.

The "miracle teas" marketed specifically as natural H. pylori cures do not have evidence that they clear the infection, and they are very often the centrepiece of exactly the upsell-and-overpriced-consultation model that draws the scam complaints you may already have read. The single special honey everyone points to has some general antibacterial properties in a laboratory dish, but laboratory antibacterial activity is a long way from clearing an established infection burrowed under your stomach's mucus in a living human, and the evidence for it actually eradicating H. pylori in people is not there. The all-natural "three-pronged" and "60-day" protocols that position themselves against antibiotics are selling you the worst trade in the chapter: a low-single-digit-to-forty-percent shot in place of the eighty-percent-plus one, plus, usually, a shopping list.

None of these get a recommendation here, not because natural means worthless, but because the specific claims being sold are not matched by the specific evidence. That distinction — claim versus evidence — is the whole game, and it is the one the sellers need you not to make.

Let me hand you a simple tool for telling honest claims from dishonest ones, because you are going to keep encountering both long after you finish this book. Ask three questions of any remedy. First: is the claim "alongside treatment" or "instead of treatment"? Anything positioning itself as a *replacement* for antibiotics has already failed the test, because the evidence does not support replacement for anything. Second: does the evidence actually show what the marketing implies — clearing the infection, confirmed by a proper test — or does it show something narrower, like reducing colonisation or activity in a laboratory dish? The gap between "killed it in a petri dish" and "cleared it in a human" is where most of the overselling lives. Third, and bluntest: is the person making the claim also the person selling the product? When the same hand that offers the evidence also holds out the payment terminal, the evidence deserves extra suspicion, not less. Run those three questions over anything you read about treating H. pylori, and most of the noise filters itself out. The handful of honest adjuncts in this chapter pass all three. The teas and the protocols and the miracle cures fail at least one, usually all of them.

This, by the way, is exactly why this book has nothing to sell you. The moment a book about remedies has a product behind it, you can no longer apply that third question to the book itself, and so you can no longer fully trust it. The empty hands are not a gimmick. They are the structural condition that lets you believe a single word of the assessment above.

### Why this chapter exists at all

Step back and see what just happened, because it is the point of the book in miniature.

You came in distrusting the sellers, and that distrust was correct. This chapter did not tell you to abandon your suspicion and trust a different product instead. It told you the actual state of the evidence — that a few adjuncts (mastic gum, sulforaphane, L. reuteri) have real but modest support as additions to proper treatment, that several heavily marketed remedies do not have the evidence their marketing implies, and that *none* of them replace the antibiotics that actually clear the infection. And it did all that with nothing to sell you, which is the only position from which any of it can be trusted.

I want to validate that distrust explicitly, because you may have wondered whether being so suspicious made you cynical or difficult. It did not. It made you right. The H. pylori niche is genuinely saturated with people converting fear into sales — the teas, the honeys, the practitioner models built on expensive panels and branded supplements, the "natural cure" promises that quietly position themselves against the treatment that actually works. Your instinct that something was off was accurate perception, not paranoia. The appropriate response to a field full of selling is exactly the wariness you brought. The only adjustment this chapter asks is to point that wariness precisely — at the claims that outrun their evidence and the hands that hold a product, rather than at all natural approaches indiscriminately. Keep the distrust. Aim it well. It has served you, and it will keep serving you long after you put this book down.

There is a freedom in this that I hope you feel. The natural-remedy world tries to make you responsible for an enormous, anxious research project — comparing products, reading reviews, agonising over which tea or powder or strain to trust, as though clearing your infection depended on you becoming an amateur expert in supplements. It does not. The thing that clears your infection is the prescription your doctor writes. The adjuncts are, at most, a modest optional extra to discuss with that same doctor. You are not required to navigate the supplement aisle at all, and you are certainly not required to trust it. The weight the sellers tried to put on your shoulders — the sense that getting better depends on making the right purchase — was never real. You can set it down.

So the practical takeaway is calm and clear. If you are curious about adding an evidence-backed adjunct, the move is not to buy something a book or a website recommended. The move is to raise it with the doctor supervising your treatment — to ask whether something like mastic gum, sulforaphane, or an L. reuteri probiotic might sensibly fit alongside your regimen, given the timing and interactions that genuinely matter. The treatment clears the infection. The honest adjuncts, used alongside and chosen with your doctor, may help at the margins. The sellers, you can now confidently ignore. You were right about them all along.
