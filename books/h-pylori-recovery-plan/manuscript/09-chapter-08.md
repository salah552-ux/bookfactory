# Chapter 8

## How to Know It's Actually Gone

*How do I make sure it's actually gone?*

This is the question that closes the loop — the one that has been humming underneath the whole book, ever since the chapter where you stood in your kitchen unsure whether your treatment had worked. You finished the antibiotics. The pain eased, mostly. And then the doubt crept in: *but is it really gone, or is it just quiet?* For a frightening number of people, nobody ever answers that question, because nobody ever tests them again. This chapter makes sure you are not one of them.

Here is the heart of it, stated up front: you cannot know the infection is gone by how you feel. Feeling better is not proof. The only way to know is a proper test of cure, done the right way, at the right time. And getting that test, and getting it right, is the difference between hoping you are clear and actually being clear.

---

### Why symptom relief is not proof

Start with the trap, because it catches almost everyone. Your symptoms improved after treatment, so it feels obvious that the infection must be gone. But that feeling, however reasonable, is not evidence, and here is why.

Treatment knocks the infection back hard. Even when it does not fully clear it, it can suppress the bacteria enough to calm the inflammation and ease the pain for a while. So you can feel genuinely, substantially better and still be harbouring a surviving population of H. pylori that will, in time, build back up and bring the symptoms with it. Symptom relief tells you the treatment did *something*. It does not tell you it did *everything*. The gap between "better" and "gone" is exactly the gap that confirmation testing exists to close.

This is also why so many people end up in the "still positive after antibiotics" situation feeling blindsided. They felt better, assumed it was over, were never re-tested, and only discovered months later — when the symptoms returned — that it had never fully cleared. A test of cure is what would have caught it. It is the step that turns a hopeful assumption into a known fact.

There is a deeper reason this matters that connects back to the very beginning of the book, and it is worth making explicit. The cancer-risk reduction you read about — the whole reason clearing this infection is worth doing — depends on the infection actually being *cleared*. A suppressed-but-surviving infection is not a cleared one. The inflammation it drives, quietly, over the long term, is the thing the treatment was meant to stop, and a course that knocked the bacteria back without finishing them off has not stopped it. So confirming eradication is not bureaucratic box-ticking. It is the step that converts "I took some antibiotics" into "the risk I was treating is actually gone." Without the confirmation, you do not know whether you got the benefit you went through all of that to get. The test of cure is how you collect on the treatment.

---

### What a test of cure actually is

A test of cure is simply a test, done after treatment, to confirm the infection is gone. There are two good ones for this purpose, and one common test that is wrong for the job — and knowing the difference protects you from a false result.

The two good ones are the **stool antigen test** and the **urea breath test**. The stool antigen test looks for traces of the bacterium in a stool sample. The urea breath test — which you may have met before treatment — works by having you swallow a harmless labelled form of urea and then measuring your breath; if H. pylori is present, its urease enzyme splits the urea and produces a tell-tale signal you breathe out. Both are good, direct ways of asking "is the bug still here right now?" Either is suitable for confirming eradication.

The test that is *not* suitable is a **blood antibody test**, also called serology — and this is one of the most important things in the chapter, because getting it wrong leads people badly astray. A blood antibody test looks for antibodies your immune system made against H. pylori. The problem is that those antibodies persist in your blood long after the infection itself has cleared — sometimes for years. So a positive blood test after treatment does not mean the infection is still there; it may simply mean your body remembers having had it. Using a blood antibody test as a test of cure is a recipe for false alarm. It cannot tell "still infected" from "cleared but remembered," and so it cannot do this job. If anyone offers you a blood test to confirm eradication, that is the moment to ask for a stool antigen or breath test instead.

The distinction is worth making vivid, because it trips up even careful people. Antibodies are not the bacteria themselves; they are your immune system's memory of having met the bacteria. Think of them like the scorch marks left after a fire. The fire can be long out — the bacteria long gone — and the scorch marks remain, sometimes for years, a record that a fire once happened here. A blood test reads the scorch marks. It cannot tell you whether the fire is still burning or burned out months ago; it only tells you there was a fire at some point. That is genuinely useful for a first-ever diagnosis in someone never treated — it answers "has this person ever had it?" — but it is exactly the wrong question after treatment, when what you need to know is "is it still here *now*?" The stool antigen and breath tests look for the fire itself, not its memory, which is why they, and only they, can confirm a cure. If you take one technical fact out of this chapter, make it this: after treatment, you want a test that looks for the bug, not a test that looks for your body's memory of the bug.

---

### The timing that makes or breaks the result

A test of cure done at the wrong time, or under the wrong conditions, can give you a false negative — a result that says "all clear" when the infection is actually still there. Two timing rules protect you from that, and they are worth more than almost anything else in this chapter.

**Wait at least four weeks after your last antibiotic dose.** Immediately after treatment, the bacteria are suppressed and beaten down even if some survived, and testing too soon can miss the survivors and tell you the coast is clear when it is not. Giving it at least four weeks lets any surviving bacteria recover to detectable levels, so that the test sees what is actually there rather than a temporarily flattened population. Test too early and you risk a falsely reassuring result — the worst kind, because it sends you off believing you are clear when you are not.

**Stop the acid-suppressing tablets for about two weeks before the test.** This one catches people constantly. Proton pump inhibitors — the acid-suppressing tablets — suppress H. pylori as well, which means that if you are still taking them when you test, they can mask the infection and produce a false negative. So you generally need to come off the PPI for around two weeks before a test of cure. This needs to be done in coordination with your doctor, of course, especially if you are taking the acid suppression for symptoms — but it is a real and important step, and skipping it can quietly invalidate your result.

Put those two rules together and the recipe is clear: a stool antigen or urea breath test, at least four weeks after the last antibiotic, having been off the acid-suppressing tablets for about two weeks beforehand. Get those conditions right, and the result means something. Get them wrong, and you may be celebrating a clearance that did not happen.

It is worth being a little wary here, because these conditions are exactly the kind of detail that gets dropped in a busy system. A re-test gets booked too soon because that was the next available slot. Nobody mentions stopping the acid-suppressing tablets, because the person booking the test did not know it mattered, or assumed someone else would say it. You turn up, do the test, get a negative result, and walk away reassured — when in fact the timing or the PPI may have produced that negative regardless of whether the infection was truly gone. This is not a reason for paranoia, but it is a reason to be the person in the room who knows the conditions and quietly checks them. *Is it at least four weeks since my last antibiotic? Should I stop the acid tablets beforehand, and for how long?* Those two questions protect you from a falsely clean result, and you are now the one most likely to remember to ask them.

The two-week PPI pause deserves a special flag, because it is the rule most often missed and the easiest to get tangled up in. If you have been taking the acid-suppressing tablets for ongoing symptoms, coming off them for two weeks can feel daunting — what if the burning comes back while you are off them? This is precisely the kind of thing to plan with your doctor rather than navigate alone: there may be an alternative way to manage symptoms in that window, and your doctor can advise. The point is not to white-knuckle through two weeks of returning pain. The point is to coordinate the pause so the test is valid, in a way that is manageable for you. Skipping the pause to stay comfortable, though, risks buying that comfort with a meaningless result — which serves no one.

---

### What a positive result actually means — and doesn't

Let's prepare you for both outcomes, calmly, so that neither catches you off guard. If your test of cure comes back negative — no infection detected, under the right conditions — that is the result you want, and it means what it says: the infection is gone. You can take that result and the closure it brings and move forward. No lingering asterisk, no "but what if." A properly conducted negative test of cure is the real thing.

If it comes back positive, here is what it means and, just as importantly, what it does not mean. It means the infection is still present and needs another go — which routes you straight to the rescue pathway, with its second-line and rescue regimens and its clear evidence that even repeatedly failed infections can be cleared. What it does *not* mean is that you are out of options, that you did something wrong, or that the situation is hopeless. A positive test of cure is simply the information that step one did not finish the job, handed to you at the point where it is easiest to act on. People treat a positive re-test as a catastrophe; it is closer to a checkpoint. You have learned where you stand, you know the next move, and you are far better placed than the person who never tested and simply drifted, still infected and unaware. Whichever way the result lands, testing leaves you better off than not testing. There is genuinely no version of this where knowing hurts you.

### Why this step is not optional

It is tempting, especially when you feel better, to skip the test of cure. The treatment was miserable, the symptoms have eased, you would rather just move on and not think about it. I understand that completely, and I want to gently talk you out of it, because this is the step that gives you the one thing you have wanted since the beginning: to actually *know*.

Without confirmation, you are left in exactly the limbo that has been so corrosive — feeling better but never sure, the doubt always able to creep back in. With confirmation, you get an answer. Either the infection is gone, and you can genuinely close the chapter, stop scanning your stomach for the return of symptoms, and move on with real rather than hoped-for peace of mind. Or it is not gone, and you have found that out now, deliberately, while it is straightforward to act on — rather than months later when the symptoms have returned and you are back at the start, having lost the time. Both outcomes are better than not knowing. Knowing is the entire value of the test.

Think about what the limbo actually costs, because it is easy to underestimate from outside it. Without confirmation, every future stomach ache becomes a question mark. A bad meal, an off day, a flicker of the old burning, and the fear comes straight back: *is it back, or did it never leave?* You have no way to answer, so the anxiety has nowhere to land and simply circulates, ready to flare at any twinge for months or years. A confirmed clearance closes that loop permanently. The next time your stomach grumbles, you can attribute it to a dodgy lunch rather than a returning infection, because you *know* the infection is gone. That knowledge is not a luxury. It is the difference between a stomach ache that is just a stomach ache and a stomach ache that reopens the whole 2am spiral. The test buys you that, for the price of one well-timed appointment, and it is one of the best trades in this entire book.

And if the test does come back positive — if you are confirmed still positive — that is not the end of anything. That is precisely the situation the rescue-pathway chapter was written for. A positive test of cure is not a defeat; it is information, and it routes you straight onto a clear next step. You are never worse off for knowing.

### The closure this gives you

This is the chapter where the thread that opened the book finally gets tied off.

You started, many pages ago, frightened and uncertain, possibly already wondering whether a treatment had truly worked. Now you know exactly how to answer that — not by guessing from your symptoms, but with a proper test of cure: a stool antigen or breath test, at least four weeks after the antibiotics, off the acid-suppressing tablets for about two weeks, and never a blood antibody test, because antibodies linger and lie. You know to ask for it, to time it correctly, and to insist on the right kind of test. That is a degree of control over your own care that most people never get, simply because nobody told them the question to ask.

Consider how different this is from where the book began. At the start, you were at the mercy of whatever your symptoms happened to be doing — feeling worse, feeling better, never knowing what any of it meant, the uncertainty itself a source of fear. Now you have a way to step outside the guessing entirely and get a definitive answer from the bacterium's own chemistry. You are no longer reading tea leaves in your own stomach. You have a test, you know its rules, and you know how to make it tell you the truth. That is what genuine control over a health situation feels like — not the false control of a "natural protocol" you white-knuckle through, but the real control of knowing exactly which question to ask, of whom, and under what conditions, to get an answer you can rely on. Few people with this infection ever reach that point. You have, and it is the closing of the loop the whole book set out to close.

If there is one practical thing to do the moment you finish your course, it is to put the test of cure on the calendar straight away — not as a vague intention but as an actual plan, timed correctly. Count four weeks from your last antibiotic dose, note when you will need to come off the acid-suppressing tablets beforehand, and arrange the test for the right window. Doing this immediately, while it is fresh and while you are still in the mindset of treating this properly, is how you avoid the most common failure: the slow drift into "I feel fine, I'll sort it out later," which becomes "I never got round to it," which becomes a quiet uncertainty you carry for years. The test only works if it actually happens. Make it happen by planning it the day the course ends.

So ask it. When your course is finished, do not let the matter dissolve into "I feel better, so it's probably fine." Book the test of cure, get the conditions right, and get the answer. Whichever way it comes back, you will finally know where you stand — and knowing is what lets you stop bracing and start living. That is the closure the whole book has been building toward, and it is one good appointment away.

> - Feeling better is not proof the infection is gone. Treatment can suppress H. pylori without fully clearing it, so a test of cure is the only way to actually know.

> - Don't skip the test just because you feel better. A clear result gives you real closure; a positive result is caught now, while it's easy to act on.
