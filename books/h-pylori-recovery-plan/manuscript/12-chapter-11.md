# Chapter 11

## Your Recovery Plan on One Page

Here is everything, distilled.

You have read the whole book now, or you have flipped straight to this chapter because you wanted the plan without the preamble — and either way is fine, because this is the chapter built to be torn out, photographed, kept in a notes app, and carried into your next appointment. Everything below has been explained and sourced in the chapters before it. This is the map with the explanations stripped away, so you can hold the whole thing in one hand.

Nothing here is new. Nothing here replaces your doctor. This is your plan, assembled from what the evidence says, ready to act on.

---

### The mindset you start from

Before any step, the frame that holds it all together: **the risk is real enough to treat, and low enough not to panic.** You take this seriously, and you do not lie awake over it. Both at once. That is the posture for everything that follows — calm action, not dread.

And the second frame: **you understand why, not just what.** You are not a passenger in your own treatment. You are the informed person in the room who knows the questions to ask.

Why begin a one-page plan with a mindset rather than a step? Because the mindset is what holds the steps together when things get hard or frightening. A plan made of actions alone falls apart the moment fear creeps back in — you forget why you are doing any of it and the panic takes over again. A plan anchored in proportion survives the wobble. When the old 2am fear returns, as it occasionally will, you do not start from scratch; you return to the frame — real enough to treat, low enough not to panic — and the steps make sense again. The two frames above are not throat-clearing before the real plan. They *are* the foundation the plan stands on. Read them first, and reread them whenever the rest threatens to scatter.

---

### The plan, step by step

**1. Put the cancer fear in proportion — and keep it there.**
H. pylori is linked to most stomach cancers across whole populations, which is why clearing it is worth doing. But for you as an individual, the absolute risk is low, and clearing the infection lowers it further. Treat it like a seatbelt: sensible, proportionate, not an emergency. You have done the spiralling. You are done with it now.

**2. Get the right treatment — the current one, not the old one.**
The preferred first-line treatment changed in 2024 to optimised bismuth quadruple therapy (a PPI, bismuth, a nitroimidazole, and a tetracycline, usually about 14 days). The older clarithromycin triple therapy was demoted, partly because of resistance — and if that's what you were given, it may be why you're still positive. Your doctor prescribes the actual medicine. Your job is the informed conversation.

*Why this matters:* the single most common avoidable reason for a failed first treatment is being given the outdated regimen by a clinician who hasn't updated to the 2024 guidance. You cannot prescribe for yourself, and you shouldn't try — but you can make sure the current standard is on the table by asking about it. That one informed question may be the difference between clearing it this round and joining the large group who go positive again.

**3. Survive the course — and finish it exactly.**
Expect a metallic taste, nausea, diarrhoea, fatigue — common and temporary. Take the acid-suppressing tablet at the right time (usually before meals); its timing is load-bearing. Build reminders on day one, plan for the day-five-to-eight slump, and don't stop just because you feel better. A large share of failures happen in people whose bacteria weren't even resistant — meaning how you take it is one of the biggest things you control.

*Why this matters:* this is the step where your own actions move the odds most. You cannot control whether your strain is resistant, but you have near-total control over whether you finish the course exactly as directed — and a large share of failures come down to exactly that. Two weeks of careful, complete, correctly timed treatment is one of the most powerful things you personally bring to the whole process. Tie the finish line to the calendar, not to how you feel.

**4. If it fails, don't despair — there's a pathway.**
A failed course is common, not a personal failing. Ask for a referral to a gastroenterologist. Ask about susceptibility testing before the next regimen. Know that bismuth quadruple therapy (as second-line), rifabutin-based rescue, and furazolidone-based rescue exist — the last documented to clear infections even after five prior failures. You are very unlikely to be out of options.

*Why this matters:* this is the step almost no other book gives you, and it is the antidote to the worst fear in this whole condition — the fear that you are untreatable. You are not. "Difficult to treat" means it takes a specialist and possibly more than one further attempt; it does not mean unbeatable. A regimen documented to work after five prior failures is the proof. If you take one thing from this step, take that: the pathway extends well past wherever you are standing.

**5. Eat to settle the lining — not to cure.**
No diet clears the infection. But you can reduce symptom load: ease off very salty foods, cured and smoked meats, excess coffee and alcohol, and any acidic or spicy foods that personally trigger you. Support with easily digestible proteins, cooked vegetables, soluble fibre, gentle hydration. Eat smaller and more often so acid isn't left against an empty stomach.

*Why this matters:* the goal here is comfort and support, not a cure, and keeping that straight protects you from two traps — delaying real treatment in favour of a "healing diet," and turning eating into a fearful, joyless test. Most of the restrictions are temporary, led by your own comfort, and meant to be livable. The one principle behind all of it: don't irritate an inflamed surface, and don't fuel the inflammation. Reason from that and you don't need anyone's list.

**6. Use only the honest adjuncts — alongside, never instead.**
Natural approaches alone clear the infection in only about 10–40% of people, versus 80%+ for guideline antibiotics. A few adjuncts have real but modest evidence as add-ons — mastic gum, broccoli-sprout sulforaphane, L. reuteri — and *Saccharomyces boulardii* for tolerating the diarrhoea. Raise any of them with your doctor; never use them in place of treatment; buy nothing on a website's say-so.

*Why this matters:* this is where the selling lives, and where your hard-earned distrust pays off. The test for any remedy is simple — is it offered alongside treatment or instead of it; does the evidence show clearing the infection in people or just activity in a dish; and is the person making the claim also the one selling the product? Run those three questions over anything you read and most of the noise filters itself out. You are not required to navigate the supplement aisle at all. The prescription clears the infection; the adjuncts are an optional extra for your doctor to weigh.

**7. Confirm it's actually gone — don't guess from how you feel.**
Get a test of cure: a stool antigen test or a urea breath test, at least four weeks after your last antibiotic, off the acid-suppressing tablets for about two weeks beforehand. Never a blood antibody test — antibodies linger and mislead. Feeling better is not proof. The test is.

*Why this matters:* this is the step most often skipped, and skipping it is how people end up unknowingly still infected, blindsided months later when symptoms return. It is also the step that closes the loop on the cancer-risk reduction you went through treatment to get — a suppressed-but-surviving infection has not delivered that benefit. Get the conditions right (the four weeks, the two weeks off the acid tablets) or the result is meaningless. This is the appointment that turns "I hope it's gone" into "I know it's gone."

**8. Handle the family question calmly.**
Reinfection after a confirmed clearance is uncommon in adults in good conditions. The infection clusters in households through ordinary closeness, so raise the family question with your doctor — especially if a relative has symptoms like yours — but carry no guilt. There's no fault here, only a sensible question.

*Why this matters:* the "did I give it to my family?" fear is caring but misframed. This is something about half the world carries, usually acquired in childhood through routes no one controls. Much of what looks like the infection "coming back" is really an infection that was never confirmed gone in the first place — so confirming yours properly removes most of that risk too. Raise the testing question; carry none of the blame.

**9. Recover gently afterward.**
Once it's cleared, support your gut's rebuild with varied plants and fibre, reasonable fermented foods, hydration, gentle habits, and patience. Your gut largely heals itself given time and decent conditions. No special protocol, no branded system, nothing to buy.

### How the nine steps fit together

It is worth seeing the shape of those nine steps as a whole, because they are not a random checklist — they are a sequence, and each one sets up the next. The first step settles your mind, so you can act instead of spiral. The next three get you the right treatment and through it. The fifth catches you if it fails. Steps five through eight surround the treatment with sensible support — the diet that eases symptoms, the honest adjuncts, the confirmation that it worked, the calm handling of the family question. And the ninth lets you recover and move on. Read top to bottom, they trace the entire arc of this condition, from the frightened moment of diagnosis to the quiet moment of being well again. Wherever you are on that arc right now, the steps below and after you are your map of what comes next.

You will not need all nine at once, and you will not need them in a single sitting. Newly diagnosed, you live mostly in steps one through four for the next few weeks. Still positive after a course, you jump to step five. Finished and feeling better, you are at step seven, confirming. The plan meets you wherever you are. The point is that there is always a clearly defined next step, and you now know what it is — which is the opposite of the formless dread you started with.

### The conversation to have with your doctor

If you do one thing with this plan, take these questions to your next appointment. You don't need to remember the science — you need to ask the right things and let your doctor do the rest.

- *I'm still positive (or newly diagnosed) — is optimised bismuth quadruple therapy the right first-line treatment for me, given the 2024 guidance?*
- *Exactly how and when do I take each tablet — especially the acid-suppressing one, relative to meals?*
- *If this course fails, what's the plan? Can we consider a referral to a gastroenterologist, and susceptibility testing before the next regimen?*
- *When and how should I do a test of cure to confirm it's gone — and can we make sure it's a stool antigen or breath test, not a blood test?*
- *Should anyone in my family be tested?*
- *Is there an evidence-based adjunct that might sensibly fit alongside my treatment?*

That is the appointment you now know how to have. It is a completely different appointment from the one you had at the start — the one where you were handed tablets and a "don't worry" and sent home to your search engine. This time you walk in informed, and you walk out with a plan.

Keep these six questions somewhere you will actually find them when the appointment comes — in your phone, on a card in your wallet, wherever you will look. The worst time to try to remember them is in the room itself, nervous and rushed, with the clock running. The best preparation is to have them ready in advance so that all you have to do in the moment is read. You do not need to memorise anything in this book. You need to know it exists, know where to find it, and bring the relevant page with you. That is the whole trick of turning knowledge into a better appointment: not holding it all in your head, but having it written down and to hand exactly when it counts.

### If you only do three things

A nine-step plan is thorough, but life is busy and fear narrows attention, so let me strip it down to the irreducible core — the three things that matter most, in case everything else slips away.

First, get the *current* treatment, not the outdated one, and finish it exactly as directed. That single combination — the right regimen, taken completely and correctly — is the foundation everything else rests on, and it is where most avoidable failures happen.

Second, confirm it worked with a proper test of cure, timed correctly, never a blood test. This is the step that turns hope into knowledge and catches a failure while it is still easy to act on.

Third, if it fails, do not despair — get to a gastroenterologist and onto the rescue pathway. Failure is common and beatable, and the pathway extends far past wherever you are standing.

Treatment, confirmation, and a plan for failure. If the full nine steps are more than you can hold right now, hold those three. They are the spine of getting better, and everything else in the plan supports them.

### What you are holding now

Look back at where this began. You tested positive, you googled it, and a calm medical problem became the most frightening thing in your life. Now you are holding a single page that contains the whole of it: the fear in proportion, the right treatment to ask for, how to get through it, what to do if it fails, what to eat, which helpers are honest, how to confirm you're clear, how to handle your family, and how to recover. Nine steps and six questions. That is the entire book, in your hand.

There is something worth noticing about how this plan is shaped, because it is the opposite of how the fear arrived. The fear came all at once — a single overwhelming wave of cancer and contagion and dread, undifferentiated, impossible to act on. The plan comes in pieces — nine discrete, ordered, doable steps, each one small enough to actually take. That is not an accident of formatting. It is the whole strategy for turning panic into action. Panic is a problem too big to grasp. A plan is that same problem broken into parts small enough to hold. You cannot act on "I might have cancer." You can absolutely act on "ask my doctor whether bismuth quadruple therapy is right for me." The plan works because it has done the breaking-down for you, and every overwhelming fear you walked in with now has a specific, modest step attached to it. That is what it means to convert dread into a plan, and it is, in the end, most of what this book has been doing.

Notice, finally, what is *not* on this page. There is no supplement to buy, no tea to order, no programme to enrol in, no product of any kind. Every step is either something your doctor does, something you ask, or something free and sensible you do yourself. That absence is deliberate, and it is the whole reason you can trust the plan. A plan with something to sell you is bent, however slightly, toward the sale. This one has nothing to sell, so it can be bent only toward your getting better. Hold onto that as you carry the page out into the world. The most valuable thing on it may be the thing that is missing.
