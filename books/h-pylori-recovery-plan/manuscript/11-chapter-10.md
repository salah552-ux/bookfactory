# Chapter 10

## Rebuilding Your Gut After the Antibiotics

The treatment is over. You took the tablets, you got through the metallic-taste fortnight, you confirmed the infection is gone. And now you are left with a stomach and a gut that have been through a great deal — two weeks of strong antibiotics on top of however long the infection was quietly inflaming things — and a reasonable question: now what? How do I actually recover?

This is the gentlest chapter in the book, because it is the one where the crisis is behind you. The frightening part is done. The infection that scared you is cleared. What remains is not a fight but a recovery, and recovery has a different pace and a different feel. It is slower, kinder, and largely a matter of giving your gut the conditions to settle back into itself. Let me show you how to support that — honestly, modestly, without overselling what food and time can do.

---

### What the antibiotics did to your wider gut

To understand the rebuild, understand what was disturbed. The antibiotics that cleared your H. pylori were not precision-guided. They could not target only the one bacterium in your stomach and leave everything else untouched. They swept more broadly, and along the way they disturbed the vast, complex population of bacteria living throughout your gut — the community that, in a healthy state, helps with digestion, keeps things running smoothly, and generally does quiet good work in the background.

This is why so many people feel a bit off in their digestion even after a successful course — looser or more unsettled than usual, not quite back to normal. That is the disturbed gut community finding its feet again. The good news is that the gut is resilient, and for most people that community does recover and rebalance over time on its own. The rebuild is less about doing something dramatic and more about supporting a recovery that is already inclined to happen, and not getting in its way.

It helps to picture the gut community less like a delicate ornament that shatters and more like a lawn. A heavy disturbance — a fortnight of antibiotics — is like a spell of harsh weather across the grass. For a while it looks patchy and worse for wear. But a lawn is not destroyed by bad weather; given decent conditions and a little time, it grows back, often thicker than before. Your gut bacteria are similar. They were knocked back, not annihilated, and the community has a strong natural tendency to regrow and rebalance. This is why the rebuild does not require heroic intervention. You are not reconstructing something from nothing. You are tending a lawn that wants to grow back anyway — giving it decent conditions, a bit of patience, and the good sense not to keep trampling it while it recovers.

This framing also guards you against a specific anxiety that catches a lot of people after antibiotics — the worry that the course "destroyed your gut" or "wrecked your microbiome" for good, a phrase the wellness internet repeats constantly. It is not true for the vast majority of people. A single treatment course is a disturbance, not a demolition, and the resilience of the gut community is genuinely remarkable. You did not have to choose between clearing a harmful infection and keeping a healthy gut. You cleared the infection, your gut was temporarily disturbed, and it recovers. Both things are fine.

### How to support the recovery

The supportive measures here are gentle, sensible, and free of magic. I am going to keep the claims modest, because honest modesty is the whole register of this book, and because the truth is genuinely modest — these things *may support* your recovery; they do not perform miracles, and nobody should tell you they do.

**Eat to feed the good bacteria.** A diet with plenty of varied plants, vegetables, and fibre gives the recovering gut community the kind of food it thrives on. Fibre, in particular, is the raw material that many beneficial gut bacteria feed on, so a varied, fibre-containing diet supports their return. This is not a special "gut-healing" regime to buy into; it is ordinary, varied, plant-containing eating, which happens to be what a recovering gut likes.

The word to hold onto here is *variety*, because it matters more than any single "superfood." A healthy gut community is a diverse one, many different kinds of bacteria coexisting, and different bacteria thrive on different foods. So the most useful thing you can do is eat a range — different vegetables, different fibres, different plants across the week — rather than fixating on one or two foods reputed to be good for the gut. There is no single magic ingredient that rebuilds a microbiome, whatever the labels claim. There is just varied, ordinary, plant-rich eating over time, which feeds a wide range of beneficial bacteria and lets the diversity re-establish itself. If you find yourself being sold a specific expensive food as the key to gut recovery, you can let it go. The key is variety, and variety is free.

There is also a gentle timing point worth knowing. Straight after treatment, if your gut is still a little unsettled, you may not tolerate a sudden flood of high-fibre food well — it can feel like a lot for a system that is still finding its feet. So build the fibre and variety back up at a comfortable pace rather than all at once. This is the same principle as everything else in the recovery: gentle, gradual, led by how you feel. There is no rush to hit some ideal diet by next week. You have time, and easing into it tends to sit better than charging at it.

**Fermented foods, in reasonable amounts.** Foods like yoghurt, kefir, and other fermented foods can contribute beneficial bacteria and are a gentle, food-based way to support the rebalancing — introduced at a comfortable pace and in reasonable amounts, led by your own tolerance. As with everywhere else in this book, this is a category and a mechanism, not a product to purchase; ordinary fermented foods from ordinary shelves, not a branded protocol.

A note on the "comfortable pace" part, because it matters. Coming straight off treatment, your stomach may still be a little sensitive, and piling in large amounts of fermented or acidic food too fast can backfire. Start small — a few spoonfuls of yoghurt, a modest serving — and let your own comfort be the guide for how much and how quickly to add. There is no race here and no quota to hit. If something does not sit well, ease back and try again later. The goal is to gently reintroduce variety and beneficial bacteria as your gut settles, not to flood a still-recovering system in pursuit of some ideal of "gut health." Slow and tolerance-led wins. This is recovery, and recovery rewards gentleness.

**Hydration and the gentle eating habits you already learned.** The smaller, more frequent, gentler way of eating that helped your inflamed lining during treatment is also kind to a recovering gut. You do not need to abandon it the moment the course ends. Easing back toward normal eating at a comfortable pace, rather than diving straight into the richest and heaviest foods, gives everything time to settle. Think of it as a gradual return rather than a sudden switch — there is no day on which you are abruptly "allowed everything again," just a comfortable easing back as your stomach and gut tell you they are ready. Let how you feel set the pace, and you will rarely go wrong.

**Patience, which is the real active ingredient.** More than any food, the thing your gut needs is time. Recovery is gradual. You may not feel completely back to normal for some weeks, and that is ordinary and expected, not a sign that something is wrong. The disturbed community rebalances on its own schedule, and the most useful thing you can do is support it gently and let it. Patience is not a consolation prize here. It is genuinely the main mechanism.

Patience is hard, though, precisely because you have just spent a fortnight measuring your progress in tablets and symptoms, and recovery does not come with that kind of scoreboard. There is no test that says "your gut is now 80 percent rebuilt," no clear finish line you cross. Things just gradually feel more normal, unevenly, with good days and slightly off days, until one week you realise you have not thought about your digestion in a while. That is what recovery actually looks like — not a dramatic return to form but a quiet fading of the problem into the background. If you go looking for a sharp before-and-after moment, you will worry that it is not happening. It is happening. It is simply happening in the way recovery usually does, which is slowly and without announcement.

A gentle warning fits here, because this is the stage where some people undo their own calm. Having just been through a health scare, it is tempting to over-monitor the recovery — to scrutinise every bowel movement, every twinge, every day that is not perfectly normal, treating each as evidence that something has gone wrong. That vigilance, understandable as it is, tends to manufacture anxiety rather than catch problems. An unsettled gut for a few weeks after antibiotics is expected, not alarming. The kindest thing you can do for your recovery is to stop watching it so closely and let it get on with what it is naturally inclined to do. If something genuinely concerning arises — significant, persistent, or worsening symptoms — that is a reason to see your doctor, as always. But ordinary, gradually improving unevenness is not a problem to solve. It is recovery, and recovery does not need supervising.

### What recovery does not require

It is as useful to know what you do *not* need to do as what you do, because the post-antibiotic period is a prime hunting ground for unnecessary interventions. You do not need an expensive probiotic regimen — while certain probiotics have specific evidence in specific situations, a sweeping daily probiotic programme is not a requirement for ordinary recovery, and your gut will largely rebalance without one. You do not need a "gut cleanse" or a "detox," concepts that have little grounding and can do more harm than good to a system that is simply trying to settle. You do not need to eliminate whole food groups out of fear, or follow an elaborate restrictive protocol, or buy anything marketed specifically at post-antibiotic gut repair. The recovering gut is not a problem to be aggressively managed. It is a system returning to balance, and the kindest thing you can do is feed it varied, ordinary food and otherwise leave it alone.

This matters because the urge to "do something" after an illness is strong, and the market is happy to sell into that urge. But here, doing less is often doing better. An ordinary varied diet, some fermented foods if you like them, patience, and the resumption of normal life is genuinely the whole of what most people need. If that feels anticlimactic — if part of you wants a more dramatic recovery regimen to feel properly looked after — notice that feeling and let it go. The undramatic version is the accurate one, and it has the considerable merit of being free, simple, and exactly as effective as your gut's own resilience allows.

### Keeping the claims honest

I want to pause and be straight with you, because "gut rebuild after antibiotics" is a phrase the wellness industry has thoroughly colonised, and you deserve to know where the honest line sits.

These measures *may support* your gut's recovery. They are sensible, low-risk, and grounded in how the gut actually works. What they are not is a medical repair protocol, a guaranteed timeline, or a reason to spend money on special powders and programmes. Your gut largely heals itself, given decent conditions and time; what you are doing is providing decent conditions, not performing a repair. If anyone offers you an expensive, branded, multi-step "gut restoration system" off the back of your antibiotic course, you are now equipped to recognise it for what it is — the same selling, in a new costume. Varied food, some fermented foods if you like them, hydration, gentle habits, and patience. That is the honest rebuild, and it costs nothing.

### The shape of where you are now

Look at the distance you have travelled. You opened this book frightened, googling cancer at 2am, unsure whether your treatment had worked, distrustful of everyone trying to sell you something. You are arriving at a chapter about *recovery* — about the gentle, ordinary business of letting a gut settle back to normal after the hard part is done.

It is worth pausing to actually feel that shift, rather than rushing past it to the next task, because you have earned the moment. For weeks or months, your stomach has been a source of fear — a place where something frightening lived, where pain kept returning, where every twinge raised a question about cancer. And now it is becoming, once again, just your stomach: an ordinary part of an ordinary body, recovering quietly, asking nothing of you but a little patience and some decent food. That transformation — from a site of dread back into an unremarkable part of you that you do not have to think about — is the real destination of this whole book. Not just a cleared infection, but a stomach you are no longer afraid of. Let yourself notice when you arrive there. It is a genuine homecoming, and you brought yourself to it.

That shift, from crisis to recovery, is the whole arc. The infection is cleared, or being cleared, the right way. The fear has been put in proportion. And what is left is not a battle but a gentle return to normal, supported by varied food, a little patience, and the ordinary resilience of a body that is, given half a chance, very good at healing itself. You support it, you give it time, and you let it. That is the rebuild, and it is a far quieter, kinder place than the one where you started.
