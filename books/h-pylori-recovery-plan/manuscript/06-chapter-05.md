# Chapter 5

## When Antibiotics Fail: The Rescue Pathway

> A brief note before this chapter. It names specific rescue regimens and doses so you can understand the options and discuss them with a specialist. None of it is a prescription. These are difficult-to-treat situations that belong in the hands of a gastroenterologist, who chooses, adjusts, and supervises the medicine. The book briefs you for that conversation. It does not replace it.

If you have been treated once and you are still positive, you are not out of options. If you have been treated twice and you are still positive, you are not out of options. There is a clear, evidence-based pathway forward even after several failures — and this is the chapter that lays it out, because almost no other book written for people like you bothers to.

That gap is not an accident. The failed-treatment reader is the one the rest of the field abandons. The diet books assume you will clear it with diet. The "natural cure" books assume you never needed antibiotics at all. The thin overview guides stop at "take your tablets and you'll be fine." None of them are written for the person standing in their kitchen with a second positive result, frightened that the well has run dry. That person is who this chapter is for. If that is you, read on, because the situation is far more hopeful than it feels right now.

---

### First, the thing you most need to hear

It is not your fault.

I know that the second or third positive result lands like a verdict on you personally — as though your body is uniquely stubborn, or you took the tablets wrong, or there is something defective about you that the bacteria can sense. None of that is what is happening. Treatment failure is built into the numbers. Around 28.7 percent of first-line treatments fail — close to three in ten. That is not a fringe outcome that only happens to people who did something wrong. It is a routine, expected, well-documented share of cases, and clinicians plan for it precisely because it is so common.

And remember the figure from the last chapter: a large portion of failures — between 27 and 41 percent — happen even when the bacteria were never resistant to the drugs. Failure is often about the treatment recipe, the timing, the adherence, the acid suppression, the resistance of a particular strain — a tangle of factors, most of which are nothing to do with your worth or your effort. The right response to a failed course is not shame. It is a different, smarter next course. Let's look at what that is.

I want to dwell on the shame for a moment, because it is doing real damage and it deserves to be named directly. A failed treatment lands differently from most medical setbacks. When a treatment for, say, high blood pressure does not work, nobody feels personally defective about it — they just try a different one. But a persistent infection somehow gets internalised. It starts to feel like the bacteria are *winning against you specifically*, like your body is uniquely hospitable to them, like there is something wrong with you at a level deeper than a bug in your stomach. None of that is real. The bacteria are not clever and they are not targeting you; they are a strain with a particular resistance profile that the particular regimen you took did not happen to overcome. That is a logistics problem, not a verdict on you. The reframe that helps most is this: a failed course is not a closed door, it is a *data point*. It tells you and your doctor something useful about what your infection needs next. Used that way, the failure becomes the first step of the solution rather than evidence of doom.

---

### Step one: the right test before the next prescription

Before reaching for another combination of drugs, the single most valuable thing in difficult cases is information about the enemy. Specifically: what is your particular strain of H. pylori actually resistant to?

This is called susceptibility testing, and it does for H. pylori roughly what it does for any stubborn infection — it works out which antibiotics the bug you are carrying can still be killed by, and which ones it has learned to shrug off. After a failure, blindly trying another standard combination is a gamble. Testing turns the gamble into a targeted choice. If your strain is resistant to clarithromycin and levofloxacin but sensitive to something else, that completely changes which regimen makes sense, and it stops you wasting another two miserable weeks on drugs that were never going to work.

Susceptibility testing is not always easy to obtain, and it is not always done, but in a difficult or repeatedly failed case it becomes genuinely important, and it is a reasonable thing to ask a specialist about directly: *given that I've failed treatment, can we test what my strain is actually susceptible to before we choose the next regimen?* That is a sophisticated, useful question, and it is exactly the kind of thing a referral to a gastroenterologist is for.

To see why testing changes everything, picture the alternative. Without it, a doctor facing a failed infection is reasoning from probabilities — "this combination usually works, let's try it" — which is sound but blind, because it cannot account for what *your* particular strain has already proven it can survive. Each blind attempt costs you another two weeks of demanding treatment, another round of side effects, and another disappointment if it fails. Testing converts that blind sequence into a targeted one. If your strain is shown to be resistant to certain drugs, those are taken off the table immediately, and the next regimen is built from what is actually likely to work against the specific infection you carry. It is the difference between trying keys at random and being handed the one that fits the lock. In a first, uncomplicated infection, the random-key approach usually works quickly enough that testing is not worth the trouble. In a repeatedly failed one, it can save you from a demoralising cycle of further failures, which is exactly why it moves from "optional" to "worth pushing for" the moment standard treatment has let you down more than once.

### Step two: the second-line logic

The structure of what comes next depends partly on what came first, and there is a clean logic to it that is worth understanding.

If your first treatment was *not* bismuth quadruple therapy — if, as we discussed, you were given the older clarithromycin triple therapy — then bismuth quadruple therapy is the preferred second-line choice. In a sense, the failure of the old regimen routes you onto the current standard. This is one of the more straightforward situations, and it is also one of the most common, because so many people were started on the outdated recipe. The "fix" is, in effect, to give you the treatment you would now be given first.

If you have *already* had bismuth quadruple therapy and it failed, the logic shifts toward the rescue regimens — the more specialised combinations reserved for the genuinely difficult cases. The principle running through all of it is sensible: do not simply repeat what already failed, and where possible let testing or careful reasoning guide which drugs are most likely to work next, rather than cycling blindly through combinations. Each subsequent regimen is chosen to avoid the drugs that have already proven useless against your strain.

This is also exactly the point where care should move into specialist hands if it has not already. A repeatedly failed H. pylori infection is not a routine primary-care problem any more. It is a gastroenterologist's problem, and asking for that referral is not being difficult — it is getting yourself to the person whose job this specifically is.

Some people hesitate to ask for a referral, as though it were an escalation that might offend their GP or mark them out as a difficult patient. Let me put that worry to rest. Referral is not a complaint about your GP; it is the system working as designed. General practice handles the common path well, and a straightforward H. pylori infection that clears on first treatment is firmly within that path. But an infection that has resisted one or more courses has, by definition, stepped off the common path and onto the specialist one — and matching the right level of care to the difficulty of the problem is exactly what referral exists to do. A good GP will often suggest it before you have to ask. If yours does not, asking is not awkward; it is simply moving your problem to the right desk. You would not feel awkward asking to see a specialist for any other persistent, treatment-resistant condition, and this is no different.

It is also worth understanding what the specialist brings that makes the referral worthwhile, so the step feels like progress rather than just more waiting. A gastroenterologist sees difficult H. pylori cases regularly, is fluent in the rescue regimens we are about to discuss, can arrange and interpret susceptibility testing, and can weigh the rescue options against your particular history in a way that a generalist managing a thousand other conditions reasonably cannot. You are not just being passed along. You are being moved to the person with the most relevant tools and the most practice using them. For a stubborn infection, that is precisely where you want to be.

### Step three: the rescue regimens, explained

These are the combinations that exist for difficult-to-treat infections — the ones the field has developed precisely so that "still positive after standard treatment" does not mean "untreatable." I am describing them so you understand that they exist and roughly what they involve, not so you can request a specific one off a menu. Which one fits you is a clinical judgement.

The first is **rifabutin-based rescue**. A documented regimen pairs rifabutin at 150 mg with amoxicillin at 1 gram and a proton pump inhibitor, taken twice daily for fourteen days. Rifabutin is an antibiotic that H. pylori has had relatively little exposure to, which is much of the point — resistance to it is uncommon, so it can work where the more heavily used drugs have failed. It is established as an option for difficult-to-treat patients, a genuine tool held in reserve for exactly the situation you may be in. Your doctor prescribes this, supervises it, and decides whether it is right for you; the book simply tells you it is on the table.

The second is **furazolidone-based rescue**, and this one carries a piece of information that I think every repeatedly failed patient deserves to hear, because of what it does to the feeling of hopelessness. Furazolidone-based regimens have been documented to achieve eradication even after as many as five previous failed attempts, in infections caused by multi-resistant strains. Five failures. Let that land. The existence of a regimen that has cleared infections after five prior failures means that even the most stubborn, most discouraging case in this book's likely readership is not a closed door. People who had failed five times got better. The pathway did not run out. Again — this is a specialist's regimen, prescribed and supervised, not something to self-administer; but its existence is the proof that "out of options" is almost never actually true.

The reason I am giving you the names and even the doses is not so you can play doctor. It is so that when a gastroenterologist mentions rifabutin or furazolidone, the words are not frightening unfamiliar territory — so you can recognise them, ask sensible questions about them, and understand that you are being offered a known, evidence-based path rather than a desperate experiment. Informed patients have better conversations. That is the entire purpose.

There is a particular kind of fear that takes hold when treatment keeps failing, and it is the fear that you are being improvised at — that each new prescription is a guess, that nobody really knows what to do with you, that you have wandered off the edge of the map into territory where the doctors are just trying things. Knowing that named, studied rescue regimens exist is the antidote to exactly that fear. You are not off the map. The map extends well past where you are standing. The rescue pathway is a charted, researched, written-down sequence that the field developed precisely because infections like yours were always going to happen. When a specialist reaches for rifabutin or furazolidone, they are not improvising; they are following a route that other patients have walked to a cure before you. That is a very different feeling from being experimented on, and it happens to be the accurate one.

The honest message running underneath all of this — and it is the message stat after stat in this chapter quietly supports — is that "difficult to treat" is not the same as "impossible to treat." Difficult means it takes a specialist, the right tests, and possibly more than one further attempt. It does not mean the infection is unbeatable. The whole structure of the rescue pathway, all the way down to a regimen documented to work after five prior failures, exists to make exactly that point: the difficulty is real, and so is the path through it.

### Why these particular drugs work where others failed

It is worth understanding, in plain terms, why the rescue regimens succeed against infections that defeated standard treatment, because the logic is reassuring once you see it. The standard antibiotics — clarithromycin, levofloxacin, and the others used first — have been prescribed enormously, for H. pylori and for many other infections, over decades. All that use gave the bacteria extensive exposure and, in many places, the opportunity to develop resistance. The rescue drugs are different precisely because they have been used far less against this bacterium. Rifabutin, for instance, has historically been reserved for other purposes, so H. pylori has had little chance to learn to survive it — which is exactly why it can work when the heavily used drugs cannot. The rescue pathway, in other words, deliberately reaches for the drugs the bacteria have not been trained against.

This is also why the rescue regimens are held in reserve rather than used first. If everyone received rifabutin up front, the bacteria would gradually develop resistance to it too, and the field would lose one of its most valuable backstops. Keeping these drugs in reserve preserves their effectiveness for the people who genuinely need them — people like you, if you have already failed the standard options. So when a specialist reaches for a rescue regimen in your case, they are not scraping the bottom of the barrel. They are opening a carefully preserved reserve, kept effective precisely for difficult infections like yours. That is a far more hopeful framing than "we've run out of the usual options," and it is the accurate one.

### Why the regional resistance numbers matter to you

You may come across, or your specialist may mention, resistance figures that sound alarming — metronidazole resistance reported around 43 percent in some regional studies, levofloxacin resistance around 34 percent in others. I want to put those in context so they inform you rather than frighten you.

These are regional study figures, which means they describe particular populations and places, and they vary considerably from one region to another. They are not a verdict on your personal strain. What they explain is *why* susceptibility testing and thoughtful regimen choice matter so much — because resistance to some of the older, more heavily used drugs is genuinely common in some places, which is exactly why the rescue pathway leans on drugs like rifabutin that the bacteria have seen less of. The high regional resistance to some antibiotics is the reason the rescue regimens are built around different ones. The numbers are not there to scare you. They are the logic behind the plan.

### What to actually do with this chapter

If you are reading this from inside a failed treatment, here is the concrete shape of your next move, none of which involves a pharmacy you walk into alone.

Ask for a referral to a gastroenterologist if you are not already under one. Ask whether susceptibility testing is possible before the next regimen is chosen. Go in knowing that bismuth quadruple therapy, rifabutin-based rescue, and furazolidone-based rescue exist, so that when these are discussed you are an informed participant rather than a frightened bystander. And carry with you the single most important fact in this chapter: a regimen exists that has cleared this infection after five prior failures, which means your case, however discouraging it feels tonight, is very unlikely to be the unwinnable one.

Let me say one more thing to the reader who has failed not once but twice, or more, because you are carrying the heaviest version of this fear and you deserve the most direct answer. Multiple failures do not move you toward "untreatable." They move you toward "needs the specialised end of the pathway" — susceptibility testing, a gastroenterologist, the rescue regimens held in reserve for exactly your situation. The existence of a furazolidone-based regimen documented to clear infections after five prior failures is not a footnote; it is a message addressed specifically to you. It says that the people who designed this pathway anticipated patients who had failed many times and built a route through anyway. You have not exhausted medicine. You have reached the part of medicine that was built for difficult cases, which is a different and far more hopeful thing.

The fear that there is nothing left to try is, for almost everyone who has it, simply not true. There is a pathway. It has steps. It has named, evidence-based regimens at each stage. It belongs in a specialist's hands, and getting it into those hands is the move. You are not at the end of the road. You are at the part of the road where you need a better map and a better-qualified guide — and you now know to ask for both.
