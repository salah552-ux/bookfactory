# Chapter 2

## The Cancer Question, Answered With the Real Numbers

> A brief note before this chapter. It deals directly with cancer risk, calmly and with real numbers. If you are in an especially raw moment, you can read it later — but it is here precisely to take the fear apart, so when you are ready, this is the chapter that does it.

Let's say the thing out loud, because pretending it isn't there has never helped anyone. The question underneath all the others, the one that sent you to your phone in the first place, is this: *am I going to get cancer?*

You have been carrying that question around since the diagnosis. Maybe you have not said it to anyone. Maybe you said it to your doctor and got a wave of the hand and a "let's not get ahead of ourselves." So you took it home and you fed it into a search engine instead, and the search engine, which has no bedside manner and no idea who you are, gave you the scariest version of the truth with none of the context that makes it bearable.

This chapter is the context. We are going to look straight at the frightening numbers — not flinch from them, not bury them — and then we are going to put the other numbers next to them, the ones the headline left out. By the end you will not have to choose between "it's nothing" and "it's a death sentence," because the truth is neither, and the truth is more useful than both.

---

### Why your fear is the correct starting point

Before a single statistic, this: your fear is not an overreaction, and it is not a character flaw. It is a sane response to real information delivered badly.

People who test positive for H. pylori show measurably higher anxiety than people who test negative. That is not a guess or a sympathetic generalisation — it has been measured, and the difference is significant. So if you have felt more on edge since the diagnosis, more likely to scan your body for new symptoms, more prone to the 2am spiral, you are reacting exactly the way the research predicts. You are normal. The problem was never your reaction. The problem was being handed a cancer-linked diagnosis and then left alone with it.

So we are not going to talk you out of the fear. We are going to give it the one thing that actually dissolves it. Information, in proportion.

There is a particular cruelty in how this fear gets installed. You did the responsible thing — you noticed symptoms, you got tested, you took the diagnosis seriously. And the reward for being responsible was a piece of information delivered with no context, at the worst possible time of day, by a source that profits from your clicks rather than your calm. The same diagnosis, delivered by an unhurried doctor with twenty minutes and a whiteboard, would have frightened you a fraction as much. The fear you are carrying is not really proportional to the risk. It is proportional to the *way you found out*. That distinction matters, because it means the fear can be brought down to its proper size without denying anything true — simply by supplying the context that was missing the first time.

Notice, too, that the anxiety feeds itself. A frightened mind goes looking for confirmation, and the internet always has more frightening material to supply, so one search becomes ten, and each one tightens the knot. This is not weakness; it is how anxiety works in everyone. The way out is not more searching. It is the right information, once, properly framed — which is what the rest of this chapter is.

---

### The frightening number, stated plainly

Here is the part the headline got right, because you deserve to hear it confirmed rather than fudged.

H. pylori is the single biggest cause of stomach cancer in the world. Around 76 percent of the stomach cancers projected to occur in recent generations are attributable to this infection — roughly three out of four. That is a real figure, drawn from work published in *Nature Medicine* in September 2025 and reflected in the World Health Organization's cancer-agency reporting the same year. The same analysis projects something on the order of 15.6 million lifetime stomach-cancer cases across the people born between 2008 and 2017 worldwide, the large majority of them linked to this bacterium.

And it is classified, formally, as a Group 1 carcinogen. That is the top tier — the category for things proven to cause cancer in humans, the same bracket as tobacco smoke. Your search engine did not invent that. It is true.

I am not going to soften any of that, because you would not trust me if I did. You came here for the real numbers, and those are the real numbers, and they are the reason your doctor wants this infection gone rather than ignored.

Now let me show you everything that sentence leaves out.

---

### The number the headline left out

Read this next part slowly, because it is the hinge the whole chapter turns on.

"H. pylori causes three out of four stomach cancers" and "you are very unlikely to get stomach cancer" are both true at the same time. They are not in tension. They are answers to two completely different questions.

The 76 percent figure answers a question about *populations*. Out of all the stomach cancers that happen, what share trace back to this bug? Three-quarters. That is a statement about the disease, not about you. It tells you what causes stomach cancer when stomach cancer occurs. It says nothing, on its own, about how likely it is to occur in any one person who carries the infection — and that second question is the one you actually care about.

So here is the second number, the one that answers *your* question. When researchers pooled the trials measuring what eradication actually does, treating the infection reduced the risk of stomach cancer with a relative risk of 0.61 — a meaningful drop. But the figure that should change how you breathe tonight is this one: the number needed to treat is roughly 332. That means you would have to successfully clear the infection in about 332 people to prevent a single case of stomach cancer.

Sit with that for a second, because it is doing a lot of quiet work. It tells you that out of every several hundred people carrying H. pylori, the overwhelming majority were never going to develop stomach cancer in the first place. The infection raises the risk — really, genuinely raises it, which is why clearing it is worth doing — and the absolute risk it raises, for any one individual, still starts from a low base and stays low.

Both things are true. The risk is real enough to treat. It is low enough not to panic.

Let me make the number needed to treat even more concrete, because it is the single most powerful fact in this book and it deserves to be felt, not just read. Picture a large school hall filled with people, all of them carrying H. pylori, all of them — let's say several hundred of them, a real crowd. Now imagine successfully clearing the infection in every single one. For all that effort, across that whole crowd, you would expect to prevent roughly one case of stomach cancer. One. Which means that in that entire hall of infected people, the overwhelming majority were never going to develop stomach cancer whether they were treated or not. The infection raised their risk a little; for almost all of them, a little more than a small number is still a small number. You are one face in that hall. The odds were always on your side, and treatment nudges them further in your favour.

That is what an NNT of 332 actually means in human terms. It is not a cold statistic. It is a crowd of people, almost none of whom were ever in real danger, and you standing among them.

There is a second thing the figure quietly tells you, and it is worth drawing out. If clearing the infection prevented stomach cancer in a large share of carriers, the number needed to treat would be small — you would only need to treat a handful of people to prevent each case. The fact that you have to treat hundreds tells you, directly, that stomach cancer is an uncommon outcome of carrying H. pylori. The size of the NNT is itself the reassurance. A big number needed to treat is the mathematical fingerprint of a low individual risk.

### Holding both numbers at once

If your mind is trying to collapse those two facts into one — to decide it must *really* be either "dangerous" or "fine" — that instinct is understandable, and it is exactly the instinct the search engine exploited. Headlines need one emotion. Reality holds two.

Think of it the way you already think about other risks without losing sleep. Driving a car raises your risk of dying in a crash, measurably, every time you get in. The risk is real — real enough that you wear a seatbelt, real enough that car safety is a serious field. And yet you do not sit in your driveway paralysed, because the absolute odds on any given trip are low. You treat the risk seriously *and* you live your life. You wear the seatbelt and you drive to work.

Treating your H. pylori is the seatbelt. It is the sensible, proportionate response to a real risk. It is not an emergency evacuation, because there is no fire. It is the seatbelt, and you are about to put it on, and then you are going to drive to work.

That is what proportion feels like. Not denial, not panic. Action without dread.

If the seatbelt comparison feels too neat, try another one that sits even closer to home. You almost certainly know that prolonged sun exposure raises the risk of skin cancer — it is, like H. pylori, a genuine, established cause of a genuine cancer. And yet you do not refuse to ever go outside. You wear sunscreen, you avoid burning, you take the risk seriously in proportion, and then you live in the daylight like everyone else. Nobody spends their life indoors in terror of the sun, even though the sun causes cancer, because somewhere along the way the risk got framed properly and folded into ordinary life. H. pylori deserves exactly the same treatment in your mind. It is a real cause of a real cancer, you are going to take the sensible precaution — clearing it — and then you are going to go back out into the daylight. The precaution is the point. The terror was never warranted.

Here is why the framing slips so easily into panic, so you can catch it when it happens. The mind is very bad at telling the difference between "this causes cancer" and "this will give me cancer." Those two sentences feel almost identical at 2am, but they are worlds apart. Tobacco causes lung cancer — and most people who have ever smoked do not die of lung cancer. The sun causes skin cancer — and most sun exposure harms no one. H. pylori causes stomach cancer — and most people who carry it never develop it. "Causes" is a statement about what is *capable* of leading to a disease. It is not a prophecy about you. Once you can hear the difference between those two sentences, half the fear simply falls away, because you realise the headline was speaking the first sentence and your fear heard the second.

### Why treating it is genuinely worth doing

I want to be careful here, because there is a lazy version of reassurance that tips over into "so it doesn't matter," and that version is wrong and I will not give it to you.

It matters. The reason the medical world takes H. pylori seriously, the reason the guidelines say to find it and clear it, is that eradication is one of the genuinely effective things you can do to lower stomach-cancer risk. That relative-risk drop is real. You are not being treated for nothing. You are doing the single most useful thing a person can do about this particular risk, and you are doing it at the easy end of the process — clearing a treatable infection — rather than the hard end.

This is the good news inside the scary news. The thing that causes the risk is the same thing you are about to remove. The cause and the cure are the same target. That is not true of most cancers, where the causes are tangled and the prevention is vague. Here it is unusually clean: there is a known cause, it is sitting in your stomach, and it can be cleared. You happen to have one of the most addressable cancer risks there is.

Think about how unusual that actually is. For most of the things that frighten people about cancer, there is nothing concrete to do — you cannot remove your genes, you cannot undo decades of living, you cannot point to a single thing and say "that is the cause, take it out." Prevention, for most cancers, is a fog of "eat well, exercise, hope." H. pylori is the rare exception where prevention is a specific, finishable action. There is a named cause. It is reachable. A two-week course of medicine can clear it. You are not being asked to wage a vague, lifelong, anxious campaign against an invisible threat. You are being asked to do one concrete thing, once, and then confirm it worked. That is about the most favourable position a person can be in with respect to a cancer risk, and you are in it.

So when the fear whispers that you are unlucky to have found this, turn it around. You are, if anything, fortunate — fortunate that the thing raising your risk announced itself with symptoms, fortunate that it was found, and fortunate that it belongs to the small category of cancer risks you can actually do something definitive about. The diagnosis did not hand you a danger. It handed you a chance to remove one.

### What about the symptoms I already have?

Here is a fear that often hides underneath the cancer fear, so let's pull it into the light. *If this thing causes cancer, does my burning and gnawing mean the damage is already happening?*

No. The burning and gnawing is gastritis — an inflamed, irritated stomach lining, exactly as we mapped out in the last chapter. It is uncomfortable and it deserves treatment, but it is not cancer, and it is not the early flicker of cancer. The progression from chronic inflammation toward anything dangerous unfolds over many years and usually decades, through slow stages, in a minority of people, and clearing the infection is what stops that progression before it starts. Your symptoms are a reason to treat the infection. They are not evidence that time has run out. Time has not run out. You are catching this at the stage where catching it does the most good.

There are, however, specific symptoms that always warrant prompt medical attention regardless of this reassurance, and you should know them plainly: blood in your stool or vomit, black or tarry stools, vomit that looks like coffee grounds, unexplained weight loss, difficulty swallowing, or persistent vomiting. Those are not part of ordinary gastritis, and they need a doctor's eyes without delay. For most people reading this, they will never appear. But you should know where the line is, so that you can stop scanning for vague catastrophe and watch for the specific things that actually matter.

Naming those warning signs precisely is, oddly, one of the most calming things this chapter can do for you. Vague fear is the worst kind, because it attaches to everything — every twinge becomes a possible catastrophe, every ordinary ache gets interpreted as the beginning of the end. When you know exactly which specific signs would genuinely warrant urgent attention, you can stop treating every sensation in your abdomen as a verdict. The burning you feel is gastritis. The fullness is gastritis. The ordinary discomfort of an inflamed stomach is gastritis. None of it is on the warning list, and the warning list is short and specific and almost certainly never going to apply to you. You can put down the constant self-monitoring. You know what to watch for, and it is not the thing you have been feeling.

### Why your doctor seemed so calm

One more thing, because it has probably been nagging at you. If H. pylori is a Group 1 carcinogen behind three-quarters of stomach cancers, why did your doctor seem so relaxed about it? Why the wave of the hand, the "don't worry"?

The answer is not that your doctor was careless. It is that your doctor was holding both numbers at once, automatically, the way you are now learning to. To someone who treats this routinely, H. pylori is a common, treatable infection with a low individual cancer risk and a clear protocol — genuinely not an emergency, genuinely manageable. The calm was real and warranted. The problem was only that the calm was never explained. "Don't worry" is true, but it is useless without the reasoning, because you cannot simply switch off a fear on command. Your doctor had the proportion in their head and forgot to hand it to you. This chapter is the explanation that should have come with the reassurance. Now that you have it, the calm your doctor felt can finally be yours too — not as an instruction to obey, but as a conclusion you have reached for yourself, from the actual numbers.

### The proportion you get to keep

Let me put the whole chapter into a single frame you can carry out of here.

You are one person. The 76 percent figure was never about you — it described a disease across whole populations. The number that describes your situation is the one that says it takes clearing this infection in hundreds of people to prevent one cancer, which means your personal odds, even untreated, started low, and you are about to lower them further by doing the obvious sensible thing. You are not the unlucky one in the headline. You are the ordinary person quietly removing a risk before it has a chance to matter.

The fear you walked in with was not irrational. It was a real risk, described without its context, at 2am, by a machine that does not know you. Now you have the context. The risk is real enough to treat — so you will treat it. It is low enough not to panic — so you can stop bracing for the worst.

That is the proportion. Hold onto it. The rest of this book is about the treating part — what actually works now, how to get through it, and exactly what to do if it doesn't work the first time. The fear has had its chapter. From here on, we deal in plans.
