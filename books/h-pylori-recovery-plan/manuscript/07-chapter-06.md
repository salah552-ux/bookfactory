# Chapter 6

## The Stomach-Lining Diet

*What am I allowed to eat?*

You have probably typed some version of that question into a search bar by now, and what came back was almost certainly contradictory, alarming, and weirdly specific. Endless lists of forbidden foods. Miracle foods that "kill H. pylori." Elimination diets so punishing they read like penance. You closed the tab more confused than when you opened it, and a little hungry, and not sure whether you were allowed the toast.

So let me set the frame honestly before we go near a single food, because the frame is what makes everything else make sense. No diet clears H. pylori. The infection is cleared by antibiotics, prescribed and supervised by your doctor — that is the biology, and no plate of food changes it. What the right way of eating *can* do is real but more modest: it can reduce the symptom load on an inflamed stomach, give an irritated lining the quiet it needs to settle, and avoid the things that independently make stomach trouble worse. That is worth a great deal when you are in pain. It is just not a cure, and anyone selling it as one is selling.

With that settled, here is what actually helps — and, more importantly, why.

---

### Start with why, not with a list

The reason most H. pylori diet advice fails you is that it hands you a list of rules with no reasons, so the moment a rule is inconvenient you abandon it, and the moment two lists disagree you are lost. Reasons are sturdier than rules. So let's understand the mechanism first, and the food choices will follow almost on their own.

Your stomach lining, right now, is inflamed. That is the gastritis we mapped out earlier — a raw, oversensitive surface, irritated by the infection and by your immune system's response to it. An inflamed surface behaves like any other inflamed surface on your body. Think of a graze on your knee, or a patch of sunburn. It is fine if you leave it alone. It complains loudly the moment something rough, acidic, or harsh touches it.

So the entire logic of eating well during this period is simple: stop irritating the inflamed surface, and stop pouring fuel on the inflammation. Everything below is just that single idea, applied to actual food. You are not on a cure diet. You are giving a raw surface the conditions to quiet down.

This single principle is also your defence against every contradictory list you will ever encounter. The reason the online advice is so confusing is that it presents food as a set of arbitrary commandments — this is "good," that is "bad" — with no underlying reason, so the lists multiply and disagree and leave you paralysed. But once you hold the principle, you can reason about any food for yourself. You do not need a list that happens to include the exact thing on your plate. You need to ask one question: *is this likely to irritate a raw, inflamed surface, or to feed the inflammation?* If the answer is yes, ease off it for now. If the answer is no, it is probably fine. That question turns you from someone anxiously consulting lists into someone who actually understands their own stomach — which is the entire difference this book is trying to make, applied here to dinner.

---

### What to reduce, and the honest reason for each

Some of what follows is about your comfort right now — the foods that worsen the burning and gnawing. Some of it is about longer-term stomach health, and I will be straight with you about which is which, because lumping them together is how diet advice turns into fear.

**Very salty foods, and cured or smoked meats.** Here is where I need to be careful and exact. Diets very high in salt and in cured and smoked meats carry an independent association with stomach-cancer risk — that is, separate from H. pylori, these foods have been linked to higher risk over the long term. I am telling you that as a plain fact, not as a scare. It is not that a salty meal is dangerous, or that you have done damage by eating bacon. It is that, over years, a pattern heavy in these foods is one of the things stomach-health guidance suggests easing off. Given that you are already addressing your stomach's health, gently reducing the very saltiest and the most heavily cured and smoked foods is a sensible, low-cost adjustment. Calm, not fear.

**Excess coffee and alcohol.** Both can stimulate acid and irritate an already-raw lining. "Excess" is the operative word — this is about easing off, especially while symptoms are active, not necessarily about lifelong total abstinence. If your morning coffee on an empty stomach reliably sets off the burning, that is your stomach telling you something useful. Listen to it for now.

**Highly acidic and spicy foods, if they trigger you.** This one is personal, and that is why the lists you found online were so contradictory — they tried to make a universal rule out of something individual. Some people find citrus, tomatoes, vinegary things, or spicy food set their symptoms off; others tolerate them fine. The principle is not "these foods are bad." The principle is: if a food reliably makes your burning or gnawing worse, ease off it while your lining is inflamed, and you can reintroduce it as things settle. You are allowed to be the expert on your own stomach here.

Notice what this is not. It is not a long forbidden list to memorise in fear. It is four reasonable adjustments, each with a clear reason, most of them temporary, all of them in the service of giving an inflamed surface some peace.

The distinction between "temporary while symptomatic" and "sensible long-term" is one the online lists almost never make, and it causes a great deal of unnecessary misery, so let me draw it clearly. The acidic and spicy foods, the coffee on an empty stomach — these are mostly about your comfort *right now*, while the lining is inflamed. As things settle, especially once the infection is cleared, you can reasonably test reintroducing them and keep whatever does not bother you. They are not banned for life; they are paused while your stomach is raw. The salt and the heavily cured and smoked meats are a slightly different category — there the gentle reduction is a sensible long-term habit for stomach health generally, not just a symptomatic measure. Keeping these two categories straight saves you from either of two mistakes: treating a temporary comfort measure as a life sentence, or dismissing a sensible long-term adjustment as something you can drop the moment you feel better. Know which is which, and the whole framework becomes livable rather than oppressive.

---

### What to support, and why it helps

The other half of eating well is not about avoidance at all. It is about choosing foods that are easy on the lining and that support a stomach trying to settle.

**Easily digestible proteins.** Your body needs protein to repair tissue, and an inflamed stomach copes better with proteins that are gentle and simple to process — well-cooked, not heavily fried, not buried in rich sauces. Think eggs, fish, tender poultry, pulses if you tolerate them. The goal is nourishment without making the stomach work hard or sit heavy.

**Cooked vegetables.** Cooking softens vegetables and makes them far gentler on a raw lining than raw, fibrous, scratchy versions. A pile of raw salad can be surprisingly hard on an irritated stomach; the same vegetables, cooked soft, are soothing and easy. This is not forever — it is for now, while things are inflamed.

**Soluble fibre.** The gentle, soft kind of fibre — found in things like oats, cooked carrots, bananas — adds bulk and supports steady digestion without the scratchiness of coarse, insoluble fibre. It is the kind of fibre that helps things move smoothly without abrading the surface.

**Fermented foods, in small amounts, if you tolerate them.** A little yoghurt or other fermented food can support the broader gut, particularly relevant if you have been on antibiotics that disturbed it. Keep it modest and tolerance-led — some people find fermented or acidic foods irritating while symptoms are active, so this is a "small amounts, see how you feel" suggestion, not a mandate.

**Hydration, spaced around meals.** Staying well hydrated supports the whole system, and spacing fluids around meals rather than flooding the stomach all at once tends to sit more comfortably. Small, steady, gentle — the theme of the whole chapter, applied to water.

Notice that the "support" list is not a list of magic stomach-healing foods, and I want to be clear about that, because the internet is full of foods supposedly anointed with special power against H. pylori. There is no food that heals your lining the way the antibiotics clear the infection. What these foods do is gentler and more honest: they nourish you without irritating a raw surface, they are easy for an inflamed stomach to handle, and they keep you eating well at a time when fear of food can quietly push people toward eating badly or barely at all. That is their entire job, and it is a worthwhile one. A well-nourished body recovering from an infection is in a better position than an anxious, under-fed one living on toast and worry. The support foods are not medicine. They are simply the kind, sensible way to keep eating while your stomach settles.

The other quiet benefit of choosing gentle, easily managed foods is that it removes a layer of daily anxiety. When your stomach has been hurting, mealtimes can become fraught — a small gamble three times a day, each one carrying the question "will this set it off?" Leaning toward the calm, cooked, simple options for a while takes most of the gamble out of it. You are not constantly testing your luck. You are eating in a way that is very unlikely to provoke a flare, which lets you stop bracing around food and gives the lining a steadier, calmer environment to settle in. Predictability, here, is its own kind of relief.

### A word about the foods sold as "H. pylori killers"

You will have seen, in your searching, specific foods promoted as natural weapons against the bacterium — certain plants, sprouts, oils, and so on, each with a confident claim that it fights or kills H. pylori. I am going to deal with the evidence on a couple of these properly in the next chapter, on adjuncts, because some of them do have a real if modest evidence base. But within a chapter about *diet*, I want to draw a clean line, because the line is what keeps you sane.

There is a difference between eating to settle an inflamed stomach — which is what this chapter is about, and which genuinely helps your comfort — and eating to kill the infection, which food essentially does not do at any meaningful level on its own. When a food is sold as a "natural H. pylori killer," it is borrowing the language of treatment for something that, at best, belongs in the modest-adjunct category and, at worst, has no good evidence at all. So as you read about foods online, keep the two questions separate. "Will this soothe my symptoms?" is a fair question and this chapter answers it. "Will this clear my infection?" has, for food, essentially one honest answer: no, the antibiotics do that. Keeping those two questions apart protects you from the most expensive mistake in this whole area — eating hopefully instead of treating properly.

### How to eat, not just what

The pattern of eating often matters as much as the contents, and this connects straight back to the mechanism we started with.

Remember why the pain is worse on an empty stomach: with no food to buffer it, acid sits directly against the inflamed lining. The practical answer is smaller, more frequent meals rather than a couple of large ones with long, empty, acidic gaps between them. Keeping a little something in the stomach more regularly means the lining is less often left exposed to unbuffered acid. You are smoothing out the peaks and troughs.

Eating slowly and not overfilling the stomach helps too, because an inflamed stomach that empties sluggishly does not cope well with being stuffed. Large meals sit heavy and worsen the bloating and fullness. Gentle and frequent beats large and infrequent, almost every time, for a stomach in this state.

And try not to eat your largest meal right before lying down. Lying flat with a full, acidic stomach makes reflux and discomfort more likely. Giving yourself a window upright after eating is a small, free adjustment that many people find takes the edge off their evening symptoms.

A word about the emotional side of all this, because food is never only about food. Being told your stomach is inflamed and that certain foods irritate it can tip quickly into a kind of fearful, joyless eating — every meal a minefield, every craving a guilt trip, the whole pleasure of eating drained out of it. That is not the goal here, and it is not necessary. The adjustments in this chapter are temporary, modest, and led by your own comfort, not by a moral code about "clean" eating. You have not failed if you have a coffee, or a slice of pizza, or a glass of wine. You are simply managing an inflamed surface for a while, and managing it sensibly, not perfectly. The aim is a calmer stomach and a normal life, not a punishing regime you white-knuckle through. If a particular food reliably hurts, ease off it; if it doesn't, enjoy it. That is the whole of the discipline required, and it is meant to be livable.

---

### The honest limits of all this

I want to close by holding the line I opened with, because it is what separates this chapter from the marketing you have been wading through.

None of this heals your ulcer. None of this cures your gastritis. None of this clears the infection. What it does is reduce the load on an inflamed stomach so you are more comfortable, support a lining that is trying to settle, and ease off the few foods that carry an independent long-term association with stomach trouble. Those are genuine, worthwhile things. They are also honest things — modest, mechanism-based, and free of the magical thinking that surrounds food and this infection everywhere you look.

The real healing of your stomach lining follows the clearing of the infection. Remove the bacterium, the inflammation settles, and the lining gets its genuine chance to recover — and eating this way supports that process and keeps you more comfortable while it happens. Food is the supportive cast here, not the lead. That is the truth, it is less exciting than a miracle diet, and it is the version that will actually serve you.

I labour this point because the alternative does real harm, and you have probably already felt its pull. When food is sold as the cure, two bad things follow. First, people delay or avoid the actual treatment — the antibiotics that genuinely clear the infection — in favour of a diet that cannot, losing time while the infection keeps inflaming their stomach. Second, even people who do get treated end up burdened with a punishing, fear-soaked relationship with eating, convinced that one wrong meal will undo everything, that food is a constant test they keep failing. Both of those are unnecessary and both flow from the same lie: that the diet is doing the curing. Once you hold the honest version — that food supports your comfort and the antibiotics clear the infection — both burdens lift. You can get proper treatment without guilt about not having "fixed it with food," and you can eat sensibly without terror, because no single meal carries the weight the miracle-diet story put on it. The honest framing is not just more accurate. It is kinder, and it is safer.

Before we close, a brief word on listening to your own body, because it is the most reliable guide you have and the one the lists ignore. Your stomach will tell you, fairly clearly, what it tolerates and what it does not — a food that reliably brings on the burning is giving you real information, and a food that sits comfortably is giving you real information too. The general principles in this chapter are the starting framework, but you are the one running the experiment in your own body, and your observations override any generic list. Keep a loose mental note of what consistently bothers you and what does not, ease off the former while your lining is inflamed, and trust the latter. This is not about rigid rules imposed from outside; it is about paying attention to a stomach that is, in its own way, telling you what it needs. Over a week or two you will build up a personal sense of your own triggers that is worth more than any article you could find online.

You do not need to eat in fear, you do not need a punishing elimination regime, and you are, in fact, allowed the toast — ideally with something gentle on it, not on a completely empty stomach, and not doused in chilli while your lining is raw. Reasonable, kind, mechanism-led eating. That is the whole of it.

> **What this means for you**
>
> - No diet clears H. pylori — antibiotics do. Eating well can reduce symptom load, soothe an inflamed lining, and ease off foods with independent long-term risk. That's worthwhile, but it's support, not cure.
> - Reduce (mostly temporarily): very salty foods and cured/smoked meats (an independent long-term stomach-cancer association — stated calmly, not as a scare), excess coffee and alcohol, and any acidic or spicy foods that personally trigger your burning.
> - Support: easily digestible proteins, cooked (not raw) vegetables, soluble fibre, small amounts of fermented food if tolerated, and steady hydration spaced around meals.
> - Eat smaller and more often so acid is rarely left sitting against an empty, inflamed stomach — and don't lie down straight after your largest meal.
> - The lining truly heals once the infection is cleared. Food keeps you comfortable and supports that process; it isn't the cure.
