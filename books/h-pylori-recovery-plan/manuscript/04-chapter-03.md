# Chapter 3

## The Treatment That Actually Works

> A brief note before this chapter. It describes the medicines used to clear H. pylori so you can understand them and discuss them. It does not prescribe them. The drugs and doses that are right for you are chosen and supervised by your doctor, who knows your history, your allergies, and what you have already taken.

There is a reasonable chance you were given the wrong treatment. Not negligently, not maliciously — just out of date. And if your re-test came back positive, or your symptoms came crawling back, the explanation may be sitting in that single fact.

That sentence is going to sound alarming for about thirty seconds, and then, once I explain it, it is going to be one of the most useful things you learn in this book. Because if part of the reason your first round failed is that it was the old recipe rather than the current one, then the fix is not mysterious. The fix is knowing what the current recipe is, so you can walk into your next appointment and have an informed conversation about it. That is what this chapter hands you.

Let's start with what changed, and why.

---

### The old standard, and why it stopped working

For a long time — years, the better part of two decades — the standard first treatment for H. pylori was something called clarithromycin triple therapy. Three medicines together: a proton pump inhibitor to lower stomach acid, plus two antibiotics, one of which was clarithromycin. If you were handed a week of tablets and told it would clear things up, there is a good chance this, or something close to it, is what you got. It was the default for so long that many doctors reach for it on muscle memory.

The trouble is that the bacteria changed faster than the prescriptions did.

Antibiotics work by killing bacteria, but bacteria are not passive. Over millions of treatment courses worldwide, the strains of H. pylori that happened to survive clarithromycin passed on whatever let them survive, and their descendants spread. This is resistance, and it is not a moral failing of anyone involved — it is simply what happens when you use an antibiotic widely for long enough. The drug that worked beautifully in 2005 works far less reliably now, because the population of bacteria it faces is not the same population.

How much resistance? In the United States, clarithromycin resistance now runs somewhere between about 22 and 31 percent depending on the study and the region. Roughly a quarter to a third of infections are carrying a bug that the headline antibiotic in the old standard simply does not reliably kill. When the central drug in your regimen fails to work in that many people, the regimen as a whole starts failing in a lot of people. That is the mechanism behind a great deal of "I took the tablets and I'm still positive."

So the bacterium got tougher, the old recipe got weaker, and for a while the prescriptions did not catch up. Now they have.

It helps to understand *why* resistance makes a whole regimen fail, rather than just weaken it a bit, because the logic is not obvious. Clarithromycin triple therapy leaned heavily on clarithromycin as its main weapon against the bacteria. The other components supported it, but clarithromycin did much of the killing. So when a strain is resistant to clarithromycin, you are not removing one of three equal soldiers — you are removing the one carrying most of the firepower, and leaving the rest to do a job they were never strong enough to do alone. That is why a treatment can fail outright in someone with a resistant strain rather than just work a little less well. The regimen was built around a drug the bacteria had learned to survive. This, more than anything, is why the guidelines moved toward a combination that does not depend so completely on any single antibiotic.

And here is the part that should land personally if your first round failed: there was no way for you to know any of this. You took the tablets you were given, exactly as told, and they did not work — not because you did anything wrong, but because the recipe itself had quietly become unreliable while no one updated the prescription. The failure was baked into the regimen before you ever swallowed a tablet. That is worth holding onto the next time the guilt creeps in. You did not fail the treatment. In a real sense, the treatment failed you.

---

### What the 2024 guideline actually says

In 2024, the American College of Gastroenterology — one of the bodies that sets the standard for how this infection should be treated — published updated guidance, and it made two changes that matter enormously to you.

First, it changed which treatment comes first. The preferred first-line treatment is now something called optimised bismuth quadruple therapy. Four components rather than three: a proton pump inhibitor to suppress acid, bismuth, an antibiotic from the nitroimidazole family such as metronidazole, and a tetracycline antibiotic. Four agents, working together, typically taken for around fourteen days. You will see this written as "BQT," and that is all the letters mean — bismuth quadruple therapy. The four-part combination is deliberately built to be harder for the bacteria to dodge, because hitting the infection from several directions at once leaves it far fewer ways to survive.

Second — and this is the part that may explain your own experience — the guideline removed clarithromycin triple therapy from its list of recommended first-line options altogether. It is no longer the default. The guidance now advises against reaching for clarithromycin-based regimens, and against levofloxacin-based ones too, unless testing has specifically confirmed that the particular strain you are carrying is still susceptible to them. In other words: do not gamble on the old drug in the dark. Either confirm it will work, or use something that does not depend on it.

Read those two changes together and a quiet story emerges. The treatment that was standard for years is the treatment that has been demoted, for exactly the reason it kept failing. If that was what you were given, your positive re-test is not a sign that your body is broken or that the infection is unbeatable. It may simply be a sign that you got the old recipe, and the current one is built to do better.

You might be wondering why four drugs instead of three solves the resistance problem, and the answer is worth a moment because it is genuinely clever. The trouble with leaning on a single key antibiotic — clarithromycin — was that the bacteria only had to defeat that one drug to survive the whole regimen. Bismuth quadruple therapy spreads the attack. Bismuth itself works against the bacteria in its own way, through several mechanisms that resistance does not easily overcome. The nitroimidazole and the tetracycline come at the infection from different directions again. So instead of one main weapon with a known weakness, you have four contributions working together, and a strain would have to defeat all of them at once to survive — a far taller order. This is the same principle behind combination treatment for other stubborn infections: hit the target from several angles so that resistance to any one angle is not enough. The four-part design is not just "more drugs for the sake of it." It is a deliberate answer to exactly the problem that sank the old regimen.

This also explains why the current treatment, while more of a faff to take, tends to be more reliable. The thoroughness that makes it slightly harder to swallow is the same thoroughness that makes it harder for the bacteria to escape. When you find yourself, partway through, resenting the sheer number of tablets, it can help to remember that the inconvenience and the effectiveness are the same thing seen from two sides. The reason it works better is the reason it is more of a chore. That is a trade worth making.

---

### Why this isn't your doctor's failing either

It would be easy to read all that as "my doctor messed up," and I want to steer you away from that, because it is neither fair nor useful.

Guidelines change, and they do not reach every clinic on the same day. A busy general practitioner managing hundreds of conditions cannot always have the very latest specialist guidance for every infection at their fingertips. The clarithromycin regimen was correct, standard, evidence-based practice for a very long time, and plenty of careful doctors were still prescribing it well after the ground had begun to shift. This is not a story about a bad doctor. It is a story about how medicine actually moves — unevenly, with a lag — and about why a reader who understands the current standard can be a genuinely useful participant in their own care.

You are not going to march in and correct anyone. You are going to walk in able to ask a good question: *I read that bismuth quadruple therapy is now the preferred first-line option and that clarithromycin triple therapy was removed — is that the right approach for me, especially as I'm still positive?* That is a different appointment than the one you had last time. That is the appointment where you are informed.

Consider what that single question actually accomplishes in the room. It signals, gently, that you understand the treatment landscape has moved — which invites your doctor to engage with the current guidance rather than reach for the default on autopilot. It frames the request around *you* and your situation, not around catching anyone out. And it puts the most relevant fact — that you are still positive, that the first attempt did not work — right at the centre, where it belongs, because a persistent infection genuinely changes the calculation. You are not being a difficult patient. You are being a useful one. The doctors who treat this well will welcome a patient who arrives with that level of understanding, because it makes the whole encounter more efficient and more likely to land on the right plan.

There is also a quiet confidence that comes from knowing the landscape, and it changes how the appointment feels from your side. The last time, you were a passenger — frightened, uninformed, accepting whatever you were handed. This time, you understand what good treatment looks like, so you can tell whether the conversation is heading somewhere sensible. That does not mean overriding your doctor; it means being able to participate, ask, and understand rather than simply nod and hope. The difference between those two experiences is enormous, and it is entirely a product of knowing what you now know.

### What good treatment actually looks like

So you have a sense of what to expect, here is the shape of a proper course — not as a protocol to follow, but as a picture to recognise.

It is more tablets than you might expect, and that surprises people. Four components, several of them taken multiple times a day, for around two weeks, can mean a genuinely fiddly daily routine — a handful in the morning, a handful at night, some of them with food, some on an empty stomach, the acid-suppressing tablet timed deliberately. It can feel like a lot. It is supposed to be a lot. The thoroughness is the point. A combination this comprehensive is what gives the bacteria the fewest places to hide, and the difference between a regimen that works and one that fails is very often the difference between taking it exactly as directed and taking it approximately.

The acid-suppressing tablet — the proton pump inhibitor, or PPI — deserves a special mention, because its timing is not arbitrary. These antibiotics work much better against H. pylori when stomach acid is kept low, and the PPI is what keeps it low. Taken correctly, usually before meals, it changes the environment in your stomach so the antibiotics can actually do their job. Taken haphazardly, it undermines the whole effort. This is why "I took most of them, more or less, around the right times" is not the same as completing the course, and it is a thread we will pull on properly in the next chapter, because getting through treatment without quitting is its own skill.

The length matters too. Around fourteen days is not padding. Shorter courses were tried, in the hope of making treatment easier to tolerate, and for the optimised quadruple regimen the longer course is what the evidence supports for reliable clearance. Finishing the whole thing, even when you feel better halfway through — and you may well feel better halfway through — is part of what makes it work.

It is worth naming, plainly, that this is a demanding treatment, because no one is served by pretending otherwise. A fortnight of several daily doses, some timed around meals, some not, with side effects that can be genuinely unpleasant, is a real commitment, and people who go in expecting "a quick course of tablets" can be blindsided by how much it asks of them. Knowing in advance that it is demanding is not discouraging — it is the opposite. The people most likely to abandon a course partway are the ones who expected it to be easy and were ambushed by how hard it was. The people most likely to finish are the ones who knew what they were signing up for and had braced for it. So brace for it. It is two weeks of real effort, it has a clear end date, and on the far side of it is the genuine prospect of clearing this for good. Going in clear-eyed about the difficulty is one of the best things you can do for your odds, and the whole next chapter is devoted to getting you through it.

---

### What "optimised" actually means

You may have noticed I keep saying "optimised" bismuth quadruple therapy, not just "bismuth quadruple therapy," and the word is doing real work, so let me unpack it. Bismuth quadruple therapy in some form has existed for a long time. What the 2024 guidance emphasises is doing it *properly* — at the right doses, for the right duration, with the acid suppression handled correctly — because an under-dosed or too-short version of the same four drugs does not perform nearly as well. The difference between a quadruple therapy that clears the infection and one that fails can be the difference between "optimised" and "approximate."

This matters to you for a practical reason. It means that "I'm on bismuth quadruple therapy" is not, by itself, a guarantee of the best odds — the details of how it is dosed and how long it runs matter too. You do not need to second-guess your doctor's prescribing, and you should not try. But it is another reason the "exactly how and when do I take each tablet, and for how long?" question is so valuable: getting the regimen *optimised* in practice, through correct dosing and full completion, is part of what makes the difference. The guidance did not just change which drugs; it underlined doing them right.

### The conversation this chapter is really for

Everything in this chapter points toward one appointment, so let me make the purpose of it explicit.

You now understand that the standard treatment changed in 2024, that the previously dominant regimen was demoted because of resistance, that the current preferred first-line approach is bismuth quadruple therapy, and that the old clarithromycin-based and levofloxacin-based regimens should not be used blind. You understand that the course is deliberately thorough, that the acid-suppressing tablet's timing is load-bearing, and that finishing it properly is half the battle. That is genuinely more than most people carry into the room.

What you do with it is not to self-prescribe — you cannot, and you should not want to, because your doctor knows things about you that no book does: your allergies, your kidney function, your other medicines, what you have already taken and how you tolerated it. What you do is bring informed questions, and let the prescriber do the prescribing. The book gives you the briefing. The doctor gives you the medicine. That division of labour is not a limitation of this book; it is the whole correct design of how this should work.

And if you are reading this already on the far side of a failed course — already re-tested, already positive again, already braced for the news that there is nothing left to try — hold on, because there is a great deal left to try, and the chapter that lays it out is coming. First, though, the practical matter of actually surviving the treatment in front of you, side effects and all, without giving up before it has had its chance.
