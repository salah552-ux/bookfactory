# Chapter 4

## Surviving Treatment: Side Effects, Adherence, and the Mistakes That Sabotage It

Nobody warns you properly about what the treatment feels like. They hand you the tablets, they say "you might get a bit of an upset stomach," and then on day four you are sitting on the bathroom floor with a mouth that tastes like you have been sucking a battery, nauseous, exhausted, wondering whether the cure is worse than the disease. You are not imagining it. The treatment for H. pylori is genuinely hard to take, and pretending otherwise has caused more people to quit halfway than almost anything else.

So this chapter does two things. It tells you the truth about what is coming, so that none of it frightens you into stopping. And it shows you how to get through the full course, because finishing it — properly, completely, on schedule — is not a bonus. It is very often the difference between clearing the infection and ending up still positive.

Let's start with why finishing matters so much, because once you understand that, every grim day becomes a day with a point.

---

### The mistake that sabotages more treatments than resistance does

Here is a number that should change how you take your tablets. Somewhere between 27 and 41 percent of treatment failures happen in people whose bacteria were never resistant to the drugs in the first place.

Read that again, because it overturns the obvious assumption. You would think that when treatment fails, it fails because the bug was too tough — resistant, unkillable, beyond the reach of the antibiotics. And sometimes that is exactly what happens. But in a large share of failures, the bacteria were perfectly killable. The drugs would have worked. Something else went wrong.

That "something else" is, very often, the way the treatment was taken. Doses missed. The course stopped early because the side effects were awful or because the symptoms cleared up and it felt finished. The acid-suppressing tablet taken carelessly, at the wrong times, so the antibiotics never got the low-acid environment they needed. None of these feel like a big deal in the moment. Each one feels like a small, forgivable slip on a hard week. But together they are one of the leading reasons a winnable treatment is lost.

This is, oddly, encouraging. It means a meaningful chunk of your odds is in your own hands. You cannot control whether your strain is resistant. You can absolutely control whether you finish the course exactly as directed. That part is yours.

Most of medicine does not work like this. For most conditions, the outcome is largely out of your hands once the prescription is written — you take what you are given and the biology does what it does. H. pylori treatment is unusual in how much the patient's own execution affects the result. That cuts both ways, and it is worth being honest about both. It means a sloppy fortnight can lose a winnable infection, which is sobering. But it also means a careful fortnight can swing the odds meaningfully in your favour, which is empowering. You are not a passive recipient of this treatment. You are an active participant in whether it works, and the size of your contribution is genuinely large. Knowing that should not make you anxious about getting it perfect. It should make you feel that the effort is worth it — because, unusually, the effort actually moves the needle.

---

### Why the acid tablet's timing is not a detail

We touched on this in the last chapter, but it earns repeating here because it is where so many people quietly go wrong.

The proton pump inhibitor — the PPI, the acid-suppressing tablet — is not there to settle your stomach as a comfort. It is there to change the chemistry of your stomach so the antibiotics can work. Several of the antibiotics used against H. pylori are simply more effective when stomach acid is kept low and steady. The PPI is the thing that keeps it low. That is its job, and its job depends on timing.

These tablets generally work best taken before food, typically around half an hour before a meal, so that they are active when your stomach gears up to produce acid. Swallowed randomly, with no relation to meals, they suppress acid far less effectively, and the antibiotics lose the environment they were counting on. You can take every antibiotic dose faithfully and still undercut the whole regimen by getting the PPI timing wrong, because the antibiotics were relying on the PPI to set the stage.

So when your doctor or pharmacist tells you when to take each thing, that instruction is not bureaucratic fussiness. It is mechanism. Ask them to write it down. Ask them specifically: *when exactly do I take the acid tablet relative to food?* It is one of the highest-value questions you can ask, and it costs nothing.

### What the side effects actually are, and which ones to expect

Let's walk through what is coming, so none of it ambushes you. Knowing a symptom is expected robs it of its power to frighten you into quitting.

The **metallic or bitter taste** is the one almost everyone mentions, and it is most associated with metronidazole, one of the antibiotics commonly used. It is harmless. It is also genuinely unpleasant — a persistent taste of coins or batteries that food does not quite cut through. It will fade once the course is finished. In the meantime, it is a nuisance, not a warning sign.

**Nausea** is common, partly from the antibiotics and partly because your stomach is already inflamed. Taking tablets with food where you are told that is allowed, eating small and bland rather than large and rich, and staying gently hydrated all help. The nausea is real, and it is survivable, and it ends.

**Diarrhoea** is the other big one, and it has a specific cause worth understanding. Antibiotics do not only kill H. pylori — they disturb the broader population of bacteria living in your gut, the ones that help things run smoothly. Disrupt that population and the gut can become loose and unsettled. This is called antibiotic-associated diarrhoea, and it is one of the most common reasons people abandon a course early. We will deal with it directly in a moment, because there is something genuinely useful you can do about it.

There may also be **fatigue, headache, and a general washed-out feeling** — the cumulative toll of two weeks of strong medicine and a body fighting an infection. Rest where you can. This is not the time to push yourself to perform at full capacity. The course is short. The fog lifts when it ends.

It helps, genuinely, to lower your expectations of yourself for the fortnight in advance. People often go into treatment assuming they will carry on at full speed — full work, full social calendar, full everything — and then feel as though they are failing when the side effects make that hard. You are not failing. You are taking strong medicine while your body fights an infection, and feeling below par is the expected cost of that, not a sign of weakness. If you can lighten your load a little for those two weeks — fewer commitments, more rest, lower standards for what counts as a productive day — you will tolerate the course far better and resent it far less. Treat the fortnight as a dip you are deliberately planning for, not an interruption to be powered through. The work and the social life will still be there in two weeks. The window to clear this infection properly is now.

One honest note: a metallic taste, nausea, mild diarrhoea, and tiredness are expected. Severe, watery, or bloody diarrhoea, a high fever, or signs of an allergic reaction such as a rash, swelling, or difficulty breathing are not expected, and they mean you should contact your doctor rather than push through. Knowing the difference lets you tolerate the ordinary misery without ignoring the rare genuine warning.

### The helper that has real evidence for the diarrhoea

Here is the one adjunct that belongs in a chapter about getting through treatment, because its evidence is specifically about tolerability rather than about killing the bacteria.

A particular yeast used as a probiotic, *Saccharomyces boulardii*, has reasonable evidence that it reduces antibiotic-associated diarrhoea during H. pylori treatment. Notice carefully what that claim is and is not. It is not that this yeast clears the infection — it does not, and anyone who tells you it does is overselling. The claim is narrower and more honest: taken alongside the antibiotics, it can lessen the diarrhoea that those antibiotics cause, which makes the course easier to tolerate, which makes you more likely to finish it.

And finishing it, as we have established, is a large part of whether it works. So the value of this is indirect but real. It does not fight the bacteria. It helps you stay in the fight. That is a legitimate and worthwhile thing for an adjunct to do, and it is exactly the kind of modest, evidence-anchored help this book is willing to point you toward — as something to discuss with your doctor or pharmacist, who can tell you whether it fits with the rest of your regimen, not as something to grab off a shelf because a book said so.

I am naming a category and a mechanism here, not a brand or a product. There are many versions of this on sale, wrapped in a great deal of marketing. The thing that matters is the substance and the evidence behind it, and the right person to confirm it belongs in your particular case is the one supervising your treatment.

Why mention it at all, in a book so careful about not sending you shopping? Because the diarrhoea is one of the single most common reasons people quit, and quitting, as we have established, is one of the most common reasons treatment fails — so anything with real evidence for keeping you in the course earns its place. The distinction this book holds is not "never mention anything you might take." It is "never sell you anything, never oversell the evidence, and always route the decision through your doctor." This particular yeast clears that bar: the claim is honest and narrow, the evidence is specific, and the decision belongs with the person supervising your treatment, who can check it fits with everything else you are taking. That is the difference between a useful, sourced mention and a sales pitch, and it is a line this book will hold all the way through.

### How to actually finish the course

So here is the practical scaffolding — the unglamorous, concrete tactics that get a hard two weeks done.

Build the routine before you need it. On day one, when you are still feeling relatively normal, set up whatever reminders you need — alarms on your phone, a pill organiser, a note on the fridge — so that taking the tablets correctly does not depend on you remembering through a fog of nausea on day six. Decision-making gets harder as the side effects build. Make the decisions in advance, while it is easy.

Get the timing in writing. Before you leave the pharmacy, have it written down: which tablet, how many, when, and crucially, which ones go with food and which on an empty stomach, and exactly when the acid tablet goes relative to meals. Do not trust this to memory. A regimen with four components and several daily doses is genuinely hard to track, and a written schedule turns it from a daily puzzle into a checklist.

Eat to cushion it. Small, plain, frequent meals tend to sit better than large or rich ones, and they help with both the nausea and the empty-stomach burning. This is not the fortnight for spicy, acidic, or heavy food. The diet chapter later will go into what helps your stomach lining settle in general; for now, during treatment specifically, gentle and bland is the whole strategy.

Plan for the low point. There is often a midpoint slump — around days five to eight — where the side effects have accumulated and the end still feels far off and quitting starts to look reasonable. Know it is coming. That moment is not a sign that the treatment is failing or that you cannot do it. It is the predictable middle of a hard course, and it passes. If you can recognise it as "this is the dip everyone hits" rather than "this is unbearable and pointless," you are far more likely to get to the other side.

It genuinely helps to name that slump in advance, the way you would warn a friend about the hard mile in a long walk. The danger of the midpoint is not the side effects themselves; it is the story the tired mind tells about them. Around day six, depleted and fed up, it is easy to slide from "this is unpleasant" into "this is pointless and I should stop" — and that second thought is the one that loses treatments. But you can disarm it ahead of time simply by expecting it. When the day-six version of you starts whispering that quitting would be reasonable, the prepared part of you can answer: *this is the dip I was told about, it means the course is working its way through, and it passes.* Naming the slump before it arrives turns it from a crisis into a milestone — an unpleasant but expected stretch you knew was coming and planned to walk straight through.

Recruit some support if you can. Tell someone close to you that you are doing a hard two-week course and roughly what to expect, so that there is a person who understands why you are off-colour and can encourage you through the slump rather than wondering why you are irritable and tired. You do not have to do this alone or in silence. A bit of understanding from one other person — someone who will remind you that the fortnight has an end and that finishing matters — can make the difference on the days when your own resolve is thin. There is no prize for white-knuckling it privately. Let someone help you over the line.

And do not stop just because you feel better. You may well feel noticeably better partway through, as the antibiotics begin knocking the infection back and the inflammation starts to ease. That improvement is not the finish line. The bacteria are not gone the moment the pain lifts. Stopping early because the symptoms cleared is one of the classic ways a treatment that was working becomes a treatment that failed. Feeling better is a reason to keep going, not a reason to stop.

This trap deserves a closer look, because it is so intuitive that smart, careful people fall into it constantly. The logic feels airtight: the medicine is to stop the symptoms, the symptoms have stopped, therefore the medicine has done its job. But that logic quietly confuses two different things — relief and eradication. The antibiotics knock the bacterial population down quickly, and a smaller population means less inflammation and less pain, often within days. The survivors, though, are still there. Stop now, and they regrow, and within weeks you are back where you started, except that you have also given the surviving bacteria a partial exposure to the antibiotics — which is exactly the kind of thing that can nudge a strain toward resistance. So stopping early is worse than useless. It is the move most likely to leave you both still infected and harder to treat next time. The full course exists to wipe out the survivors, not just to quiet the symptoms, and that work is not finished when the pain lifts. Set this expectation now, today, before you feel better — because the version of you on day eight, feeling almost normal, is going to need to have already decided that "almost normal" is not "done."

A practical trick helps here: tie the finish line to the calendar, not to how you feel. Mark the final day of the course somewhere you will see it, and treat that date as the only thing that ends the treatment. Not the day the pain stops. Not the day you feel like yourself again. The date. Feelings will try to renegotiate the deal partway through; the calendar will not. Let the calendar win.

### The point of all this misery

Let's end where it helps to end — with why any of this is worth it.

The two weeks are awful for a lot of people, and I am not going to dress that up. But they are two weeks. The infection, untreated, is a chronic problem measured in years. You are trading a fortnight of feeling rough for the genuine prospect of clearing something that has been quietly inflaming your stomach and feeding your fear. That is a good trade, and it is a short one, and it has an end date you can see from here.

Every faithfully taken dose, every correctly timed acid tablet, every grim midpoint day you got through without quitting is loading the odds in your favour — including the large share of the odds that has nothing to do with resistance and everything to do with how the course was taken. That part was always yours. Now you know how to use it.

When the course is done, there is one more crucial step that a surprising number of people skip, and it is the only way to actually know whether all this worked: confirming the infection is gone. We will get there. But first, the chapter for anyone whose course has already finished and already failed — the one that almost no other book bothers to write.
