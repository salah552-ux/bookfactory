## Appendix A — Glossary

Plain-English definitions of the terms used in this book. None of these require a medical background to understand — they are here so that no piece of jargon ever stands between you and your own care.

**Adjunct.** Something used *alongside* the main treatment to support it — never instead of it. In this book, the natural approaches with real evidence are adjuncts: helpers added to proper antibiotic treatment, not replacements for it.

**Antibiotic resistance.** When a bacterium has changed in ways that let it survive an antibiotic that used to kill it. H. pylori has become more resistant to some older antibiotics over time, which is part of why treatments have changed.

**Bismuth quadruple therapy (BQT).** The preferred first-line treatment for H. pylori under the 2024 guidance. "Quadruple" because it combines four components — a proton pump inhibitor, bismuth, a nitroimidazole antibiotic, and a tetracycline antibiotic — usually taken for around 14 days. The four-part attack leaves the bacteria fewer ways to survive.

**Eradication.** The clearing of the infection — the goal of treatment. In this book, eradication is something the antibiotics and your doctor achieve. The book itself does not eradicate anything; it explains and prepares.

**Gastritis.** Inflammation of the stomach lining. It is the source of most of the burning and gnawing pain H. pylori causes — an inflamed, oversensitive surface reacting to stomach acid.

**Gastric mucosa.** The stomach lining — the wall of cells, protected by a layer of mucus, that H. pylori burrows beneath.

**Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori).** A spiral-shaped bacterium that survives in the stomach by burrowing under the protective mucus and neutralising acid around itself. It is a very common chronic infection and the leading cause of stomach and duodenal ulcers.

**Number needed to treat (NNT).** A way of expressing how much benefit a treatment delivers across a population. An NNT of about 332 for eradication and stomach cancer means you would need to successfully clear the infection in roughly 332 people to prevent one case of stomach cancer — which is why most carriers were never going to develop it.

**Proton pump inhibitor (PPI).** The acid-suppressing tablet used as part of treatment. It lowers stomach acid, which both helps the antibiotics work and is why it must be stopped for about two weeks before a test of cure (because it can mask the infection and cause a false-negative result).

**Relative risk.** A way of comparing the risk of an outcome between two groups. A relative risk of 0.61 for eradication and stomach cancer means clearing the infection lowers the risk meaningfully compared with leaving it untreated.

**Rescue therapy.** The treatments used when standard first- and second-line treatments have failed — for example, rifabutin-based or furazolidone-based regimens, reserved for difficult-to-treat infections and supervised by a specialist.

**Serology (blood antibody test).** A blood test that detects antibodies against H. pylori. It is *not* suitable as a test of cure, because antibodies persist for years after the infection clears, so a positive result after treatment does not mean the infection is still there.

**Stool antigen test.** A test that looks for traces of H. pylori in a stool sample. It is a suitable test of cure when done at the right time.

**Susceptibility testing.** Testing to work out which antibiotics a particular strain of H. pylori can still be killed by. Especially valuable after a failed treatment, because it turns the choice of the next regimen from a blind gamble into a targeted decision.

**Test of cure.** A test done after treatment to confirm the infection is actually gone. A stool antigen test or urea breath test, done at least four weeks after the last antibiotic and off PPIs for about two weeks beforehand. Symptom relief is not a substitute for it.

**Urea breath test.** A test in which you swallow a harmless labelled form of urea; if H. pylori is present, its urease enzyme splits the urea and produces a signal detectable in your breath. Used both to diagnose the infection and to confirm eradication.

**Urease.** An enzyme produced by H. pylori that neutralises stomach acid around the bacterium by breaking down urea into ammonia. It is both how the bacterium survives and, via the breath test, how it is detected.

**Adherence.** Taking your treatment exactly as directed — the right doses, at the right times, for the full course. With H. pylori, adherence is unusually important, because a large share of treatment failures occur in people whose bacteria were never resistant, with incomplete or mistimed treatment a major factor.

**Bismuth.** One of the four components of bismuth quadruple therapy. It acts against the bacteria through several mechanisms that resistance does not easily overcome, which is part of why the four-drug combination is harder for H. pylori to survive.

**Carcinogen (Group 1).** A substance or agent classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer as a definite, proven cause of cancer in humans. H. pylori is in this category, alongside agents such as tobacco smoke — a classification about what can cause cancer across populations, not a prediction about any one person.

**Duodenum.** The first part of the small intestine, just beyond the stomach. H. pylori can cause ulcers here as well as in the stomach itself.

**Gastroenterologist.** A doctor who specialises in the digestive system. A persistent or repeatedly failed H. pylori infection is the kind of problem a gastroenterologist is specifically equipped to handle, which is why referral becomes appropriate after standard treatment fails.

**Intestinal metaplasia.** A change in which the cells of the stomach lining come to resemble intestinal cells, one of the slow steps that can occur over many years of unaddressed inflammation. Clearing the infection halts the inflammation that drives this process.

**Nitroimidazole.** A class of antibiotic (for example, metronidazole) used as one component of bismuth quadruple therapy. The metallic taste many people experience during treatment is particularly associated with this drug.

**Rifabutin.** An antibiotic used in rescue therapy for difficult-to-treat H. pylori. Because it has been used relatively little against this bacterium, resistance to it is uncommon, which is why it can work where heavily used drugs have failed.

**Tetracycline.** A class of antibiotic forming one component of bismuth quadruple therapy.

## Appendix B — Questions to Ask Your Doctor

A printable checklist. Take it to your appointment. You don't need to ask all of them — lead with the ones that fit where you are right now.

**If you are newly diagnosed:**

- [ ] Given the 2024 guidance, is optimised bismuth quadruple therapy the right first-line treatment for me?
- [ ] Exactly how and when do I take each tablet — particularly the acid-suppressing one, relative to meals? (Ask for this in writing.)
- [ ] How long is the course, and why does finishing all of it matter?
- [ ] What side effects should I expect, and which ones mean I should call you rather than push through?
- [ ] When and how should I do a test of cure to confirm it's gone?

**If you have been treated and are still positive:**

- [ ] What treatment did I have, and what's the right next step given that it failed?
- [ ] Can we consider susceptibility testing before choosing the next regimen?
- [ ] Should I be referred to a gastroenterologist?
- [ ] What rescue options exist if standard treatments keep failing?
- [ ] When should I re-test to confirm the next course worked?

**For everyone:**

- [ ] When and how should I do a test of cure — and can we make sure it's a stool antigen or breath test, not a blood antibody test, done at least four weeks after the antibiotics and off the acid tablets for about two weeks beforehand?
- [ ] Given that H. pylori can cluster in households, should anyone in my family be tested?
- [ ] Is there an evidence-based adjunct that might sensibly fit alongside my treatment, given timing and interactions?
- [ ] Are any of my other medications or conditions relevant to the treatment choice?

## Appendix C — The Sources, by Chapter

This book promised to cite its sources by name, and this is where it keeps that promise. Every statistic and clinical claim in the preceding chapters traces to the named, published sources below. Nothing in this book was invented; everything was sourced.

**On treatment and resistance (Chapters 3, 5, 8):**
The 2024 American College of Gastroenterology (ACG) clinical guideline on the treatment of H. pylori infection — the basis for optimised bismuth quadruple therapy as preferred first-line treatment, the removal of clarithromycin triple therapy as a first-line option, the cautions around clarithromycin- and levofloxacin-based regimens, the US clarithromycin resistance figures, and the test-of-cure standard.

**On treatment failure (Chapters 1, 4, 5):**
The treatment-failure literature documenting the roughly 28.7% first-line failure rate and the finding that between 27% and 41% of failures occur even when the bacteria are not resistant to the antibiotics used (including the analyses catalogued in the PMC literature on H. pylori treatment outcomes).

**On the rescue pathway (Chapter 5):**
The published literature on rifabutin-based rescue therapy (rifabutin 150 mg + amoxicillin 1 g + PPI, twice daily, for 14 days) for difficult-to-treat patients; and on furazolidone-based rescue therapy, documented to achieve eradication even after as many as five prior failed attempts with multi-resistant strains. Regional antibiotic-resistance figures are drawn from regional resistance studies and labelled as such in the text.

**On cancer risk (Chapter 2):**
*Nature Medicine*, September 2025 (article s41591-025-03793-6), and the World Health Organization's International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) press release No. 368 of 7 July 2025 — the sources for the projected ~15.6 million lifetime gastric cancer cases, the ~76% attributable to H. pylori, and the Group 1 carcinogen classification. The eradication and cancer-risk reduction figures (relative risk 0.61, number needed to treat ≈ 332) are drawn from the updated meta-analysis in the PMC literature on eradication and gastric cancer prevention.

**On anxiety in H. pylori patients (Chapters 1, 2):**
The clinical literature documenting significantly higher measured anxiety scores in H. pylori-positive patients compared with negative patients.

**On the diet framework (Chapter 6):**
The independent association between very high salt intake, cured and smoked meats, and gastric-cancer risk, as reflected in stomach-health and cancer-prevention guidance.

**On the adjuncts (Chapters 4, 8):**
The cited figures on mastic gum combined with triple therapy (around 92% versus around 63% for antibiotics alone), with the acid-environment and PPI-timing caveat; the 2024 *Frontiers in Medicine* systematic review on broccoli-sprout sulforaphane reducing H. pylori colonisation without fully clearing it by breath-test standards, alongside the related broccoli/probiotic eradication trial literature; the 2025 meta-analysis on *Lactobacillus reuteri* added to triple therapy improving eradication and tolerability; and the established literature on *Saccharomyces boulardii* reducing antibiotic-associated diarrhoea during eradication therapy.

*Full source URLs and identifiers are held in the book's research file and are available on request. Every clinical figure in this book is traceable to a named, published source. None was invented, and where a source reported a range, the range was preserved rather than collapsed into a single figure.*

## Full Medical Disclaimer

This book is intended for informational and educational purposes only. It is not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Helicobacter pylori is a bacterial infection that requires prescription medication; it cannot be treated by anything you read in a book. The information here is based on the author's research of published clinical sources and is not intended to replace the guidance of a qualified healthcare provider.

Always seek the advice of your physician, gastroenterologist, or other qualified health professional with any questions you may have regarding a medical condition, your symptoms, or your treatment options. Never disregard professional medical advice, or delay in seeking it, because of something you have read in this book. The drugs, doses, and regimens described in this book are described so that you can understand and discuss your options; they are not a prescription, and the treatment that is right for you is chosen and supervised by your doctor.

Seek urgent medical care if you have any of these warning signs: blood in your stool or vomit, black or tarry stools, vomit that looks like coffee grounds, unexplained weight loss, difficulty swallowing, or persistent vomiting. These are alarm features that need to be evaluated by a doctor without delay.

Results described or implied in this book are not guaranteed and will vary by individual. If you are experiencing a medical emergency, contact emergency services immediately.
