## EMAIL 2 — VALUE (the "still positive" reframe)
**Subject:** Still positive after antibiotics? You didn't fail — the protocol did
**Preview text:** Roughly 28.7% of first-line treatments fail. Here's what actually happens next.
**Send:** 48 hours after sign-up

If your re-test came back positive, the first thing to know is that it's common — roughly 28.7% of first-line treatments fail. You are not an unusual case, and you almost certainly did nothing wrong.

Here's the part that surprises people: a large share of failures — somewhere between 27% and 41% — happen even when the bacteria were *never* resistant to the antibiotics used. In other words, resistance isn't the whole story. Adherence and timing matter just as much: finishing the full course exactly as directed, and taking the acid-suppressing tablet (the PPI) properly, because it helps the antibiotics work. A course that was missed, mistimed, or stopped early when the side effects got grim is a very ordinary reason for a failure — and a fixable one.

So what actually happens next? There's a clear pathway, and it's the part most guides skip entirely. If your first round wasn't bismuth quadruple therapy, that's usually the preferred second-line. Beyond that, there are genuine rescue regimens for difficult cases — and susceptibility testing can turn the next attempt from a blind guess into a targeted choice.

Your useful next step: if you're still positive, the conversation to have is with your doctor about second-line treatment and a possible gastroenterology referral with susceptibility testing — not another tea. Your cheat sheet has the exact questions for that conversation under "still positive."
