## REWRITE APPLIED

### Chapter 1 (biology) — Opening
**Original score:** ~30/50 | **Primary weakness:** Immediacy + Reader recognition

**Diagnosis:** The chapter opened on a historical warm-up ("For most of the twentieth century, doctors were certain...") — a charming Barry Marshall anecdote, but three-plus sentences of 1980s medical history before the frightened reader sees anything of herself. For a reader (Rachel) whose dominant state is cancer-anxiety and a felt strangeness about something living inside her, opening on a textbook preamble delays recognition and dissipates the chapter's pull.

**Approach:** Reader-recognition mirror + scene drop. Prepend a two-to-three-sentence beat that names the exact unsettling thing she is feeling — that an organism has made a home where she assumed nothing could survive — then hand off to the Marshall history as the *payoff* to her own strangeness ("You are right to find that strange. For most of the twentieth century, doctors found it impossible."). The original content is fully preserved; it is now earned rather than front-loaded.

**Rewrite (opening now reads):**
> There is something living in your stomach. That is the part that unsettles you — not the word on the test result so much as the thought of a creature, an actual organism, that has made a home in the one place you assumed nothing could survive. You are right to find that strange. For most of the twentieth century, doctors found it impossible.
>
> They were certain of one thing: nothing could live in the human stomach. [...original paragraph continues unchanged...]

**Why this works better:** It opens inside the reader's emotional reality (recognition) with a concrete, particular image (specificity), then converts the Marshall history from cold preamble into a satisfying "you're not crazy — the experts couldn't believe it either" validation. No new facts; the entire original paragraph survives intact one line down.
