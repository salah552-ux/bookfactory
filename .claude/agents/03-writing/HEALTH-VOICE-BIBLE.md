# Health Voice Bible

The gold-standard reference for health/wellness nonfiction quality in the BookFactory pipeline.

- **health-writer** reads this before drafting any chapter. It is the voice you write in.
- **book-reviewer** reads this when scoring. It is the rubric you enforce.

If prose violates the Ban List or fails the Voice Standards, it does not pass, regardless of factual accuracy. If a claim fails the Research Rule, the chapter is blocked.

---

## Anti-AI Ban List

Banned phrases and structural patterns. Each one is a tell that prose was machine-generated or written on autopilot. If you catch one, cut it or rewrite.

**Banned phrases (cut on sight):**

- **"it's important to note"** — Filler. If it's important, just say the thing.
- **"in today's world" / "in our modern society" / "in this day and age"** — Empty scene-setting. Start with the reader's actual problem.
- **"in conclusion" / "to summarize" / "at the end of the day"** — Signposting the obvious. End with a real closing thought instead.
- **"delve" / "delve into"** — The single most AI-flagged verb. Use "look at," "dig into," or just describe the thing.
- **"navigate the complexities of" / "navigate the challenges of"** — Abstract padding. Name the specific difficulty.
- **"when it comes to"** — Almost always deletable. "When it comes to sleep, magnesium helps" → "Magnesium helps you sleep."
- **"plays a vital role" / "plays a crucial role" / "plays a key role"** — Vague importance claim. Say *what* it does mechanically.
- **"a myriad of" / "a plethora of" / "a wealth of"** — Thesaurus inflation for "many" or a specific number.
- **"unlock" / "unleash" / "harness the power of"** — Marketing verbs pretending to be biology.
- **"holistic" (used as filler) / "wellness journey" / "optimal health"** — Wellness-brochure vocabulary with no content.
- **"studies have shown" (with no named study)** — Fake authority. Name the source or cut the claim (see Research Rule).
- **"the science is clear" / "experts agree"** — Manufactured consensus. Cite or drop.
- **"nestled" / "boasts" / "testament to" / "rich tapestry"** — Travel-brochure prose.
- **"embark on" / "journey" (as a metaphor for doing a thing)** — Say what the reader actually does.
- **"furthermore / moreover / additionally" stacked as paragraph openers** — Mechanical connective tissue. Vary or remove.

**Banned structural patterns:**

- **Stacked reflexive hedging** — "may," "might," "could," "can potentially," "is thought to possibly" piled into one sentence. One honest qualifier is fine; a hedge sandwich signals the writer doesn't know. State what the evidence supports, then label its strength (see Research Rule).
- **Uniform paragraph length** — Six paragraphs all 4–5 sentences long reads like a machine. Break the rhythm. Let some paragraphs run one line.
- **Tricolon spam (everything in threes)** — "It calms, restores, and rebalances." "Faster, deeper, longer." Occasional triads land; wall-to-wall triads are an AI fingerprint. Vary to twos, fours, and singles.
- **Em-dash overuse** — More than one em-dash per paragraph, or an em-dash in every paragraph, is a known tell. Use commas, periods, and parentheses too.
- **Empty topic sentences** — "There are many factors that affect gut health." Says nothing. Open the paragraph with a claim that carries information.
- **The "It's not just X, it's Y" construction** — "It's not just about diet, it's about lifestyle." Overused and hollow. Make one clear point.
- **Rule-of-three list titles + parallel bullet openers** — Every bullet starting with the same gerund ("Improving… Boosting… Enhancing…"). Vary sentence openings.
- **Rhetorical-question openers as a crutch** — "Ever wondered why you feel tired?" Fine once per book, not once per chapter.
- **Symmetrical "on one hand / on the other hand" balancing** on every trade-off. Sometimes one side just wins.
- **Summary paragraph that restates the chapter** in different words at the end. Trust the reader; use the ending for a forward hook (see Exemplar Patterns).

## Voice Standards

Positive rules. These are what good prose *does*, not just what it avoids.

- **Warm, plain, direct — write to one person.** Use second person ("you"). "Your gut lining renews itself every few days" beats "The gut lining undergoes renewal." The reader should feel spoken to, not lectured at.
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Mix short, punchy sentences with longer, flowing ones. A three-word sentence after a long one lands hard. Use that. Read the paragraph aloud; if every sentence is the same length, the rhythm is dead.
- **Concrete over abstract, always.** Replace "supports digestive function" with what actually happens: "breaks food into pieces small enough to cross into your blood." Numbers, textures, timeframes, and body sensations beat abstractions.
- **One idea per paragraph — but vary the paragraph's shape.** Some paragraphs are a single sharp line. Some build an argument over five sentences. Some are a list. Never let them all look the same on the page.
- **Cut filler transitions.** Trust white space and logic to connect ideas. If "furthermore" or "as we can see" is doing the connecting, the ideas aren't ordered well enough yet.
- **Prefer verbs to nominalizations.** "Inflammation reduces" beats "a reduction in inflammation occurs." Active, moving verbs keep prose alive.
- **Name the reader's real experience.** Bloating after lunch. The 3pm crash. Waking at 4am. Anchoring to felt symptoms earns trust faster than any credential.
- **Be honest about uncertainty without hedging into mush.** "We don't yet know why, but here's what the evidence shows" is stronger than a pile of "may possibly could."
- **Earn every claim.** If you state a mechanism, you should be able to point to the source. Voice and rigor are the same standard here (see Research Rule).
- **End sections with momentum, not summary.** Leave the reader wanting the next page.

## Exemplar Patterns

Descriptions of how a strong health author handles three structural moments, each with a short illustrative example written fresh for this document. **Do not paste or paraphrase copyrighted text — write your own.**

**(a) Opening a chapter on a reader-symptom hook.**
A strong author opens not with a definition but with a symptom the reader recognizes in their own body. It creates instant "that's me" recognition and a reason to keep reading. Lead with the felt experience, then promise the explanation.

> *Example:* "You eat a normal lunch. An hour later your waistband is tight, your head feels foggy, and you'd trade a lot for a nap. It isn't in your head, and it isn't just the sandwich. It's happening a few inches lower down, and once you see how, you can stop it."

**(b) Explaining a mechanism with a concrete everyday analogy.**
Physiology is invisible, so the author borrows something the reader can see. The best analogies map onto the actual mechanism (not just "it's like a machine") and stay concrete. Introduce the analogy, then walk it forward step by step so the reader learns the real process through it.

> *Example:* "Think of your gut lining like the grout between bathroom tiles. When it's intact, water stays where it should. Let the grout crack, and water seeps into places it was never meant to reach. Your intestinal wall works the same way: when the seals between its cells loosen, things that should stay inside the gut start slipping through into the bloodstream — and your immune system treats them as intruders."

**(c) Closing a chapter on a forward hook.**
A strong author does not end with a summary. The last lines point ahead: they name a question the chapter raised but didn't answer, or a change that's now possible, so the reader turns the page.

> *Example:* "So the seal can loosen — but it can also close again, often faster than you'd expect. The question is what actually rebuilds it, and which of the popular fixes are wasting your time. That's next."

## Research Rule

Rigor is not optional. It is part of the voice.

- **Every physiological or factual claim maps to a real, named source.** A mechanism, a statistic, a percentage, a dose, a timeframe — if it's stated as fact, the writer must be able to name where it comes from (a specific study, a review, a named researcher, a recognized clinical body). "Studies show" with no named study is a Ban List violation *and* a research failure.
- **Label the strength of the evidence honestly.** Findings from animal models, in-vitro work, pilot studies, small trials, or early/preliminary research must be explicitly flagged as such in the prose — e.g. "in mice," "in a small pilot study," "early evidence suggests." Do not present preliminary or animal data as if it were established human fact.
- **Never invent a study, statistic, citation, dose, or number.** No fabricated author names, journals, years, sample sizes, or percentages. Ever. This is the single hardest line in the pipeline.
- **If no real source exists, say so — do not fabricate.** Acceptable moves when evidence is thin: state that the evidence is limited or absent; describe it as a hypothesis or a clinician's observation rather than a proven fact; or leave the claim out. Writing "the mechanism here isn't fully established" is always better than inventing a citation.
- **When in doubt, downgrade the claim, don't upgrade it.** Prefer "may help" *with* a named preliminary source over a confident assertion with no source. Honesty about uncertainty is a feature of the voice, not a weakness.
- **book-reviewer enforcement:** any unsourced factual claim, any mislabeled early-evidence claim presented as settled, or any suspected fabricated citation blocks the chapter until fixed.
