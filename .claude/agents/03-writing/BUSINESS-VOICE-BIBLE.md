# Business Voice Bible

The gold-standard reference for business, self-help, productivity, finance, entrepreneurship, and personal-development quality in the BookFactory pipeline.

- **business-writer** reads this before drafting any chapter. It is the voice you write in.
- **book-reviewer** reads this when scoring. It is the rubric you enforce.

If prose violates the Ban List or fails the Voice Standards, it does not pass, regardless of how sound the underlying idea is. If a claim fails the Research Rule, the chapter is blocked.

---

## Anti-AI Ban List

Banned phrases and structural patterns. Each one is a tell that prose was machine-generated, ghost-written on autopilot, or lifted from LinkedIn. If you catch one, cut it or rewrite.

**Universal AI tells (cut on sight — these apply to every genre in the factory):**

- **"it's important to note" / "it's worth noting"** — Filler. If it matters, just say it first.
- **"in today's world" / "in this day and age" / "now more than ever"** — Empty scene-setting. Open on the reader's actual problem.
- **"in conclusion" / "to summarize" / "at the end of the day"** — Signposting the obvious. End with a real closing thought.
- **"delve" / "delve into"** — The single most AI-flagged verb. Use "look at," "break down," or just describe the thing.
- **"a myriad of" / "a plethora of" / "a wealth of"** — Thesaurus inflation for "many" or a specific number.
- **"navigate the complexities of" / "when it comes to"** — Abstract padding. Name the specific difficulty. "When it comes to hiring, speed matters" → "Hire slowly."
- **"furthermore / moreover / additionally" stacked as paragraph openers** — Mechanical connective tissue.
- **"studies have shown" / "research suggests" / "experts agree" (with no named source)** — Fake authority. Name it or cut it (see Research Rule).

**Business/self-help-specific tells:**

- **Corporate-speak** — "leverage synergies," "move the needle," "circle back," "low-hanging fruit," "boil the ocean," "at scale," "best-in-class," "value-add," "paradigm shift," "think outside the box." Say the plain thing. "Leverage our synergies" means "work together" — so write that.
- **Guru hype without evidence** — "skyrocket," "10x your life," "explode your revenue," "unlock your true potential," "crush it," "the one weird trick," "this will change everything." Any multiplier or superlative needs a real, cited number behind it or it comes out.
- **Fake urgency** — "you can't afford to wait," "the window is closing," "everyone else is already doing this," "read this before it's too late." Manufactured pressure that no fact supports. Cut it.
- **Listicle sameness** — every section the same shape: bold claim, three parallel bullets, tidy takeaway, repeat. Vary the container. Some ideas are a story. Some are one hard sentence. Some are a table. Never let every section look identical on the page.
- **Empty frameworks and acronyms for their own sake** — inventing "The 5 C's of Success" or a forced acronym (S.M.A.R.T.E.R., the P.O.W.E.R. method) when the idea would land better in plain language. A framework earns its name only if it makes the idea easier to remember and use. If the acronym is doing the work the thinking should, cut it.
- **The "It's not just X, it's Y" construction** — "It's not about working harder, it's about working smarter." Overused and hollow. Make one clear point.
- **Rhetorical-question openers as a crutch** — "Have you ever wondered why some people succeed and others don't?" Fine once in a book, not once a chapter.
- **Tricolon spam (everything in threes)** — "Faster, cheaper, better." "Plan, execute, win." Occasional triads land; wall-to-wall triads are an AI fingerprint.
- **The summary paragraph that restates the chapter** in different words at the end. Trust the reader; end on an action instead (see Exemplar Patterns).

## Voice Standards

Positive rules. These are what strong business prose *does*, not just what it avoids.

- **Punchy and direct — say the thing.** Lead with the claim, not the wind-up. "Most meetings should be emails" beats "There are a number of considerations when evaluating whether a meeting is the appropriate format."
- **One idea per section.** Not three, not five. One core idea per chapter, developed, not a buffet of half-ideas. If you can't compress the chapter to a single sentence, it isn't focused enough yet.
- **A concrete example within reach of every claim.** Every abstract point earns a real person, company, number, or moment within a sentence or two. Abstractions don't persuade; specifics do. "Constraints help" → "When Basecamp capped itself at a 40-hour week, it shipped more, not less" (only if real and sourced — see Research Rule).
- **Talk to one reader, in second person.** Not "professionals often struggle with prioritization." You: "You have twelve open tabs and none of them is the thing that matters." Name the reader's real Tuesday, not an abstract cohort.
- **Earn authority with specifics, not adjectives.** Not "this is a powerful, transformative, game-changing approach." Show the mechanism, the number, the before-and-after. Detail is credibility; adjectives are the absence of it.
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Short sentence for the point. Longer sentence to develop it. A three-word line after a long one lands hard. Read it aloud; flat rhythm means a flat page.
- **Prefer verbs to nominalizations.** "Decide faster" beats "the acceleration of decision-making." Active, moving verbs keep prose alive.
- **Be honest about trade-offs.** The strongest business writing admits what a tactic costs and who it doesn't work for. Certainty on everything reads as a sales pitch; earned nuance reads as expertise.
- **Close with momentum toward action, not summary.** Leave the reader with something to do, not a recap of what they just read.

## Exemplar Patterns

Descriptions of how a strong business/self-help author handles three structural moments, each with a short illustrative example written fresh for this document. **Do not paste or paraphrase any copyrighted text — write your own. Any example person or company below is illustrative and labelled as such.**

**(a) Opening on the reader's felt pain at work or in life.**
A strong author opens not with a definition or a statistic but with a moment the reader recognises from their own week — the specific, slightly humiliating, entirely familiar experience. It creates instant "that's me," then promises a way out.

> *Example:* "It's 4:50 on Friday. You've been busy since seven and you cannot name one thing you actually finished. You answered everyone, attended everything, moved nothing. This isn't a time-management problem, and a new app won't fix it. It's a question you never got to ask yourself, and we're going to ask it now."

**(b) Teaching a concept through one concrete mini-story, then the principle.**
A strong author leads with a single, specific story — a person, a decision, a result — and only then extracts the rule from it. The story makes the principle stick; the principle makes the story matter. Where the story is invented, it is clearly framed as an example, not passed off as a real case.

> *Example:* "Imagine a manager — call her Dana — who cut her team's status meetings from weekly to never, and replaced them with three sentences everyone posted each morning: what I finished, what I'm on, where I'm stuck. Within a month, the people who used to hide behind meetings had nowhere to hide, and the people who actually worked stopped losing an hour a week to theatre. The principle underneath it is simple: a status meeting is usually a written update that got lost. Write the update. Skip the meeting."

**(c) Closing a chapter with one action the reader can take today.**
A strong author ends not with a summary but with a single, small, unambiguous action — something the reader can do in the next day without permission, budget, or a new tool. One action, not a checklist of ten.

> *Example:* "So here's the one thing to do before tomorrow ends: open your calendar, find the recurring meeting you dread most, and cancel the next instance. Don't reform it. Cancel it, and watch what actually breaks. Almost nothing will. That empty hour is where the next chapter starts."

## Research Rule

Rigor is not optional. It is part of the voice — and in this factory it is a hard gate.

- **Every statistic, study, or data point needs a real, named source.** A percentage, a dollar figure, a "companies that do X grow Y% faster," a survey result — if it's stated as fact, name where it comes from (a specific study, report, named researcher, company disclosure, or recognised body), drawn from `RESEARCH.md` or `FACTS.md`. "Studies have shown" with no named study is both a Ban List violation and a research failure.
- **Never invent a statistic, study, citation, company result, or number.** No fabricated survey figures, no made-up author names, journals, years, sample sizes, or growth percentages. Ever. This is the single hardest line in the pipeline.
- **No invented case studies presented as real.** If you use a real person or company, the facts about them must be accurate and sourced. If you need a hypothetical to teach a point, label it clearly — "imagine a founder," "suppose you run a small team," "here's an illustrative example." An invented story dressed up as a real case is a fabrication and blocks the chapter.
- **No income, results, or outcome promises.** Do not promise the reader they will earn a figure, hit a rank, double revenue, or achieve a specific result. Business and financial outcomes depend on factors outside any book. Describe what a tactic did in a sourced example and what it might do; never guarantee what it will do for the reader. (This also keeps the book on the right side of FTC and platform compliance.)
- **Label the strength of evidence honestly.** A single company anecdote is not proof of a universal law. One study is not settled science. Say "in one study," "one team found," "this worked for X — your context may differ." Overclaiming from thin evidence is a tell of the hype register this Bible bans.
- **When a real source doesn't exist, downgrade the claim — don't fabricate one.** State that the evidence is limited, frame it as the author's observation or a reasonable hypothesis, or leave it out. "I don't have hard data on this, but in my experience…" is always better than a fabricated citation.
- **book-reviewer enforcement:** any unsourced statistic, any invented case study presented as real, any income/results promise, or any suspected fabricated citation blocks the chapter until fixed.
