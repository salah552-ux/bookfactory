# Fiction Voice Bible

The gold-standard reference for fiction quality in the BookFactory pipeline — literary, thriller, romance, sci-fi, fantasy, historical, horror, YA, **and mystery/cosy crime**.

- **fiction-writer** and **murder-mystery-writer** read this before drafting any chapter. It is the voice you write in.
- **book-reviewer** reads this when scoring. It is the rubric you enforce.

If prose violates the Ban List or fails the Voice Standards, it does not pass, regardless of how clever the plot is. If a fact about a character or the world fails the Research Rule (continuity), the chapter is blocked.

For mystery/cosy crime, this Bible is **additive** to the murder-mystery-writer's existing fair-play, CLUE-MAP, and calibration rules — it does not replace them. Where a genre calibration and this Bible both apply, follow both; where they conflict on voice, the Bible wins.

## Anti-AI Ban List

Banned phrases and structural patterns. Each one is a tell that prose was machine-generated or written on autopilot. If you catch one, cut it or rewrite.

**Universal AI tells (cut on sight — these apply to every genre in the factory):**

- **"it's important to note" / "it's worth noting"** — Filler. Cut it and state the thing.
- **"in today's world" / "in this day and age"** — Empty scene-setting. Open on the scene, not the era.
- **"in conclusion" / "to summarize" / "at the end of the day"** — Signposting the obvious.
- **"delve" / "delve into"** — The single most AI-flagged verb. Just describe what happens.
- **"a myriad of" / "a plethora of" / "a wealth of"** — Thesaurus inflation for "many" or a real number.
- **"unlock" / "unleash" / "harness"** — Marketing verbs, wrong register for fiction.
- **"nestled" / "boasts" / "testament to" / "rich tapestry"** — Travel-brochure prose.
- **"furthermore / moreover / additionally" stacked as openers** — Mechanical connective tissue. Vary or remove.
- **"studies have shown" / "experts agree"** — Fake authority; wrong for fiction and unsourced (see Research Rule).

**Fiction-specific tells (the ones that mark amateur or machine prose):**

- **Purple-prose stacking** — three ornate adjectives before every noun, similes piled on metaphors ("the crimson, blood-red, scarlet dawn"). One precise image beats three decorative ones. Cut the stack to the sharpest single choice.
- **"Little did they know" / "if only she had known" / "what happened next would change everything"** — Cheap foreshadowing that announces the author's hand. Let dread come from the scene, not the narrator winking.
- **Over-explained emotion / telling-not-showing** — "She felt a deep sadness wash over her as she realized how alone she truly was." Show it: what her hands do, what she notices, what she can't finish saying. Never name the emotion and then explain it.
- **Every-sentence-a-metaphor** — figurative language on autopilot until nothing is literal and nothing lands. Metaphor is a spice, not the meal. Most sentences should be plain and concrete.
- **Dialogue-tag inflation** — "he exclaimed," "she interjected," "he expostulated," "she enthused," plus an adverb ("she said angrily"). Use "said" and "asked" almost always; let the line and the action carry the tone.
- **Uniform scene shape** — every scene opening with the weather or a character waking; every scene the same length; every chapter the same beat. Vary entry points, lengths, and rhythms.
- **The mirror trick** — a character studying their reflection to inventory their own hair, eyes, and build for the reader. Reveal appearance through action and others' reactions instead.
- **Stacked hedging in narration** — "she thought she might perhaps have possibly seen something." Commit to what the POV character perceives, or withhold it cleanly.
- **"Suddenly," "just then," "all of a sudden"** — If the event is sudden, the syntax should be; the word is redundant.
- **Emotion-weather mirroring on every beat** — rain for grief, sunshine for joy, storms for the climax. Occasional pathetic fallacy is fine; wall-to-wall is a tell.

## Voice Standards

Positive rules. These are what strong fiction *does*, not just what it avoids.

- **Show, don't show off.** Concrete action and sensory detail carry emotion. "Her knuckles whitened on the railing" tells the reader more than "she was terrified." The goal is that the reader feels it before they can name it — never the reverse.
- **Anchor every scene in specific, unexpected sensory detail.** Not "the kitchen was old." The specific smell, the exact quality of light, the sound just at the edge of hearing. The non-visual sense (temperature, texture, smell) lands hardest. At least a couple of precise, memorable details per scene.
- **Give each character a distinct voice.** On the page, the reader should be able to tell who is speaking with the tags removed — through vocabulary, sentence length, rhythm, what they dodge, what they never say. Two characters who sound the same are one character.
- **Build every scene on goal, conflict, and turn.** The POV character wants something; something is in the way; by the end, the situation has changed — a little or a lot. A scene where nothing turns is a scene to cut or merge.
- **Vary rhythm deliberately.** Short sentence for impact. Longer, flowing sentences for immersion and momentum. A three-word line after a long one lands like a slap. Read the paragraph aloud; if every sentence is the same length, the rhythm is dead.
- **Use interiority in moderation.** A line of a character's thought, placed well, is powerful. Paragraphs of rumination stall the page. Prefer action and dialogue; drop into interiority for the sentence that matters, then move.
- **Trust subtext.** Characters rarely say exactly what they mean. Write the surface line, then make sure something different is being communicated underneath. What a character won't say is often the loudest thing in the scene.
- **Earn the turn.** A reversal or revelation should feel both surprising and inevitable — surprising because the reader didn't see it, inevitable because, looking back, the groundwork was there (see Research Rule on continuity and fair play).
- **End scenes and chapters with momentum, not summary.** Leave the reader needing the next page.

## Exemplar Patterns

Descriptions of how a strong novelist handles three structural moments, each with a short illustrative example written fresh for this document. **Do not paste or paraphrase any copyrighted text — write your own.**

**(a) Opening a chapter mid-motion with a question planted.**
A strong author drops the reader into something already happening — an action, an exchange, a decision underway — and lets a question surface inside the motion, so the reader reads on to answer it. No throat-clearing, no weather report, no waking up. Movement first; the hook is a question the scene raises, not a promise the narrator makes.

> *Example:* "The second time the doorbell rang, Mara stopped counting the money and started hiding it. She didn't know yet who was on the step. She knew only that no one who meant her well came at this hour, and that the envelope in her hand was thicker than her rent."

**(b) Revealing character through action and dialogue, not narration.**
A strong author lets behaviour do the work the narrator could have done and shouldn't. Instead of stating that a man is controlling, or that a woman is grieving, the author shows the small physical tell and the line that means more than it says — and lets the reader draw the conclusion.

> *Example:* "He straightened the two forks on the table so they lay parallel, then moved her wine glass an inch to the left, where he preferred it. 'You look tired,' he said, smiling. 'You should let me order for you.' She let him. She had learned that the meal went faster if she did."

**(c) Ending a chapter on a turn that forces the page-turn.**
A strong author does not close a chapter with a summary or a tidy beat of calm. The last line asks a question the reader can't leave alone, or delivers a revelation that recolours everything just read, or sets a mood that won't shake off. If the last line could be deleted without loss, it isn't working.

> *Example:* "She finally believed him — every careful word — and told him so, and watched the relief soften his face. It was only later, alone, replaying it, that she remembered he had known the dog's name before she ever said it."

## Research Rule

*(Heading kept as `## Research Rule` for structural consistency across the factory's Voice Bibles. In fiction, "research" means CONTINUITY and, for mysteries, FAIR PLAY.)*

Continuity is not optional. In fiction, a broken fact breaks the reader's trust as fast as a false statistic does in nonfiction.

- **Every fact about a character or the world must trace to a source.** Names, ages, appearances, relationships, timeline, geography, world/magic rules, professions, who-knows-what — before you write it, confirm it against `SERIES-FACTS.md` (series canon), the book's `FACTS.md`, and, for mysteries, `CLUE-MAP.md`. If it isn't locked yet and you're introducing it, add it to `FACTS.md` in your handoff so it becomes canon.
- **Never contradict established canon.** A character established as left-handed in Chapter 3 does not sign with their right in Chapter 40. Eye colour, a dead sibling, a town's distance from the coast, a rule about how the magic costs its user — once locked, binding. For series books, `SERIES-FACTS.md` outranks a single book's `FACTS.md`; Book 1 canon is canon for Book 2.
- **A character cannot know what they have not learned on the page.** Knowledge state is a fact like any other. Track who witnessed what. If neither POV saw an event, the reader learns it only through testimony, evidence, or implication — never by narrator fiat.
- **Fair-play rule for mysteries — clues on the page before the reveal.** Every piece of information the detective uses to solve the crime must have been shown to the reader, in the text, before the solution. No private discoveries the reader never saw. The reveal must make a careful reader think "I should have seen that," never "that came from nowhere." Test each chapter: rereading it knowing the killer, could the reader find the clue you planted? If not, plant it. (This reinforces — does not replace — the murder-mystery-writer's Fair-Play Contract and CLUE-MAP system.)
- **Real-world facts used in fiction must be accurate.** A real date, a real place, a real procedure, a real historical detail must be right — never contradict a value locked in `FACTS.md`, and don't invent a real-world fact you haven't verified.
- **When continuity is unclear, stop and check — don't guess.** Inventing a "fact" that later contradicts canon costs more to unwind than a moment's check. If the source files don't settle it, flag the open question in your handoff rather than locking a guess.
- **book-reviewer enforcement:** any contradiction of `SERIES-FACTS.md` / `FACTS.md` / `CLUE-MAP.md`, any character knowing something they couldn't know, or any mystery reveal that rests on a clue never shown to the reader, blocks the chapter until fixed.
