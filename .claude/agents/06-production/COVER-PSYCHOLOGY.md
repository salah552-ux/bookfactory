# Cover Psychology — What Sells a Book on Amazon KDP
## Required reading for design-agent before any cover decision

This file is the source of truth for cover commercial decisions. It is not subject to aesthetic override. Every cover the design-agent produces must pass the Section 9 checklist before being presented to the user.

All reader and competitor claims in this file are grounded in two source files for the current book — `MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md` and `COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md`. Where a claim is general design principle rather than book-specific data, it is marked `[principle]`. No invented statistics appear in this document.

## 1. THE PURCHASE MOMENT — WHAT IS ACTUALLY HAPPENING

A reader is not browsing a bookshop. They are on their phone at 9pm. Their gut is bloated again. They have already tried the obvious things — removing gluten, taking a probiotic, the previous gut health book they were recommended (MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md §6 — Target Reader Profile). They are scrolling Amazon search results for "gut health" or "bloating relief." Each cover on their phone is roughly 80–120px wide. The thumb moves every 0.3–0.6 seconds `[principle: mobile scroll behaviour]`. The cover's job is not to look good — it is to make the thumb stop.

Everything the design-agent produces must be evaluated against this specific moment, not against a design portfolio or a desktop preview at full size.

**The three-layer purchase sequence:**
1. **Thumb stop** (under 1 second) — something in the cover registers at thumbnail size and interrupts the scroll
2. **Title read** (1–2 seconds) — the reader taps or pauses to read the title text. If it speaks to their specific situation, they tap through to the product page
3. **Blurb scan** (5–10 seconds) — they read the first two lines of the description. If those lines name their pain and promise a specific solution, they buy

The cover only has to win Step 1. The title wins Step 2. The blurb wins Step 3. Every design decision must be evaluated against Step 1 alone.

**Implication:** a cover that looks beautiful at full size in a design review but fails the thumb-stop test at 100px is a commercial failure regardless of its aesthetic quality. Beauty without a thumb-stop is decoration.

## 2. THE AMAZON SEARCH RESULTS PAGE — WHAT THE READER ACTUALLY SEES

Cover design without an understanding of the actual selling surface is design in a vacuum.

**Mobile search results (the dominant browsing context for this niche):**
- Amazon mobile shows covers approximately 80–120px wide, two per row on most phones, against a white background
- The cover image is on the left; the title text and price appear to the right
- The cover IS NOT accompanied by its title text in the same visual unit — the title text is a separate, smaller line
- The thumb scrolls vertically through approximately 4–6 covers per screen, then onto the next 4–6
- A reader on a 6.1" phone passing through "gut health" search results will scroll past 16–24 covers in approximately 6–8 seconds before deciding to slow down

**Desktop search results:**
- Covers display at approximately 160–200px wide
- 3 covers per row on standard widescreens, 4 on ultrawides
- Larger but more covers competing for attention simultaneously

**The competing field for this book (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §1):**
The reader's search results page will contain approximately 19 other gut health covers. Of the 20 self-pub and trad-pub titles harvested for this book:
- Multiple use blue, teal, or green clinical colour palettes
- Multiple use food photography (vegetables, plates, probiotic capsules)
- Multiple use the word "gut" in the title (15 of 20)
- Multiple position on "beginners" (4 of 20)
- Zero use the word "trigger" or "reset" — keyword and visual gap (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §5)

**Implication:** the design problem is not "design a beautiful cover" — it is "design a cover that does not pattern-match to the other 19 covers it will appear alongside." Differentiation is the strategy at this scale.

## 3. WHAT STOPS THE THUMB — VISUAL TRIGGER HIERARCHY

These are the visual elements that interrupt a scroll, ranked for the gut health short-guide context.

### Tier 1 — Reliable thumb-stops

- **High contrast at thumbnail.** A cover that resolves to either a dark mass or a light mass at 100px stops fewer thumbs than one with a clear light/dark contrast at the same size `[principle: contrast and saliency in peripheral vision]`. The title must be readable as a shape before the letters become legible.
- **Bold typography that fills the canvas.** When a compressed sans-serif (Bebas Neue, Oswald Bold) or a heavy display serif fills 60–80% of the cover, the typographic shape itself is the hook. The reader does not need to read it — they sense authority and specificity.
- **Colour that does not pattern-match the shelf.** If most competitors use blue/teal/green, a warm amber, deep terracotta or near-black cover stops the thumb because it does not look like its neighbours. Pattern interruption is the hook.

### Tier 2 — Conditional thumb-stops

- **A number in the title.** A numeral like "7" reads before words at thumbnail scale and signals a specific commitment, not a vague promise. Especially powerful when the numeral is larger than the surrounding type. ("7-Day Gut Reset" — the 7 should be the largest readable element.)
- **A single, strong symbolic element.** Not a photograph. A simple symbolic graphic that encodes the book's promise — a compass for direction-finding, a key for unlocking, a log page for tracking — provided it is decodable at 100px without explanation. If it requires more than half a second to interpret at thumbnail, remove it.

### Tier 3 — Never stop the thumb (specific to gut health niche)

- **Food photography.** Saturated visual cliché in this category (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §5). Thumb passes through.
- **Anatomical illustrations.** Gut diagrams, intestine cross-sections, microbiome bacteria visuals. Reads as clinical and impersonal at thumbnail. The reader who has tried clinical books is not stopped by a clinical visual.
- **Wellness pastels.** Sage green, soft cream, lavender pink. The reader has been disappointed by covers in this palette before — they associate the aesthetic with the generic protocols that failed them (MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md §6 — purchase emotion: "Desperation tempered by cautious hope... disappointed by previous gut health books").
- **Busy compositions.** Multiple visual elements competing for attention. Nothing dominates. At 100px the cover reads as visual mush.
- **Script, decorative, or thin/light-weight fonts.** Illegible at 100px. The title becomes a texture, not a hook.

## 4. THE READER AT PURCHASE — EMOTIONAL STATE FOR THIS BOOK

The gut health reader in MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md §6 is specific. The cover must speak to this reader, not a generic health-book reader.

**Who they are (verbatim from MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md):**
- Adults aged 25–55, primarily women
- Have experienced unexplained bloating, IBS-type symptoms, or persistent digestive discomfort for months or years
- Have already tried generic advice — removing gluten, reducing dairy, taking probiotics — without consistent relief
- Self-directed health researchers, not passive patients

**Their purchase emotion (verbatim from MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md):**
"Desperation tempered by cautious hope. They have been disappointed by previous gut health books but will try one more if the promise is specific enough."

**What this reader is NOT:**
- Not browsing for inspiration
- Not in a positive wellness-aspirational state
- Not a beginner (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §1 — the "beginners" framing alienates them)
- Not trusting of warmth-and-hope marketing — they have been let down by it before

**What this means for the cover:**

The cover must communicate **specificity and authority**, not warmth and hope. Every wellness cover in the category communicates warmth and hope. This reader has been let down by warmth and hope. They respond to precision and confidence.

The emotional promise the cover must encode in 0.3 seconds is: **"This was built for someone exactly like you — someone who has already tried the obvious things."**

**Specific emotional triggers for this reader (mapped from MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md and COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md):**
- "Find YOUR trigger" — personalisation language (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §5: "your" is absent from competitor titles)
- "7 days" — short, specific, achievable. Contrasts with the 4-week protocols they have already abandoned (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §2 Gap 4 — commitment length mismatch)
- "Reset" — implies reversibility (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §5 — zero competitor titles use "reset")
- A tracking, log, or diagnostic visual element — signals "this is a diagnostic tool, not another generic protocol" (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §2 Gap 1 — no diagnostic before the protocol)

## 5. COLOUR — TERRITORY AND STRATEGY

These are not universal design rules. They are specific to the gut health Amazon UK shelf as observed in COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md.

| Colour territory | Signal to this reader | Current market status |
|------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Clinical blue / teal | Medical, cold, generic | Saturated — pattern-matches the wellness shelf |
| Sage / mint green | Gentle, wellness, "healing" | Saturated — reader associates with disappointed expectations |
| Pastel pink / lavender | Feminine wellness, soft | Reads as low authority for this audience |
| Plain white with food photography | Recipe book, clinical FODMAP | Reader has seen this; thumb passes through |
| Warm amber / burnt sienna / terracotta | Earthy authority, warmth without softness | Available — competitive analysis identifies as visual gap (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §5) |
| Deep navy / near-black | Maximum authority, investigative non-fiction | Available — signals depth and seriousness |
| Cream / off-white | Approachable, editorial | Rare as primary cover background in this niche |

**For the gut health short-guide series:**
Warm earth tones (amber #d4830a, terracotta, ochre — or near-black with warm accents) communicate warmth (a personal issue; coldness alienates) combined with authority (they need to trust the protocol). The territory owns an empty lane on the shelf.

**Specific guidance from COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §5:**
> "Warm amber or ochre tones rather than clinical blue/green or food-photography white — communicates approachability without the wellness-book cliché."

**Series-specific design system note (from DESIGN-FEEDBACK.md):**
The Fix Your Gut for Good series anchor book uses deep navy (#0E1B2C) + cream (#F3EEE3) + oxblood (#7A1E1E) + gold (#C8A15A) and scored 47/50. This series DNA is locked. Individual book covers may shift the warm accent (amber, terracotta, ochre) for differentiation within the series, but the navy/cream/gold spine must remain.

**Do not change the colour territory because something else "looks nicer." The gap is the strategy.**

## 6. TYPOGRAPHY — DECISION RULES, NOT PRINCIPLES

Every font sends a signal. For this reader, the wrong font signal triggers the same skepticism that ruined the previous gut health book for them.

| Font / treatment | Signal | Use for this reader? |
|-----------------|--------|----------------------|
| Bebas Neue Bold (compressed, all-caps) | System, precision, tool | YES — signals practical protocol |
| Oswald Bold | Authority, directness, no-nonsense | YES — same signal |
| Libre Baskerville Bold | Editorial, literary, authoritative | YES — for series brand line and interior |
| Heavy serif display (e.g. Playfair Black) | Editorial weight, considered | YES — at scale |
| Rounded sans-serif (Quicksand, Comfortaa) | Approachable, gentle, soft | NO — reads as another wellness book |
| Script / handwritten | Personal, artisanal | NO — illegible at 100px, signals low authority |
| Thin / light weight (Roboto Light, etc.) | Minimal, premium | NO — disappears at thumbnail size |
| Italic display | Editorial, lyrical | NO — reduces thumbnail legibility |

### Decision rules for the title block

These rules eliminate guesswork. Apply them in order.

**Rule T1 — Title length determines treatment:**
- If the title is 1–4 words: single-line treatment at maximum canvas width. Title text occupies 60–80% of canvas width.
- If the title is 5–6 words: two-line break with the most powerful word on line 2, set 10–15% larger than line 1.
- If the title is 7+ words: split into title (3–5 words) and subtitle (the rest) — the subtitle is no longer part of the title block.

**Rule T2 — Numerals are the largest readable elements:**
- If the title contains a number (7-Day, 4-Week, 21-Day), the numeral is set 15–25% larger than the surrounding word. The numeral does the thumb-stop work.

**Rule T3 — Subtitle sizing:**
- Subtitle must be set at minimum 64px on a 1600×2560 canvas (DESIGN-FEEDBACK.md 2026-04-17 — "48px subtitle on 1600px canvas is unreadable at Amazon thumbnail size. Minimum 64px for subtitle, 70px+ preferred").
- Subtitle must wrap to a maximum of 2 lines (DESIGN-FEEDBACK.md 2026-04-17 — "3-line subtitle... scored lower than 2-line subtitle").
- Reduce horizontal padding (minimum 80px each side) before reducing font size to make the subtitle fit on 2 lines.

**Rule T4 — Author name:**
- Author name appears at the bottom in a contrasting accent colour (gold for the Fix Your Gut for Good series — DESIGN-FEEDBACK.md).
- Size: 36–48px. Not larger — the author is not the brand here, the series is.
- Flanked by thin horizontal rules (300px wide, 2px, 0.9 opacity — DESIGN-FEEDBACK.md v3 learning).

**Rule T5 — Series brand line:**
- Series name (e.g. "FIX YOUR GUT FOR GOOD") appears at the top of the cover, tracked uppercase, letter-spacing 0.3em, 28–32px, brand accent colour.
- This is the visual signal that ties book 2 to book 1. It is non-negotiable across the series.

## 7. PRICE/LENGTH SIGNAL — THE REVIEW-BOMB TRIGGER

MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md §5 states this directly: "Do not price above £2.99 for guides under 7,000 words — pricing/length mismatch is the documented primary review-bomb trigger in this category."

The cover is part of the price/length signal. If the cover looks like a 200-page reference book and the reader buys it expecting that, the 5,500-word short guide they receive triggers a 1-star review for length mismatch. The cover must match the product reality.

**Decision rules for cover/price/length matching:**

**Rule P1 — Match cover authority to the £2.99 ceiling, not above:**
For short guides (5,000–7,000 words) priced FREE → £0.99 → £2.99: design the cover to look like a confident, well-published £2.99 short guide. Typography-led, single strong visual idea, no busy reference-book composition. If the cover signals "£12.99 comprehensive reference," readers feel cheated by the length.

**Rule P2 — Do not signal length:**
- Avoid: detailed chapter teasers on the cover, "Includes 50+ recipes" style copy, "The Complete Guide to..." framing — all signal long-form reference.
- Use: a single strong promise that is achievable in a short guide ("Find Your Trigger in 48 Hours," "The 7-Day Reset," "Calm Your Gut in One Week").

**Rule P3 — Authority comes from typography, not density:**
A bold typography-led cover with a single visual idea signals authority at any price point. A dense cover with many elements signals "reference work" and triggers length expectations.

**Specific to short guides at the £0.99–£2.99 ladder (this book's positioning per MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md §5):**
Design for the £2.99 authority ceiling. If the cover looks worth £2.99, it will convert at every price point below it. If it looks worth £0.99, it will underperform even when free `[principle: perceived value precedes price comparison]`.

## 8. SERIES COMMERCIAL LOGIC — EVERY COVER IS AN ADVERTISEMENT FOR EVERY OTHER BOOK

Each book in the *Fix Your Gut for Good* series is a standalone product AND a billboard for every other book in the series. A reader who buys book 1 and recognises the series DNA on book 2's cover treats book 2 as the next chapter, not a separate purchase decision `[principle: brand recognition reduces purchase friction in the same vertical]`.

**Why this matters commercially:**
- A reader who reads book 1 to completion has demonstrated the book delivers value. When they encounter book 2 on their "more from this author" recommendation, the series-DNA visual recognition is a trust shortcut — they do not re-evaluate the author, only the topic relevance.
- A reader who has NOT read book 1 but sees book 2 on the shelf alongside book 1 with consistent design infers a back catalogue. A back catalogue signals authority.
- A reader who buys the £0.99 entry book and finishes it is the highest-converting audience for the £4.99–£9.99 anchor book in the series — but only if they recognise the anchor as the same brand (MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md §10 — CTA to *Fix Your Gut for Good* anchor).

### Series consistency rules (Fix Your Gut for Good)

**Rule S1 — Spine architecture:**
- Background colour: #0E1B2C deep navy across all books
- Cream text (#F3EEE3) for title and series brand
- Gold accent (#C8A15A) for author name and dividers
- Oxblood accent (#7A1E1E) for editorial marks (strikethrough, rules)

**Rule S2 — Layout grid:**
- Series brand line at top: same position, same size, same tracking on every book
- Author block at bottom: same position, same gold rule treatment, same name typography
- Title block fills the central two-thirds, treatment varies by book within the series typography system

**Rule S3 — Typography system:**
- Title font: Libre Baskerville Bold or Bebas Neue compressed sans (locked at series level)
- Author font: same across all books
- Series brand font: same across all books

**Rule S4 — Differentiation within the series:**
Each book may take its own warm accent within the navy/cream/gold spine — book 1 oxblood, book 2 could take terracotta, book 3 ochre. This lets each book differentiate at thumbnail while preserving series recognition.

**Rule S5 — What never changes:**
- Author name treatment (S.A. Ibrahim in gold at the bottom — DESIGN-FEEDBACK.md)
- Series brand line position (top, tracked uppercase)
- Navy/cream/gold colour spine

The series is the asset. Individual covers serve the series before they serve their own novelty.

## 9. THE GO/NO-GO CHECKLIST — BINARY TESTS BEFORE PRESENTATION

Before presenting any cover to the user, the design-agent must answer all 7 questions YES. Each question is a binary test with a specific pass criterion — not a subjective judgment.

**Q1 — Thumbnail legibility test (100px squint).**
Resize the cover to 100×160px against a white background. At that size, are the title's main word(s) readable as a deliberate typographic shape (not just texture)?
- PASS: title is readable or its shape is clearly typography.
- FAIL: title resolves to indistinct texture, or contrast collapses to one tone.

**Q2 — Pattern interruption test.**
Place the cover thumbnail next to thumbnails of at least 5 competitor covers from COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §1. Does this cover look visually different from the cluster?
- PASS: the cover's dominant colour or composition does not match any other cover in the lineup.
- FAIL: the cover blends into the cluster (same colour territory, same compositional approach, same imagery type).

**Q3 — Competitor trap test (see Section 10 below).**
Cross-check the cover against the COMPETITOR TRAP list. Does the cover contain any element on that list?
- PASS: zero items from the trap list are present.
- FAIL: any item from the trap list is present — rebuild.

**Q4 — Reader-emotion test.**
Read the MARKET-INTELLIGENCE.md §6 purchase emotion again: "Desperation tempered by cautious hope." Does the cover signal specificity and authority, or warmth and hope?
- PASS: cover signals precision/authority/specificity to the experienced reader.
- FAIL: cover signals warmth/wellness/aspirational tone (the signature of the books that have already disappointed this reader).

**Q5 — Price/length match test.**
Does the cover visually match what a £2.99 short guide should look like for this niche (Section 7)?
- PASS: typography-led, single strong visual idea, no reference-book density.
- FAIL: cover signals comprehensive reference work, recipe book, or premium hardback.

**Q6 — Series DNA test.**
Does the cover follow the Fix Your Gut for Good series rules (Section 8)?
- PASS: navy/cream/gold spine present, series brand line at top, author block at bottom with gold rules, no publisher imprint.
- FAIL: any element of the series DNA is missing or substituted.

**Q7 — Title content test.**
Does the cover text match KDP-LISTING.md exactly — title, subtitle, author name, series brand?
- PASS: every word matches the listing copy character-for-character.
- FAIL: any paraphrase, abbreviation, or alternative wording is present.

A cover that answers YES to all seven is commercially viable. A cover that answers NO to any one of them is not — regardless of whether it looks attractive.

**The design-agent must include the 7 answers in DESIGN-PACKAGE.md before presenting the cover.**

## 10. COMPETITOR TRAP — VISUAL ELEMENTS THAT FAIL AUTOMATICALLY

These are the visual elements used by the top 20 gut health covers on Amazon UK (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §1 and §5). Any cover containing one or more of these elements pattern-matches to the competitors and fails Q3 of the checklist. The design-agent must verify zero traps are present before presenting any cover.

### TRAP LIST (any one = automatic rebuild)

1. **Food photography on the front cover.** Vegetables, plates of food, smoothies, fruit. Used across the category. (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §5: "zero competitors avoid food photography; this alone creates visual separation.")
2. **Gut / intestine / digestive anatomy illustrations.** Microbiome bacteria visuals, cross-sections of the gut, stomach diagrams.
3. **Probiotic / supplement bottle imagery.** Capsules, pills, supplement containers.
4. **Clinical blue or teal background** as the dominant colour (#0066cc through #00aaaa range and adjacent).
5. **Sage / mint green background** as the dominant colour. The wellness-disappointment palette.
6. **Pastel pink / lavender background** as the dominant colour. Reads low-authority.
7. **Plain white background with a single food photo centred.** The recipe-book cliché.
8. **The word "Beginners" in the title or subtitle on the cover.** Alienates the experienced reader who has tried and failed at beginner advice (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §1 — "the 'beginner' framing is its ceiling").
9. **"Complete Guide" framing on the cover.** Signals reference-book length; triggers price/length mismatch for a short guide (Section 7).
10. **"Science-Backed" / "Evidence-Based" / "Doctor-Recommended" stamp graphics on the cover.** The category overuses these claims and they no longer build trust without an actual cited authority figure (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §2 Gap 3).
11. **Generic "stars" or "rating" graphics** that aren't from a verified source — implies social proof that doesn't exist.
12. **Script or handwritten title fonts.** Illegible at 100px (Section 6).
13. **Thin or light-weight title fonts.** Disappear at thumbnail (Section 6).
14. **Three or more competing visual elements** in the central focal area. Busy composition = thumb passes through (Section 3).
15. **"Heal" or "Cure" verbs in title position.** Compliance risk + reader skepticism — these readers have heard "heal your gut" everywhere (COMPETITIVE-ANALYSIS.md §5: "Calm is both compliance-clean and emotionally resonant").
16. **Author photograph on the front cover.** Implies celebrity-author leverage that does not exist for a self-pub launch — signals desperation.
17. **Publisher imprint on the cover** (when BOOK-CONFIG.sh has no PUBLISHER_IMPRINT set — design-agent.md rule).
18. **Multiple emoji-like icons** (a checkmark + a clock + a leaf, for example). Signals low-effort design template.

If any item from this list is present, the cover fails Q3 of the checklist. Rebuild.
